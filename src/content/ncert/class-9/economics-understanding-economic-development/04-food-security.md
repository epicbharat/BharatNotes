**Why this chapter matters for UPSC:** Food security is one of the most directly policy-relevant topics in GS3. UPSC regularly asks about: the three dimensions of food security, FCI functions, MSP vs CIP distinction, NFSA 2013 coverage, problems with PDS, PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (free grains scheme), and India's rank on the Global Hunger Index. The COVID-19 period demonstrated food security's central importance when PMGKAY distributed free grains to ~80 crore people.

**Contemporary hook:** From January 2024, the Government of India merged the National Food Security Act (NFSA) subsidy with the **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY)** into a single unified scheme — providing **5 kg of free foodgrains** per person per month to approximately **81.35 crore beneficiaries** (out of the ~80 crore entitled under NFSA). This is now the world's largest free food distribution programme. UPSC Prelims and Mains 2025–26 will test this merger and its implications.

## PART 1 — Quick Reference Tables

### 📌 Key Fact: Three Dimensions of Food Security

| Dimension | Definition | India's Challenge |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Food Availability** | Adequate food physically available in the country — through domestic production + imports + buffer stocks | India is largely self-sufficient; exports rice and wheat; buffer stocks sometimes above norms |
| **Food Access (Economic Access)** | People have enough income or resources to buy/acquire food | Major challenge — hunger despite adequate availability; poor cannot afford food |
| **Food Absorption (Utilisation)** | Food consumed is properly utilised by the body; depends on safe water, sanitation, health care | India has high malnutrition despite adequate calorie intake — "hidden hunger" (micronutrient deficiency); sanitation gap |

### Food Corporation of India (FCI)

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Established** | 1965 |
| **Governing Act** | Food Corporations Act, 1964 |
| **Headquarters** | New Delhi |
| **Functions** | Procurement, storage, transportation, and distribution of foodgrains under the National Food Security Act; price support operations (MSP procurement) |
| **Procurement** | Mainly rice and wheat; from Punjab, Haryana, MP, Andhra Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, UP |
| **Storage capacity** | Over 80 million metric tonnes (owns + hired storage) |

### Key Prices in India's Food System

| Price | Full Form | Definition | Who Sets It |
|-------|----------|-----------|------------|
| **MSP** | Minimum Support Price | Minimum price at which government commits to buy produce from farmers; floor price to protect farmers | Cabinet Committee on Economic Affairs (CCEA), based on CACP recommendation |
| **CIP** | Central Issue Price | Price at which FCI sells grain to states for distribution through PDS | Government of India |
| **Retail (PDS) Price** | — | Price at which beneficiaries buy from ration shops (below CIP) | State governments subsidise further |
| **Economic Cost** | — | FCI's total cost: MSP paid + operational costs; determines government's food subsidy | FCI |

**The food subsidy = Economic Cost − CIP (the difference is borne by the government)**

### National Food Security Act, 2013 (NFSA)

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Passed** | September 2013 |
| **Coverage** | **75% of rural population**; **50% of urban population** — combined ~67% of total population (~80 crore) |
| **Entitlement** | 5 kg of foodgrains per person per month |
| **Subsidised price** | Rice: ₹3/kg; Wheat: ₹2/kg; Coarse cereals: ₹1/kg |
| **Priority Households (PHH)** | Most beneficiaries; entitled to 5 kg/person/month |
| **Antyodaya Anna Yojana (AAY)** | Poorest of the poor; 35 kg/household per month (higher quantity) |
| **Other provisions** | Maternity benefit (₹6,000); midday meals; ICDS nutrition support |

### PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY)

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Original launch** | April 2020 (during COVID-19) — free 5 kg additional grain beyond NFSA entitlement |
| **Current status** | From January 2024, merged with NFSA; now provides 5 kg **free** (₹0 cost) per person per month to all NFSA beneficiaries |
| **Beneficiaries** | ~81.35 crore persons |
| **Ministry** | Ministry of Consumer Affairs, Food and Public Distribution |
| **Significance** | World's largest free food distribution programme; grains previously sold at ₹1–3/kg are now free |

### Global Hunger Index (GHI)

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Published by** | Concern Worldwide (Ireland) and Welthungerhilfe (Germany) |
| **Frequency** | Annual (October) |
| **Methodology** | Combines 4 indicators: undernourishment, child stunting, child wasting, child mortality |
| **India's rank** | **105 out of 127 countries** (GHI 2023) — "Serious" hunger category |

## PART 2 — Chapter Narrative

### What Is Food Security?

The United Nations' definition: Food security exists when **all people, at all times, have physical and economic access to sufficient, safe, and nutritious food to meet their dietary needs and food preferences for an active and healthy life.**

This definition has four pillars: **Availability, Access, Utilisation, and Stability** (the last is about consistency over time). The Class 9 NCERT focuses on the first three.

**Why does hunger persist when India produces enough food?**
This is the central puzzle. India is one of the world's largest producers of rice, wheat, pulses, milk, and vegetables. It is a net exporter of foodgrains in many years. Yet:
- 189 million Indians are undernourished (FAO 2023)
- 35.5% of children under 5 are stunted (NFHS-5, 2019–21)
- 19.3% of children under 5 are wasted (NFHS-5)
- India ranks 105/127 on the Global Hunger Index 2023

The answer: **Access** and **Utilisation** failures, not Availability failure. Poor people cannot afford food even when it is available. And even when food is consumed, poor sanitation and hygiene mean nutrients are poorly absorbed.

### India's Food Production Journey: From Scarcity to Surplus

**Pre-independence and early independence:** India was chronically food insecure. The **Bengal Famine of 1943** killed 2–3 million people — a result of wartime food diversion, hoarding, and distribution failures (not purely a production failure, as economist Amartya Sen demonstrated — he won the 1998 Nobel Prize partly for this analysis).

At independence in 1947, India's food situation was dire. PL-480 (US food aid) saved India from famine in the 1950s–60s. **"Ship to Mouth"** described India's dependence on US grain imports.

**Green Revolution (1960s–70s):** High-yielding variety (HYV) seeds (especially for wheat and rice), chemical fertilisers, irrigation, and support prices transformed India from a food-deficit country to a food-surplus country. India's wheat production rose from ~11 million tonnes (1966) to over 100 million tonnes today.

**Current situation:** India's total foodgrain production in 2023–24 was approximately **310–320 million tonnes** (rice + wheat + coarse cereals + pulses). India exports rice (~20 million tonnes) and wheat. Yet hunger persists — due to distribution failures and poverty.

### The Public Distribution System (PDS)

The **Public Distribution System** is India's main mechanism for distributing subsidised food to the poor through a network of **"ration shops"** (Fair Price Shops — FPS).

**How PDS works:**
1. Government announces **MSP** for farmers
2. **FCI** (and state agencies) procure at MSP from mandis
3. FCI stores grain in central pool
4. States request allocation; FCI releases grain at **Central Issue Price (CIP)**
5. States distribute through Fair Price Shops at further subsidised retail prices
6. Beneficiaries identified under NFSA purchase at ₹1–3/kg (or now FREE under merged PMGKAY)

### 💡 Explainer: Problems with PDS and Reforms

The PDS has faced chronic problems:
1. **Ghost beneficiaries / fake ration cards:** Cards issued to people who don't exist or aren't eligible
2. **Leakages:** Grain diverted from ration shops to the open market (black market)
3. **Exclusion errors:** Genuine poor excluded from beneficiary lists (estimation issues, bureaucratic delays)
4. **Inclusion errors:** Non-poor included in beneficiary lists
5. **Poor quality grain:** Sometimes inferior quality grain distributed
6. **Irregular supply:** Ration shops sometimes don't receive their quota

**Reforms:**
- **End-to-End Computerisation:** FPS linked to central servers; biometric authentication via Aadhaar
- **One Nation One Ration Card (ONORC):** Migrant workers can access PDS at any FPS in India (not just their home state); dramatically improved portability
- **Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) for food:** Pilot in some states — cash transferred instead of grain
- **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (free grains):** Reduces the incentive to divert grain (less valuable if free)

### 🎯 UPSC Connect: One Nation One Ration Card

**One Nation One Ration Card (ONORC)** — launched in 2019, fully implemented across all states/UTs by 2022 — allows any NFSA beneficiary to buy subsidised grain at any Fair Price Shop in India using their Aadhaar-linked ration card.

This is critically important for **migrant workers** who move between states for work (construction workers, domestic workers, farm labourers). Previously, a migrant from Bihar working in Mumbai had no access to ration shop benefits in Mumbai — they were excluded from food security while away from home.

ONORC has recorded over 100 crore transactions since implementation. It was one of India's welfare reforms highlighted by the Finance Commission and various policy reports as a success.

### Food Corporation of India (FCI)

The **FCI was established in 1965** to operationalise the National Food Policy. It was created under the **Food Corporations Act, 1964**. FCI operates at the intersection of farmer support (MSP procurement) and consumer protection (distributing affordable food through PDS).

**Three functions of FCI:**
1. **Price Support Operations:** Procure grain at MSP from farmers, preventing prices from falling below MSP
2. **Buffer Stock Maintenance:** Maintain strategic reserves of grain (buffer stock norms: minimum and maximum limits)
3. **Distribution:** Supply grain to states for distribution through PDS

**Buffer stock:** India maintains a **central pool** of foodgrain stocks. The government sets **minimum buffer stock norms** (the minimum to be maintained for food security) and **strategic reserve** norms. In years of high procurement (especially Punjab/Haryana for wheat/rice), the buffer stock often exceeds norms — creating storage problems and grain wastage.

**Buffer stock norms** (April 1 requirement): Wheat 75.2 lakh MT; Rice 135.4 lakh MT (approximate; revised periodically by government).

### Minimum Support Price (MSP)

The **MSP** is the price at which the central government (through FCI and state agencies) commits to buy agricultural produce from farmers. It is announced before sowing season so farmers know the floor price for their crop.

**How MSP is fixed:**
- Based on recommendations of the **Commission for Agricultural Costs and Prices (CACP)**
- CACP considers: cost of production (A2 — actual expenses; A2+FL — actual expenses + family labour imputed; C2 — comprehensive cost including land rent), demand-supply situation, inter-crop price parity
- Approved by Cabinet Committee on Economic Affairs (CCEA)

**MSP for Kharif 2024–25 (selected crops):**
- Paddy (Common): ₹2,300/quintal
- Cotton (Medium staple): ₹7,121/quintal
- Groundnut: ₹6,760/quintal
- These are announced around the start of kharif sowing season (May–June)

**Controversies:**
- MSP is only effective where government procurement infrastructure exists (mainly Punjab, Haryana, MP for wheat/rice)
- Farmers in south India, east India often don't benefit from MSP due to poor procurement
- 2020–21 farm laws protests demanded **legal guarantee of MSP** (the laws were repealed in November 2021 after prolonged protests)

### Food Insecurity Groups

Despite overall improvement, certain groups remain chronically food insecure:

**Rural poor:** Agricultural labourers, marginal farmers (below 2.5 acres), bonded/migrant labourers

**Urban poor:** Slum dwellers, street vendors, daily wage workers, homeless population

**Women and girls:** Gender discrimination in intra-household food distribution; women often eat last; lactating and pregnant women face higher nutritional risk

**Children:** Especially under-5; malnutrition causes stunting (low height-for-age) and wasting (low weight-for-height); irreversible cognitive damage from early malnutrition

**Disaster-affected populations:** Floods, droughts, cyclones cause temporary food insecurity even in food-surplus regions

**Scheduled Castes and Tribes:** Structurally excluded from economic opportunities; higher poverty → higher food insecurity

### 📌 Key Fact: Malnutrition vs Hunger

| Concept | Definition | India Data |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Hunger (calorie deficiency)** | Insufficient calorie intake | 189 million undernourished (FAO 2023) |
| **Stunting** | Low height-for-age; chronic malnutrition | 35.5% of children under 5 (NFHS-5) |
| **Wasting** | Low weight-for-height; acute malnutrition | 19.3% of children under 5 (NFHS-5) |
| **Hidden hunger** | Micronutrient deficiency (iron, iodine, Vitamin A, zinc) without overt hunger | Affects majority of India's population |
| **Obesity** | Excess energy intake; undernutrition and obesity can co-exist | Rising in urban India; dual burden of malnutrition |

**Amartya Sen's contribution:** His work showed that famines are rarely caused by food *shortage* — they are caused by *entitlement failures* (people lose their income/access to food). The 1943 Bengal Famine was a policy failure, not a crop failure. His 1998 Nobel Prize was partly for this theory of "entitlement approach to famines."

### Midday Meal Scheme

**PM POSHAN Shakti Nirman** (formerly Midday Meal Scheme, 1995):
- Provides free cooked lunch to children in government schools (Class I–VIII)
- ~11.80 crore children benefited (2022–23)
- Improves school enrolment, attendance, and nutrition simultaneously
- One of India's most successful food-linked social interventions
- Funded by central government (food cost) + state government (cooking cost, cook wages)

### 🔗 Beyond the Book: India vs Global Hunger Index — The Controversy

India's government disputes the GHI ranking (105/127, "Serious" category, 2023) on methodological grounds:
1. **Undernourishment estimate:** The GHI uses a FAO estimate of undernourishment that India says is based on a small, unrepresentative survey of 3,000 households
2. **PMGKAY not counted:** The massive free grain distribution under PMGKAY reportedly not adequately reflected
3. **Improving indicators not captured:** India's IMR (25/1,000) and stunting (35.5%) have both improved significantly per NFHS-5, but critics say the pace is still slow

**International perspective:** The debate is important — if India's methodological objections are valid, the GHI overstates hunger; if not, India's self-assessment understates it. UPSC candidates should know both positions.

## PART 3 — Frameworks & Mnemonics

### Three Dimensions of Food Security — "AAU"

**A**vailability (production + imports + stocks) → **A**ccess (economic access = income to buy food) → **U**tilisation (absorption by body — depends on health, water, sanitation)

"**AAU** = All About Utilisation" — all three must work for food security

### FCI Functions — Three "Ps"

**P**rocure (at MSP) → **P**reserve (buffer stocks) → **P**rovide (to PDS/NFSA)

### NFSA Coverage Numbers

**75% rural + 50% urban = ~67% of population = ~80 crore beneficiaries**

"**75-50-67**" — Rural 75, Urban 50, Overall 67

### PDS Price Chain

**MSP** (farmer gets) → **Economic Cost** (FCI's total cost) → **CIP** (FCI sells to state) → **PDS Price** (consumer pays at ration shop, now FREE under PMGKAY)

"**MSP → EC → CIP → Free**" — each step is lower than previous (subsidy absorbed by government)

## Exam Strategy

**Prelims approach:** Know FCI establishment year (1965), NFSA 2013 coverage (75% rural, 50% urban, 5 kg/person/month), PMGKAY (free grains from Jan 2024, 81 crore beneficiaries), MSP (floor price for farmers, set by CCEA on CACP recommendation), Global Hunger Index 2023 (India: 105/127, "Serious"). Know the distinction between MSP (farmer price) and CIP (government selling price to states).

**Mains approach (GS3):** Key questions: Is PDS effective? Evaluate reform (One Nation One Ration Card, digitisation). India's food paradox (surpluses but malnutrition). Amartya Sen's entitlement theory. PMGKAY as world's largest free food programme — fiscal cost and benefits. GHI controversy — government position vs international assessment. DBT vs in-kind transfer debate for food.

**High-yield distinctions:** Hunger (calorie deficit) vs malnutrition (can be calorie adequate but micronutrient deficient — "hidden hunger"); stunting (chronic, long-term) vs wasting (acute, short-term); MSP (procurement price from farmers) vs CIP (issue price to states) vs PDS retail price (consumer pays).

## Previous Year Questions (PYQs)

### Prelims

**1.** The Food Corporation of India (FCI) was established under:
(a) Food Security Act, 1966   (b) Food Corporations Act, 1964
(c) Essential Commodities Act, 1955   (d) Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954

*Answer: (b) — FCI was established under the Food Corporations Act, 1964; it began operations in 1965.*

**2.** Under the National Food Security Act, 2013, what quantity of foodgrains is an eligible individual (Priority Household) entitled to per month?
(a) 3 kg   (b) 5 kg   (c) 7 kg   (d) 10 kg

*Answer: (b) — Priority Household beneficiaries are entitled to 5 kg per person per month.*

**3.** One Nation One Ration Card (ONORC) was introduced primarily to benefit:
(a) Farmers to sell their produce across state borders
(b) Migrant workers to access PDS benefits in any state
(c) Urban consumers to buy subsidised grain online
(d) Exporters to qualify for food subsidy

*Answer: (b) — ONORC was introduced to allow migrant workers and other NFSA beneficiaries to access their subsidised grain entitlement at any Fair Price Shop across India.*

### Mains

**1.** "India's food security architecture — comprising FCI, MSP, PDS, and the NFSA — has both strengths and systemic weaknesses. Critically evaluate and suggest reforms." *(GS3, 250 words)*

*Answer framework:* Strengths (large buffer stocks, NFSA coverage of 80 crore, PMGKAY free grains, ONORC portability) → Weaknesses (FCI storage losses, MSP benefiting only Punjab/Haryana, PDS leakages, exclusion errors in beneficiary lists, fiscal cost of ₹2 lakh crore+ food subsidy) → Reforms (further digitisation, DBT debate, direct income support like PM-KISAN instead of price support, targeted nutrition programmes, improving last-mile delivery).

**2.** Explain the concept of food security. How does India's Global Hunger Index ranking reveal the gap between food availability and food security? *(GS3, 200 words)*

*Answer framework:* Define food security (3 dimensions — availability, access, utilisation) → India's paradox: world's 2nd largest rice exporter, yet 105/127 on GHI → Sen's entitlement theory (hunger = access failure, not availability failure) → Children's malnutrition data (stunting 35.5%, wasting 19.3%) → Schemes (NFSA, PMGKAY, Midday Meal) addressing access → Sanitation and health (Swachh Bharat, ICDS) addressing utilisation → Government's GHI methodology disputes → Conclusion: multi-pronged approach needed beyond just grain distribution.
