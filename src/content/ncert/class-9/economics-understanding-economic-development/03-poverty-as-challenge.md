**Why this chapter matters for UPSC:** Poverty is a core GS3 topic and connects heavily to GS2 (social justice, welfare schemes). UPSC tests: poverty line methodology (Tendulkar vs Rangarajan), Head Count Ratio, multi-dimensional poverty, state-wise differences, causes of poverty, and anti-poverty programmes. The dramatic decline in poverty — from ~55% in 1973 to single digits by recent estimates — is one of India's most important developmental achievements and a frequent Mains topic.

**Contemporary hook:** India's multidimensional poverty headcount fell from **29.17% (2013–14) to 11.28% (2022–23)** according to NITI Aayog's National Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI) 2023, lifting approximately **415 million people** out of multidimensional poverty over the decade. This achievement — the fastest poverty reduction in modern history for a large country — is directly testable in UPSC Mains 2025–26.

## PART 2 — Chapter Narrative

### What Is Poverty?

Poverty is the inability to meet minimum basic needs — food, clothing, shelter, health care, and education. But defining and measuring poverty is more complex than it appears:

**Absolute poverty:** Below a fixed minimum standard of consumption/income (poverty line approach). India's poverty line is set at the minimum consumption needed to meet basic needs.

**Relative poverty:** Below the average or median income of a society (used more in developed countries). India primarily uses absolute poverty measures.

**Multidimensional poverty:** Goes beyond income to measure deprivation across health (nutrition, child mortality), education (years of schooling, school attendance), and living standards (cooking fuel, sanitation, drinking water, electricity, housing, assets).

### The Poverty Line: India's Approach

The traditional Indian approach (from the Planning Commission era) set the poverty line based on **caloric intake**:
- **Rural:** 2,400 kilocalories per person per day
- **Urban:** 2,100 kilocalories per person per day (urban people do less physical work)

The minimum food consumption needed to meet these caloric requirements was monetised to set the poverty line. States with higher costs of living have their own poverty lines.

### 💡 Explainer: Why Different Committees Give Different Poverty Numbers

**Lakdawala Committee (1993, used until 2011):** Used caloric norm as the sole criterion. Did not adequately account for rising costs of health, education, and transport. Critics said the line was "too low" and underestimated poverty.

**Tendulkar Committee (2009):** Shifted from calories-only to a broader consumption basket — including non-food expenditure on health and education alongside food. Used NSSO's household consumption survey data. Gave a more realistic but still controversial picture. Estimated poverty at **21.9% in 2011–12** — higher than Lakdawala would have shown.

**Rangarajan Committee (2014):** Argued Tendulkar was still too low. Used a broader basket and higher caloric norms. Found poverty at **29.5% in 2011–12** — significantly higher than Tendulkar. This committee's recommendations were not adopted for official planning purposes.

**NITI Aayog MPI (2021, 2023):** Following the UNDP's global MPI methodology. Measures 10 indicators across 3 dimensions: health (nutrition, child mortality), education (years of schooling, school attendance), and living standards (cooking fuel, sanitation, drinking water, electricity, housing, assets). This is now India's most policy-relevant poverty measure.

### Head Count Ratio (HCR)

The **Head Count Ratio (HCR)** is simply the percentage of the population living below the poverty line. It is the most commonly cited poverty statistic.

**Limitation of HCR:** It tells you *how many* people are poor but not *how poor* they are. Two countries could have the same HCR but one could have people just marginally below the line (shallow poverty) while the other has people in deep deprivation (deep poverty). The **Poverty Gap** measures this depth.

### Poverty Trends in India: A Story of Progress

India has achieved one of the fastest poverty reductions in history:

**Phase 1 (1947–1980): Poverty stagnated.** Despite independence and planned development, poverty remained stubbornly high (~50–55%). Factors: low agricultural productivity, slow industrial growth, population growth exceeding income growth.

**Phase 2 (1980–2004): Gradual decline.** Green Revolution raised agricultural productivity; new economic policies; early liberalisation (1991 reforms). Poverty fell from ~55% (1973) to ~27% (2004–05).

**Phase 3 (2004–2024): Accelerated decline.** MGNREGA (2005), PMJDY financial inclusion (2014), PM-KISAN, direct benefit transfers, food security (NFSA 2013), PMAY housing, Ayushman Bharat health insurance — comprehensive welfare architecture reduced poverty sharply.

### 🎯 UPSC Connect: India's Poverty Reduction Achievement

The NITI Aayog National MPI 2023 documents India's remarkable achievement:
- **415 million people** escaped multidimensional poverty between 2013–14 and 2022–23
- This is the **largest poverty reduction achieved by any single country** in that period
- The **incidence** of multidimensional poverty fell from 29.17% to **11.28%**
- Even **Aspirational Districts** (formerly "backward districts") showed the fastest improvement — showing targeted governance works

This achievement was cited by the UN Secretary-General and UNDP as a global development success. UPSC Mains has asked about this achievement repeatedly.

### Vulnerable Groups: Who Remains Poor?

Despite overall progress, certain groups face disproportionate poverty:

**Scheduled Castes (SCs):** Face structural discrimination, limited access to productive assets (land), historically denied educational opportunities. MPI headcount significantly higher than national average.

**Scheduled Tribes (STs):** Forest-dependent communities, often excluded from development benefits. Poverty rates in tribal-dominated districts (Jharkhand, Odisha, MP, Chhattisgarh) among the highest in India.

**Women-headed households:** Women heading households (widows, abandoned, divorced, single mothers) face multiple deprivations — lower income, less access to credit, more care burden.

**Urban poor (slum dwellers):** Often migrants from rural areas; precarious informal employment; lack access to basic services despite living in cities.

**Children:** Child malnutrition (measured by NFHS-5): 35.5% of children under 5 are stunted (low height-for-age); 19.3% are wasted (low weight-for-height). Child poverty is not just about income but about nutrition outcomes.

### Causes of Poverty in India

Poverty in India has multiple inter-connected causes:

**Historical causes:**
- **Colonial exploitation:** The colonial economy drained India's wealth through the "drain of wealth" (critiqued by Dadabhai Naoroji in *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India*). De-industrialisation (decline of Indian textiles, crafts), forced specialisation in raw material export, destruction of self-sufficient village economies.
- This created structural poverty that persisted even after independence.

**Economic causes:**
- **Low agricultural productivity:** Small and fragmented land holdings, poor irrigation, insufficient technology
- **Slow employment growth:** Industry did not absorb surplus agricultural labour fast enough
- **Unequal distribution of land and assets:** Land concentration in few hands; landless labourers with no assets

**Social causes:**
- **Caste discrimination:** Dalits and tribal communities historically denied education, property, and economic opportunity
- **Gender discrimination:** Women's low education and labour force participation reduces household income and well-being
- **Large family size:** More dependents per earning adult; lower savings and investment in each child

**Governance causes:**
- Poor targeting of welfare programmes; leakages in PDS; corruption

### 📌 Key Fact: Anti-Poverty Programmes — Key Features

| Programme | Launch | Ministry | Key Feature |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------------|
| **MGNREGA** | 2005 (Act); 2006 (implementation) | Rural Development | Legal right to 100 days unskilled work; rural households |
| **PMAY-G** (Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana — Gramin) | 2016 | Rural Development | Housing for All: free pucca houses for rural poor |
| **PMAY-U** (Urban) | 2015 | Housing and Urban Affairs | Affordable housing for urban poor |
| **PMJDY** (Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana) | 2014 | Finance | Zero-balance bank accounts; financial inclusion; ~54 crore accounts (2024) |
| **PM Garib Kalyan Anna Yojana (PMGKAY)** | 2020 (extended indefinitely from Jan 2024) | Consumer Affairs/Food | Free 5 kg foodgrain per month to ~81 crore beneficiaries |
| **NFSA (National Food Security Act)** | 2013 | Consumer Affairs/Food | Legal right to subsidised food; 75% rural, 50% urban population; ₹1–3/kg |
| **PM-KISAN** | 2019 | Agriculture | ₹6,000/year direct income support to all farmers with cultivable land |
| **Ayushman Bharat — PMJAY** | 2018 | Health | ₹5 lakh/year health insurance to ~50 crore beneficiaries (poorest 40%) |
| **DBT (Direct Benefit Transfer)** | 2013 (systematic) | Finance | Transfer subsidies/benefits directly to Aadhaar-linked bank accounts; reduces leakages |

### 🔗 Beyond the Book: Global Poverty Context

**World Bank international poverty lines (2017 PPP, updated 2022):**
- **Extreme poverty:** $2.15/person/day — globally, ~700 million people remain in extreme poverty (~9% of world population)
- **Lower-middle-income poverty:** $3.65/person/day
- **Upper-middle-income poverty:** $6.85/person/day

India, given its lower-middle-income status, is measured against the $2.15 line for international comparisons. India's share of the global extreme poor has fallen dramatically — from ~500 million (2000) to under 70–80 million (2022 estimates).

**Global context:** Sub-Saharan Africa now accounts for the majority of the world's extreme poor. The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–21) reversed global poverty reduction gains — for the first time in 20 years, extreme poverty rose. India's pre-pandemic welfare programmes (especially PDS/PMGKAY) cushioned the impact on Indian poor.

## PART 3 — Frameworks & Mnemonics

### Poverty Measurement — Three Levels

**Income poverty** (money income below poverty line) → **Consumption poverty** (Tendulkar/Rangarajan — what you consume) → **Multidimensional poverty** (NITI MPI — health, education, living standards)

"**ICM**" — "**I**ncome, **C**onsumption, **M**ultidimensional" — each progressively more comprehensive

### Key Committees on Poverty Line

**L**akdawala (1993, calories only) → **T**endulkar (2009, consumption basket, 21.9% in 2011–12) → **R**angarajan (2014, broader basket, 29.5% in 2011–12)

"**LTR**" — "**L**ow **T**o **R**eal" (each progressively more realistic/higher poverty count)

### BIMARU vs High Performing States

**BIMARU states** (high poverty): Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh (+ Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha)

**Low poverty states:** Kerala (~0.71% MPI), Goa, Himachal Pradesh, Tamil Nadu

The BIMARU states have the double burden: large populations AND high poverty.

### Anti-Poverty Measures — UPSC Checklist

- **Employment:** MGNREGA (rural), Start-Up India (entrepreneurship)
- **Housing:** PMAY-G (rural), PMAY-U (urban)
- **Food security:** NFSA 2013, PMGKAY (free grains), Midday Meal Scheme
- **Financial inclusion:** PMJDY (bank accounts), DBT (direct transfers)
- **Health:** Ayushman Bharat PMJAY (health insurance)
- **Income support:** PM-KISAN (farmers), Old Age Pension schemes

## Exam Strategy

**Prelims approach:** Know poverty line methodologies (Tendulkar — 21.9% in 2011–12; Rangarajan — 29.5% in 2011–12; NITI MPI 2023 — 11.28%). Know NFSA 2013 coverage (75% rural, 50% urban). Know MGNREGA (100 days, 2005, rural households). Know World Bank extreme poverty line ($2.15/day, 2017 PPP).

**Mains approach (GS3):** The 415 million poverty reduction story is the most important current statistic. Use NITI Aayog MPI 2023 data. Questions: causes of poverty (multi-factor); state-wise variation (BIMARU vs Kerala model); effectiveness of anti-poverty programmes; role of social discrimination in perpetuating poverty; future challenges (climate change impact on poor, informal worker vulnerability).

**High-yield distinctions:** Tendulkar (21.9%) vs Rangarajan (29.5%) — both for 2011–12, different methodologies; Head Count Ratio (how many) vs Poverty Gap (how deep); Absolute poverty vs Multidimensional poverty.
