**Why this chapter matters for UPSC:** Chapter 2 introduces the concept of human capital — foundational to GS3 (Indian Economy) and GS2 (social justice) questions on education, health, skills development, employment, and India's demographic dividend. UPSC regularly asks about unemployment types in India, the gender gap in labour force participation, MGNREGA effectiveness, and Skill India outcomes. The demographic dividend window (when India's working-age population is largest) is a live policy issue directly rooted in this chapter.

**Contemporary hook:** India surpassed China as the world's most populous country in 2023 (~1.44 billion). Whether this becomes a dividend or a burden depends entirely on the quality of human capital — education, health, and skills — which is exactly what this chapter addresses. The Union Budget 2024–25 made skill development and education among its top five priorities, citing India's demographic window (expected to last until ~2050).

## PART 1 — Quick Reference Tables

### 📌 Key Fact: Human Capital vs Physical Capital

| Dimension | Physical Capital | Human Capital |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Nature | Tangible assets (machinery, buildings, infrastructure) | Intangible (education, skills, health, knowledge) |
| Creation | Investment in physical assets | Investment in education and health |
| Mobility | Cannot move on its own | Moves with the person |
| Depreciation | Physical wear and tear | Can be maintained/upgraded through lifelong learning |
| Key insight | Drives growth in short run | Drives sustainable, inclusive long-run growth |

### Types of Unemployment in India

| Type | Definition | Who It Affects | Example |
|------|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Seasonal unemployment** | People unable to find work during off-season | Agricultural workers; tourism workers | Farmers unemployed after harvest; construction workers in monsoon season |
| **Disguised unemployment** | More people employed in a job than required; marginal productivity = zero | Agriculture predominantly | 5 people doing work that 2 can do efficiently on a farm |
| **Structural unemployment** | Workers lack skills demanded by the economy | Educated youth in traditional economy; workers replaced by technology | Engineering graduates unable to find jobs due to skill mismatch; mill workers when textile mills close |
| **Cyclical unemployment** | Due to economic slowdown/recession | All sectors | Job losses during COVID-19 lockdowns, 2008 financial crisis |
| **Frictional unemployment** | Between jobs (transitional) | All workers | Time spent looking for a new job after leaving the old one |
| **Educated unemployment** | Young graduates unable to find jobs matching their qualifications | Urban educated youth | Overcrowded fields like BA/BSc with no matching jobs |

**India's most characteristic types:** Disguised unemployment (agriculture), seasonal unemployment (agriculture and construction), and educated/structural unemployment (urban youth with degrees but no skills).

## PART 2 — Chapter Narrative

### People as an Asset, Not a Liability

Traditional economics treated land, labour, and capital as the three factors of production. Modern economics — especially after the work of economists like **Theodore Schultz** and **Gary Becker** — recognises that *human beings, when educated and healthy, are the most important productive asset*.

This is the concept of **human capital** — the stock of skills, knowledge, and health embodied in people, built through investment in education and health care.

The chapter's central argument: A large population is not automatically a burden. If that population is educated, skilled, and healthy, it becomes **people as a resource** — a productive economic force. If it is uneducated, unskilled, and unhealthy, it becomes a burden on the economy.

### 💡 Explainer: Human Development vs Human Capital

| Dimension | Human Capital (Economic lens) | Human Development (UNDP lens) |
|-----------|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Concern | How people contribute to economic production | How people live a full, dignified life |
| Measurement | Returns to education/skills in the labour market | HDI (Health + Education + Income) |
| Limitation | Treats people as means to economic ends | Broader; includes freedom, agency, gender equality |
| Key index | No single index | Human Development Index (HDI) |

India's **HDI rank** in 2022 (UNDP Human Development Report 2023/24): **134 out of 193 countries** — placing India in the **medium human development** category with an HDI value of 0.644.

### Economic Activities: Primary, Secondary, Tertiary

**Primary activities:** Directly use natural resources. India still has ~45% of its workforce in agriculture (primary sector), though agriculture's share of GDP has fallen to ~18–20%. This divergence — a large share of labour in a sector with a small share of GDP — is the fundamental structural problem of Indian agriculture.

**Secondary activities:** Manufacturing and industry. India's target is to raise manufacturing to 25% of GDP (currently ~15–16%) through the **Make in India** initiative (launched 2014) and Production Linked Incentive (PLI) schemes.

**Tertiary activities:** Services. India's IT sector, financial services, and trade have driven the tertiary sector to ~55% of GDP. India is a global leader in IT services (Infosys, TCS, Wipro — $250+ billion industry) and business process outsourcing (BPO).

### Quality of Population

The economic contribution of people depends not just on their number but their **quality** — their education, health, and skills:

**Education:**
- Higher education increases productivity (more skilled workers)
- Reduces fertility (educated women have fewer, healthier children)
- Increases earnings (returns to education estimated at 8–12% per additional year in India)
- Reduces poverty

**Health:**
- Healthy workers are more productive
- Lower infant mortality → lower fertility rates → smaller family sizes → more savings per child
- Life expectancy in India has risen from ~32 years at independence (1947) to ~70.9 years (2019–23)
- IMR has fallen from 146 per 1,000 (1960) to 25 per 1,000 (2022)

### 🎯 UPSC Connect: India's Education Challenge

Despite improvements, India faces several education quality challenges:
1. **Learning outcomes deficit:** Large number of enrolled children cannot read or do basic arithmetic. Annual Status of Education Report (ASER) consistently shows poor learning outcomes.
2. **Dropout rates:** Particularly high at secondary level, especially for girls.
3. **Higher education quality:** India has many IITs/IIMs but also thousands of colleges with poor quality. **Gross Enrolment Ratio (GER)** in higher education: ~28% (2022–23) — below global average.
4. **NEP 2020:** National Education Policy 2020 — the first comprehensive overhaul since 1986 — emphasises mother-tongue instruction, coding/digital literacy, vocational education, multidisciplinary education.

### Unemployment in India — The Structural Problem

**Agricultural disguised unemployment** is India's most pervasive unemployment type:
- Indian agriculture employs ~45% of the workforce but contributes only ~18% of GDP
- Many farm workers are marginally productive — removing some workers would not reduce output
- Surplus labour in agriculture creates rural poverty and migration pressure
- Disguised unemployment is also called **underemployment**

**Urban educated unemployment:**
- India produces millions of graduates annually (over 9 million per year from colleges)
- Jobs available in the formal economy are far fewer
- Structural mismatch: graduates have academic degrees but not the skills employers want
- **India's unemployment rate** (PLFS 2022–23): ~3.2% (open unemployment) — but this masks huge underemployment, informal employment, and low-quality work

### 📌 Key Fact: Labour Force in India

Key definitions:
- **Labour Force Participation Rate (LFPR):** % of working-age population (15+ years) that is either working or seeking work
- **Worker Population Ratio (WPR):** % of working-age population that is actually working
- **Unemployment Rate (UR):** % of labour force that is unemployed

India data (PLFS 2022–23):
- Female LFPR: ~37% — a significant improvement from ~23% (2017–18), but still low globally
- Male LFPR: ~73%
- Urban female LFPR is much lower than rural female LFPR (women employed in agriculture in rural areas)
- Unpaid care work (cooking, childcare, elder care) done overwhelmingly by women is not counted in official employment statistics

### Women and Work: The Gender Gap

India has a **significant gender gap in labour force participation**. Several reasons:
1. **Social norms:** "Income effect" — as household income rises (especially male income), women withdraw from the labour force (women's work seen as stigma in middle-class families)
2. **Safety:** Women reluctant to work outside home due to safety concerns
3. **Unpaid domestic work:** Women do 90%+ of unpaid household work (cooking, childcare, care of elderly) — invisible in GDP statistics but enormous economic contribution
4. **Lack of quality jobs:** Industries like garments/textiles/agriculture employ women, but formal sector jobs are male-dominated

**Positive trends:**
- Female LFPR has risen sharply since 2019: ~23% (2017–18) → ~37% (2022–23)
- Rise driven by rural female workers (agriculture, MGNREGA)
- Urban female LFPR remains ~25%

### Government Schemes for Employment and Skills

**MGNREGA (Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005):**
- Guarantees **100 days of paid unskilled manual work** per year to every rural household
- **Legal right** to work (demand-driven, not supply-driven)
- Wage paid: State-specific MGNREGA wage (roughly ₹220–330/day depending on state, 2024)
- Benefits: Rural income support, creation of rural infrastructure (ponds, check dams, roads), women empowerment (women constitute ~55% of MGNREGA workers)
- Criticism: Leakages, low wages, delayed payments, temporary work
- Budget 2024–25 allocation: ₹86,000 crore

**PM Kaushal Vikas Yojana (PMKVY):**
- Flagship skill development scheme under **Skill India Mission** (launched 2015)
- Provides free, short-term skill training to youth
- Run by the **National Skill Development Corporation (NSDC)** under Ministry of Skill Development
- PMKVY 4.0 (2022–26): targets 40 lakh youth; Industry 4.0 skills (AI, robotics, cloud computing)
- Criticism: Training quality variable; placement rates often poor

**Skill India Mission:**
- Umbrella mission including PMKVY, Pradhan Mantri Kaushal Kendras (PMKKs), apprenticeship promotion
- Target: Train 400 million people in various skills by 2022 (partially achieved, target extended)
- **Ministry of Skill Development and Entrepreneurship** is the nodal ministry

### 🔗 Beyond the Book: India's Demographic Dividend

India is in a unique phase where its **working-age population (15–64 years)** is at its peak relative to dependents (children and elderly). This is called the **demographic dividend** — similar to what East Asian economies (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan) exploited to achieve rapid growth in the 1960s–80s.

**India's demographic dividend window:** Approximately 2005–2055 (though the peak is around 2030–2035). India adds about **12 million new workers** to the labour force every year.

Whether India harvests this dividend depends on:
1. **Education and skills** — can the new workers find productive jobs?
2. **Health** — are workers healthy enough to be productive?
3. **Job creation** — is the economy growing fast enough to absorb new workers?
4. **Female participation** — can India bring more women into the workforce?

If India fails to provide quality education and jobs, the dividend becomes a **demographic disaster** — a large, unemployed, frustrated youth population.

**States differ:** South Indian states (Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Andhra) are past the demographic dividend peak (older populations, lower fertility). Bihar, UP, Rajasthan, MP are still in the early dividend phase — large young populations, but with lower human capital indicators.

## PART 3 — Frameworks & Mnemonics

### Three Sectors — Easy Anchor

**P**rimary (produces raw materials from nature) → **S**econdary (processes raw materials) → **T**ertiary (services)

"**PST**" — "**P**eople **S**ell **T**hings" (but increasingly, people provide services)

### Types of Unemployment — India's Big Three

**D**isguised (agriculture — too many workers) + **S**easonal (agriculture off-season) + **E**ducated/Structural (skill mismatch)

**"DSE"** — "**D**isguised **S**easonal **E**ducated"

### Human Capital Investment — Two Channels

**E**ducation + **H**ealth = Better Human Capital = Higher Productivity = Economic Growth

"**EH** = **EC**onomic **G**rowth" (Education + Health → Economic Growth)

### Demographic Dividend Key Numbers

- Working age (15–64): largest ever cohort right now
- India adds ~12 million new workers/year
- Dividend window: ~2005–2055 (peak: 2030–2035)
- Female LFPR must rise to fully harvest dividend

## Exam Strategy

**Prelims approach:** Know the three types of unemployment most common in India (disguised, seasonal, educated/structural). Know MGNREGA basics (100 days, rural, demand-driven, passed 2005). Know that female LFPR in India is low but rising. Know India's HDI rank (~134).

**Mains approach (GS3):** Questions on demographic dividend ("Can India's demographic dividend be a challenge if not managed?"), unemployment in India (type-wise analysis), MGNREGA effectiveness, education-employment gap, skill development schemes, female LFPR. Always use current data (PLFS 2022–23, HDI 2023) for Mains value addition.

**High-yield distinctions:** Human capital (economic tool) vs human development (UNDP's broader concept); disguised unemployment (surplus labour, marginal productivity zero) vs seasonal unemployment (time-based, agriculture off-season); demographic dividend vs demographic disaster (same young population, different outcomes depending on education and jobs).

## Previous Year Questions (PYQs)

### Prelims

**1.** Which of the following is the MOST prevalent form of unemployment in rural India?
(a) Cyclical unemployment   (b) Frictional unemployment
(c) Disguised unemployment   (d) Structural unemployment

*Answer: (c) — Disguised unemployment (more workers in agriculture than needed; marginal productivity = zero) is India's most characteristic rural unemployment.*

**2.** Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act (MGNREGA) guarantees:
(a) 100 days of skilled employment per year to rural households
(b) 150 days of unskilled manual work per year to BPL families
(c) 100 days of unskilled manual work per year to rural households
(d) 200 days of employment per year to SC/ST households

*Answer: (c) — MGNREGA guarantees 100 days of unskilled manual work per year to any rural household.*

**3.** India's demographic dividend refers to:
(a) The large number of children below 14 years
(b) The period when the working-age population is proportionately largest relative to dependents
(c) The GDP contribution of the IT sector
(d) India surpassing China in population in 2023

*Answer: (b) — Demographic dividend is the economic growth potential when the working-age (15–64) population is largest relative to dependents.*
