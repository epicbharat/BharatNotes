**Why this chapter matters for UPSC:** This chapter forms the gateway to UPSC Environment and Ecology questions — biodiversity hotspots, protected area categories, flagship conservation projects, biosphere reserves, and IUCN status of flagship species. UPSC Prelims regularly asks about vegetation type characteristics, Sundarbans, biosphere reserves in UNESCO network, Project Tiger tiger count, and the distinction between national parks vs wildlife sanctuaries vs biosphere reserves.

**Contemporary hook:** India released the **results of the 5th All India Tiger Census (2022)** in April 2023, showing **3,167 tigers** — the largest wild tiger population in the world (~70% of the global wild tiger population). India achieved its target of doubling tigers 4 years ahead of the 2022 deadline set at the 2010 St. Petersburg Summit. This milestone for Project Tiger (1973) is a direct connection from this chapter to current affairs.

### National Parks vs Wildlife Sanctuaries vs Biosphere Reserves

| Feature | National Park | Wildlife Sanctuary | Biosphere Reserve |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Governing law** | Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 | Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 | Not under WPA; notified by MoEFCC |
| **Protection level** | Highest; no human activity permitted inside | Lesser; limited human activity allowed | Has zones (core, buffer, transition); human habitation in transition zone |
| **Grazing, forestry** | Prohibited | May be allowed to some extent | Allowed in buffer and transition zones |
| **Boundary** | Fixed by legislation | Fixed, but may be modified | Flexible |
| **Buffer zone** | No; surrounding area separate | No | Yes (core, buffer, transition) |
| **Purpose** | Protecting wildlife and habitat | Protecting specific animals | Conservation + sustainable use + research/education |
| **Example** | Jim Corbett NP, Kanha NP | Chilika (bird), Kaziranga (rhino, before NP) | Nilgiri, Sundarban |

## PART 2 — Chapter Narrative

### Factors Affecting Natural Vegetation

India's extraordinary biodiversity — 2 of the world's 34 biodiversity hotspots are in India — results from the interplay of:

**1. Rainfall:** The most critical factor. More rainfall → denser, taller, evergreen forests. Less rainfall → smaller, deciduous, or thorn vegetation.
**2. Temperature:** Determines the type of species. High temperatures support tropical species; lower Himalayan elevations support temperate species; high altitudes support alpine vegetation.
**3. Soil type:** Black cotton soil (regur) supports scrub and dry deciduous; alluvial supports riverine forests; laterite supports dry deciduous and thorn.
**4. Altitude:** A transect up a Himalayan mountain shows successive belts — tropical at base, temperate in middle, coniferous higher, alpine meadows (bugyals) near snowline, and permanent snow at the top. This is called altitudinal zonation.

### Types of Natural Vegetation

**1. Tropical Evergreen Forests:**
Found where rainfall exceeds 200 cm and temperature stays high. Dense, multi-storeyed canopy; trees do not shed leaves all at once (hence "evergreen"). Commercially valuable timber: ebony, mahogany, rosewood.

Major regions:
- **Western Ghats:** Particularly Kerala, Karnataka, Goa, and the Shola forests (high-altitude evergreen patches in Nilgiris)
- **Andaman and Nicobar Islands:** Rich tropical forests with endemic species
- **Northeast India:** Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Nagaland, Meghalaya — semi-evergreen transitional forests

These forests have the **highest biodiversity** in India — the Western Ghats and Eastern Himalayas are both internationally recognised biodiversity hotspots.

### 💡 Explainer: Why the Western Ghats is a Global Biodiversity Hotspot

The Western Ghats (also called the Sahyadri range) is one of the world's 8 "hottest" biodiversity hotspots — areas with exceptional plant endemism AND serious threat. Key facts:
- Spans approximately 1,600 km from Gujarat/Maharashtra to Kerala/Tamil Nadu
- Houses about 5,000 plant species (of which ~1,700 are endemic)
- Contains 139 mammal species, 508 bird species, 179 amphibian species (many endemic)
- UNESCO recognised as a World Heritage Site in 2012 (39 sub-sites covering ~3,300 sq km)
- Threatened by deforestation, mining, road construction, hydropower projects

The 2023 report by the High-Level Working Group on Western Ghats (Kasturirangan Committee recommendations of 2013) remains a source of controversy — recommending eco-sensitive zone delineation that some states resist.

**2. Tropical Deciduous Forests (Monsoon Forests):**
India's most widespread forest type. Trees shed leaves for 6–8 weeks in the dry season (February–April) to conserve moisture.

**Moist Deciduous (100–200 cm rainfall):**
- Teak (*Tectona grandis*) is the commercially most valuable tree — used for furniture, ship-building
- Sal (*Shorea robusta*) — major timber tree; found in MP, Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, UP foothills
- Sandalwood (Karnataka), bamboo (northeast)
- Found in: Eastern slopes of Western Ghats, MP, UP terai, Jharkhand, Odisha, parts of Maharashtra

**Dry Deciduous (70–100 cm rainfall):**
- More open forests; grass understory
- Teak, sal, khair, axlewood
- Found in: Large parts of the Deccan Plateau, MP, Rajasthan (east), UP

**3. Tropical Thorn Forests:**
Where rainfall is below 50 cm. Vegetation is sparse, thorny, and drought-adapted.
- Trees: Babool (*Acacia nilotica*), ber (*Ziziphus*), khejri (*Prosopis cineraria*) — the state tree of Rajasthan
- Grasses: coarse, sparse
- Found in: Rajasthan, Gujarat, Haryana, Punjab (arid zones)
- Khejri is sacred to the Bishnoi community — the 1730 Chipko of Khejarli (Bishnoi sacrifice to save khejri trees) is the inspiration for the modern Chipko Movement

**4. Montane Forests:**
Himalayan forests arranged in altitudinal zones:

| Altitude | Forest Type | Key Species |
|----------|------------|-------------|
| 1,000–2,000 m | Temperate broadleaf | Oak, chestnut, birch |
| 1,500–3,000 m | Coniferous (Himalayas) | Chir pine (*Pinus roxburghii*), deodar cedar (*Cedrus deodara*) |
| 2,500–3,500 m | Silver fir, spruce, rhododendron | Fir, spruce, Himalayan silver birch |
| 3,500–4,000 m | Alpine shrubs | Junipers, rhododendron, dwarf shrubs |
| Above 4,000 m | Alpine meadows (bugyals) + tundra | Mosses, lichens, cushion plants |

**Deodar** (*Cedrus deodara*) is the national tree of India (Himalayas) — sacred to Hindus and excellent timber.

**Southern Montane Forests (Shola forests):** Distinctive high-altitude tropical montane evergreen forests in the Nilgiris, Anamalai, and Palani hills of peninsular India. Shola forest patches alternate with montane grasslands in a unique pattern. Home to Nilgiri tahr, liontail macaque.

**5. Mangrove Forests:**
Found in tidal intertidal zones — where land meets the sea and receives brackish water. Specialised adaptations: breathing roots (pneumatophores), prop roots, salt-excreting leaves.

**Sundarbans (West Bengal/Bangladesh):** The world's largest mangrove forest — a UNESCO World Heritage Site and Ramsar site — covering ~10,000 sq km (about 4,000 sq km on the Indian side). Named after the **Sundari tree** (*Heritiera fomes*). Home to the **Royal Bengal Tiger** — the only mangrove-dwelling tiger population in the world. Also home to Irrawaddy dolphins, estuarine crocodiles, Gangetic dolphins.

Other major mangrove areas: Andaman & Nicobar, Mahanadi–Brahmani delta (Odisha), Godavari–Krishna delta (Andhra Pradesh), Gulf of Kutch (Gujarat), Pichavaram (Tamil Nadu).

**Threats to mangroves:** Coastal development, aquaculture (shrimp farming), sea level rise, cyclones.

### Wildlife in India

**India's biodiversity position:**
- India covers 2.4% of world's land but hosts ~8% of world's biodiversity
- 45,000 plant species (including 15,000 flowering plants); 89,000 animal species
- **2 biodiversity hotspots:** Western Ghats and Sri Lanka; Eastern Himalayas

**Key wildlife — IUCN status (as of 2024):**

### 🎯 UPSC Connect: Project Tiger

Project Tiger was launched on **1 April 1973** under the leadership of Prime Minister Indira Gandhi, during the Wildlife Week celebrations.

**Key milestones:**
- Original 9 tiger reserves at launch (Jim Corbett, Kanha, Ranthambore, Bandipur, etc.)
- The **National Tiger Conservation Authority (NTCA)** was established under the Wildlife Protection (Amendment) Act, 2006 — to provide statutory authority to Project Tiger
- Tiger reserves now number **54** (as of 2024)
- All India Tiger Estimates: 1,411 (2006) → 1,706 (2010) → 2,226 (2014) → 2,967 (2018) → **3,167 (2022)**
- India has ~70% of world's wild tigers

### Biosphere Reserves: Conservation Zones

A **biosphere reserve** is a special category of protected area with three concentric zones:

1. **Core Zone:** Strictly protected; no human activity; core biodiversity
2. **Buffer Zone:** Limited research and education activities
3. **Transition Zone (Cooperation Zone):** Human settlements, tourism, agriculture — sustainable use

India has **18 biosphere reserves** notified by the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC). Of these, **12 are in UNESCO's World Network of Biosphere Reserves** (as of March 2024 — when Panna was added in 2020 as the most recent).

**Note:** Biosphere reserves may overlap with national parks or wildlife sanctuaries. For example:
- **Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve** contains Mudumalai NP, Nagarhole NP, Bandipur NP
- **Sundarban Biosphere Reserve** contains the Sundarban Tiger Reserve and Sundarban NP

### 🔗 Beyond the Book: Biodiversity Hotspots and India

A **biodiversity hotspot** (concept by Norman Myers, 1988; currently maintained by Conservation International) is a region with:
- At least 1,500 endemic vascular plant species (>0.5% of world total)
- Has lost at least 70% of its original habitat

India contains parts of **two hotspots:**
1. **Western Ghats and Sri Lanka:** One of the 8 globally most important hotspots; 129 amphibian species (47 endemic); Western Ghats is UNESCO World Heritage Site
2. **Himalayas (Eastern Himalayas):** Includes Bhutan, Nepal, northeast India; extraordinary floral diversity

**Note:** India's two hotspots are different from biosphere reserves — hotspots are a global scientific classification, not a legal protection category.

### Wildlife Schedule I (WPA 1972) — Absolute Protection

Schedule I of the Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 gives absolute protection to wildlife — no hunting, killing, poisoning, or trapping. This includes: tigers, lions, elephants, rhinoceros, snow leopards, clouded leopards, dolphins, gharials, sea turtles.

## Exam Strategy

**Prelims approach:** Know all 5 vegetation types and their rainfall thresholds. Know that Sundarbans is the world's largest mangrove; UNESCO World Heritage Site. Know Project Tiger launch year (1973), current tiger count (3,167, 2022). Know the 12 UNESCO biosphere reserves (especially Nilgiri — India's first in 2000). Know the distinction: national park (highest protection, no human activity) vs wildlife sanctuary (limited human use) vs biosphere reserve (zones).

**Mains approach (GS3):** Tiger conservation success story; challenges (man-wildlife conflict, habitat fragmentation, climate change); Eastern Ghats and Western Ghats biodiversity loss; Forest Rights Act vs conservation. Use current data (3,167 tigers, 2022 census) for value addition.

**High-yield distinctions:** National Park vs Wildlife Sanctuary vs Biosphere Reserve (legal basis, protection level, human activity); Biodiversity Hotspot vs Biosphere Reserve (scientific designation vs legal category); Sundarban (mangrove + tiger reserve + UNESCO WHS + Ramsar site — multiple overlapping designations).

## Previous Year Questions (PYQs)

### Prelims

**1.** Which of the following is the world's largest mangrove forest?
(a) Pichavaram (Tamil Nadu)   (b) Bhitarkanika (Odisha)
(c) Sundarbans (West Bengal/Bangladesh)   (d) Gulf of Mannar

*Answer: (c) — Sundarbans (~10,000 sq km) is the world's largest mangrove forest.*

**2.** India's first biosphere reserve to be included in UNESCO's World Network of Biosphere Reserves was:
(a) Gulf of Mannar   (b) Nilgiri   (c) Sundarban   (d) Nanda Devi

*Answer: (b) — Nilgiri Biosphere Reserve was included in UNESCO's WNBR in 2000, making it India's first.*

**3.** Consider the following statements about National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries:
1. In a national park, no human activity is permitted, while in a wildlife sanctuary, some human activities may be allowed
2. Both National Parks and Wildlife Sanctuaries are governed by the Wildlife Protection Act, 1972
3. Biosphere Reserves are also governed by the Wildlife Protection Act, 1972
Select the correct answer:
(a) 1 and 2 only   (b) 1, 2 and 3   (c) 2 and 3 only   (d) 1 only

*Answer: (a) — Statements 1 and 2 are correct. Biosphere Reserves are NOT under the Wildlife Protection Act; they are notified separately by MoEFCC.*
