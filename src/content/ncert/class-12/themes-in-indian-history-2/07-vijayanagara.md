- **Founded:** 1336 CE

- **UNESCO status:** Hampi inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1986
- **ASI excavation:** Archaeological Survey of India has been excavating Hampi since the 1970s
- **Sage credited with founding:** Vidyaranya (according to tradition)

## PART 2: NCERT CHAPTER NOTES

### 1. The Context: Why Vijayanagara?

The NCERT frames this chapter around a central methodological question: how do historians read a ruined city as a historical source? Vijayanagara (modern Hampi) is studied through three overlapping types of evidence:

1. **Archaeological remains** — stone temples, platforms, water channels, fortification walls
2. **Travellers' accounts** — Abdur Razzaq, Nicolo de Conti, Domingo Paes, and Fernao Nuniz
3. **Literary sources** — Krishnadevaraya's Telugu work *Amuktamalyada*; Sanskrit and Telugu court literature

Vijayanagara was founded in 1336 CE by Harihara I and Bukka Raya I, brothers from the Sangama clan. According to tradition, they were guided by the sage Vidyaranya to establish the city on the south bank of the Tungabhadra River. The empire lasted until approximately 1646 CE, spanning three centuries and four dynasties.

The word "Vijayanagara" means "City of Victory" in Sanskrit and Kannada. The site is also referred to in contemporary sources as "Bisnaga" (by Portuguese travellers) and "Vidyanagara" in some traditions.

---

### 2. The Four Dynasties

**Sangama dynasty (1336–1485 CE):** Founded the empire. The most important ruler of this period, besides the founders, was **Devaraya II** (r. 1422–1446 CE), who expanded the empire and employed Muslim cavalry and archers in his army — an acknowledgement that the empire needed military modernisation to compete with the Deccan Sultanates. Both Nicolo de Conti (c. 1420 CE) and Abdur Razzaq (1443 CE) visited during the Sangama period.

**Saluva dynasty (1485–1505 CE):** A brief interregnum. Saluva Narasimha Deva Raya seized the throne in 1485, overthrowing the last Sangama ruler. The dynasty lasted only two generations (three rulers). When Narasimha Raya II was assassinated in 1505, the Tuluva warlord Vira Narasimha effectively ended the Saluva line.

**Tuluva dynasty (1505–1570 CE):** The peak of Vijayanagara power. Krishnadevaraya (r. 1509–1529 CE) is the defining figure — the greatest ruler the empire produced. Both Domingo Paes (c. 1520–22 CE) and Fernao Nuniz visited during and shortly after this period. After Krishnadevaraya, the empire was effectively controlled by his brother-in-law **Aliya Rama Raya**, who acted as regent.

**Aravidu dynasty (1542–1646 CE):** The last dynasty. Aliya Rama Raya's brother Tirumala Deva Raya founded the Aravidu dynasty after Rama Raya's death at Talikota (1565). The Aravidu rulers shifted the capital first to Penukonda, then to Chandragiri (near Tirupati). The last ruler was Sri Ranga III (r. 1642–1646 CE).

---

### 3. Krishnadevaraya (r. 1509–1529 CE): The Empire's Greatest Ruler

Krishnadevaraya is regarded as one of the greatest rulers in Indian history. He presided over Vijayanagara at its political, military, and cultural zenith.

**Military achievements:**
- Conquered the **Raichur Doab** (the fertile tract between the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers) from Bijapur in 1512 CE — ending decades of conflict over this strategically vital region
- Defeated the **Gajapati kingdom of Odisha** (1513–1514 CE), bringing northern territories under Vijayanagara control
- Subjugated multiple Deccan Sultanates, defeating the Sultan of Bijapur decisively in 1520 CE
- Formed a commercial and military alliance with the Portuguese (who had captured Goa in 1510 CE) to secure the horse trade and cut off supplies to his rivals

**Literary and cultural patronage:**
- Wrote **Amuktamalyada** (also called *Amuktamalyada* — "She Who Gives a Garland of Immortal Flowers"), a major Telugu poem on the story of Andal (the Vaishnava bhakti saint) and her devotion to Vishnu
- Patron of the **Ashtadiggajas** — the eight celebrated Telugu poets at his court, considered the "eight elephants supporting the directions" of Telugu literature
- His reign is considered the golden age of Telugu literature

**Temple construction:**
- Built the **Mahanavami Dibba** (stepped platform) to celebrate his victory at Udayagiri
- Expanded the **Hazara Rama Temple** complex (his private royal chapel)
- Made major donations to **Tirupati-Tirumala** — jewels, villages, and land grants
- Extended and embellished numerous temples across the empire

Domingo Paes, the Portuguese traveller who visited Hampi during Krishnadevaraya's reign (c. 1520–22 CE), described the king as a man of cheerful disposition, great justice, and capable of enduring fatigue, and the city itself as "the best-provided city in the world."

---

### 💡 Explainer: How Historians Read a Ruined Capital

Hampi was sacked and systematically burned by the armies of the Deccan Sultanate coalition for several months after the Battle of Talikota (1565). What survived this destruction is almost entirely stone. Wooden structures — royal palaces, the great bazaars, ordinary residential buildings — were consumed by fire. This creates a fundamental bias in the archaeological record: what we can see today is dominated by religious buildings (temples, gopuras, shrines) and royal ceremonial structures (the Mahanavami Dibba, elephant stables, the Lotus Mahal). The lived experience of the city's 500,000+ inhabitants — their homes, workshops, markets, organic life — is largely invisible.

This is why travellers' accounts are so important. Abdur Razzaq described bazaars where precious stones were sold openly; Domingo Paes described the colonnaded market street in front of the Virupaksha temple; Fernao Nuniz recorded administrative structures and court protocol. These accounts fill the gap that archaeology cannot — but they come with their own limitations (elite access, language barriers, cultural assumptions) discussed in Section 6.

The NCERT's methodological lesson: the surviving record is shaped by what was built in stone and what was not destroyed. Reading a ruined city requires constant awareness of what has been lost.

### 4. Urban Planning and the City's Layout

The NCERT describes Vijayanagara as organised into three broad zones that archaeologists use to analyse the city:

**1. The Sacred Centre:** Concentrated along the north bank of the Tungabhadra and in the rocky terrain near the Matanga hill. This zone contains the oldest temples — primarily the Virupaksha temple complex — and served as the religious heart of the city. The Virupaksha temple, dedicated to Shiva (in his form as Virupaksha), predates the Vijayanagara Empire and was the principal royal sanctuary.

**2. The Urban Core:** The residential and commercial zone that surrounded the sacred centre and extended toward the royal enclosure. This is where markets, craft quarters, and residential neighbourhoods were located. Very little of this zone survives — it was primarily built of perishable materials (wood, brick). Travellers' accounts are the primary source for understanding this zone.

**3. The Royal Centre:** A separate, heavily fortified zone about 1 km south of the sacred centre. This is where the major surviving secular monuments are located: the Mahanavami Dibba, the Lotus Mahal, the elephant stables, the queen's bath, and the underground Virupaksha temple. This zone was enclosed within walls with multiple gateways.

**Key monuments within the city:**

**Mahanavami Dibba (Dasara Dibba):** A massive three-tiered stepped platform, 12 metres high with a base of approximately 35 square metres. Built by Krishnadevaraya to celebrate his conquest of Udayagiri. Used for the Mahanavami (Navaratri) festival — a 9-day royal ceremony at which the king displayed his power through military parades, elephant processions, and distribution of gifts. The carvings on its walls depict court life, foreign envoys, hunting scenes, and festival activities.

**Lotus Mahal:** An elegant two-storey pavilion in the zenana (women's) enclosure. Its distinctive design blends Dravidian and Islamic architectural elements — arched recesses with lotus-bud decorations. It was likely used as a pleasure pavilion or a council chamber within the secluded women's quarter.

**Elephant Stables:** Eleven large compartments arranged in a row, covered by alternating square and octagonal domed roofs. The domes show clear influence from Islamic architecture used in the Deccan Sultanates. The stables housed the royal war elephants — a key military asset.

**Vitthala (Vittala) Temple:** Located about 2.5 km northeast of the royal centre, near the Tungabhadra. The temple was begun in the 15th century and significantly expanded during Krishnadevaraya's reign. It is dedicated to Vishnu (in his form as Vitthala/Vitthoba). Its most famous features are the 56 musical pillars in the Ranga Mantapa (the main pillared hall), which are said to produce tonal sounds when struck, and the stone chariot (ratha) in the courtyard, originally dedicated to Garuda, Vishnu's vehicle. The stone chariot, now a symbol of Hampi, was intended to replicate a temple chariot in stone.

**Hazara Rama Temple:** Located within the royal enclosure, this was the private royal chapel of the Vijayanagara kings. It is dedicated to Rama and takes its name from the hundreds (hazara) of Rama images depicted on its walls. The walls carry extraordinarily detailed sculptural friezes narrating the entire Ramayana — described as a "story in stone." It was not a public temple; access was restricted to the royal family.

### 5. The Water System

Vijayanagara was located in a semi-arid zone — the Deccan Plateau receives limited and erratic rainfall. Sustaining a major urban population of hundreds of thousands required sophisticated hydraulic engineering. The empire's water management system was as crucial as its military system.

**Key components of the water system:**

**Hiriya Canal:** One of the most important waterworks, constructed during the Sangama dynasty. The Hiriya Canal drew water from a dam across the Tungabhadra River and ran through the valley separating the sacred centre from the urban core. It irrigated the agricultural lands within and around the city, allowing cultivation close to the capital.

**Kamalapura Tank:** A large reservoir near the city's southeastern edge. Water fed from the Tungabhadra via canal (the Raya Canal) filled this tank, which then supplied habitations in the Kamalapura area — part of the capital's residential zone.

**Network of smaller tanks and channels:** Across the empire's agricultural hinterland, the Vijayanagara kings built a dense network of tanks (artificial reservoirs), check dams, aqueducts, and canals. Water stored in these structures made agriculture possible in the Deccan's dry season.

The NCERT specifically highlights the hydraulic system as evidence that Vijayanagara's imperial power was exercised not only through military conquest but through the management of the landscape itself — controlling water was controlling food, population, and loyalty.

---

### 🔗 Beyond the Book: The Mahanavami Festival

The Mahanavami festival — a 9-day royal ceremony corresponding to the modern Navaratri/Dasara — was among the most important events in the Vijayanagara calendar. Both Abdur Razzaq (1443 CE) and Domingo Paes (c. 1520–22 CE) described it in detail, making their accounts essential sources for understanding how the Vijayanagara kings performed and communicated power.

During Mahanavami, the king sat on his jewelled throne atop the Mahanavami Dibba and received the homage of his provincial governors (nayakas) who came to the capital to pledge loyalty. The ceremonies included military parades, elephant and horse processions, wrestling matches, acrobatic performances, distribution of gifts to subjects and Brahmins, and animal sacrifices.

The festival connected royal power directly with religious legitimacy: the king appeared as a protector of dharma, a warrior-king who defended the Hindu order. The display of military strength (war elephants, cavalry, infantry) and religious munificence (gifts to temples and Brahmins) communicated that the king's authority was simultaneously martial and sacred. This dual legitimation was central to how Vijayanagara kings ruled.

The NCERT uses the Mahanavami festival as an example of how a public ceremony can be read as a political text — analysing not just what happened but what the ceremony was meant to communicate.

### 6. Religious Traditions

**Vaishnava foundation:** The Vijayanagara royal family was primarily Vaishnava in personal devotion. Krishnadevaraya's devotion to Vitthoba (a form of Vishnu worshipped at Pandharpur, Maharashtra) was intense — the *Amuktamalyada* reflects this. Royal titles often invoked Vishnu: Krishnadevaraya used the title *Yavana-rajya-sthapanacharya* (Establisher of the Hindu Kingdom against the Yavanas — i.e., Muslims) among others.

**Shaiva patronage:** Despite royal Vaishnava preference, the Virupaksha temple (the oldest and most sacred site at Hampi, dedicated to Shiva as Virupaksha) remained the principal royal temple. The king was crowned at the Virupaksha temple and received its blessings as the empire's protector. The Vijayanagara kings maintained the Shaiva tradition alongside Vaishnavism.

**Jain patronage:** The Vijayanagara period saw continued patronage of Jain temples and institutions in Karnataka. Earlier Ganga and Hoysala traditions of Jain support were not broken — pragmatic pluralism served the empire's diverse mercantile and administrative communities.

**Tirumala-Tirupati:** The Tirupati temple (dedicated to Venkateswara, a form of Vishnu) in the Seshachalam hills received enormous patronage from Vijayanagara rulers — donations of jewellery, gold, land grants, and establishment of endowments. Krishnadevaraya is particularly associated with major gifts to Tirupati. The temple's wealth during this period partly reflects Vijayanagara's largesse.

**Religious pragmatism:** Despite the empire's Hindu identity and its role as a bulwark against the Deccan Sultanates, Vijayanagara was not religiously exclusive. The city had Muslim quarters, mosques, and Islamic merchants. Muslim cavalry commanders served in the army. The horse trade with Arab and Portuguese merchants was too important to be sacrificed to religious solidarity. This pragmatism is a key NCERT point — the empire's Hindu ideology was real, but it coexisted with practical accommodation of Muslim soldiers, traders, and artisans.

### 7. The Fall: Battle of Talikota (1565 CE)

**Background:** By the 1550s–60s, Aliya Rama Raya — the son-in-law and regent of Krishnadevaraya's successor — was the effective ruler of Vijayanagara. He pursued an aggressive, manipulative policy of intervening in the conflicts between the Deccan Sultanates, playing them against each other. This strategy proved ultimately fatal: it united the Sultanates against him.

**The coalition:** Four of the five Deccan Sultanates — **Bijapur** (under Ali Adil Shah I), **Ahmadnagar** (under Hussain Nizam Shah I), **Bidar** (under Ali Barid Shah I), and **Golconda** (under Ibrahim Quli Qutb Shah Wali) — formed an alliance. Their union was cemented by inter-dynastic marriages.

**The battle:** Fought on **23 January 1565 CE** at a location near Talikota (also called Rakshasi-Tangadi), on the banks of the Krishna River in present-day Karnataka. The battle was initially going in Vijayanagara's favour, but two Muslim commanders in Rama Raya's army switched sides mid-battle, turning the tide. Rama Raya was captured and immediately beheaded.

**Aftermath:** With their commander dead, the Vijayanagara army collapsed. The Sultanate armies marched on the capital and sacked it for several months — destroying palaces, temples, markets, and residences systematically. The city was never repopulated as a major urban centre.

**The empire after Talikota:** Tirumala Deva Raya (Rama Raya's brother) escaped to Penukonda with the royal treasury and established the Aravidu dynasty there. The empire survived in significantly reduced form — primarily controlling parts of Andhra Pradesh and Tamil Nadu — until the last Aravidu ruler Sri Ranga III was deposed around 1646 CE, ending the empire's 310-year existence.

### 📌 Key Fact: Hampi UNESCO and Archaeological Survey

Hampi was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in **1986** under the name "Group of Monuments at Hampi." The site encompasses approximately 4,187 hectares in the Tungabhadra basin in present-day Vijayanagara district (formerly Bellary/Ballari district), Karnataka. It contains over 1,600 surviving remains — temples, royal pavilions, water structures, gateways, memorial structures, and fortifications.

The Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) has been conducting systematic excavations at Hampi since the 1970s. The 1565 destruction — while catastrophic for the city's inhabitants — paradoxically preserved the site for archaeology: because the capital was abandoned rather than continuously inhabited and rebuilt, the Vijayanagara-period remains were not overlaid and obscured by later construction.

## PART 3: MAINS ANSWER FRAMEWORKS

### Framework 1 — Capital as Imperial Ideology (GS1, 15 marks)

**Question:** "The city of Vijayanagara was not merely a capital but a statement of imperial ideology. Discuss with reference to its layout, monuments, and ceremonies."

#### Introduction
- A capital city is never merely a functional settlement — it is a political text
- Vijayanagara (modern Hampi), the 14th–16th century capital, was deliberately constructed to project royal power, religious legitimacy, and cosmological order
- Its layout, monuments, and ceremonies all communicated the same message: the king as protector of dharma and conqueror of the world

#### Body A — Layout as Ideology
- Three-zone organisation (sacred centre, urban core, royal enclosure) placed the sacred at the city's heart and royal authority at its protected core
- The royal enclosure's multiple fortification walls symbolised layers of protection and inaccessibility — access to the king was controlled, graded, and ritually regulated
- The city's position between rocky hills (natural fortifications) and the Tungabhadra (a sacred river) gave it a cosmologically significant setting — the founder's choice of site was itself a statement

#### Body B — Monuments as Political Statements
- **Mahanavami Dibba:** not merely a viewing stand — a throne elevated above the landscape; the king surveying his forces from this height was enacting cosmological sovereignty: the ruler as the axis of the world
- **Hazara Rama Temple:** a private royal chapel with public architectural ambition — the Ramayana friezes connected the king to Rama (the ideal dharmic king), implying that the Vijayanagara ruler was Rama's earthly successor
- **Vittala Temple complex:** its extraordinary ornamentation was not accidental; the investment of royal resources in temple-building was simultaneously religious piety and political communication — the wealthiest, most elaborately decorated temples in South India belonged to the wealthiest empire

#### Body C — The Mahanavami Ceremony as Power
- The annual Navaratri/Mahanavami festival was the empire's premier political theatre
- Provincial governors (nayakas) attended in person to pledge loyalty — the festival was simultaneously a religious observance and a loyalty-binding political ritual
- Abdur Razzaq and Domingo Paes both described the ceremony's magnificence — their awe was part of the ceremony's intent: to impress foreign observers and domestic subjects alike
- Distribution of gifts (dana) to Brahmins, subjects, and temples completed the image of the king as a generous, dharmic ruler

#### Nuance / Critical Edge
- Survival bias in the archaeological record: what we read as ideology is partly shaped by what survived (stone) versus what was destroyed (wood, markets, residential areas)
- The ideological reading of monuments must acknowledge that ceremonial spaces were also functional — the Mahanavami Dibba was used, not just built as symbol

#### Conclusion
- Vijayanagara's ruins tell us what its builders wanted to survive: stone temples, royal platforms, ceremonial spaces; the wooden palaces and bazaars are gone
- This survival bias is itself ideological — what the Vijayanagara kings built to last was the religious and royal architecture that communicated their power
- Reading these ruins as a political text, rather than merely an architectural inventory, is how historians recover the ideology of this imperial city

### Framework 2 — Travellers' Accounts: Value and Limits (GS1, 10 marks)

**Question:** "How do travellers' accounts help us understand Vijayanagara? What are the limitations of these accounts?"

#### Introduction
- No major Indian literary source provides a sustained description of daily life in Vijayanagara
- The accounts of foreign travellers — Nicolo de Conti (c. 1420 CE), Abdur Razzaq (1443 CE), Domingo Paes (c. 1520 CE), and Fernao Nuniz (c. 1535 CE) — are therefore indispensable supplements to the archaeological record

#### Body A — What Travellers Contribute
- **The urban fabric:** Domingo Paes described city walls, gates, markets, and bazaars — particularly the colonnaded market street in front of the Virupaksha temple — that no longer survive as physical structures; without his account, we would have no description of the city's commercial life
- **Scale and population:** Abdur Razzaq estimated the city's enormous population and described its seven concentric fortification walls — giving historians a sense of the city's physical extent that archaeology alone cannot fully reconstruct
- **Ceremonies and social life:** both Razzaq and Paes described the Mahanavami festival in detail, providing evidence for how royal power was displayed and communicated
- **Economic life:** references to open gem markets, horse traders, the diversity of goods available, and the number of merchants give economic historians essential data

#### Body B — Limitations of Travellers' Accounts
- **Elite access only:** all travellers accessed Vijayanagara through royal invitation or merchant networks; they saw what rulers and courtiers wanted them to see; ordinary people's lives — artisans, peasants, women outside the court — are largely absent
- **Cultural filters:** Abdur Razzaq brought an Islamic diplomatic framework; Paes brought Portuguese commercial and military interests; each interpreted what they saw through their own cultural categories
- **Language barrier:** most travellers used interpreters; what they heard was filtered through bilingual intermediaries whose interests and knowledge shaped what was communicated
- **Agenda and purpose:** travellers wrote for audiences back home; dramatic, unusual, and spectacular details were emphasised; the mundane was ignored; this selection bias distorts the picture

#### Nuance / Critical Edge
- The travellers' biases do not negate their value — they make them richer sources if read critically; knowing why Abdur Razzaq was impressed helps us understand what the Vijayanagara court intended to communicate

#### Conclusion
- Travellers' accounts must be read critically — as sources shaped by the traveller's position, cultural background, and intended audience
- When used alongside archaeological evidence and literary sources, they complete a picture that neither source could provide alone

### Framework 3 — Water, Geography, and Imperial Power (GS1, 10 marks)

**Question:** "Examine the water management system of Vijayanagara. What does it reveal about the relationship between geography, technology, and imperial power?"

#### Introduction
- Vijayanagara was located in one of the driest parts of peninsular India — the Deccan Plateau, where annual rainfall is erratic and rivers run at low levels for much of the year
- That a city of several hundred thousand people could exist and thrive in this landscape for over two centuries was a product of hydraulic engineering on a vast scale

#### Body A — Geography as Constraint and Opportunity
- The Tungabhadra River provided the primary water source — but its flow was seasonal and unreliable
- The rocky terrain (granite outcrops) provided natural sites for dam construction and water storage
- The semi-arid climate meant that agricultural success — and therefore food security for the capital and army — depended entirely on irrigation, not rain

#### Body B — Technology as Imperial Expression
- The **Hiriya Canal** (Sangama dynasty): drew water from a dam across the Tungabhadra and irrigated the cultivated valley between the sacred centre and urban core — integrating agriculture into the city's spatial plan
- The **Kamalapura Tank** and the network of smaller tanks: stored monsoon runoff for use in the dry months; created by earthen and stone embankments that required major labour mobilisation — only an imperial state could organise such construction
- Aqueducts and stone-lined channels carried water across the rocky terrain to temples, royal baths, and residential areas
- The tank network extended beyond the capital to the agricultural hinterland — sustaining the tax base that funded the army and royal patronage

#### Body C — Water Control as Political Power
- The ability to build and maintain hydraulic infrastructure required a state capable of mobilising labour, organising engineers, and maintaining structures over generations
- Control of water was control of agriculture; control of agriculture was control of revenue; control of revenue was control of military power
- The Vijayanagara kings' investment in irrigation was simultaneously an act of benevolence (providing water to farmers) and an act of power (making farmers dependent on the state's infrastructure)
- The NCERT makes this explicit: the hydraulic system was as central to Vijayanagara's imperial power as its military system

#### Nuance / Critical Edge
- The NCERT's methodological point: imperial power in pre-modern India was exercised not only through conquest and ceremony but through landscape transformation — a dimension often overlooked in political history
- Compare with other pre-modern empires (Chola tank systems, Mauryan irrigation) to show this was a pan-Indian pattern, not unique to Vijayanagara

#### Conclusion
- Vijayanagara's water management system reveals that imperial power in pre-modern South Asia was exercised through the transformation of the natural landscape
- The empire's durability — spanning 310 years in a semi-arid zone — was partly a product of hydraulic infrastructure that tied population, agriculture, and urban life to the state's engineering capacity
