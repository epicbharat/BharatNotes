### Racial Segregation in Colonial Urban Planning

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Civil lines | Exclusively European residential area in colonial towns; wide roads, bungalows with large compounds, clubs, churches; separate water supply; away from the "native" bazaar |

## PART 2: CHAPTER NARRATIVE

### 1. Colonial Urbanisation: A New Urban Order

Colonial urbanisation in India did not simply continue the patterns of pre-colonial urban life — it created a new urban order. The cities that rose under British rule (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, New Delhi) were built on different principles, in different places, and for different purposes than the Mughal cities that preceded them.

Pre-colonial Indian cities were typically built around:
- A royal court (fort-palace complex) providing patronage and administration
- A commercial zone (bazaar) serving the court and regional trade
- Religious centres (temples, mosques, dargahs) around which population clustered
- Craft production districts (karkhanas — workshops producing for the court)

Colonial cities were built around:
- A fort and military garrison
- An administrative zone (the Writers' Building in Calcutta; government secretariats)
- A port connecting the colony to the global trade network
- A residential segregation that spatially embodied racial hierarchy

The pre-colonial cities that had been based on court patronage — Murshidabad, Surat, Masulipatnam — declined as their courts were abolished or marginalised. New cities grew at the coastal intersections of colonial trade routes.

---

### 2. Calcutta: From Trading Post to Imperial Capital

Calcutta's foundation story begins with **Job Charnock** of the East India Company, who established a trading post at Sutanuti on the eastern bank of the Hooghly River in 1690. Three villages — Sutanuti, Kalikata, and Gobindapur — were granted to the Company by the Mughal emperor in 1698. **Fort William** was built on the present site of the Maidan.

**The Black Hole of Calcutta (1756):** When Siraj ud-Daulah, the Nawab of Bengal, captured Calcutta in 1756, he imprisoned British survivors in the Company's fortified chamber — later called the "Black Hole." The British commander Robert Holwell later claimed 123 of 146 prisoners died in one night. This story was used for decades in British colonial mythology to justify harsh treatment of Indians — but later historians have questioned the scale and even the facts. Regardless of the truth of the specific claims, it served as a defining colonial narrative.

**Robert Clive and the Battle of Plassey (1757):** The Company retook Calcutta in early 1757, and Clive's victory at **Plassey** in June 1757 (more a transaction than a battle — Mir Jafar was bribed to defect from Siraj) effectively made the Company the sovereign power in Bengal. Fort William was rebuilt to Clive's design, surrounded by the open **Maidan** — the large open space cleared for military fields of fire that became Calcutta's defining feature.

**Calcutta as Imperial Capital:** Calcutta was the capital of British India from 1773 (when Warren Hastings became the first Governor-General under the Regulating Act 1773) until **1911**, when the capital was shifted to Delhi. It was the largest city in the British Empire outside Britain itself for much of the 19th century.

**Architecture and spatial structure:** Calcutta's architecture reflected its role as an imperial capital. The Writers' Building (1780) was the East India Company's administrative centre. The Government House (now Raj Bhavan, built 1803 by Lord Wellesley — modelled on Kedleston Hall in Derbyshire) was the Governor-General's residence. Dalhousie Square (now Binoy-Badal-Dinesh Bagh) was surrounded by banks, insurance companies, and trading houses. The **Victoria Memorial** (completed 1921, designed by William Emerson in the Italian Renaissance style) was built to memorialise Queen Victoria after her death in 1901 — opened in 1921 by the Prince of Wales.

The city was divided informally: the northern part ("Black Town" in colonial terminology, the offensive term used by the British) was where the Indian middle and trading classes lived and worked — densely settled, with a vibrant cultural life that gave rise to the **Bengal Renaissance**. The southern and central zone around the Maidan was the European administrative and residential area.

---

### 💡 Explainer: The Maidan and Urban Space

The **Maidan** — roughly 3 square kilometres of open, flat grassland in central Calcutta — is the most distinctive feature of Calcutta's urban geography. Its origin was military: Fort William's defensive perimeter required a clear field of fire, so all buildings were demolished within cannon range of the fort in 1757. This created the Maidan as a by-product of military necessity.

Over time, the Maidan became the city's lungs: a space for recreation, sport (cricket, horse racing, football), political rallies, and daily walks. The Maidan's existence meant that Calcutta's European residential zones developed to the south (around Park Street and Chowringhee Road), while the Indian commercial and residential zones were pushed north. The spatial arrangement of colonial Calcutta — the open green at the centre, the fort, the Government House, the European clubs and gardens — expressed the power geometry of colonialism.

### 3. Bombay: The City Built from the Sea

Bombay's urban development is inseparable from the remarkable engineering project of land reclamation. The Portuguese had acquired seven islands in the mid-16th century from the Sultan of Gujarat. These islands were transferred to England in 1661 as part of the dowry of Catherine of Braganza (Portuguese princess who married King Charles II). The East India Company received them in 1668.

The seven islands were connected by a series of reclamation projects, beginning with the **Hornby Vellard** (1782–1784) that connected the Worli and Mahim islands and excluding the sea from a large stretch of Bombay. Further reclamation in the 19th century created much of what is now South Mumbai.

**Victoria Terminus (Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj Terminus / CSMT):** The most iconic symbol of Victorian Bombay is the Victoria Terminus, designed by British architect **F.W. Stevens** and built between **1878 and 1887** — the year of Queen Victoria's Golden Jubilee. It is a masterpiece of **High Victorian Gothic** architecture: the stone dome, pointed arches, turrets, gargoyles, and profuse stone carving are Gothic Revival elements; they are combined with Indian decorative elements like peacocks and other indigenous imagery. The result is a building that is quintessentially colonial — European architecture adapted with Indian details. It was listed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004**.

**Bombay Plan:** In the 1860s–1880s, the Bombay Improvement Trust began systematic urban planning — creating new residential suburbs, expanding roads, and beginning the segregation of European and Indian residential areas. The Back Bay Reclamation Scheme of the early 20th century extended the city further.

**Gothic public buildings:** The 1860s saw a cluster of major Gothic Revival public buildings in Bombay: the Bombay High Court (1878), the Bombay University Rajabai Clock Tower (1878), and the former Secretariat — all designed by James Fuller in the Victorian Gothic style. These buildings cluster around the Oval Maidan (a smaller version of Calcutta's open space concept) and remain defining features of South Mumbai.

---

### 4. Madras: Fort St. George and the Oldest British Settlement

**Fort St. George** is the oldest British fort in India. Francis Day of the English East India Company acquired land from the local Nayak ruler at **Madraspatam** in 1639, and Fort St. George was completed on **St. George's Day (23 April 1644)**. Around it grew the settlement of Madras.

The development of Madras was initially less spectacular than Calcutta or Bombay — Madras had a poorer harbour than Bombay and a smaller hinterland than Bengal. But it became the capital of the Madras Presidency, covering most of South India.

**George Town:** The area immediately north of Fort St. George, known as **George Town** (historically also called "Black Town" in colonial usage — a term reflecting the racial geography of colonial cities), was the Indian commercial and residential zone.

**Chepauk:** The Palace at Chepauk, south of Fort St. George, was the residence of the Nawabs of the Carnatic — the Muslim rulers of South India who were progressively undermined and subordinated by the East India Company through the 18th century. The palace was later taken over by the British government and became part of the Madras administrative complex.

**Indo-Saracenic architecture:** Madras developed a distinctive architectural style — **Indo-Saracenic** — that combined Mughal domes and arches, Rajput chhatris, Gothic pointed arches, and classical European elements. The Madras High Court (1892) is considered one of the finest examples. This style was also used extensively in Mysore and Hyderabad.

### 5. Hill Stations: The Imperial Summer Retreat

Hill stations were a peculiar product of colonial urban culture — towns built in the mountains primarily for the benefit of European colonial officials, their families, and Indian elites who could afford to join them.

The rationale was medical: plains India, especially during the hot season (April–June), was considered dangerous to Europeans because of heat, malaria, and cholera. The hills were cooler and (relatively) drier. British medical thinking in the 19th century also associated the hills with "bracing" air that improved health and moral character.

**Simla (Shimla):** Simla became the **Summer Capital of British India** from 1864 onwards (formalised under Lord Lawrence as Viceroy). Every summer, the entire apparatus of the Government of India — the Viceroy, the secretariats, the telegraph lines — moved from Calcutta (later Delhi) to Simla in the Himalayan foothills.

Simla's built environment was deliberately English — the **Christ Church** (1857), the **Viceregal Lodge** (now Rashtrapati Nivas; built 1888 in Gothic style), the **Mall** (a European-style shopping promenade), the **Gaiety Theatre** — all were designed to create a piece of England in the mountains. Rickshas replaced horses on the Mall for European residents; Indians were largely excluded from the European social spaces.

The NCERT uses Simla to make a point about colonial geography: the hill station was a spatial enactment of racial ideology, a place where colonial racial hierarchy was expressed in architecture, planning, and social rules (which hotels would admit Indians; which clubs; which stretches of road).

---

### 6. New Delhi: Planning the Imperial Capital

The decision to move the capital from Calcutta to Delhi was announced at the **Delhi Durbar of December 1911** — a grand imperial ceremony at which King George V and Queen Mary appeared before the Indian princes and dignitaries. The announcement was both symbolic (returning to Delhi, the seat of Mughal imperial power, was a deliberate statement about the depth and continuity of the new imperial order) and practical (Calcutta was increasingly the home of Indian nationalist politics; Delhi was more central geographically).

**The architects:** The new capital was planned by two British architects — **Sir Edwin Lutyens** (1869–1944) and **Sir Herbert Baker** (1862–1946):
- Lutyens designed the **Viceroy's House** (now Rashtrapati Bhavan) and the overall urban plan
- Baker designed the two **Secretariat buildings** flanking the processional route and the **Parliament House** (Council House)
- **Robert Tor Russell** designed **Connaught Place** — the large circular commercial and retail centre

**The design debate:** Lutyens and Baker had a famous falling-out over the **gradient of Raisina Hill** — the rise up which the processional way (Rajpath / Kingsway) approached the Viceroy's House. Baker insisted the Secretariats should sit on higher ground flanking the approach, which meant that the Viceroy's House disappeared behind them as one walked up the hill — never fully visible until one was almost upon it. Lutyens called this the greatest architectural mistake he had ever made and argued bitterly with Baker about it. The result is that the formal axis of New Delhi does not produce the triumphant view of the Viceroy's House that Lutyens had designed. The sightline issue remains today on Kartavya Path (formerly Rajpath).

**Architectural synthesis:** Lutyens's Viceroy's House (now Rashtrapati Bhavan) is a remarkable synthesis. The building has a classical Western massing — a grand rectangular palace with a central dome. But the dome itself draws on the Great Stupa at Sanchi; the capitals of the columns are Buddhist bell capitals; the *chhatris* (domed kiosks) on the roofline are Mughal; the *jali* (latticed stone screens) draw on Rajput architecture. Lutyens called this the "New Delhi Order" — a style unique to this one building.

**Inauguration:** New Delhi was officially inaugurated on **13 February 1931** by **Viceroy Lord Irwin**. Construction had taken 20 years, enormously over budget, and the new capital was inaugurated just as the nationalist movement — the Civil Disobedience movement following the Dandi March of 1930 — was challenging British rule most intensely. The irony was not lost on contemporaries.

---

### 📌 Key Fact: Rashtrapati Bhavan's Name Change

The Viceroy's House (designed by Lutyens; completed 1929; occupied by Viceroys from 1931) was renamed **Rashtrapati Bhavan** ("President's House") on **26 January 1950** — the day India became a republic and **Dr. Rajendra Prasad** became the first President of India. The name change was both symbolic (from the colonial Viceroy to the elected President) and practical. The building itself was unchanged — the architectural synthesis that Lutyens created remained, now the home of the democratic republic.

### 7. Census as Historical Source

The **colonial census** — conducted every 10 years from 1881 onwards (though earlier censuses existed from 1872) — is one of the most important and most controversial historical sources produced by the colonial state.

**What the census recorded:**
- Population size and growth
- Occupation categories
- Caste and religion (from 1871 / 1881)
- Age and sex
- Literacy
- Geographic distribution

**Why it matters for historians:** The census created, for the first time in India's history, a systematic enumeration of the entire population. It provides demographic data that no other source type offers at this scale.

**Why it is controversial:**
1. **Caste classification:** The census required every Indian to identify with a single caste. In practice, caste identities were fluid, contextual, and varied by region. The census imposed rigid boundaries on a complex reality. The result — a fixed, enumerated caste hierarchy — arguably hardened caste identities that had been more fluid
2. **Religious categories:** Classifying Indians as "Hindu," "Muslim," "Christian," etc. imposed discrete religious identities on a society where religious practice was often syncretic and overlapping. This "communalisation" of identity has been linked to the growth of communal politics in the early 20th century
3. **Occupation categories:** The census classified Indians into occupational categories derived from European economic models; these often distorted Indian realities
4. **The act of enumeration as political:** Being counted in a particular caste or religious category had political consequences — it determined representation in colonial councils, eligibility for government jobs (under reservation policies), and eventually the mapping of electoral constituencies

The NCERT's methodological point: the census is a historical source that must be read with awareness that it actively shaped the reality it claimed merely to describe.

### 🔗 Beyond the Book: Simla Convention (1914) — Geopolitics from a Hill Station

The **Simla Convention of 1914** — negotiated at the summer capital between British India, Tibet, and China — established the McMahon Line as the boundary between British India and Tibet. China never ratified it. The McMahon Line remains the disputed boundary at the centre of the **India-China border dispute** today. The fact that a boundary with consequences into the 21st century was drawn in a hill station, from a colonial perspective, by officials who had limited knowledge of the terrain and the populations they were dividing, is itself a comment on colonial geography.

**2.** "The colonial census, introduced in India from 1881, was not a neutral exercise in counting people. It created the very identities it claimed to measure." Critically examine this argument. *(GS1, 10 marks)*
