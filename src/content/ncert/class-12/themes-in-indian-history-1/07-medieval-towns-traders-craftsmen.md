|---------|---------|

| Combatants | Vijayanagara (Aliya Rama Raya) vs. coalition of Bijapur, Ahmadnagar, Bidar, and Golconda Sultanates |

## PART 2: CHAPTER NARRATIVE

### 1. Founding the Empire (1336 CE)

The Vijayanagara Empire was founded in **1336 CE** by brothers **Harihara I** and **Bukka Raya I** of the Sangama dynasty. "Vijayanagara" means "City of Victory." The city was built on the southern bank of the **Tungabhadra River** at modern **Hampi, Karnataka**.

The site was chosen for its natural defences (granite boulders; the river), its sacred geography (the Virupaksha temple was already a pilgrimage site), and its access to water (the Tungabhadra enabled a sophisticated hydraulic system). The empire positioned itself explicitly as a defender of "Hindu dharma" against Sultanate expansion — this ideology shaped its architecture, ritual, and political culture.

---

### 2. The Golden Age: Krishnadevaraya (r. 1509-1529 CE)

The greatest Vijayanagara ruler was **Krishnadevaraya** of the Tuluva dynasty. Under him:
- The empire reached its greatest territorial extent (defeated Gajapati kings of Odisha; dominated the Deccan)
- He maintained friendly relations with the Portuguese at Goa, who supplied vital Arabian horses for cavalry
- He was himself a poet — his Telugu epic *Amuktamalyada* is a classic of Telugu literature
- He patronised the **Ashtadiggajas** (eight celebrated Telugu poets of his court)
- Major architectural projects: Vitthala temple, Hazara Rama reliefs, monolithic Narasimha statue (1528 CE)

---

### 💡 Explainer: Why Hampi?

**Military:** Natural granite fortifications; multiple concentric walls enclosing agricultural land — the city could survive a long siege. **Sacred:** The ancient Virupaksha temple gave the site mythological depth (associated with the Ramayana's Kishkinda). **Water:** In the semi-arid Deccan, the Tungabhadra and the empire's hydraulic engineering (Hiriya canal, tanks, embankments) made intensive agriculture and a large urban population possible. The city at its height may have housed 500,000 to 1,000,000 people.

---

### 3. Hampi: Urban Layout and Architecture

The capital had three distinct zones:

**Sacred Centre:** Virupaksha temple with its 50-metre gopuram was the spiritual heart. The king's ritual chariot procession centred here.

**Royal Centre:** The royal enclosure contained the Hazara Rama temple, Lotus Mahal, Elephant Stables, Queen's Bath, and the Mahanavami Dibba — a platform from which the king watched the Mahanavami festival. Domingo Paes described the Mahanavami (nine-night festival) in extraordinary detail — for ten days the king sat in public view receiving tribute, watching processions, battles, and performances.

**Urban Core:** Dense bazaars, craftsmen's workshops, temples. The Krishnapura Bazaar — a wide colonnade-flanked avenue — is partially visible today. Paes compared it to the finest markets of Lisbon.

**Architectural Syncretism:** Despite being positioned as a "Hindu" counter to Muslim Sultanates, Vijayanagara absorbed Islamic architectural elements — pointed arches (Elephant Stables), domes (Lotus Mahal roofline). This reflects the reality that the empire employed Muslim soldiers, traders, and craftsmen.

---

### 📌 Key Fact: The Vitthala Temple's Musical Pillars

The Vitthala temple colonnade contains 56 monolithic granite pillars carved so that when struck they produce musical tones at different pitches. The British, on hearing about this, cut open two pillars to investigate — the cuts are still visible today. The stone "chariot" in front of the temple is actually a **shrine (ratha)** — it once had wooden wheels that rotated. It is not a working chariot and never was.

---

### 4. Water Management

In the semi-arid Deccan, the Vijayanagara kings were masters of hydraulic engineering. The **Hiriya canal** and branch canals carried water from the Tungabhadra to fields and urban tanks. The **Kamalapuram tank** fed water into the royal centre. Large reservoirs were created by embankments across natural drainage lines. The hydraulic system extended beyond the capital — enabling cultivation of sugarcane, cotton, and commercial crops across the empire.

---

### 5. Trade and Economy

Vijayanagara was one of the great commercial cities of the 15th-16th century world:
- **Diamonds** from the Kollur mine region (modern Andhra Pradesh) — traded globally
- **Horses** imported from Arabia and Persia via Portuguese at Goa — the most strategically vital commodity; the Deccan could not breed cavalry horses
- **Cotton textiles** from coastal areas; **Spices** from Malabar
- **Foreign merchants:** Arab, Persian, Portuguese, Central Asian; Chinese porcelain found at Hampi shows East Asian trade connections

The horse trade gave the Portuguese and Arab traders real political leverage over the empire.

---

### 6. Abdur Razzaq's Account

**Abdur Razzaq Samarqandi** (Timurid scholar; ambassador of Shah Rukh) visited Vijayanagara in **1443 CE** under **Devaraya II**. His chronicle *Matla-us-Sa'dayn* describes Hampi: "The bazaars are extremely long and broad... no person, high or low, walks about without a garland of flowers in their hands." He described the empire extending from Ceylon to Orissa. His account is the most vivid external description of Vijayanagara at a high point of its power.

---

### 7. Archaeology and UNESCO

**Colin Mackenzie** (1754-1821), first Surveyor-General of India, conducted the first systematic survey of Hampi in the early 19th century. The **Vijayanagara Metropolitan Survey** (John Fritz, George Michell, M.S. Nagaraja Rao, 1970s onwards) produced the most comprehensive archaeological map.

Hampi (the "Group of Monuments at Hampi") was inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1986** — "an extraordinary testimony to the vanished civilization" of the Vijayanagara Empire.

### Framework: Significance of Vijayanagara (Mains)

1. **Political:** Last great pre-Mughal Hindu empire; 230 years' survival against Deccan Sultanates
2. **Cultural:** Telugu and Kannada literature patronage; Carnatic music foundations; Dravida temple style
3. **Economic:** Commercial node on Indian Ocean trade; diamond, horse trade with Portuguese
4. **Architectural:** Hampi as planned imperial capital; hydraulic engineering; syncretic style
5. **Historiographical:** External traveller accounts; colonial survey (Mackenzie); modern archaeology; UNESCO

## Exam Strategy

**Prelims focus:**
- Abdur Razzaq visited under **Devaraya II** (Sangama), NOT under Krishnadevaraya (Tuluva)
- Krishnadevaraya is **Tuluva** dynasty, NOT Sangama
- Battle of Talikota: **four** Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur, Ahmadnagar, Bidar, Golconda)
- Hampi UNESCO: **1986**
- The Vitthala "chariot" is a **shrine (ratha)**, not a working chariot

**Mains focus:**
- Architecture as historical evidence — ideology, economy, social structure
- Water management as factor in imperial success
- Foreign travellers as historical sources — apply source criticism to Razzaq and Paes
- Battle of Talikota — both internal weakness (Aliya Rama Raya's over-centralisation; Muslim commanders' defection) and external defeat

## Previous Year Questions

### Prelims

**1.** Which of the following about the Vijayanagara Empire is/are correct?
1. Founded by Harihara I and Bukka Raya I in 1336 CE
2. Hampi was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999
3. The Vitthala temple is known for its musical pillars

(a) 1 and 3 only  (b) 1 only  (c) 2 and 3 only  (d) 1, 2 and 3

**Answer: (a)** — Statement 2 is incorrect: Hampi was inscribed **1986**, not 1999.

---

**2.** Abdur Razzaq, the Persian ambassador who described Vijayanagara in detail, visited during the reign of:
(a) Krishnadevaraya  (b) Devaraya II  (c) Aliya Rama Raya  (d) Bukka Raya I

**Answer: (b)** — Abdur Razzaq visited in 1443 CE during the reign of **Devaraya II** (Sangama dynasty).

---

**3.** The Battle of Talikota (1565 CE) resulted in Vijayanagara's defeat by a coalition of how many Deccan Sultanates?
(a) Two  (b) Three  (c) Four  (d) Five

**Answer: (c)** — All **four** Deccan Sultanates: Bijapur, Ahmadnagar, Bidar, and Golconda.

### Mains

**1.** "The monuments at Hampi are not merely aesthetic achievements — they are historical documents revealing the political ideology, economic priorities, and social structure of the Vijayanagara Empire." Discuss with reference to at least three monuments. *(GS1, 15 marks)*

**Approach:** Virupaksha temple (religious legitimacy; continuity with pre-Vijayanagara sacred geography), Hazara Rama temple (Ramayana iconography as royal ideology — king as Ram), Lotus Mahal / Elephant Stables (syncretic architecture — Islamic elements in a Hindu imperial setting), hydraulic system (economic base), Krishnapura Bazaar (commercial wealth, Indian Ocean trade); conclude on value and limits of archaeological evidence.

---

**2.** "The decline of Vijayanagara was as much a product of internal political weakness as of external military defeat." Critically examine with reference to the Battle of Talikota (1565 CE). *(GS1, 10 marks)*

**Approach:** Internal — Aliya Rama Raya's over-centralisation marginalised the royal family; reported defection of Muslim commanders from Vijayanagara's own army at Talikota; factional rivalry among nobles; External — combined military superiority of four Sultanates, artillery; assess relative weight; note empire persisted (Aravidu dynasty) but permanently weakened; conclude both factors were necessary causes.
