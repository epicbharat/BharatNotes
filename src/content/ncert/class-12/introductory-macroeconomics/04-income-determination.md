## PART 1 — Quick Reference Tables

### Table 4.1 — Key Propensities: Consumption and Savings

| Concept | Symbol | Formula | Range | Relationship |
|---------|--------|---------|-------|-------------|
| **Average Propensity to Consume** | APC | C / Y | Can be > 1 at low income | APC + APS = 1 |
| **Marginal Propensity to Consume** | MPC | ΔC / ΔY | 0 < MPC < 1 | MPC + MPS = 1 |
| **Average Propensity to Save** | APS | S / Y | Can be negative at low income | APC + APS = 1 |
| **Marginal Propensity to Save** | MPS | ΔS / ΔY | 0 < MPS < 1 | MPC + MPS = 1 |

**Key insight:** For Keynes, MPC is stable in the short run. In developing economies like India, MPC tends to be **higher** (closer to 1) because lower-income households spend most of any additional income.

### Table 4.2 — The Keynesian Multiplier

| Concept | Formula | Implication |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Investment Multiplier (k)** | k = 1 / MPS = 1 / (1 − MPC) | Higher MPC → larger multiplier |
| **Change in equilibrium income** | ΔY = k × ΔI | Small investment can trigger large output change |
| **Government expenditure multiplier** | k_G = 1 / MPS | Same as investment multiplier |
| **Tax multiplier** | k_T = −MPC / MPS | Negative; tax cut smaller effect than spending rise |
| **Balanced budget multiplier** | 1 | Equal rise in G and T raises Y by same amount |

Example: If MPC = 0.8, then MPS = 0.2, and k = 1/0.2 = **5**. An ₹100 crore increase in government spending raises national income by ₹500 crore.

## PART 2 — Chapter Narrative

### What Determines Income and Output?

A central question in macroeconomics is: what determines how much an economy produces and how many people are employed? Classical economists believed the economy self-adjusts to full employment. Keynes, writing in the shadow of the Great Depression, argued that the economy could settle at any level of output — including well below full employment.

The Keynesian model of income determination is built on a fundamental premise: **in the short run, output is demand-determined**. The economy produces as much as is demanded of it. If demand is low, output and employment will be low — even if prices and wages are "right."

> 💡 **Explainer: Why does demand determine output in the short run?**
>
> In the short run, prices and wages are **sticky** — they do not adjust instantly. Firms cannot lower wages when demand falls (labour contracts, morale effects, social norms). So when demand falls, firms respond by cutting output and employment, not prices. This is why recessions happen: output collapses even though the productive capacity (capital, labour) still exists. Once demand recovers, output can quickly bounce back — the capacity was always there.

---

### Aggregate Demand: The Engine of Output

**Aggregate Demand (AD)** is the total demand for goods and services in the economy at a given price level. In a two-sector closed economy (households and firms):

**AD = C + I**

Where:
- **C = Consumption** — household spending on goods and services
- **I = Investment** — firms' spending on capital goods

In a four-sector open economy:
**AD = C + I + G + (X − M)**

**Aggregate Supply (AS)** in the Keynesian short run is the economy's willingness to produce at any output level (since prices are fixed, AS is effectively horizontal or 45°-line representation).

---

### The Consumption Function

Keynes's most important behavioural equation is the **consumption function**:

**C = C̄ + bY**

Where:
- **C̄** = autonomous consumption (the minimum consumption even when income = 0; financed by past savings or borrowing)
- **b** = MPC (marginal propensity to consume)
- **Y** = disposable income

**Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC):**

MPC = ΔC / ΔY = the fraction of each additional rupee of income spent on consumption.

Keynes's **Fundamental Psychological Law:** As income rises, consumption rises, but by less than the rise in income. Therefore, 0 < MPC < 1.

**Average Propensity to Consume (APC):**

APC = C / Y = the fraction of total income spent on consumption.

At low incomes: APC > 1 (households borrow or dis-save to consume).
At high incomes: APC < 1 (households save a portion of income).
APC falls as income rises.

> 📌 **Key Fact:** In India, household consumption (Private Final Consumption Expenditure) accounts for approximately **55–58% of GDP**. MPC in India is estimated to be relatively high (~0.7–0.8) compared to rich countries, because lower-income households have less savings buffer and spend most incremental income. This implies a **larger fiscal multiplier** for India than for high-income economies.

---

### The Savings Function

Since income is either consumed or saved:
**Y = C + S → S = Y − C**

Substituting the consumption function:
**S = −C̄ + (1 − b)Y = −C̄ + sY**

Where s = MPS = 1 − MPC = marginal propensity to save.

**APS = S / Y** — the fraction of total income saved.
At low incomes, APS is negative (dissaving). At high incomes, APS rises.

**Relationship between APC and APS:**

APC + APS = 1 (always, since C + S = Y)
MPC + MPS = 1 (always, since ΔC + ΔS = ΔY)

> 🎯 **UPSC Connect:** India's household savings rate has been a macroeconomic concern. The **Gross Domestic Savings Rate** (savings as % of GDP) declined from ~34% in 2011-12 to approximately 30-32% in recent years. Within this, household financial savings declined while physical savings (real estate, gold) remained high — reducing the pool of loanable funds available for productive investment.

---

### Investment

Investment in the Keynesian model refers to **Gross Fixed Capital Formation** — firms' spending on new capital goods (machinery, buildings, software) and changes in inventories.

**Types of investment:**
- **Autonomous investment:** Independent of current income — determined by interest rates, business expectations, technology, government policy.
- **Induced investment:** Rises with income (via the accelerator principle — when output rises, firms need more capital).

**Determinants of Investment:**
1. **Expected rate of return (Marginal Efficiency of Capital — MEC)**: Investment is profitable if return > cost of capital.
2. **Rate of interest (cost of borrowing):** Investment and interest rate have an **inverse relationship**.
3. **Business confidence (animal spirits):** Keynes emphasised that investment depends heavily on entrepreneurs' expectations — what he called "animal spirits."
4. **Capacity utilisation:** If existing capacity is underutilised, firms delay new investment.
5. **Government policy:** Tax incentives (accelerated depreciation, investment allowances) stimulate investment.

In Keynes's two-sector model, investment is taken as **autonomous** (fixed, independent of income) for simplicity.

---

### Equilibrium Output: Where AD Meets AS

**Equilibrium condition:**

In the Keynesian model, equilibrium is achieved when:
**AD = AS (Output)**

Alternatively, using the **saving-investment equality:**
**S = I** (equilibrium condition)

**The 45° Diagram:**

In the Keynesian cross diagram, the 45° line represents all points where output = aggregate spending (AS = output produced). The AD line is flatter (slope = MPC). Equilibrium is at the intersection.

If AD > AS (at current output): Inventories fall → firms increase production → output rises toward equilibrium.
If AD < AS (at current output): Inventories rise → firms cut production → output falls toward equilibrium.

**Equilibrium output formula:**

If AD = C̄ + bY + Ī:

At equilibrium: Y = C̄ + bY + Ī
Y − bY = C̄ + Ī
Y(1 − b) = C̄ + Ī
**Y* = (C̄ + Ī) / (1 − b) = (C̄ + Ī) × k**

Where k = 1/(1−b) = 1/MPS is the multiplier.

---

### The Keynesian Multiplier

The **investment multiplier** shows how a change in autonomous spending creates a magnified change in equilibrium income.

**k = ΔY / ΔI = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS**

**The Multiplier Process:**

Suppose government spends an extra ₹100 crore building a road (MPC = 0.8):
- Round 1: Road workers earn ₹100 crore → spend ₹80 crore on food, clothing, etc.
- Round 2: Food and clothing sellers earn ₹80 crore → spend ₹64 crore.
- Round 3: ₹64 crore → spend ₹51.2 crore.
- Sum of all rounds: ₹100 + ₹80 + ₹64 + ... = ₹100 × (1/0.2) = **₹500 crore**

The initial ₹100 crore injection creates **₹500 crore** of additional national income — a multiplier of 5.

> 💡 **Explainer: Why does the multiplier work?**
>
> Because one person's spending is another person's income. When government spends, the recipients earn income and spend a fraction of it. This spending becomes income for others, who again spend a fraction. Each round is smaller (because some is saved), but the series converges to a finite sum. The higher the MPC (more spending, less saving per round), the larger the multiplier.

**Multiplier and Leakages:**

The multiplier is reduced by leakages from the spending stream:
- **Savings** (domestic): Main leakage in the closed-economy model.
- **Taxes:** Government takes a portion of income.
- **Imports:** Spending "leaks" to foreign producers.

In an open economy: **k = 1 / (MPS + MPT + MPM)** where MPT = marginal propensity to tax, MPM = marginal propensity to import.

This is why real-world multipliers are much smaller than the simple 1/MPS formula suggests.

> 📌 **Key Fact:** Empirical estimates of India's fiscal multiplier range from 1.3 to 1.6 in the short run for productive public investment (infrastructure). However, the multiplier can be less than 1 for current expenditure (wages, subsidies) due to import leakages and crowding out of private investment.

---

### Full Employment vs Equilibrium Output

A key Keynesian insight: **equilibrium output ≠ full employment output**.

The economy can be in equilibrium — no tendency to change — at a level of output below what is needed to employ everyone willing to work.

**Full employment output (Y_f):** The output level at which all factors are fully utilised (no involuntary unemployment).

**Deflationary Gap (Recessionary Gap):**

When Y* < Y_f:

Deflationary gap = Y_f − Y* (the shortfall in AD)
OR measured as: the excess of AS over AD at the full employment level.

The economy is producing below potential. Unemployment exists. Solution: **expansionary fiscal/monetary policy** to boost AD.

**Inflationary Gap:**

When Y* > Y_f (i.e., AD pushes beyond full employment):

Inflationary gap = Y* − Y_f (excess demand at full employment)

The economy is trying to produce more than its physical capacity — the result is **demand-pull inflation**, not real output growth. Solution: **contractionary policy** to reduce AD.

> 🎯 **UPSC Connect:** India's **output gap** — the difference between actual and potential GDP — is a tool used by RBI and Finance Ministry. A negative output gap (below potential) signals room for expansionary policy without causing inflation. The COVID-19 shock created a massive negative output gap in FY2020-21, justifying India's ₹20 lakh crore Atmanirbhar Bharat stimulus package.

---

### The Paradox of Thrift

One of Keynes's most counter-intuitive results: if all households simultaneously decide to save more (spend less), national income falls. As income falls, total saving may actually decline (or stay the same) — not rise.

**The fallacy of composition:** What is prudent for an individual (save more) can be disastrous for the economy as a whole (aggregate demand collapses, income falls, unemployment rises).

This is why, during a recession, governments are reluctant to follow household logic and "tighten belts." Keynes argued the government must spend MORE during downturns, even if it means running deficits.

> 🔗 **Beyond the Book:** The paradox of thrift was central to the 2008-09 global financial crisis debate. When private sector savings shot up (everyone feared job loss), governments worldwide launched stimulus packages to prevent the paradox from deepening the recession. India's own fiscal stimulus in 2008-09 (reducing excise duties, increasing NREGA allocations) drew on this logic.

---

### Fiscal Policy to Close Output Gaps

Fiscal policy — government taxation and expenditure decisions — is the primary Keynesian tool for managing aggregate demand.

**Expansionary Fiscal Policy (to close deflationary gap):**
- Increase government expenditure (G) — infrastructure, social spending.
- Reduce taxes — increasing disposable income and hence consumption.
- Increase transfer payments (subsidies, welfare benefits).

Effect: AD rises → output rises → employment rises → income multiplied via multiplier.

**Contractionary Fiscal Policy (to close inflationary gap):**
- Decrease government expenditure.
- Increase taxes.
- Reduce transfer payments.

Effect: AD falls → excess demand pressure on prices reduced.

**Automatic Stabilisers:**

Some fiscal mechanisms stabilise the economy automatically, without discretionary policy action:
- **Progressive income tax:** In a boom, higher incomes push taxpayers into higher brackets, draining excess demand. In recession, tax liabilities fall automatically.
- **Unemployment benefits:** In recession, benefits automatically rise, supporting consumption.
- **Corporate profit taxes:** In booms, higher profits increase tax collection.

These automatic stabilisers reduce the amplitude of economic cycles without requiring parliamentary action.

## PART 3 — Frameworks & Mnemonics

### Framework 1: Equilibrium Output — The Bridge Analogy

Think of the economy as a bridge supported by demand (AD):
- If the bridge is too short (AD < Y_f): deflationary gap — economy sags below potential.
- If the bridge is too long (AD > Y_f): inflationary gap — demand overshoots capacity, price pressures build.
- Fiscal policy = adding or removing bridge sections to reach the perfect span (Y_f).

### Framework 2: The Multiplier Chain — RISE

Each round of spending:
- **R**eceived as income
- **I**nduces further spending (at rate MPC)
- **S**aved (at rate MPS) — the leakage
- **E**ventually converges to ΔY = k × ΔI

### Framework 3: APC/APS and Income Level

```
Low income household:   APC > 1 (borrows/dissaves), APS < 0
Middle income:          APC approaching 1, APS small positive
High income:            APC < 1, APS positive and growing
```

### Framework 4: Policy Matrix

| Situation | AD vs Y_f | Policy | Tools |
|-----------|----------|--------|-------|
| Recession | AD < Y_f | Expansionary | ↑G, ↓Tax, ↓Repo |
| Overheating | AD > Y_f | Contractionary | ↓G, ↑Tax, ↑Repo |
| Stagflation | AD low + inflation | Dilemma | Supply-side policy needed |

### Framework 5: Paradox of Thrift in 3 Steps

1. All households try to save more → spend less
2. ↓ spending → ↓ income for everyone (circular flow contracts)
3. ↓ income → can't save more (income too low) — the attempted increase in thrift fails collectively

- Keynesian vs classical debate — frame the answer around market failure, sticky prices, and the short-run demand side.

(a) 0.25
(b) 2
(c) 4
(d) 7.5

*Answer: (c) — k = 1 / (1 − 0.75) = 1 / 0.25 = 4*

**Q2.** The "Paradox of Thrift" in Keynesian economics refers to:
(a) Higher savings leading to higher investment in all cases
(b) Individual saving being rational but collectively self-defeating during recession
(c) Government saving reducing private spending
(d) High MPS always being beneficial for economic growth

*Answer: (b)*

**Q3.** In the Keynesian model, if Aggregate Demand exceeds the full employment output level, the result is:
(a) A deflationary gap and unemployment
(b) An inflationary gap and demand-pull inflation
(c) Balanced growth with full employment
(d) A reduction in investment

*Answer: (b)*
