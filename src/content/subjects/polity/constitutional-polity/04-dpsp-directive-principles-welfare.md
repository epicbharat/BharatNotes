## What Are Directive Principles of State Policy?

The Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSPs) are contained in **Part IV of the Constitution (Articles 36–51)**. They are guidelines to the Central and State governments for framing policies and laws with the aim of establishing a **welfare state** in India.

The concept was borrowed from the **Irish Constitution** (1937), which in turn was influenced by the Spanish Constitution.

### Key Characteristics

- **Non-justiciable:** DPSPs cannot be enforced in a court of law — Article 37 explicitly states that they are not enforceable
- **Fundamental in governance:** Article 37 says they are "fundamental in the governance of the country and it shall be the duty of the State to apply these principles in making laws"
- **Positive obligations on the State:** While Fundamental Rights (Part III) are largely negative obligations (the State must *not* do certain things), DPSPs are positive obligations (the State *must* work towards certain goals)
- **Comprehensive social goal:** Together, they represent a blueprint for a **socio-economic democracy**

## Part IV: All Articles 36–51

**Article 36** — Definition of "State" (same as Article 12 — includes Parliament, state legislatures, local and other authorities)

**Article 37** — Application of DPSPs: not justiciable but fundamental in governance; State duty to apply them

## DPSP vs Fundamental Rights — The Great Constitutional Conflict

This is one of the most important areas for UPSC. The judiciary evolved its position through a series of landmark cases.

### Timeline of the Conflict

**Phase 1 — Fundamental Rights prevail:**

**State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951):**
The Supreme Court held that DPSPs must conform to and run subordinate to Fundamental Rights. In case of conflict, Fundamental Rights prevail. The Court struck down the Communal Government Order (reservations in education) as violating Article 15(1).

**Parliamentary Response:** Parliament passed the **1st Constitutional Amendment (1951)**, inserting Article 15(4) to allow the state to make special provisions for backward classes.

**Phase 2 — Parliament vs Judiciary escalates:**

**I.C. Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967):**
An 11-judge bench (6:5 majority) held that Parliament **cannot amend Fundamental Rights** at all, even to implement DPSPs. Fundamental Rights were placed on an unamendable pedestal.

**Parliamentary Response:** Parliament passed the **24th Amendment (1971)** overruling Golaknath — empowering Parliament to amend any provision of the Constitution including Part III.

**Phase 3 — Basic Structure doctrine:**

**Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973):**
A 13-judge bench (7:6) — the largest bench in Indian constitutional history — held:
- Parliament can amend any part of the Constitution **including Fundamental Rights**
- But Parliament **cannot alter the Basic Structure** of the Constitution
- Fundamental Rights and DPSPs are **complementary**, not contradictory — they together form the Constitution's soul

The **25th Amendment (1971)** inserted Article 31C providing that laws implementing Article 39(b) and (c) [equitable distribution of resources] shall not be void for violating Articles 14, 19, or 31.

**Phase 4 — 42nd Amendment overreach:**

The **42nd Amendment (1976)** extended Article 31C to cover **all DPSPs** — any law implementing any DPSP would be immune from challenge under Part III Fundamental Rights. This effectively made DPSPs supreme over Fundamental Rights.

**Phase 5 — Minerva Mills restores balance:**

**Minerva Mills Ltd. v. Union of India (1980):**
The Supreme Court (5:0 bench) declared the extended Article 31C (from 42nd Amendment) **unconstitutional** on the grounds that it violated the **Basic Structure** of the Constitution. The Court held:
- The Indian Constitution is founded on a **balance** between Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles
- Neither should be sacrificed for the other
- Harmony and balance between Part III and Part IV is itself a **basic feature** of the Constitution
- Article 31C was valid only to the limited extent inserted by the 25th Amendment (i.e., protecting laws for Article 39(b) and (c) only)

**Current Position (Post-Minerva Mills):**
- DPSPs are **subordinate** to Fundamental Rights in cases of direct conflict
- But courts apply the **doctrine of harmonious construction** to read both together wherever possible
- Article 31C protects laws implementing Articles 39(b) and (c) specifically

## Article 31C — Current Legal Status

**Inserted by:** 25th Amendment Act, 1971
**Original scope:** Protected laws implementing Articles 39(b) and (c) from challenge under Articles 14, 19, and 31
**Extended by:** 42nd Amendment (1976) to cover all DPSPs
**Struck down:** Minerva Mills (1980) struck down the extension
**Current status:** Article 31C is valid only for its original purpose — protecting laws for equitable distribution of material resources [Art. 39(b)] and prevention of wealth concentration [Art. 39(c)]

---

## Harmonious Construction Doctrine

When there is an apparent conflict between a Fundamental Right and a DPSP, courts apply the **doctrine of harmonious construction** to read both provisions together in a manner that gives effect to both. Direct conflict is treated as a last resort.

**Example:** The right to carry on any occupation (Article 19(1)(g)) and the DPSP on just conditions of work (Article 42) — courts read these harmoniously to uphold reasonable labour legislation as a reasonable restriction.

## DPSPs in Practice — Implementation Examples

| DPSP | Implementation |
|------|---------------|
| Article 39A (free legal aid) | Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987; NALSA (National Legal Services Authority) |
| Article 40 (village panchayats) | 73rd Constitutional Amendment (1992) — Panchayati Raj institutions |
| Article 41 (right to work) | MGNREGA — Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005 |
| Article 44 (Uniform Civil Code) | Shah Bano case (1985); still debated; Goa has a common civil code |
| Article 45 (education for children) | Right to Education Act, 2009; 86th Amendment made education a Fundamental Right under Article 21A |
| Article 47 (public health) | Prohibition policies in states; regulation of tobacco |
| Article 48A (environment) | Environment Protection Act, 1986; Forest Conservation Act |
| Article 50 (separation of judiciary) | Code of Criminal Procedure provisions; various state reforms |

## Exam Strategy

**Frequently examined in both Prelims and Mains GS2.** The conflict between Fundamental Rights and DPSPs is a perennial Mains topic.

**Key things to know for Prelims:**
- Which articles belong to which classification (Socialistic/Gandhian/Liberal-intellectual)
- New DPSPs added by which amendment (42nd, 44th, 97th)
- Article 31C — current legal status after Minerva Mills
- Cases in chronological order: Champakam (1951) → Golaknath (1967) → Kesavananda (1973) → Minerva Mills (1980)

**For Mains:**
- Use the **timeline approach** to explain the judicial evolution
- Emphasise the **Minerva Mills balance principle** — Basic Structure = harmony between FRs and DPSPs
- Link DPSPs to welfare state implementation — MNREGA, RTE, NALSA
- Discuss Article 44 (UCC) as a perennially relevant DPSP

**Do not confuse:**
- 42nd Amendment (1976, DPSPs strengthened) vs 44th Amendment (1978, FR Art 38(2) added, Article 20 & 21 rights reinforced)
- Article 37 (non-justiciable nature) vs Article 13 (Fundamental Rights — laws inconsistent with them are void)
- Golaknath (Parliament cannot amend FRs at all) vs Kesavananda (Parliament can amend FRs but not Basic Structure)
