## Overview

The Right to Information (RTI) Act 2005 is India's landmark transparency legislation. Combined with digital governance initiatives and social accountability mechanisms, it forms the backbone of India's transparency architecture. This topic is highly relevant across GS2 (Governance, Accountability), GS4 (Ethics, Integrity), and Essay.

---

## RTI Act 2005: Background and Origins

The RTI movement in India grew from grassroots activism, particularly the **Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan (MKSS)** led by **Aruna Roy** in Rajasthan during the 1990s. MKSS demanded public hearings on government expenditure records — these became the forerunner of the social audit model.

Key milestones:
- **1996:** MKSS-led campaigns for access to muster rolls and village works records
- **2002:** Freedom of Information Act (weak, never notified)
- **15 June 2005:** RTI Act enacted; came into force **12 October 2005**

The Act replaced the Official Secrets Act 1923 culture of administrative secrecy with a regime of **presumption of openness**.

## Key Provisions of the RTI Act 2005

### Section 2 — Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Public Authority** | Any authority/body established under the Constitution, law, government notification; includes NGOs substantially financed by government |
| **Information** | Any material in any form including records, documents, memos, e-mails, opinions, advices, press releases, circulars, orders, logbooks, contracts, reports, papers, samples, models, data material held in electronic form |
| **Record** | Any document, manuscript, file, microfilm, microfiche, facsimile copy of a document, reproduction of image embodied in such microfilm, magnetic or electronic recording |
| **Right to Information** | Includes right to inspect works, documents, records; take notes, extracts, or certified copies; obtain samples of material; obtain information in diskette/floppy where information is stored in a computer |

### Section 3 — Citizens' Right

Every **citizen** (not companies or foreigners) has the right to information from public authorities.

### Section 4 — Suo Motu Disclosure (Proactive Disclosure)

Public authorities must proactively publish **17 categories** of information without waiting for requests, including:
- Particulars of organisation, functions, and duties
- Powers and duties of officers and employees
- Procedure followed in decision-making
- Rules, regulations, instructions, manuals
- Particulars of facilities available to citizens
- Budget allocated and disbursements made
- Manner of execution of subsidy programmes
- Particulars of recipients of concessions, permits, authorisations
- Information available in electronic form

### Section 6 — Application Procedure

- Application to be made to the **Public Information Officer (PIO)** of the concerned public authority
- Application fee: **₹10** (for Central Government)
- Must state name, contact details, and information sought
- No reason required for seeking information

### Section 7 — Time Limits

| Situation | Time Limit |
|-----------|------------|
| Normal requests | **30 days** from receipt |
| Information relating to life or liberty | **48 hours** |
| Request received through APIO | **35 days** |
| Third party information (Section 11) | **40 days** |

### Section 8 — Exemptions from Disclosure

**10 categories of exempt information:**

| # | Category | Details |
|---|----------|---------|
| 1 | National security | Information affecting sovereignty, integrity, security, strategic, scientific, economic interests or relations with foreign state or leads to incitement of offence |
| 2 | Expressly forbidden | Information forbidden to be published by any court of law or tribunal or disclosure of which may constitute contempt of court |
| 3 | Parliamentary privilege | Breach of privilege of Parliament or State Legislature |
| 4 | Commercial confidence | Trade secrets, intellectual property, fiduciary relationship — where disclosure would harm competitive position unless larger public interest warrants |
| 5 | Fiduciary relationship | Information available to a person in fiduciary relationship |
| 6 | Foreign government | Information received in confidence from a foreign government |
| 7 | Endangering life or safety | Information that would endanger the life or physical safety of any person |
| 8 | Obstruct investigation | Impede process of investigation or apprehension or prosecution of offenders |
| 9 | Cabinet papers | Cabinet papers including records of deliberations of Council of Ministers, Secretaries and other officers *(exception: after decision is taken and matter is complete)* |
| 10 | Personal information | Which has no relationship to any public activity or interest, or which would cause unwarranted invasion of privacy of the individual |

**Note:** Exemptions are not absolute. If larger public interest overrides, protected information can be disclosed (except items 1, 2, and matters under Section 24 intelligence agencies).

**Section 8(3):** Information exempted under Section 8(1) can be disclosed after **20 years** have elapsed (except items 1, 2, 3, and 6).

### Section 11 — Third Party Information

If information requested concerns or was supplied by a third party, the PIO must inform the third party within 5 days and give them 10 days to respond. Third party's response must be considered before disclosure.

## Public Information Officers (PIOs) and APIOs

**Public Information Officer (PIO):**
- Designated by every public authority
- Receives and processes RTI requests
- Can seek assistance of any other officer
- Liable for penalties under Section 20

**Assistant Public Information Officer (APIO):**
- Receives applications and forwards to PIOs
- Located at sub-divisional and block levels for accessibility

---

## Appeals Under RTI: Section 19

### First Appeal
- To **First Appellate Authority** (an officer senior to PIO) within the same public authority
- Must be filed within **30 days** of PIO's decision (or expiry of time limit)
- To be decided within **30 days** (extendable to 45 days)

### Second Appeal
- To the **Central Information Commission (CIC)** or **State Information Commission (SIC)**
- Must be filed within **90 days** of first appellate authority's decision

### Complaints (Section 18)
- Directly to CIC/SIC if no PIO appointed, request refused, not responded, etc.

---

## Central Information Commission (CIC)

### Composition
- **Chief Information Commissioner (CIC)** — appointed by the President of India
- Up to **10 Information Commissioners (ICs)** — appointed by the President
- Appointment based on recommendation of a committee: PM (Chairperson), Leader of Opposition in Lok Sabha, and a Union Cabinet Minister nominated by PM

### Powers
- CIC has powers of a civil court
- Can impose penalty up to **₹25,000** on erring PIOs
- Can recommend disciplinary action against PIOs
- Can direct disclosure of information

## RTI Amendment Act 2019 — Key Controversy

The **Right to Information (Amendment) Act 2019** (No. 24 of 2019) amended Sections 13, 16, and 27:

| Provision | Before 2019 | After Amendment 2019 |
|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Tenure** of CIC/SICs | Fixed **5 years** | Determined by **Central Government** by notification |
| **Salary** of CIC | Equivalent to **Chief Election Commissioner** | Determined by **Central Government** |
| **Salary** of ICs | Equivalent to **Election Commissioners** | Determined by **Central Government** |
| **Deductions** from salary | Provisions for pension deductions | Removed |

**Criticism:** Critics (including opposition parties and RTI activists) argued that removing fixed tenures and making service conditions subject to government discretion would undermine the **independence of CICs and SICs** and render them susceptible to government influence.

**Government's Position:** Better alignment with actual functions; ICs are not equivalent to constitutional authorities like Election Commissioners.

## RTI and Political Parties

**CIC Ruling (2013):** CIC held that national political parties — INC, BJP, NCP, CPI(M), CPI, and BSP — are "public authorities" under Section 2(h) and must comply with RTI Act.

**Non-compliance:** No political party complied with this ruling.

**Supreme Court:** In a 2023 judgment, the SC upheld that political parties are not "public authorities" under RTI, ruling in favour of the parties.

**Debate:** Whether parties receiving government facilities (land, bungalows, income tax exemptions) should be subject to RTI remains contested.

## Social Audit

### Concept and Significance

Social audit is a process by which a community critically examines the implementation of a programme using official records and through public hearings. It bridges the gap between policy intent and actual delivery.

### MGNREGS Social Audit — Section 17

- The **Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act 2005** mandates social audits under **Section 17**
- Social Audit Units (SAUs) conduct regular audits of MGNREGS works
- **Andhra Pradesh Social Audit Society** is the premier model — conducts biannual social audits of all MGNREGS expenditure in the state
- Facilitated by the **Gram Sabha** — residents verify muster rolls, measurements, and quality

### Social Audit Process
1. Verification of official records (muster rolls, bills, vouchers)
2. Physical verification of works (measurement)
3. Community meetings (public hearings — **Jan Sunwai**)
4. Findings recorded and submitted to appropriate authority

### Legal Framework
- **MGNREGA Rules (2006):** SAUs to be set up in every state
- **Social Audit Rules (2011):** Detailed procedures
- **Ministry of Rural Development Guidelines (2017):** SAUs to be independent of implementing agency

## Citizen's Charter

**Origin in India:** The concept of Citizen's Charter was first adopted at the **Conference of Chief Ministers (May 1997)**. The Department of Administrative Reforms and Public Grievances (DARPG) rolled out the initiative from **1998** under the Vajpayee government.

### Key Features
- Explicit statement of the **service standards** citizens can expect
- **Grievance redressal mechanism** details
- **Time limits** for service delivery
- Responsibility of officials

### Sevottam Framework (2nd ARC)

**Sevottam** = "Seva" (service) + "Uttam" (excellence) — a framework for assessing and improving service delivery quality, developed by DARPG in **2006** and endorsed by the **2nd Administrative Reforms Commission (ARC)**.

**Three Components of Sevottam:**

| Component | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Citizen's Charter** | Commitments on service standards, time limits |
| **Public Grievance Redressal** | Mechanism to receive and dispose complaints |
| **Service Delivery Excellence** | Efficient and effective service delivery systems |

**ISO 9001:2000** certification was linked to Sevottam compliance in some departments.

**Limitations of Citizen's Charters:** Lack of legal backing, no penalty for non-compliance, lack of awareness among citizens, non-specific commitments, weak grievance redressal.

## E-Governance Initiatives

### National e-Governance Plan (NeGP)

Launched in **2006**, NeGP aims to make government services accessible to common citizens through Common Service Centres (CSCs). It comprises **31 Mission Mode Projects (MMPs)** spanning central, state, and integrated categories.

### Digital India Programme

Launched on **1 July 2015** by PM Narendra Modi. Three vision areas:

| Vision Area | Components |
|-------------|------------|
| Digital Infrastructure as Utility | Broadband for all, universal mobile connectivity, accessible public internet |
| Governance and Services on Demand | Seamlessly integrated government services, real-time monitoring |
| Digital Empowerment of Citizens | Universal digital literacy, digital resources in Indian languages |

### Key Digital India Platforms

| Platform | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **UMANG** | Unified Mobile Application for New-age Governance; over 2,077 services from 207 departments across 32 States/UTs; 7 crore+ users |
| **DigiLocker** | Digital document wallet; 53.84 crore registered users (June 2025); 949 crore+ documents issued |
| **MyGov** | Citizen engagement platform — "Discuss, Do, Disseminate" model |
| **data.gov.in** | Open Government Data (OGD) platform — datasets from central and state government |
| **e-Courts** | Digitisation of court records and case management |
| **GeM** | Government e-Marketplace for public procurement |

### PRAGATI — Pro-Active Governance And Timely Implementation

Launched by PM Modi, PRAGATI is a **monthly video-conferencing** platform connecting the PM with secretaries of the Government of India and chief secretaries of states. It serves three purposes:
1. Addressing **grievances** of common citizens
2. Monitoring government **programmes and projects**
3. Reviewing **state government projects**

PRAGATI uses data and geo-informatics visuals to enable real-time review of stuck projects.

### Common Service Centres (CSCs)

Over 5 lakh CSCs as of 2025 act as rural digital hubs offering government and non-government services including banking, insurance, education, and healthcare at village level.

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Memorise key section numbers: Section 2 (definitions), Section 4 (proactive disclosure), Section 6 (application), Section 7 (30-day rule, 48-hour for life/liberty), Section 8 (10 exemptions), Section 11 (third party), Section 19 (appeals)
- RTI Amendment 2019: what changed (tenure, salary — determined by government)
- CIC appointment: President on PM committee recommendation
- Penalty: up to ₹25,000 on PIOs

**For Mains:**
- RTI Amendment 2019 is a standard question — present both sides (government efficiency vs. institutional independence)
- Section 4 vs Section 6: proactive vs reactive disclosure — argue for strengthening Section 4
- Social audit model: quote AP Social Audit Society as best practice
- Sevottam: three components (Charter + Grievance + Delivery Excellence)
- Link e-governance to transparency — DigiLocker, UMANG, data.gov.in as case studies
- Citizen's Charter limitation: no legal enforceability — contrast with Right to Service Acts at state level

**Key Thinkers to Quote:**
- Aruna Roy (MKSS, RTI movement)
- 2nd ARC recommendations on transparency
- SC judgment on political parties and RTI
