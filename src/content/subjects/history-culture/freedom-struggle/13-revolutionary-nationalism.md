Alongside the Gandhian mass movement, a distinct tradition of **revolutionary nationalism** challenged British rule through direct action, assassination, and armed insurrection. These revolutionaries rejected the gradualism of constitutional politics and the pace of non-violent agitation — they believed that only militant resistance could awaken the nation and demoralise the coloniser.

## Ideological Foundations

Revolutionary nationalism drew from multiple streams:
- **Tilak's assertion** that swaraj was a birthright, not a gift to be negotiated
- **European revolutionary thought** — Mazzini's writings were widely read
- **Russian nihilism and anarchism** — the idea that individual acts of political violence could destabilise empires
- Later, **Marxism and socialism** — particularly influencing the HSRA generation

A key distinction: revolutionaries did not endorse indiscriminate violence against civilians. Their targets were colonial officials and symbols of oppression — police officers, informers, and government machinery.

## Early Revolutionary Organisations

### Anushilan Samiti (1902)

Founded in Bengal, the **Anushilan Samiti** (meaning "society for the cultivation of virtue/practice") combined physical training with revolutionary organisation.

- Founded in **Calcutta (1902)** and later developed a separate **Dhaka Anushilan Samiti**
- Used gymnasiums (akharas) as recruitment and training centres
- Connected to the **Jugantar** revolutionary group and periodical
- Key figures included **Barindra Kumar Ghosh** (Aurobindo's brother) and **Aurobindo Ghosh** in its early phase
- Involved in the **Alipore Bomb Case (1908)** — Khudiram Bose and Prafulla Chaki attempted to assassinate Magistrate Kingsford; Khudiram Bose was hanged at age 18

### Abhinav Bharat Society

Founded by **Vinayak Damodar Savarkar** in Pune and London; aimed to establish contacts between revolutionary cells in India and the Indian diaspora in Europe.

## Madan Lal Dhingra (1883–1909)

**Madan Lal Dhingra** was an early revolutionary martyr who demonstrated that the struggle extended beyond India's borders.

- A student at University College London, influenced by V.D. Savarkar's India House circle
- On **1 July 1909**, at a gathering hosted by the Indian National Association in London, Dhingra shot and killed **Sir William Hutt Curzon Wyllie**, political aide-de-camp to the Secretary of State for India
- Tried at the Old Bailey; refused to recognise the court's legitimacy
- **Executed on 17 August 1909** — the first Indian to be hanged in Britain for a political act
- His statement before execution became a founding text of revolutionary nationalism: he framed his act as the duty of a patriot avenging his enslaved nation

## The Hindustan Republican Association (HRA) and HSRA

### HRA Founded (1924)

The **Hindustan Republican Association (HRA)** was founded in **October 1924** at Kanpur, with **Sachindra Nath Sanyal**, Ram Prasad Bismil, and Jogesh Chandra Chatterjee as key founders. Its goal was complete independence through armed revolution.

**Kakori Train Robbery (9 August 1925):** HRA members — led by Ram Prasad Bismil and Ashfaqullah Khan — looted a train carrying government treasury funds near Kakori, Uttar Pradesh. The British arrested dozens; **Ram Prasad Bismil, Ashfaqullah Khan, Rajendra Lahiri, and Roshan Singh** were executed in December 1927.

### HSRA (1928)

In **1928**, the HRA was renamed the **Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA)**, reflecting the organisation's shift toward socialist ideology. Key leaders:

- **Bhagat Singh** (1907–1931)
- **Chandrashekhar Azad** (1906–1931)
- **Sukhdev Thapar** (1907–1931)
- **Rajguru** (1908–1931)

## Bhagat Singh — Ideology and Actions

Bhagat Singh was not merely a bomb-thrower — he was a **Marxist intellectual** who read widely (Lenin, Marx, Trotsky) and wrote extensively from prison. He is among the most intellectually significant figures of the independence movement.

### Key Actions

**Saunders Shooting (December 1928):**
To avenge the death of **Lala Lajpat Rai** — who died from injuries sustained during a police lathi charge against anti-Simon Commission protesters on 30 October 1928 — Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, Sukhdev, and Chandrashekhar Azad planned to kill Superintendent Scott. They mistakenly shot **John Saunders** (an Assistant Superintendent of Police) in Lahore on 17 December 1928.

**Central Legislative Assembly Bombing (8 April 1929):**
Bhagat Singh and **Batukeshwar Dutt** threw two smoke bombs (designed not to kill) in the Central Legislative Assembly chamber in Delhi to protest against the Public Safety Bill and Trade Disputes Bill. They deliberately stayed and submitted to arrest, shouting **"Inquilab Zindabad"** (Long Live the Revolution). The purpose was to make the deaf hear — to use the trial as a platform for revolutionary ideas.

**The Lahore Conspiracy Case (1929–1931):**
Tried for the Saunders murder. Bhagat Singh used the trial as a political stage, making lengthy statements on imperialism and socialism. The tribunal sentenced Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru to death.

**Execution — 23 March 1931:**
Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru were hanged at **Lahore Central Jail on 23 March 1931** — reportedly 11 hours ahead of schedule to avoid public protests. Their bodies were secretly cremated and the ashes thrown into the Sutlej river. The executions caused nationwide outrage and made the three young men immortal symbols of sacrifice.

**Prison fast (1929):** Bhagat Singh and fellow prisoners launched a hunger strike demanding equal treatment for political prisoners vs. criminal prisoners — sustained for 116 days.

## Chandrashekhar Azad (1906–1931)

- Adopted the oath: "I am free (Azad), my father's name is Freedom (Swatantra), and my home is Prison (Jail)" when arrested at age 15 during the Non-Cooperation Movement
- Became the military commander of HSRA; known for his ability to evade British capture
- Trained revolutionaries in firearms and guerrilla tactics
- **Died on 27 February 1931** at Alfred Park, Allahabad (now Azad Park) — surrounded by British police, he shot himself with his last bullet rather than surrender, fulfilling his vow never to be captured alive

## Surya Sen and the Chittagong Armoury Raid (1930)

**Surya Sen** ("Master da") was a schoolteacher and revolutionary leader from Chittagong (now in Bangladesh), influenced by the Bengal revolutionary tradition.

**The Chittagong Armoury Raid — 18 April 1930:**
- Surya Sen led approximately 65 members of the **Indian Republican Army** to raid the armoury of police and auxiliary forces at Chittagong
- Captured the Police Armoury and Auxiliary Forces Armoury
- Raised the **Indian national flag** and proclaimed a provisional revolutionary government
- A telegraph wire was cut to prevent communication with Calcutta and other centres
- The British mobilised and the revolutionaries dispersed; a battle at **Jalalabad Hill** followed on 22 April 1930

**Aftermath:**
- Surya Sen evaded capture for nearly three years
- Arrested in **February 1933**; tried and executed on **12 January 1934**
- Women revolutionaries **Pritilata Waddedar** and **Kalpana Datta** participated in subsequent operations — Pritilata died leading a raid on a European club (September 1932)

## Significance of Revolutionary Nationalism

1. **Psychological impact:** Acts of daring resistance inspired millions, particularly youth, even when militarily unsuccessful
2. **Exposed British brutality:** Trials and executions of young revolutionaries generated sympathy and anger internationally
3. **Complemented mass movements:** Revolutionary action and Gandhian mass politics were not opposites — they created pressure from different directions; the British had to deal with both simultaneously
4. **Socialist dimension:** HSRA's ideology introduced class analysis and anti-capitalism into the independence discourse — a legacy that shaped post-independence economic thinking
5. **Communal unity:** Organisations like HSRA and the Chittagong group were explicitly non-communal — Hindu, Muslim, and Sikh members fought together
