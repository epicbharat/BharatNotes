The **Quit India Movement** (August 1942), also called the **August Kranti (August Revolution)**, was the most militant mass uprising of the Indian independence struggle. Launched by Mahatma Gandhi on **8 August 1942** at the Bombay session of the All India Congress Committee, it represented a decisive break from earlier Gandhian methods — a firm, unconditional demand for immediate British withdrawal.

## Background: Why 1942?

Several converging factors made 1942 a turning point:

- **World War II context:** Japan had seized Singapore (February 1942) and was advancing toward Burma and India. Britain desperately needed Indian support.
- **Failure of the Cripps Mission (March 1942):** The British government sent **Sir Stafford Cripps** to negotiate Indian co-operation in the war in exchange for future dominion status. The mission failed because:
  - It offered dominion status only **after the war**, not immediately
  - It allowed **provinces and princely states to opt out** of the proposed Indian union — unacceptable to Congress
  - It did not transfer real power during the war; the Viceroy retained ultimate authority
  - Gandhi famously called the offer a "post-dated cheque on a failing bank"
- **Rising anti-British sentiment** due to wartime shortages, inflation, and forced requisitioning of food and resources

## The Resolution and "Do or Die"

On the night of **8–9 August 1942**, the AICC passed the **Quit India Resolution** at Gowalia Tank Maidan (now August Kranti Maidan), Bombay. Gandhi delivered his historic **"Do or Die"** speech:

> *"We shall either free India or die in the attempt; we shall not live to see the perpetuation of our slavery."*

The resolution demanded:
- **Immediate end to British rule** over India
- In case of non-compliance, the largest-scale civil disobedience in Indian history

## British Response: Mass Arrests

Within hours of Gandhi's speech, the **entire Congress leadership was arrested** and imprisoned without trial — Gandhi, Nehru, Patel, Azad, and thousands of others. The movement was thus leaderless from the very beginning.

**Scale of repression:**
- Over **100,000 people imprisoned**
- Hundreds killed in police and military firing
- Villages bombed and machine-gunned from aircraft in certain regions
- The British used the Defence of India Act to suppress all protest

## The Underground Movement

With leaders in jail, the movement went underground. A new, younger generation stepped forward:

- **Aruna Asaf Ali** hoisted the Congress flag at Gowalia Tank the day after arrests (became an iconic act of defiance)
- **Ram Manohar Lohia**, **Jayaprakash Narayan**, and **Sucheta Kripalani** ran underground operations, issuing pamphlets and directing sabotage
- Communication lines, railway tracks, telegraph wires, and government buildings were targeted
- **Radio stations** were set up underground — most notably by Usha Mehta, who ran the Congress Radio (Secret Congress Radio) from Bombay

## Parallel Governments (Prati Sarkars)

The most remarkable feature of the 1942 movement was the emergence of **parallel governments** — local administrations that replaced British authority in several areas:

| Region | Leader | Duration |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Satara** (Maharashtra) | Nana Patil ("Kratisimha") | August 1943 – June 1946 (longest-running) |
| **Tamluk (Jatiya Sarkar)**, Midnapore, Bengal | Satish Samanta | December 1942 – September 1944 |
| **Ballia**, Uttar Pradesh | Chittu Pande | August 1942 (briefly) |
| **Bhagalpur**, Bihar | Local leaders | 1942–43 |

**Satara Prati Sarkar** (parallel government) was the most organised — it ran people's courts (Nyayadan Mandals), a volunteer force (Seva Dal), and militant units (Tufan Dal) that disrupted colonial communications. It lasted approximately three years.

## Key Figures

- **Mahatma Gandhi** — Initiator; arrested 9 August 1942; held at Aga Khan Palace, Pune
- **Aruna Asaf Ali** — "Heroine of the 1942 Movement"; flag-hoisting at Gowalia Tank
- **Jayaprakash Narayan** — Underground resistance; escaped Hazaribagh jail
- **Usha Mehta** — Secret Congress Radio, Bombay
- **Ram Manohar Lohia** — Underground propaganda and organisation
- **Nana Patil** — Led Satara parallel government

## Significance of the Quit India Movement

**Why it mattered:**

1. **Demonstrated mass will for freedom:** Despite brutal suppression, the movement proved that the Indian population was ready to fight for independence regardless of cost.
2. **Leaderless but spontaneous:** The uprising without Congress leadership demonstrated that ordinary Indians — workers, students, peasants — could sustain a national movement.
3. **Accelerated British rethinking:** The 1942 movement, combined with INA trials (1945) and the Naval Mutiny (1946), convinced Britain that holding India by force was no longer tenable.
4. **New leadership emerged:** JP, Lohia, Aruna Asaf Ali — the next generation of nationalist leadership was forged in this crucible.
5. **International dimension:** World attention was drawn to the contradiction of a Britain fighting for democracy while denying it to India.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- The movement was suppressed within weeks in most areas; parallel governments were exceptions
- The Congress's decision to launch a mass movement during wartime was criticised (by Ambedkar, Muslim League, and Communists who prioritised the anti-fascist war effort)
- No clear programme for post-independence governance emerged
