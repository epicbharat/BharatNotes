## Overview

After the Revolt of 1857, a new phase of Indian political life began. Western education, the press, and a growing awareness of colonial exploitation created a class of politically conscious Indians who would channel their grievances through organised platforms — most importantly, the **Indian National Congress** (1885).

---

## Factors Behind the Rise of Nationalism

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Western Education** | Created a class of English-educated Indians (Dadabhai Naoroji, Surendranath Banerjee, Gokhale) who studied European political philosophy and articulated Indian grievances in the coloniser's language |
| **The Press** | Newspapers like *Amrita Bazar Patrika*, *The Hindu*, *Kesari*, *Bombay Samachar* spread nationalist ideas and created a shared political consciousness |
| **Economic Critique** | Dadabhai Naoroji's **Drain Theory** exposed wealth transfer from India to Britain; R.C. Dutt's *Economic History of India* documented systematic exploitation |
| **Racial Discrimination** | **Ilbert Bill controversy (1883)** — proposed allowing Indian judges to try European defendants; the violent European backlash exposed racist attitudes and galvanised Indian opinion |
| **International Influences** | Irish Home Rule movement, Italian unification (Mazzini, Garibaldi), American Revolution — inspired Indian leaders |
| **Rediscovery of Indian Heritage** | Orientalist scholars (William Jones, Max Mueller) and Indian reformers revived pride in ancient Indian civilisation |
| **Administrative unification** | British railways, telegraph, postal system, and English language unintentionally connected Indians across regions — creating a sense of national unity |

## Political Organisations Before the INC

| Organisation | Year | Founder | Significance |
|-------------|------|---------|-------------|
| **Landholders' Society** | 1838 | Dwarkanath Tagore | First political organisation in India; represented zamindari interests |
| **British India Association** | 1851 | Debendranath Tagore, Radhakanta Deb | Submitted petitions to Parliament on Indian grievances |
| **Indian National Association** | 1876 | **Surendranath Banerjee** and Ananda Mohan Bose | First avowedly nationalist organisation; merged with INC later |
| **Indian Association (Poona)** | 1885 | Founded by several Poona intellectuals | Parallel to the INC |
| **Madras Mahajan Sabha** | 1884 | P. Rangaiah Naidu, S. Subramania Iyer, and others | Regional political body in Madras |
| **Bombay Presidency Association** | 1885 | Pherozeshah Mehta, Badruddin Tyabji, K.T. Telang | Leading political body in western India |

## Formation of the Indian National Congress (INC)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Founded** | **28 December 1885** |
| **Venue** | Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College, **Bombay** |
| **Founder/Organiser** | **Allan Octavian Hume** (retired British civil servant) — though the role of Indian leaders was equally important |
| **First President** | **Womesh Chunder Bonnerjee (W.C. Bonnerjee)** |
| **Delegates at First Session** | **72** |
| **Original name planned** | **Indian National Union** (changed before the first session) |
| **Original venue planned** | **Poona** (shifted to Bombay due to a plague/cholera outbreak) |
| **Notable first session delegates** | Dadabhai Naoroji, Pherozeshah Mehta, Badruddin Tyabji, Surendranath Banerjee, S. Subramania Iyer |

### "Safety Valve" Theory

Some historians argue Hume founded the INC as a "**safety valve**" — a controlled outlet for Indian discontent that would prevent another revolt like 1857. While there is some evidence for this (Hume's correspondence with the Viceroy Lord Dufferin), Indian historians like Bipan Chandra argue the "safety valve" interpretation is **reductive** — the INC became genuinely nationalist very quickly.

### Important INC Sessions

| Year | Venue | President | Significance |
|------|-------|-----------|-------------|
| 1885 | Bombay | W.C. Bonnerjee | First session; 72 delegates |
| 1886 | Calcutta | Dadabhai Naoroji | First of his 3 presidencies (1886, 1893, 1906) |
| 1887 | Madras | **Badruddin Tyabji** | First **Muslim** president of INC |
| 1888 | Allahabad | **George Yule** | First **British** president of INC |
| 1896 | Calcutta | **Rahimtulla Sayani** | Increased Muslim participation |
| 1905 | Banaras | Gokhale | Moderate-dominated; Swadeshi movement endorsed |
| 1906 | Calcutta | Dadabhai Naoroji | "Swaraj" used for the first time in a presidential address; but meant self-government within the British Empire, not independence |

## The Moderate Phase (1885–1905)

### Key Moderate Leaders

| Leader | Contribution |
|--------|-------------|
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** | "**Grand Old Man of India**"; propounded the **Drain Theory** (*Poverty and Un-British Rule in India*, 1901); thrice INC president (1886, 1893, 1906); elected to the **British Parliament** from Central Finsbury, London (1892) — first Asian MP in Britain |
| **Gopal Krishna Gokhale** | "**Political Guru**" of Gandhi; founded **Servants of India Society (1905)** — dedicated to public service; advocated social reform alongside political reform; member of Imperial Legislative Council |
| **Surendranath Banerjee** | "**Indian Burke**"; founded Indian National Association (1876); edited *The Bengalee* newspaper; twice INC president; key figure in the Swadeshi movement |
| **Pherozeshah Mehta** | "**Lion of Bombay**"; key figure in Bombay municipal politics; helped shape early Congress agenda |
| **W.C. Bonnerjee** | First INC president; prominent Calcutta barrister |
| **Mahadev Govind Ranade** | Jurist and social reformer; intellectual force behind early Indian nationalism in western India |

### Methods of the Moderates

- Sending **petitions and memoranda** to the British government
- Debating in the **legislative councils**
- Holding annual **Congress sessions** and passing resolutions
- Writing in **newspapers** to create public awareness
- Sending **delegations to England** to present Indian grievances
- Working within the British constitutional framework — "prayer, petition, and protest"

### Achievements and Limitations

| Achievements | Limitations |
|-------------|-------------|
| Indian Councils Act 1892 — expanded legislative councils | Reforms were too slow and too little |
| Raised political consciousness among the educated class | Failed to **mobilise the masses** — remained an elite movement |
| Economic critique (Drain Theory) exposed colonial exploitation | Over-reliance on **British goodwill** — assumed the British were fair |
| Laid the foundation for organised nationalism | Called "**political mendicants**" by Extremists |
| Created the INC — the vehicle that would eventually win independence | Annual sessions became repetitive talking shops |

## Economic Nationalism

### The Drain Theory in Detail

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Dadabhai Naoroji** | First articulated the drain in 1867 (paper before the East India Association); refined in *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India* (1901) |
| **R.C. Dutt** | *Economic History of India* (Vol. I: 1757–1837, published 1901; Vol. II: 1837–1900, published 1903) — systematic documentation of British economic exploitation |
| **Core argument** | India's poverty was not due to Indian laziness or backwardness but to the **systematic drain of wealth** by the colonial power |
| **Impact** | Provided the intellectual ammunition for the nationalist movement; forced the British to engage with economic arguments |

## UPSC Relevance

### Prelims Focus Areas
- INC founded: 28 December 1885, Bombay; A.O. Hume; first president W.C. Bonnerjee; 72 delegates
- First Muslim INC president: Badruddin Tyabji (1887)

- Gokhale: Political Guru of Gandhi, Servants of India Society 1905
- Surendranath Banerjee: Indian Burke, Indian National Association 1876
- "Swaraj" first used in INC presidential address: 1906 (Dadabhai Naoroji)

- Assess the role of the Moderates — were they successful or failures?
