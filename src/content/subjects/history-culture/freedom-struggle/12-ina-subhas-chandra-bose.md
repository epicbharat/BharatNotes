**Subhas Chandra Bose** (1897–1945) represents the militant, uncompromising strand of Indian nationalism. Where Gandhi sought independence through moral force and civil disobedience, Bose believed that **armed struggle** was necessary to dislodge British rule — and he pursued that belief to extraordinary lengths, building an army in occupied Southeast Asia during World War II.

## Bose Before the INA

- Born 23 January 1897 in Cuttack, Orissa; educated at Presidency College, Calcutta and Cambridge
- Topped the **Indian Civil Service exam (1920)** but resigned in protest against British rule
- Rose rapidly in Congress politics; elected **President of the Indian National Congress** twice (1938 and 1939)
- **1939 crisis:** Re-elected Congress President against Gandhi's preferred candidate (Pattabhi Sitaramayya) but resigned due to opposition from the Gandhi camp; formed the **Forward Bloc** (1939) within Congress
- Placed under house arrest by the British (1940); **escaped in disguise** in January 1941 through Afghanistan to Soviet Union, eventually reaching Germany
- In Germany, attempted to raise an **Indian Legion** from Indian POWs; met Hitler in May 1942 but received little practical support
- Travelled by submarine to Southeast Asia; arrived in **Singapore on 2 July 1943**

## The Indian National Army (INA) — Azad Hind Fauj

### First Formation (1942)
The INA was originally formed in **1942 in Singapore** by Captain Mohan Singh and Japanese officer Fujiwara Iwaichi, drawing from Indian prisoners of war captured after the **fall of Singapore** (February 1942). This first INA was poorly organised and collapsed due to disputes with Japanese authorities.

### Bose's Reorganisation (1943)
When Bose arrived in Singapore in July 1943, he took command of the demoralised INA and transformed it:
- Strength doubled to approximately **60,000 troops** at its peak — combining POWs and civilian Indian volunteers from Malaya, Singapore, and Burma
- Created women's regiment: **Rani of Jhansi Regiment**, commanded by Captain Lakshmi Sahgal
- Motto: **"Jai Hind"** (later adopted as India's national salutation)
- Battle cry: **"Delhi Chalo"** (Onward to Delhi)

## Azad Hind Government (Provisional Government of Free India)

On **21 October 1943**, Bose proclaimed the formation of the **Provisional Government of Azad Hind** (Free India) at Cathay Building, Singapore, with himself as:
- Head of State
- Prime Minister
- Minister of War and Foreign Affairs

**International recognition:** The Azad Hind Government was recognised by **nine countries**, including Japan, Germany, Italy, and several Japanese-occupied Asian nations. It controlled the **Andaman and Nicobar Islands** (renamed Shahid and Swaraj Dweep) directly.

## Military Campaign

The INA fought alongside Japanese forces in the **Burma-India front**:
- Participated in the **Imphal and Kohima campaign** (1944) — Japan's attempt to invade northeast India
- INA soldiers crossed into Indian territory and raised the Indian tricolour at **Moirang, Manipur** (April 1944)
- The Imphal-Kohima campaign failed — one of the largest land defeats in Japanese military history
- INA suffered enormous casualties from combat, disease, and starvation during the retreat

## Bose's Ideology

- **"Give me blood and I will give you freedom"** — famous exhortation to the Indian diaspora in Southeast Asia
- Combined elements of **nationalism, socialism, and authoritarianism** — admired both Mussolini's organisational model and Soviet socialist economics
- Believed that Indian independence required confronting British power militarily; rejected the gradualism of Congress constitutionalism
- Emphasised **Hindu-Muslim unity** within the INA — deliberately included soldiers of all faiths in leadership positions
- Vision for post-independence India: a strong centralised state with economic planning for development

## Death and Mystery

Bose died on **18 August 1945** in a plane crash over **Taipei (then Japanese-controlled Formosa/Taiwan)** after Japanese surrender. The crash and its circumstances have been subject to decades of controversy and multiple Indian government inquiries.

- **Shahnawaz Committee (1956):** Confirmed plane crash death
- **Khosla Commission (1974):** Also confirmed plane crash
- **Mukherjee Commission (2005):** Controversially stated the ashes at Renkoji Temple, Tokyo were not Bose's — rejected by Indian government

## The INA Trials (Red Fort Trials, 1945–46)

After Japan's surrender, the British arrested INA officers and decided to try them for **treason and waging war against the King-Emperor**.

The first trial at **Red Fort, November 1945** featured three officers:
- **Prem Kumar Sehgal** (Hindu)
- **Shah Nawaz Khan** (Muslim)
- **Gurbaksh Singh Dhillon** (Sikh)

The deliberate selection of defendants from three major religious communities — and their joint defence by Nehru, Bhulabhai Desai, and Tej Bahadur Sapru — turned the trial into a powerful symbol of Hindu-Muslim-Sikh unity.

### Public Response
- Nationwide protests erupted demanding release of INA prisoners
- The trials **united Indians across religious and political lines** — Congress, Muslim League, Hindu Mahasabha, and Communists all opposed them
- British barracks witnessed protests; a critical factor in the **Royal Indian Navy Mutiny (February 1946)**

### British Outcome
The British quietly dropped charges against most accused — public pressure made convictions politically untenable. The three main accused were convicted but immediately released.

## Significance of the INA and Bose

| Dimension | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| **Military threat** | Demonstrated that Indian soldiers would fight against Britain for independence |
| **Psychological** | Shattered the myth of British invincibility and Indian loyalty |
| **Hindu-Muslim unity** | INA was a living model of communal harmony — directly contrasted with the League-Congress divide |
| **Naval Mutiny catalyst** | INA trials directly inspired the Royal Indian Navy Mutiny, 1946 |
| **Accelerating independence** | British realised the military loyalty of Indian troops could no longer be assumed — a key factor in the decision to leave India |

Clement Attlee, British Prime Minister who oversaw the transfer of power, later stated that the INA's impact on Indian armed forces' loyalty was a major reason Britain left India in 1947.
