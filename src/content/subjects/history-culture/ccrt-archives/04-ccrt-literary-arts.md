## Key Concepts

- Indian literary tradition spans over **3,500 years** — from the Rigveda (c. 1500–1200 BCE) to contemporary writing
- **Shruti** (heard/revealed) literature: the four Vedas, Brahmanas, Aranyakas, Upanishads — the eternal canon
- **Smriti** (remembered/composed) literature: epics, Puranas, Dharmashastra, classical poetry and drama
- Sanskrit was the dominant literary language of ancient and classical India; Pali and Prakrit served Buddhist and Jain communities; Tamil is the oldest living literary language in India with continuous recorded output since c. 300 BCE
- **Bhakti and Sufi movements** (8th–17th centuries) transformed Indian literature by shifting from Sanskrit to vernacular languages, democratising literary expression
- India has **11 classical languages** (as of October 2024): Tamil, Sanskrit, Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam, Odia, Marathi, Bengali, Pali, Prakrit, and Assamese
- **Sahitya Akademi** (est. 1954) is India's National Academy of Letters; it gives annual awards in 24 languages

## Vedic Literature

The Vedic corpus is the oldest surviving body of literature in any Indo-European language. It is divided into two broad categories:

- **Shruti ("that which is heard")** — revealed texts; the Vedas, Brahmanas, Aranyakas, and Upanishads
- **Smriti ("that which is remembered")** — composed texts; epics, Puranas, Dharmashastra

### The Four Vedas (Samhitas)

The four Vedas are collections of hymns, mantras, and ritual formulae. Each has its own priest-class and ritual function:

| Veda | Content | Ritual Function | Key Feature |
|------|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Rigveda** | 10 mandalas, 1,028 hymns (suktas), 10,552 verses — hymns to deities (Agni, Indra, Varuna, Soma, Usha) | Recited by *Hotri* priest | Oldest Veda; c. 1500–1200 BCE; oldest surviving literary document in an Indo-European language |
| **Samaveda** | Derived largely from Rigveda hymns set to musical notation (saman) | Sung by *Udgatri* priest | Foundation of Indian classical music; first known system of musical notation |
| **Yajurveda** | Prose formulas (yajus) for sacrificial rituals; two recensions — Krishna (Black) and Shukla (White) Yajurveda | Recited by *Adhvaryu* priest | Most liturgical of the Vedas; covers ritual procedure in detail |
| **Atharvaveda** | Hymns dealing with everyday concerns — healing, protection, philosophy; 20 books, 730 hymns | Associated with *Brahma* priest (supervisor) | Most "popular" Veda; rich in folk traditions, incantations, and early philosophical speculation |

### Brahmanas

The Brahmanas are prose texts attached to each Veda that explain the meaning and procedure of Vedic rituals. They form the **Karma-Kanda** (action/ritual section) of the Vedic canon. Key Brahmanas include:

- **Shatapatha Brahmana** (of the Shukla Yajurveda) — the largest and most important; contains early cosmological and philosophical ideas
- **Aitareya Brahmana** and **Kaushitaki Brahmana** (of the Rigveda)
- **Taittiriya Brahmana** (of the Krishna Yajurveda)
- **Chandogya Brahmana** and **Jaiminiya Brahmana** (of the Samaveda)

### Aranyakas

The Aranyakas ("forest books") form a transitional layer between the Brahmanas and the Upanishads. They were composed by forest-dwelling hermits and mark a shift from external ritual to inner reflection. The Aranyakas reinterpret Vedic sacrifices as symbolic and philosophical acts.

### Upanishads

The Upanishads ("sitting near the teacher") are the philosophical culmination of the Vedic tradition — collectively called **Vedanta** (end of the Vedas). They explore the nature of *Brahman* (ultimate reality), *Atman* (individual self), and the relationship between the two.

- Total number: **108 Upanishads** are traditionally listed
- **10 principal Upanishads (Dasopanishad):** Isha, Kena, Katha, Prashna, Mundaka, Mandukya, Taittiriya, Aitareya, Chandogya, and Brihadaranyaka — these ten were commented upon by Adi Shankaracharya and are considered the most philosophically authoritative
- The Upanishads form the **Jnana-Kanda** (knowledge section) of the Vedic canon
- Core doctrines: **Brahman-Atman identity** ("Aham Brahmasmi" — I am Brahman); **Tat Tvam Asi** (Thou art That); *karma*, *samsara*, and *moksha*

### Vedangas (Six Auxiliary Sciences)

The Vedangas are six disciplines that developed as ancillary studies for the correct understanding and recitation of the Vedas. They are sometimes called the "limbs of the Vedas":

| Vedanga | Subject | Key Text / Significance |
|---------|---------|------------------------|
| **Shiksha** (Phonetics) | Correct pronunciation, phonology, euphony (sandhi) | Panini's Shiksha; Paniniya Shiksha |
| **Kalpa** (Ritual) | Correct performance of Vedic sacrifices and rites | Divided into Srauta Sutras (public rituals), Grihya Sutras (domestic rites), Dharma Sutras (social law) |
| **Vyakarana** (Grammar) | Sanskrit grammar and linguistic analysis | **Ashtadhyayi** by **Panini** (c. 4th century BCE) — the most scientific grammar of any ancient language; 3,959 sutras |
| **Nirukta** (Etymology) | Interpretation of difficult Vedic words | **Nirukta** by **Yaska** (c. 5th–6th century BCE) — first known work of etymology in the world |
| **Chandas** (Metrics) | Poetic metres used in Vedic and classical Sanskrit poetry | **Chanda-shastra** by **Pingala** (c. 2nd century BCE) — contains early work on binary numbers and combinatorics |
| **Jyotisha** (Astronomy) | Astronomical calculations for fixing times of Vedic sacrifices | Vedanga Jyotisha — earliest astronomical text in India |

## Sanskrit Epics and Puranas

### Ramayana

The **Valmiki Ramayana** is the first great Sanskrit epic — called the **Adi-Kavya** (original poem) and Valmiki the **Adi-Kavi** (first poet). Key facts:

- **Author:** Sage **Valmiki** — traditionally the original poet of Sanskrit literature
- **Scale:** 24,000 couplets (slokas) across **seven kandas (books)**
- **Language:** Classical Sanskrit; narrative verse form (*anustubh* metre)

The seven kandas and their themes:

| # | Kanda | Theme |
|---|-------|-------|
| 1 | **Balakanda** (77 sargas) | Birth of Rama; Vishwamitra's ashram; Sita's swayamvara |
| 2 | **Ayodhyakanda** (119 sargas) | Rama's exile; Dasharatha's death; Bharata's rule |
| 3 | **Aranyakanda** (75 sargas) | Forest exile; abduction of Sita by Ravana |
| 4 | **Kishkindhakanda** (67 sargas) | Alliance with Sugriva; Hanuman's role |
| 5 | **Sundarakanda** (68 sargas) | Hanuman's journey to Lanka; discovery of Sita |
| 6 | **Yuddhakanda** (131 sargas) | War in Lanka; Ravana's defeat; Rama's return |
| 7 | **Uttarakanda** (110 sargas) | Sita's exile; birth of Lava and Kusha; Rama's ascension |

The Uttarakanda is widely considered a later addition.

### Mahabharata

The **Mahabharata** (attributed to sage **Vyasa / Vedavyasa**) is the world's longest epic poem and an encyclopaedia of ancient Indian culture, philosophy, law, and cosmology.

- **Scale:** Over **1,00,000 slokas (verses)** — approximately seven times the length of the Iliad and Odyssey combined; sometimes stated as 1,80,000–2,00,000 lines including the prose passages
- **Structure:** **18 parvas (books)** + the **Harivamsa** as an appendix (khila)
- Three historical layers: (1) *Jaya* (8,800 verses, attributed to Vyasa), (2) *Bharata* (24,000 verses, recited by Vaishampayana), and (3) the full *Mahabharata* (1,00,000+ verses, recited by Ugrasrava Sauti)

The 18 parvas:
Adi, Sabha, Vana (Aranyaka), Virata, Udyoga, Bhishma, Drona, Karna, Shalya, Sauptika, Stri, Shanti, Anushasana, Ashvamedhika, Ashramavasika, Mausala, Mahaprasthanika, and Svargarohana.

The **Bhagavad Gita** (18 chapters; 700 verses) is contained within the Bhishma Parva and is among the most philosophically significant texts in world literature.

### Eighteen Major Puranas (Mahapuranas)

The Puranas are encyclopaedic texts dealing with cosmology, genealogy of gods and kings, Hindu philosophy, and ritual. Traditionally composed or compiled by **Sage Vyasa**, the 18 Mahapuranas are:

Vishnu, Bhagavata, Naradiya, Garuda, Padma, Varaha (Sattvic — associated with Vishnu), Brahma, Brahmanda, Brahmavaivarta, Markandeya, Bhavishya, Vamana (Rajasic — associated with Brahma), Matsya, Kurma, Linga, Shiva, Skanda, Agni (Tamasic — associated with Shiva).

**Most important for UPSC:**
- **Bhagavata Purana** — 12 skandhas; focus on Vishnu's ten avatars; important for Vaishnavism
- **Vishnu Purana** — one of the earliest; detailed cosmological and genealogical information
- **Matsya Purana** — oldest Purana; contains a version of the flood myth
- **Skanda Purana** — the largest Purana; covers tirthas (pilgrimage sites) extensively

### Legal and Political Literature

| Text | Author | Period | Significance |
|------|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Arthashastra** | **Kautilya / Chanakya** | c. 3rd century BCE (rediscovered 1905) | Treatise on statecraft, economic policy, military strategy, and administration; **first systematic text on political economy in India** |
| **Manusmriti** (Manu Dharmashastra) | Attributed to **Manu** | c. 2nd century BCE – 3rd century CE | Most influential Dharmashastra; codified Hindu social law; 12 chapters, 2,685 shlokas |
| **Yajnavalkya Smriti** | Attributed to **Yajnavalkya** | c. 1st–3rd century CE | Second most influential Dharmashastra; more systematic than Manusmriti |

## Classical Sanskrit Literature

### Kalidasa — The Foremost Classical Sanskrit Poet

**Kalidasa** (c. 4th–5th century CE; associated with the court of **Chandragupta II Vikramaditya** of the Gupta dynasty) is universally regarded as the greatest Sanskrit poet and dramatist. He composed **seven major works**:

**Three Plays (Nataka):**

| Play | Theme | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Abhijnanasakuntalam** (The Recognition of Shakuntala) | Love between King Dushyanta and Shakuntala; separation by a curse; recognition through a ring | Considered Kalidasa's masterpiece; widely regarded as one of the greatest plays ever written; first translated into English by William Jones (1789); inspired Goethe |
| **Vikramorvasiyam** (Urvashi Won by Valour) | Love between King Pururavas and celestial nymph Urvashi | Based on a Rigvedic dialogue |
| **Malavikagnimitram** (Malavika and Agnimitra) | Court romance; King Agnimitra of the Shunga dynasty | Earliest of Kalidasa's three plays |

**Two Mahakavyas (Epic Poems):**

| Poem | Theme | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Raghuvamsha** | Dynasty of Raghu — tracing the lineage of Rama through 19 cantos | Considered his greatest narrative poem |
| **Kumarasambhava** | Love of Shiva and Parvati leading to the birth of Kartikeya (Kumara) | First eight cantos are universally accepted as Kalidasa's work |

**Two Khandakavyas (Lyric Poems):**

| Poem | Theme | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Meghaduta** (The Cloud Messenger) | A Yaksha exiled from Alaka uses a cloud as messenger to his beloved | Masterpiece of *viraha* (separation) poetry; describes the geography of India from Ramgiri (Nagpur region) to the Himalayas |
| **Ritusamhara** (The Gathering of Seasons) | Describes the six seasons of India | Considered an early work; possibly his first composition |

### Other Major Classical Sanskrit Authors

| Author | Period | Works | Significance |
|--------|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Bhasa** | c. 2nd–3rd century CE | 13 plays including *Svapnavasavadatta*, *Pancharatra*, *Charudatta*, *Urubhanga*, *Karnabhara* | Possibly India's earliest known playwright; his manuscripts were rediscovered by T. Ganapati Shastri in 1912 from Kerala |
| **Shudraka** | c. 4th–6th century CE | **Mricchakatika** (The Little Clay Cart) — 10 acts | Realistic portrayal of middle-class urban life; one of the finest Sanskrit plays; hero Charudatta; heroine courtesan Vasantasena |
| **Harshavardhana** | 7th century CE | Three plays: **Ratnavali**, **Priyadarshika**, **Nagananda** | King of Kanauj and literary patron; *Nagananda* (The Joy of the Serpents) notable for its Buddhist ethic of self-sacrifice |
| **Banabhatta** | 7th century CE (court poet of Harsha) | **Harshacharita** (biography of Harsha), **Kadambari** (prose romance — the world's earliest novel in some views) | *Kadambari* was completed by his son Bhushanabhatta after Bana's death |
| **Dandin** | 7th century CE | **Dasakumaracharita** (What Ten Young Men Did), **Kavyadarsha** (Mirror of Poetry) | *Dasakumaracharita* — realistic picaresque narrative prose; *Kavyadarsha* — foundational work on Sanskrit poetics |
| **Vishakhadatta** | c. 4th–5th century CE | **Mudrarakshasa** (The Signet Ring of Rakshasa), **Devichandraguptam** | *Mudrarakshasa* deals with political intrigue around Chandragupta Maurya and Kautilya's manoeuvres against minister Rakshasa |
| **Bharavi** | 7th century CE | **Kiratarjuniya** (Arjuna and the Kirata) | Famous for the *artha-gaurava* (weight of meaning) style; Canto 15 contains a verse that reads the same in all four directions (palindrome) |
| **Magha** | 7th–8th century CE | **Shishupalavadha** (Slaying of Shishupala) | Along with Kiratarjuniya and Naishadhiyacharita, forms the trio of great post-Kalidasa mahakavyas |
| **Jayadeva** | 12th century CE | **Gitagovinda** | Lyrical poem on Radha-Krishna; profound influence on Vaishnavism, Odissi dance, and the Bhakti movement |

### Sanskrit Poetics (Kavyashastra)

| Text | Author | Theory |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Natyashastra** | **Bharata Muni** (c. 2nd century BCE – 2nd century CE) | Foundation of Indian aesthetics; defines the **Rasa theory** — 8 rasas (Navarasa with Shanta added later) |
| **Kavyadarsha** | Dandin | Three styles of poetry (Vaidarbha, Gauda, Panchali) |
| **Dhvanyaloka** | **Anandavardhana** (9th century) | Theory of *dhvani* (resonance/suggestion) — the soul of poetry |
| **Abhinavabharati** | **Abhinavagupta** (10th–11th century) | Commentary on Natyashastra; elaborated the Rasa theory |

## Pali, Prakrit, Buddhist and Jain Literature

### Pali Canon (Tipitaka)

**Pali** was the lingua franca of the Buddhist world in northern India during the time of the Buddha (c. 5th century BCE) and became the canonical language of Theravada Buddhism. The Pali Canon — called the **Tipitaka ("Three Baskets")** — is the most complete early Buddhist canonical collection:

| Pitaka | Meaning | Contents |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Vinaya Pitaka** | Basket of Discipline | Rules governing the monastic community (Sangha) — for monks (Bhikshu) and nuns (Bhikshuni) |
| **Sutta Pitaka** | Basket of Discourses | The Buddha's teachings — dialogues, sermons, and ethical instructions; includes the **Dhammapada** (423 verses on ethics) |
| **Abhidhamma Pitaka** | Basket of Higher Doctrine | Philosophical analysis and psychological classification of Buddhist teaching |

**Jataka Stories** — 547 tales of the Buddha's previous lives, contained within the Pali Canon's Khuddaka Nikaya. They are a rich source for ancient Indian social history, folklore, and secular literature.

### Buddhist Sanskrit Literature

| Work | Author | Period | Significance |
|------|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Buddhacharita** (Acts of the Buddha) | **Ashvaghosa** | c. early 2nd century CE | First Sanskrit biography of the Buddha; mahakavya in 28 cantos (only parts survive); the first major Sanskrit literary work in the classical style |
| **Saundarananda** | Ashvaghosa | c. 2nd century CE | Narrative poem on the conversion of Nanda (Buddha's half-brother) |
| **Mahavastu** | Anonymous (Mahasanghika school) | c. 1st–4th century CE | Hybrid Sanskrit biography of the Buddha |
| **Lalitavistara** | Anonymous (Sarvastivada school) | c. 3rd century CE | Life of the Buddha in Mahayana tradition |
| **Milindapanha** | Anonymous | c. 1st–2nd century CE | Pali prose dialogue between King Menander (Milinda) and monk Nagasena |

### Prakrit Literature

Prakrit languages are the Middle Indo-Aryan vernaculars that evolved from Sanskrit (or from a common proto-language). Major Prakrit literary traditions include:

- **Ardha-Magadhi** — the language of the Jain canonical texts; spoken in the Magadha and eastern India region during Mahavira's time
- **Sauraseni** — used in Sanskrit drama for female and lower-class characters
- **Maharashtri** — the prestige Prakrit for lyric poetry; used by Hala (author of the *Gathasaptasati*, an anthology of 700 Prakrit verses on love)
- **Apabhramsha** — the transitional stage between Prakrits and modern Indian languages; a bridge to vernacular literary traditions

### Jain Literature (Agamas)

The canonical texts of Jainism are called **Agamas** (or Siddhanta). They are composed in **Ardha-Magadhi Prakrit** and were originally compiled by the **Ganadharas** (chief disciples) of **Mahavira** (c. 599–527 BCE), based on his teachings.

| Category | Number | Description |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| **Angas** | 12 | Primary canonical texts; the most authoritative; the 12th Anga (*Drstivada*) is lost |
| **Upangas** | 12 | Secondary canonical texts supplementing the Angas |
| **Prakirnakas** | 10 | Miscellaneous texts |
| **Chedasutras** | 6 | Texts on monastic discipline |
| **Mulasutras** | 4 | Basic texts for monks and nuns |
| **Chulika Sutras** | 2 | Appendixes |

The **Digambara** (sky-clad) sect of Jainism does not accept the Shvetambara Agamic texts as canonical; they consider the original teachings lost, and use texts like the **Satkhandagama** and **Kasayapahuda** as authoritative.

## Tamil Sangam Literature

Tamil Sangam literature represents one of the most remarkable literary traditions in the ancient world — a body of secular, sophisticated poetry produced in the Tamil-speaking south of India between c. **300 BCE and 300 CE**.

### The Three Sangams (Legend)

Tamil tradition speaks of three legendary literary academies (Sangams) convened at Madurai under the patronage of Pandya kings:
- **First Sangam (Mutasangam):** Said to have lasted 4,400 years at the ancient city of Then-Madurai (now submerged)
- **Second Sangam:** Lasted 3,700 years at Kapadapuram (also submerged); only the **Tolkappiyam** survived
- **Third Sangam:** Lasted 1,850 years at the present Madurai; produced the surviving corpus of Sangam literature

Modern scholars consider the three-Sangam account to be legendary. The historical Sangam literature is dated to c. 300 BCE–300 CE.

### Tolkappiyam

The **Tolkappiyam** is the oldest surviving work of Tamil grammar and literary theory. Written by **Tolkappiyar**, it is traditionally attributed to the Second Sangam. It covers:
- **Phonology** (Ezhuttadhikaram)
- **Grammar and rhetoric** (Colladhikaram)
- **Poetics and subject matter** (Poruladhikaram) — including the famous **Akam** (love) and **Puram** (war/public life) classification of poetry, and the **Tinai** (landscape-emotion) system linking five landscapes to five phases of love

### The Sangam Corpus

The surviving Sangam literature is organised into two major anthologies:

**Ettuthokai (Eight Collections):**

| Text | Type | Theme |
|------|------|-------|
| Ainkurunuru | Short love poems | Akam (love in five landscapes) |
| Kuruntokai | Short poems | Akam |
| Narrinai | Short poems | Akam |
| Akananuru | Long love poems | Akam |
| Kalittokai | Poems with a refrain | Akam; only collection in kali metre |
| Patirruppattu | Ten decads on Chera kings | Puram (heroic, public themes) |
| Purananuru | 400 poems on heroism, death, society | Puram |
| Paripatal | Devotional poems (partly Akam, partly Puram) | Murugan, Vishnu worship |

**Pattupattu (Ten Idylls):**
Ten longer poems on love and heroic themes, including *Tirumurugattrupadai* (on the god Murugan) and *Porunarattrupadai*.

### The Twin Epics of Tamil

| Epic | Author | Theme | Significance |
|------|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Silappadikaram** (The Epic of the Anklet) | **Ilanko Adigal** (a Chera prince who became a monk) | Story of Kovalan and Kannagi; Kannagi's destruction of Madurai after her husband's unjust execution | Oldest surviving Tamil epic; rich in descriptions of ancient Tamil trade, society, and city life; Kannagi is a symbol of chastity and righteous wrath |
| **Manimekalai** | **Sithalai Sattanar** (a merchant-poet) | Sequel to Silappadikaram; the story of Manimekalai (Kovalan and Madhavi's daughter) who becomes a Buddhist nun | Propagates Buddhist philosophy; rare evidence of Buddhism in early South India |

### Thirukkural

- **Author:** **Thiruvalluvar** (dates uncertain; c. 1st–5th century CE)
- **Content:** 1,330 couplets (kurals) of seven words each, arranged into 133 chapters of 10 verses
- **Three books:** Aram (Virtue/Dharma), Porul (Wealth/Artha), and Kama (Love/Inbam)
- **Significance:** One of the greatest works of world ethics literature; translated into more than 80 languages; sometimes called the "Tamil Veda" (*Tirukkural* literally means "sacred couplets")
- The statue of Thiruvalluvar stands **133 feet tall** (symbolising 133 chapters) at the southern tip of India at Kanyakumari

## Medieval Bhakti and Sufi Literature

### The Bhakti Movement and Vernacular Literature

The Bhakti movement (c. 6th–17th centuries) was the most transformative force in the history of Indian literature. It shifted the literary medium from Sanskrit to vernacular languages and democratised devotional expression.

**Origins in Tamil Nadu — the Alvars and Nayanmars:**
- **Alvars** (Vaishnava saints, c. 6th–9th centuries CE) — composed the **Nalayira Divya Prabandham** (4,000 devotional verses in Tamil); 12 Alvars; their compositions were compiled by **Nathamuni** in the 9th century
- **Nayanmars** (Shaiva saints, c. 6th–9th centuries) — 63 Nayanmars; their hymns compiled as the **Tevaram** (by Appar, Sundarar, Thirugnana Sambandar) and **Tiruvachakam** (by Manikkavachagar)

### Major Bhakti Poets — North India

| Poet | Period | Region/Language | Key Works | Tradition |
|------|--------|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Kabir** | c. 1440–1518 CE | Varanasi; Hindi (Awadhi/Bhojpuri) | *Dohas* (two-line couplets), *Bijak* (compilation of his verses) | **Nirguna** Bhakti (formless God); challenged both Hindu caste hierarchy and Islamic orthodoxy; disciple of Ramananda |
| **Tulsidas** | c. 1532–1623 CE | Varanasi; **Awadhi** Hindi | **Ramcharitmanas** (1574 CE; retelling of the Ramayana in Awadhi), **Hanuman Chalisa**, *Vinayapatrika*, *Kavitavali* | Saguna Vaishnava Bhakti (devotion to Rama with form) |
| **Surdas** | c. 1478–1584 CE | Mathura-Vrindavan; **Braj Bhasha** | **Sursagar** (compilation of c. 1,00,000 poems, of which c. 8,000 survive) — vivid depictions of Krishna's childhood; *Sur-Saravali*; *Sahitya-Lahiri* | Saguna Bhakti (devotion to Krishna); disciple of Vallabhacharya; member of **Ashtachhap** (eight Braj poets) |
| **Mirabai** | c. 1498–1547 CE | Rajputana → Vrindavan; **Rajasthani and Braj Bhasha** | *Bhajans* (devotional songs) on her love for Krishna — "Mharo prabhu giridhar gopal" | Saguna Vaishnava Bhakti; her bhajans are among the most beloved in Indian musical tradition |

### Major Bhakti Poets — Maharashtra

| Poet | Period | Key Works | Significance |
|------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Dnyaneshwar (Jnaneshvara)** | c. 1275–1296 CE | **Dnyaneshwari** (Bhavartha Deepika, c. 1290 CE) — a 9,000-couplet commentary on the Bhagavad Gita in Marathi; **Amrutanubhav** | Founded the **Warkari** tradition of Maharashtra; wrote in Marathi when Sanskrit was the sole vehicle for philosophy — a revolutionary act |
| **Namdev** | c. 1270–1350 CE | *Abhangas* in Marathi; also composed in Hindi | Bridge figure between Maharashtra and the Sikh tradition — some of his compositions are in the **Guru Granth Sahib** |
| **Eknath** | c. 1533–1599 CE | *Eknathi Bhagavata* (Marathi commentary on Bhagavata Purana's 11th skanda) | Revived the Warkari tradition; known for caste reform |
| **Tukaram** | c. 1598–1650 CE | **Abhangas** (c. 4,000–4,500 surviving) — devotional poems addressed to **Vitthal (Pandurang)** of Pandharpur | Greatest poet of the Warkari tradition; his *Abhanga Gatha* is an unparalleled record of 17th-century Maharashtra's spiritual life |

### Major Bhakti Poets — Karnataka

| Poet | Period | Key Works | Significance |
|------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Basavanna** | c. 1131–1167 CE | **Vachanas** (prose-poems in Kannada) — c. 22,000 Vachanas from the Veerashaiva movement are published | Founded the **Veerashaiva (Lingayat)** movement; attacked caste, ritual formalism, and temple worship; his *Vachanas* are composed in simple, powerful Kannada prose |
| **Akkamahadevi** | 12th century CE | Vachanas addressed to Shiva as "Chenna-malliku-arjuna" | One of the first women saints of the Bhakti movement in Karnataka |
| **Purandaradasa** | c. 1484–1564 CE | *Devarnamas* (devotional songs in Kannada to Vishnu) | Considered the "father of Carnatic music"; systematised the fundamentals of Carnatic music pedagogy |

### Sufi Literature

The Sufi tradition produced an equally rich literary output alongside the Bhakti movement. Sufi literature emphasised personal love for the Divine, expressed through poetry, *qawwali*, and *masnavi* (narrative poem).

| Poet | Period | Language | Key Works | Significance |
|------|--------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Amir Khusrau** | 1253–1325 CE | Persian, Hindavi (proto-Hindi-Urdu) | *Khamsa* (five masnavis): *Matla ul-Anwar*, *Khusrau-Shirin*, *Laila-Majnun*, *Ayina-i-Iskandari*, *Hasht-Bihisht*; *Ghazals*; *Tuhfat us-Sighar* | Called "**Tuti-yi-Hind**" (Parrot of India); disciple of **Nizamuddin Auliya** (Chishti order); credited with developing **qawwali** and the proto-Hindi language **Hindavi**; introduced the *khayal* form in music |
| **Bulleh Shah** | 1680–1757 CE | Punjabi | **Kafis** (mystical songs in Punjabi) | Greatest poet of the Punjabi Sufi tradition; Qadiri order; challenged religious orthodoxy; songs remain the foundation of Punjabi folk music |
| **Shah Abdul Latif Bhittai** | 1689–1752 CE | Sindhi | **Shah Jo Risalo** (compiled posthumously) — an anthology exploring divine love through Sindhi folk heroines (Sassui, Sohni, etc.) | Greatest Sindhi poet; considered the national poet of Sindh; Sufi tradition; compiled by his disciples after his death |
| **Lal Ded (Lalleshwari)** | 14th century CE | **Kashmiri** | **Vakhs** (speech/sayings) — c. 285 mystic verses | One of the earliest recorded works in Kashmiri; founder of the *Vakhs* tradition; influenced both Shaivism and Sufism in Kashmir |

## Regional Literary Traditions

One of CCRT's most important contributions is its documentation of India's diverse regional literatures — each with its own ancient roots, literary history, and classical canon.

### Bengali

- **Earliest literary evidence:** The **Charyapada** (c. 8th–12th centuries CE) — a collection of Buddhist mystical songs (*caryagiti*) in the Vajrayana tradition; discovered in 1907 by Haraprasad Shastri at the Nepal Royal Court Library; considered the earliest known literature in Bengali (as well as related forms of Assamese and Odia)
- **Medieval period:** *Vaishnava Padavali* (devotional lyrics) — the great **Chandidas** (c. 14th–15th century CE) composed immortal love-devotion songs for Radha-Krishna
- **Mangalkavya tradition:** Poems on local deities — Manashamangal (Goddess Manasa), Chandimangal (Goddess Chandi), Annada Mangal (Goddess Annada)
- **Krishnadas Kaviraj:** Composed the **Chaitanya Charitamrita** (c. 1615 CE) — the definitive biography of Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhu (founder of the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition)
- **Modern literature:** Rabindranath Tagore (see Modern section below)

### Odia

- **Earliest texts:** Dated to around 1000 CE; the Charyapada also contributes to early Odia
- **Medieval period:** The **Panchasakha** tradition (five saints including Sarala Das, Balarama Das, Jagannatha Das, Achyutananda Das, Jasobanta Das) — 15th–16th century
- **Sarala Das** (15th century): Translated the Mahabharata into Odia (*Saaralaa Mahaabhaarata*); considered the Adi Kavi (first poet) of Odia literature
- **Jagannatha Das** (c. 1490–1550): Composed the **Odia Bhagavata** (Srimad Bhagavatam in Odia); the most revered text in Odia literary culture
- **Upendra Bhanja** (c. 1670–1720): Court poet; composed *Lavanyavati* and *Vaidehisha Vilasa*; known as "Kabi Samrat" (Emperor of Poets)

### Marathi

- **Earliest surviving text:** **Dnyaneshwari** (c. 1290 CE) by **Dnyaneshwar** — though earlier inscriptions and literature exist, this is the first major literary monument
- **Mahananubhava tradition** (12th–13th century): Founded by Chakradhar — the *Lilacharitra* (biography of Chakradhar) in simple Marathi prose is one of the earliest Marathi texts
- **Warkari tradition:** Dnyaneshwar, Namdev, Eknath, Tukaram (see Bhakti section)
- **Modern period:** Jyotirao Phule, B.R. Ambedkar — social reform literature in Marathi

### Gujarati

- **Earliest literature:** c. 10th century; the tradition of **Apabhramsha** poetry transitions into Gujarati
- **Medieval period:** **Narsinh Mehta** (c. 1414–1481 CE) — the "Adi Kavi" of Gujarati; composed *Bhajans* including the famous "Vaishnava Jan To" (made famous by Gandhi); composed *Mamaiyani Hundi* and *Shramal*
- **Mirabai** also composed some devotional songs with Gujarati influences
- **Modern period:** Literature connected to the reform movements; influence of Gandhi

### Punjabi

- **Earliest literature:** The **Janam Sakhis** (biographies of Guru Nanak, 16th century) are among the first major Punjabi texts
- **Guru Granth Sahib:** The **Adi Granth** was compiled by **Guru Arjan Dev** (the fifth Sikh Guru) and completed on **29 August 1604**. It was elevated to the status of the eternal Guru (*Guru Granth Sahib*) by **Guru Gobind Singh** (the tenth Guru). It contains hymns by six Sikh Gurus, Hindu and Muslim saints (including Kabir, Namdev, Sheikh Farid), and 36 composers in total — making it a monument of inter-religious literary synthesis
- **Shah Hussain** (c. 1538–1599): Pioneered the **Kafi** form of Sufi poetry in Punjabi
- **Bulleh Shah** (see Sufi section above)

### Telugu

- **Earliest literary work:** **Nannayya Bhattaraka** (c. 1020–1060 CE) — composed the first Telugu literary work, the **Andhra Mahabharatam** (Telugu Mahabharata); translated Adi, Sabha, and part of Aranya Parva; considered the **Adi Kavi** of Telugu
- **Tikkana** (c. 13th century) and **Errapragada** (c. 14th century) completed the Telugu Mahabharata — the three poets (*Kavi Trayam* — the three poets) are venerated in Telugu literary culture
- **Srinatha** (c. 1365–1441 CE): "Kavi Sarvabhauma" (Universal Emperor of Poets); composed *Shringaranaishadam* and *Bhimeshvara Purana*
- **Potana** (c. 1450–1510 CE): Composed the **Bhagavatam in Telugu** (Andhra Mahabhagavatamu) — the most beloved Telugu devotional text

### Kannada

- **Earliest surviving literary work:** **Kavirajamarga** (c. 850 CE) by King **Nripatunga Amoghavarsha I** of the Rashtrakuta dynasty — a treatise on Kannada poetics; the oldest surviving literary text in Kannada
- **Pampa** (c. 902–975 CE): The greatest of the early Kannada poets; composed **Vikramarjuna Vijaya** (Pampa Bharata) and *Adipurana*; his style became the model for all subsequent Kannada literature
- **Ponna** and **Ranna** — together with Pampa, form the **Ratnatraya** (Three Gems) of early Kannada literature
- **Harihara** and **Raghavanka** (12th–13th centuries): Pioneered *Ragale* (blank verse) and *Shatpadi* (six-line verse) forms
- **Kumaravyasa** (c. 15th century): Composed the **Karnata Bharata Kathamanjari** (Karnataka version of the Mahabharata)
- **Vachana literature:** Basavanna, Akkamahadevi, Allama Prabhu (12th century) — see Bhakti section

### Malayalam

- **Earliest known literary work:** **Ramacharitam** (c. late 12th or early 13th century CE) — an epic poem on the Yuddha Kanda of the Ramayana, written in Old Malayalam by **Cheeramakavi**
- **Kannassa Ramayanam:** Written by **Rama Panikkar** of the Niranam poets (c. 1350–1450 CE) — an important bridge text
- **Thunchaththu Ezhuthachan** (c. 16th century): Called the "**Father of Modern Malayalam Literature**"; reformed the script and language; composed the **Adhyatma Ramayanam Kilippattu** (Ramayana sung by the parrot) and **Mahabharatam Kilippattu**
- **Cherusseri** (c. 15th century): Composed *Krishnagatha* — the story of Krishna in early Malayalam verse
- **Kunjan Nambiar** (18th century): Pioneer of **Thullal** (a satirical performative art combining dance and song in Malayalam)

### Kashmiri

- **Earliest classical figure:** **Lal Ded (Lalleshwari)** (c. 14th century CE) — her Vakhs are the earliest known compositions in Kashmiri (see Sufi section)
- **Kalhana** (c. 12th century): Although writing in Sanskrit, Kalhana is the greatest literary figure from Kashmir; composed the **Rajatarangini** (River of Kings) in **1148 CE** — a chronicle of the kings of Kashmir from mythological times to his own era; it is the **first systematic historical chronicle in Indian literature** using historical method
- **Sheikh Nooruddin (Nund Rishi)** (c. 1377–1440 CE): Kashmiri Sufi saint whose Vakhs in Kashmiri form the foundation of the Rishi order of Sufism in Kashmir
- **Habba Khatoon** (c. 16th century): "Nightingale of Kashmir"; her lyrical songs on longing and separation are classics of Kashmiri literature

### Sindhi

- Classical tradition linked to **Shah Abdul Latif Bhittai** (see Sufi section)
- **Sami** (1743–1850): Another major Sindhi Sufi poet
- **Sachal Sarmast** (1739–1826): Sufi poet who wrote in Sindhi, Punjabi, Urdu, and Persian; known for ecstatic *kafis*

### Assamese

- **Earliest evidence:** The Charyapada tradition (c. 8th–12th centuries) includes early Assamese; **Sukumar Borkath** has established Assamese claims to several Charyapada texts
- **Madhava Kandali** (c. 14th century): Translated the Ramayana into Assamese (*Saptakanda Ramayana*) — the first Ramayana in any modern Indian language, predating Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas by approximately two centuries
- **Srimanta Shankardev** (c. 1449–1568 CE): The defining figure of Assamese cultural life; founded the **Eka Sharana Nama Dharma** (one-God devotion to Vishnu/Krishna); introduced **Sattriya** classical dance; composed *Borgeet* (devotional songs), *Ankiya Nat* (one-act plays), and *Kirtan Ghosa* (compilation of devotional songs); established the *Satra* (monastery-cultural centre) system
- **Madhavdev** (c. 1489–1596 CE): Chief disciple of Shankardev; composed *Naam Ghosa* and greatly enriched Assamese literary tradition

### Hindi and Related Languages

- **Earliest Hindi:** **Apabhramsha** and early Avadhi texts from c. 11th–13th century
- **Prithviraj Raso** (c. 12th–13th century): Attributed to **Chand Bardai** — epic poem on the Rajput king Prithviraj Chauhan; considered the earliest major work in early Hindi/Brajbhasha
- **Amir Khusrau's Hindavi compositions** (see Sufi section)
- **Kabir, Tulsidas, Surdas, Mirabai** (see Bhakti section)
- **Malik Muhammad Jayasi** (c. 1477–1542 CE): Composed **Padmavat** (1540 CE) in Awadhi — the most celebrated Sufi allegorical poem in Hindi

## Modern Indian Literature

### Rabindranath Tagore (1861–1941)

**Rabindranath Tagore** is the most universally celebrated figure in modern Indian literature. Key facts for UPSC:

- Born: **7 May 1861**, Calcutta (Jorasanko Tagore family)
- Nobel Prize: **1913 Nobel Prize in Literature** — the first Asian, first lyricist, and first non-European to win the Nobel Prize in Literature
- Basis for Nobel: English translation of **Gitanjali** (Song Offerings), published in 1912; original Bengali *Gitanjali* published in 1910
- Composed the national anthems of **India** (*Jana Gana Mana*, adopted 1950) and **Bangladesh** (*Amar Shonar Bangla*, composed 1906)
- Major works: *Gitanjali*, *Gora*, *Ghare Baire* (The Home and the World), *Chokher Bali*, *Rabindra Sangeet* (a genre of c. 2,000 songs), *Dakghar* (The Post Office), *Chandalika*
- Founded **Visva-Bharati University** at Shantiniketan (1921) — now a Central University

### Jnanpith Award (Bharatiya Jnanpith)

India's highest literary honour, awarded for the most outstanding contribution to Indian literature in any of the scheduled languages:
- Established: **1961**; first award given in **1965**
- First recipient: **G. Sankara Kurup** (Malayalam, for *Odakkuzhal*, 1965)

### Sahitya Akademi

- Established: **12 March 1954** (constituted 15 December 1952)
- National Academy of Letters; supports literature in **24 Indian languages**
- Gives annual Sahitya Akademi Award (₹1 lakh) in each of 24 languages
- Highest honour: **Sahitya Akademi Fellowship** (lifetime achievement; only living writers honoured)
- Publishes the *Encyclopaedia of Indian Literature* (6 volumes)

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Make a clear author-work table and revise it regularly — *Mricchakatika* (Shudraka), *Mudrarakshasa* (Vishakhadatta), *Harshacharita* (Banabhatta), *Kadambari* (Banabhatta), *Dasakumaracharita* (Dandin), *Buddhacharita* (Ashvaghosa), *Kiratarjuniya* (Bharavi), *Gitagovinda* (Jayadeva)
- The **Kalidasa rule:** 3 plays + 2 mahakavyas + 2 lyric poems = 7 works total
- The **Sangam test:** Tolkappiyam = grammar/poetics text; Silappadikaram = first Tamil epic (Ilanko Adigal); Manimekalai = Buddhist epic (Sithalai Sattanar); Thirukkural = 1,330 couplets, 3 books (Aram, Porul, Kama), Thiruvalluvar
- **Bhakti language map:** Tulsidas (Awadhi), Surdas (Braj Bhasha), Mirabai (Rajasthani/Braj), Kabir (mixed Hindi dialects), Tukaram (Marathi), Basavanna (Kannada Vachanas), Shankardev (Assamese)
- Classical languages: Tamil first (2004); five added October 2024; total 11

**For Mains:**
- **Sangam question:** Emphasise the *Akam* (love) and *Puram* (war-public life) classification; the five-landscape (*Tinai*) system; social history value (trade, urban life in Silappadikaram)
- **Bhakti literature question:** Stress the vernacularisation of literature, democratisation of devotion across castes, and the literary forms innovated (Doha, Abhangas, Vachanas, Kafis, Borgeet)
- **Classical Sanskrit literature:** Kalidasa as the peak of Gupta cultural achievement; link literary production to political patronage (Gupta, Harsha's court)
- **Regional literature:** Demonstrate the parallel development of literary traditions across India — avoid the error of treating Sanskrit as the only literary tradition
- When writing on "India's cultural unity in diversity," regional literary traditions are powerful evidence of local creativity within a shared civilisational framework
