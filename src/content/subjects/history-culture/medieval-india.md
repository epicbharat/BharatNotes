## Overview

Medieval Indian history spans roughly from the 8th century to the 18th century CE — from the decline of classical empires to the consolidation of colonial rule. This period witnessed the rise and fall of the Delhi Sultanate and the Mughal Empire, the flourishing of the Vijayanagara and Maratha Empires, transformative Bhakti and Sufi movements, and remarkable achievements in art, architecture, and administration.

---

## Delhi Sultanate (1206–1526)

The Delhi Sultanate was established after Muhammad of Ghor's conquests in northern India. It comprised **five successive dynasties** ruling from Delhi for over 300 years.

### The Five Dynasties

| Dynasty | Period | Founder | Key Rulers |
|---------|--------|---------|------------|
| **Mamluk (Slave)** | 1206–1290 | Qutb-ud-din Aibak | Iltutmish (1210–1236), Razia Sultan, Balban (1266–1287) |
| **Khalji** | 1290–1320 | Jalal-ud-din Khalji | Alauddin Khalji (1296–1316) |
| **Tughlaq** | 1320–1414 | Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq | Muhammad bin Tughlaq, Firoz Shah Tughlaq |
| **Sayyid** | 1414–1451 | Khizr Khan | Khizr Khan, Mubarak Shah |
| **Lodi** | 1451–1526 | Bahlul Khan Lodi | Sikandar Lodi, Ibrahim Lodi (last Sultan — defeated at Panipat, 1526) |

### Key Battles Leading to the Sultanate

| Battle | Year | Between | Outcome |
|--------|------|---------|---------|
| **First Battle of Tarain** | 1191 | Muhammad of Ghor vs Prithviraj Chauhan | Rajput victory; Ghori escaped |
| **Second Battle of Tarain** | 1192 | Muhammad of Ghor vs Prithviraj Chauhan | Decisive Ghurid victory; opened the gateway for Muslim rule in North India |

### Iqta System

The **Iqta system** was organised under Iltutmish (1210–1236). Land revenue grants (iqtas) were assigned to officials and military commanders in lieu of cash salary. **Iqtadars** collected taxes, retained a portion for salary and military expenses, and remitted the rest to the central treasury. Crucially, these grants were **non-hereditary**, keeping nobles dependent on the Sultan.

### Alauddin Khalji's Market Reforms

Alauddin Khalji (1296–1316) introduced the most comprehensive price control system in medieval India:

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Purpose** | Maintain a large standing army at low cost (234 tankas for a cavalryman) after the 1303 Mongol invasion threat |
| **Key Markets** | Mandi (central grain market) and Sera-i Adl (market for manufactured/imported goods) |
| **Controller** | Malik Qabul appointed as controller of grain markets |
| **Measures** | Banned hoarding and regrating; strict surveillance by spies; severe punishment for cheating |
| **Outcome** | Prices of grain, cloth, horses, and cattle were fixed and remained stable during his reign |
| **Legacy** | Reforms were revoked after his death by Qutbuddin Mubarak Shah |

> **UPSC Note:** Alauddin Khalji's market reforms are a favourite Prelims topic. Remember: they were driven by **military necessity** (maintaining a large cheap army), not welfare. He also introduced the practice of branding horses (dagh) and maintaining descriptive rolls of soldiers (chehra) to prevent fraud.

## Mughal Empire (1526–1857)

### Key Emperors

| Emperor | Reign | Key Achievements |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Babur** | 1526–1530 | Founded the empire; won First Battle of Panipat (21 April 1526); introduced gunpowder warfare to India |
| **Humayun** | 1530–1540, 1555–1556 | Lost empire to Sher Shah Suri; exiled for 15 years; recaptured Delhi in 1555; died in 1556 from a fall |
| **Akbar** | 1556–1605 | Greatest Mughal; administrative and religious reforms; expanded empire across most of India |
| **Jahangir** | 1605–1627 | Patron of arts and painting; Nur Jahan's political influence |
| **Shah Jahan** | 1628–1658 | Golden age of Mughal architecture — Taj Mahal, Red Fort; shifted capital to Delhi |
| **Aurangzeb** | 1658–1707 | Maximum territorial extent; reversed tolerant policies; prolonged Deccan campaigns weakened the empire |

### First Battle of Panipat (21 April 1526)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Combatants** | Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi |
| **Babur's army** | ~12,000 men equipped with matchlock muskets and cannons |
| **Ibrahim Lodi's army** | 50,000–70,000 with ~1,000 war elephants but no gunpowder weapons |
| **Tactics** | Babur used the **Tulughma** (flanking) formation and positioned carts (araba) tied together to create a defensive line |
| **Result** | Decisive Mughal victory; Ibrahim Lodi killed; end of the Delhi Sultanate |

> **Key Point:** The Battle of Panipat (1526) is significant because it introduced **gunpowder technology** in Indian warfare. Babur's combination of artillery with Central Asian cavalry tactics gave him a decisive advantage despite being vastly outnumbered.

### Akbar's Administrative Reforms

| Reform | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Mansabdari System** | Hierarchical ranking system for nobility, military commanders, and officials. "Mansab" = rank/position. Ranks assigned based on military capabilities, loyalty, and administrative competence. Dual rank: **Zat** (personal status) and **Sawar** (cavalry obligation) |
| **Todar Mal's Land Revenue (Dahsala/Zabti System)** | Raja Todar Mal appointed as Diwan in 1573. Conducted a 10-year survey of crop yields and prices (1570–1580). The Dahsala system was formally introduced in **1580**, fixing tax on each crop in cash based on average yields of 10 years |
| **Din-i-Ilahi (1582)** | Syncretic theology combining elements of Islam, Hinduism, Jainism, Zoroastrianism, and Christianity. Preceded by the establishment of the **Ibadat Khana** (House of Worship) at Fatehpur Sikri in **1575** for interfaith dialogue |
| **Sulh-i-Kul (Universal Peace)** | Policy of religious tolerance; abolished the **Jizya tax** on non-Muslims |
| **Provincial Administration** | Empire divided into **Subas** (provinces), each governed by a **Subedar**; further divided into Sarkars and Parganas |

> **Exam Tip:** The Mansabdari system had two components -- **Zat** (personal rank determining salary and status) and **Sawar** (number of horsemen the mansabdar was obligated to maintain). A mansabdar with equal Zat and Sawar ranks was of the first category. This dual-rank system is a frequently tested Prelims fact. Also note: Din-i-Ilahi (1582) was NOT a new religion -- it was a mystical order with no scriptures, priesthood, or places of worship. Only about 18 people joined it, including Birbal.

### Mughal Architecture

| Monument | Commissioner | Period | Architect |
|----------|-------------|--------|-----------|
| **Taj Mahal** (Agra) | Shah Jahan (for wife Mumtaz Mahal) | Construction began 1632; mausoleum completed 1648; complex completed 1653 | Ustad Ahmad Lahori |
| **Red Fort** (Delhi) | Shah Jahan | Construction began 13 May 1638; completed 6 April 1648 | Ustad Ahmad Lahori |
| **Fatehpur Sikri** | Akbar | Built 1571–1585; abandoned due to water scarcity | -- |
| **Humayun's Tomb** (Delhi) | Haji Begum (Humayun's widow) | Completed 1572 | Mirak Mirza Ghiyas |
| **Jama Masjid** (Delhi) | Shah Jahan | 1644–1656 | -- |
| **Buland Darwaza** (Fatehpur Sikri) | Akbar | 1601 (to commemorate Gujarat victory) | -- |

### Sher Shah Suri (1540–1545) — Interlude

Though he interrupted Mughal rule for only five years, Sher Shah Suri's administrative reforms were remarkable:

| Reform | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Grand Trunk Road** | Rebuilt and extended the ancient road from Sonargaon (Bengal) to Peshawar |
| **Rupiya** | Standardised silver coin (178 grains); became the basis for the modern rupee |
| **Revenue System** | Detailed land measurement and classification; collected revenue in cash; introduced the Patta (deed) and Qabuliyat (agreement) system |
| **Sarais (Rest Houses)** | Built a network of sarais along major roads for travellers and traders |

> **Common Mistake:** Students often attribute the Grand Trunk Road entirely to Sher Shah Suri. In reality, the ancient route existed since the Mauryan period (Uttarapatha). Sher Shah rebuilt, extended, and lined it with trees and sarais, transforming it into an efficient highway.

### Decline of the Mughal Empire (Post-1707)

| Factor | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Weak successors** | After Aurangzeb's death in 1707, rapid succession crises — in 1719 alone, four emperors ascended the throne |
| **Aurangzeb's intolerant policies** | Reimposed Jizya (1679), ordered demolition of Hindu temples — alienated Hindu officers, Rajput allies, and Maratha vassals |
| **Military stagnation** | No adoption of new military technology; mansabdars hoarded wealth instead of maintaining armies |
| **Economic exhaustion** | Prolonged Deccan campaigns (1682 onwards) and lavish construction projects drained the treasury |
| **Rise of regional powers** | Marathas, Nizam of Hyderabad, Nawabs of Bengal/Awadh/Carnatic asserted independence |
| **Foreign invasions** | Nadir Shah's invasion (1739) — sacked Delhi, carried away the Peacock Throne and Koh-i-Noor diamond; Ahmad Shah Abdali's repeated invasions |
| **Nobility factionalism** | Court divided into Iranis, Turanis, Afghans, and Indian Muslim factions |

## Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646)

### Overview

The Vijayanagara Empire was the most powerful Hindu kingdom in medieval South India, acting as a bulwark against the southward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate and the Bahmani Sultanate.

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Founded** | 1336 |
| **Founders** | Brothers **Harihara I** and **Bukka Raya I** of the Sangama dynasty |
| **Capital** | **Hampi** (present-day Karnataka; UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986) |
| **Greatest Ruler** | Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529) of the Tuluva dynasty |
| **Decisive Defeat** | Battle of Talikota (Rakkasa-Tangadi), 23 January 1565 |

### Four Dynasties

| Dynasty | Period |
|---------|--------|
| **Sangama** | 1336–1485 |
| **Saluva** | 1485–1503 |
| **Tuluva** | 1503–1565 |
| **Aravidu** | 1570–1646 |

### Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529)

Widely regarded as one of the greatest rulers in Indian history:

- Third ruler of the Tuluva dynasty
- Defeated the Sultans of Bijapur, Golconda, and the Gajapatis of Odisha
- Patron of Telugu and Sanskrit literature; his court had the famous **Ashtadiggajas** (eight great poets)
- Author of the Telugu work **Amuktamalyada**
- Empire reached its political and cultural zenith during his reign

### Battle of Talikota (23 January 1565)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Also called** | Battle of Rakkasa-Tangadi |
| **Combatants** | Vijayanagara Empire vs combined alliance of Deccan Sultanates (Ahmadnagar, Bijapur, Golconda, Bidar) |
| **Vijayanagara Ruler** | Aliya Rama Raya (regent) |
| **Result** | Decisive defeat of Vijayanagara; Hampi was sacked and devastated |
| **Significance** | Watershed moment — led to the empire's steep decline, though the Aravidu dynasty continued in diminished form until 1646 |

> **Warning:** The Battle of Talikota (1565) did NOT immediately end the Vijayanagara Empire. The Aravidu dynasty continued to rule from Penukonda and later Chandragiri until 1646. UPSC has tested this distinction. Also, the battle's correct name is Rakkasa-Tangadi (the village where it was fought); "Talikota" is the nearby town.

### Administrative Features

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Provincial Administration** | Empire divided into Rajyas (provinces), Nadus (districts), Sthalas (sub-districts), and Gramas (villages) |
| **Nayankara System** | Military governors (Nayakas) were assigned territories in exchange for military service and revenue collection |
| **Ayagar System** | Village administration through a group of 12 hereditary functionaries |
| **Economy** | Flourishing trade with Southeast Asia, China, Arabia, and Portugal; major ports: Mangalore, Honavar, Bhatkal |
| **Currency** | Gold Varaha (Pagoda) and silver Tara |

## Bhakti Movement

The Bhakti movement was a devotional trend that emerged in South India (7th–8th century) and spread across the subcontinent, emphasising personal devotion to God over rituals, caste, and priestly intermediaries.

### Two Streams of Bhakti

| Stream | Focus | Key Proponents |
|--------|-------|---------------|
| **Nirguna Bhakti** | Devotion to a formless, attributeless God | Kabir, Guru Nanak, Ravidas |
| **Saguna Bhakti** | Devotion to a personal God with form and attributes (Vishnu/Rama/Krishna/Shiva) | Ramanuja, Tulsidas, Mirabai, Chaitanya, Surdas |

### Key Bhakti Saints

| Saint | Approximate Dates | Region | Key Contribution |
|-------|-------------------|--------|------------------|
| **Ramanuja** | c. 1017–1137 | Tamil Nadu | Chief proponent of **Vishishtadvaita** (qualified non-dualism); foundational to Sri Vaishnavism; argued for devotion (bhakti) as a path to moksha accessible to all |
| **Kabir** | c. 1440–1510 | Varanasi (UP) | Radical Nirguna saint; disciple of Ramananda; opposed caste hierarchy, idol worship, and empty rituals of both Hinduism and Islam; composed dohas (couplets) |
| **Guru Nanak** | 1469–1539 | Punjab | First Sikh Guru; founder of Sikhism; Nirguna Bhakti; emphasised naam (name of God), daan (charity), ishnan (purity); established the langar (community kitchen) tradition |
| **Chaitanya Mahaprabhu** | 1486–1533 | Bengal | Krishna Bhakti; founder of **Gaudiya (Bengal) Vaishnavism**; popularised kirtan (devotional singing) and Harinam Sankirtan |
| **Mirabai** | c. 1498–1546 | Rajasthan | Devoted to Lord Krishna; defied social norms of Rajput royalty; renowned for devotional bhajans in Hindi/Rajasthani |
| **Tulsidas** | 1532–1623 | Varanasi (UP) | Author of **Ramcharitmanas** — retelling of the Ramayana in Awadhi; one of the most influential works in Hindi literature |
| **Surdas** | c. 1478–1583 | Braj region (UP) | Blind poet; Krishna devotee; composed **Sur Sagar**; known for his descriptions of Krishna's childhood |

> **Exam Tip:** For UPSC, remember the distinction between Nirguna and Saguna Bhakti. Nirguna saints (Kabir, Guru Nanak) rejected idol worship and spoke of a formless God. Saguna saints (Tulsidas, Mirabai, Chaitanya) worshipped personal deities with specific forms. Both streams rejected caste hierarchy and priestly monopoly, which is their common thread. Ramanuja is considered a precursor -- he philosophically argued for equality in devotion, laying the intellectual foundation.

## Sufi Movement

Sufism is the mystical dimension of Islam, emphasising the inner, spiritual quest for divine love and knowledge. Sufi orders (silsilahs) arrived in India from the 12th century onwards.

### Major Sufi Orders in India

| Order | Key Figure in India | Established | Characteristics |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Chishti** | Khwaja Muinuddin Chishti (arrived Ajmer, mid-12th century) | 12th century | Most popular order; emphasis on love, tolerance, openness; rejected state patronage; embraced music (sama/qawwali); dargah at Ajmer is one of India's most visited shrines |
| **Suhrawardi** | Shaikh Bahauddin Zakariya (Multan) | 13th century | Accepted state patronage; lived comparatively affluent lives; influence mainly in Punjab and Sind |
| **Naqshbandi** | Khwaja Baqi Billah; revitalised by **Ahmad Sirhindi** (Mujaddid Alf-i-Thani) | 16th century | Orthodox; opposed Akbar's Din-i-Ilahi and syncretic practices; emphasised strict adherence to Sharia |
| **Qadiri** | Arrived from Perso-Arab lands; associated with Shah Nimatullah Wali | 15th century | Orthodox orientation; teachings similar to the ulema; influence in Deccan and Punjab |

### Important Sufi Concepts

| Concept | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Khanqah** | Sufi hospice/centre for spiritual practice and community meals |
| **Sama** | Musical gathering for spiritual ecstasy (central to Chishti practice) |
| **Silsilah** | Spiritual lineage/chain connecting a Sufi master to the Prophet |
| **Murshid/Pir** | Spiritual guide/teacher |
| **Murid** | Disciple/student |
| **Walayat** | Spiritual authority or closeness to God |

> **Key Distinction:** The Chishti order is the most important for UPSC. Key Chishti saints to remember: Muinuddin Chishti (Ajmer), Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki (Delhi), Baba Farid (Pakpattan), **Nizamuddin Auliya** (Delhi — contemporary of 7 Delhi Sultans), and Salim Chishti (Fatehpur Sikri — Akbar named his son Salim/Jahangir after him).

## Maratha Empire (1674–1818)

### Rise of Shivaji

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Full name** | Shivaji Bhonsale |
| **Born** | 19 February 1630, Shivneri Fort |
| **Coronation** | **6 June 1674** at Raigad Fort; assumed the title **Chhatrapati** (supreme king) |
| **Capital** | Raigad |
| **Key achievement** | Created an independent Maratha kingdom from Bijapur Sultanate and Mughal territories |
| **Administration** | Council of Eight Ministers (**Ashtapradhan**) |
| **Revenue** | Chauth (1/4 of revenue) and Sardeshmukhi (additional 1/10) from neighbouring territories |
| **Navy** | Built a formidable naval force — considered the "Father of the Indian Navy" |
| **Death** | 3 April 1680, Raigad Fort |

### Ashtapradhan (Council of Eight Ministers)

| Title | Role |
|-------|------|
| **Peshwa** | Prime Minister — later became the effective ruler |
| **Amatya** | Finance Minister |
| **Sachiv** | Secretary — correspondence and records |
| **Mantri** | Chronicler — recorded daily activities of the court |
| **Senapati** | Commander-in-Chief |
| **Sumant** | Foreign Affairs Minister |
| **Nyayadhish** | Chief Justice |
| **Panditrao** | Religious Affairs and Charity |

### Peshwa Period

| Peshwa | Period | Key Contribution |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| **Balaji Vishwanath** | 1713–1720 | First hereditary Peshwa from the Bhat family; consolidated Maratha power; secured recognition from the Mughals |
| **Bajirao I** | 1720–1740 | Greatest Maratha military commander; expanded territory from ~3% to ~30% of the Indian subcontinent; never lost a battle |
| **Balaji Bajirao (Nanasaheb)** | 1740–1761 | Peak Maratha expansion; period ended with the disaster at Third Battle of Panipat |
| **Madhavrao I** | 1761–1772 | Rebuilt Maratha authority after Panipat; called the "saviour of the Maratha Confederacy" |

### Third Battle of Panipat (14 January 1761)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Combatants** | Maratha Confederacy vs Ahmad Shah Durrani (Afghanistan) |
| **Maratha commander** | Sadashivrao Bhau (cousin of Peshwa Balaji Bajirao) |
| **Result** | Devastating Maratha defeat; tens of thousands killed including the Peshwa's son Vishwasrao |
| **Why Marathas lost** | Lack of support from other Indian rulers (Rajputs, Jats, Nizam) due to past animosities; supply lines cut; fighting far from home territory |
| **Aftermath** | Peshwa Madhavrao I subsequently rebuilt Maratha power in core territories; but the Confederacy never fully recovered its pan-Indian dominance |

> **Common Mistake:** The Third Battle of Panipat (1761) was NOT the end of the Maratha Empire. Madhavrao I rebuilt Maratha authority in the 1760s. The empire finally ended only in 1818 after the Third Anglo-Maratha War, when the last Peshwa (Bajirao II) was defeated by the British and pensioned off.

## South Indian Kingdoms

### Chola Dynasty — Maritime Power

The Chola dynasty (9th–13th century) was the dominant power in South India and the Indian Ocean during the medieval period.

| Ruler | Reign | Key Achievements |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| **Rajaraja Chola I** | 985–1014 | Conquered southern India and the Anuradhapura kingdom of Sri Lanka; conducted systematic land survey (1000 CE) leading to reorganisation of Tamil country into valanadus; built the **Brihadeeswarar Temple** (Thanjavur, 1010) — UNESCO World Heritage Site |
| **Rajendra Chola I** | 1014–1044 | Launched **naval expedition against Srivijaya** (Southeast Asia) in **1025 CE** — first Indian king to lead overseas military campaigns; expanded Chola influence to the Ganges in the north; established **Gangaikonda Cholapuram** as the new capital |

**Chola Administration:**

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Village self-government** | Highly developed system of elected village assemblies — **Ur** (general assembly), **Sabha** (assembly of Brahmins in Brahmadeya villages), **Nagaram** (merchant assembly) |
| **Uttaramerur Inscription** | Famous inscription detailing the electoral process for village committees — candidates had to meet age, property, and education qualifications; selection by lot |
| **Naval power** | Controlled Lakshadweep-Maldives and Indian Ocean trade routes; navy projected power across the Bay of Bengal |
| **Temple as institution** | Temples functioned as economic and administrative centres — owned land, employed workers, operated as banks |

> **UPSC Relevance:** The Uttaramerur inscription (Chola period) describing village-level democratic elections is one of the most frequently tested topics in Prelims. It demonstrates that India had sophisticated local self-governance institutions centuries before modern democracy. Connect this to the 73rd Amendment (Panchayati Raj) in Mains answers for historical depth.

### Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1527)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Founded** | 3 August 1347 during a rebellion of Deccan amirs against Muhammad bin Tughlaq |
| **Founder** | Zafar Khan (Hasan Gangu / Alauddin Bahman Shah) |
| **Capital** | Gulbarga (initially), later shifted to Bidar |
| **Key minister** | Mahmud Gawan — powerful prime minister; expanded and reformed the sultanate |
| **Decline** | Internal faction fights between Deccani (local Muslims) and Afaqi/Pardesi (foreign-origin) nobles |

### Split into Five Deccan Sultanates

| Sultanate | Year of Independence | Capital |
|-----------|---------------------|---------|
| **Ahmadnagar** | 1490 | Ahmadnagar |
| **Bijapur** | 1490 | Bijapur |
| **Berar** | 1490 | Ellichpur |
| **Bidar** | c. 1492 | Bidar |
| **Golconda** | 1512 | Golconda (later Hyderabad) |

These five Deccan Sultanates later united to defeat the Vijayanagara Empire at the Battle of Talikota in 1565.

## Medieval Indian Society and Economy

### Social Structure

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Caste system** | Continued to be rigid; however, Bhakti and Sufi movements challenged caste hierarchy at the spiritual level |
| **Position of women** | Generally declined — purdah system expanded; sati continued in Rajput society; exceptions like Razia Sultan, Nur Jahan, Chand Bibi |
| **Slavery** | Prevalent under the Sultanate; Firoz Shah Tughlaq reportedly maintained 180,000 slaves |
| **Syncretic culture** | Interaction between Hindu and Islamic traditions produced new art forms, languages (Urdu), cuisine, and architectural styles |

### Economy and Trade

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Agriculture** | Backbone of the economy; new crops introduced — cotton, tobacco (post-Portuguese contact), maize |
| **Textile trade** | Indian textiles (muslin, calico, silk) were major export commodities to Southeast Asia, Middle East, and Europe |
| **Coinage** | Standardised by Iltutmish (silver tanka, copper jital); further refined by Sher Shah (silver rupiya) |
| **Maritime trade** | Gujarat, Malabar, and Coromandel coasts were major centres of Indian Ocean trade |
| **Karkhanas** | Royal workshops producing luxury goods — textiles, weapons, jewellery |

### Language and Literature

| Development | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| **Urdu** | Emerged as a composite language blending Hindi/Khariboli with Persian, Arabic, and Turkish vocabulary — initially called Hindavi or Rekhta |
| **Persian** | Court language of the Sultanate and Mughals; Abul Fazl's **Ain-i-Akbari** and **Akbarnama** are key historical sources |
| **Regional literature** | Flourished under Bhakti influence — Tulsidas (Awadhi), Kabir (Hindi), Guru Nanak (Punjabi), Chaitanya (Bengali), Krishnadevaraya (Telugu) |

## Important for UPSC

### Prelims Focus

- Five dynasties of Delhi Sultanate with founding years and key rulers
- First Battle of Panipat (1526) — Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi; introduction of gunpowder
- Mughal emperors' reign years (especially Akbar 1556–1605, Aurangzeb 1658–1707)
- Akbar's reforms: Mansabdari (Zat and Sawar), Dahsala/Zabti system (1580), Din-i-Ilahi (1582), Sulh-i-Kul
- Sher Shah Suri: Grand Trunk Road, Rupiya, revenue reforms
- Taj Mahal: commissioned 1631, completed 1648/1653; architect Ustad Ahmad Lahori
- Vijayanagara: founded 1336, Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529), Battle of Talikota (1565)
- Chola: Rajendra I's naval expedition to Srivijaya (1025 CE), Uttaramerur inscription
- Bahmani Sultanate (1347) → five Deccan Sultanates (1490–1512)
- Bhakti: Nirguna vs Saguna distinction; key saints and dates
- Sufi: Chishti order (Muinuddin Chishti, Nizamuddin Auliya); Naqshbandi vs Chishti on state patronage
- Shivaji's coronation (1674); Ashtapradhan council; Third Battle of Panipat (1761)

### Mains Dimensions

| Dimension | Sample Questions |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Administration** | Compare Mughal and Vijayanagara administrative systems. How did the Mansabdari system function? |
| **Religion** | Evaluate Akbar's religious policy. Was Din-i-Ilahi a political strategy or genuine spiritual quest? |
| **Society** | How did Bhakti and Sufi movements challenge established social hierarchies? |
| **Architecture** | Discuss the evolution of Indo-Islamic architecture from the Delhi Sultanate to the Mughals |
| **Economy** | Analyse the role of Indian Ocean trade in shaping medieval Indian society |
| **Decline** | What factors led to the decline of the Mughal Empire? Were they internal or external? |
| **Marathas** | Assess the contribution of Shivaji to the administrative and military history of India |

### Interview Angles

- Was Akbar truly tolerant or politically pragmatic?
- Did medieval Indian kingdoms have democratic elements? (Chola village assemblies)
- Why did the Vijayanagara Empire fall despite being wealthy and powerful?
- How did Bhakti saints influence Indian social reform?
- Is it accurate to describe medieval India as a period of "Hindu-Muslim conflict"?
