## Overview

The French Revolution (1789–1799) was the most transformative political event of the modern era. It destroyed the ancien régime, established the principles of popular sovereignty and individual rights, and unleashed the force of nationalism across Europe. Its ideals of **Liberty, Equality, and Fraternity** continue to shape democratic governance worldwide — and directly influenced Indian nationalist thought.

---

## Causes

| Category | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Social inequality** | The Three Estates — **First Estate** (Clergy, ~130,000), **Second Estate** (Nobility, ~350,000), and **Third Estate** (commoners, ~27 million — 98% of the population); First and Second Estates were largely exempt from taxes while the Third Estate bore the burden |
| **Economic crisis** | France was nearly bankrupt; costly involvement in the American Revolution; extravagant spending by **Louis XVI** and **Marie Antoinette** at Versailles; severe bread shortages (1788 harvest failure) |
| **Political** | Absolute monarchy under Louis XVI; lack of representation for the Third Estate; Estates-General had not been convened since 1614 |
| **Intellectual** | **Enlightenment thinkers** — **Voltaire** (freedom of expression, religious tolerance), **Rousseau** (social contract — government derives legitimacy from the people), **Montesquieu** (separation of powers), **Diderot** (Encyclopédie — spread of rational thinking) |
| **American influence** | Success of the American Revolution demonstrated that colonies could overthrow their rulers based on Enlightenment principles |

## Key Phases

### Phase 1: Estates-General & National Assembly (May–July 1789)

| Event | Date | Detail |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Estates-General convened** | 5 May 1789 | Louis XVI convened the Estates-General at Versailles to address the financial crisis; voting was by estate (not by head), meaning the Third Estate was always outvoted 2:1 |
| **National Assembly** | 17 June 1789 | Third Estate declared itself the **National Assembly** — claiming to represent the nation |
| **Tennis Court Oath** | 20 June 1789 | Locked out of their meeting hall, Assembly members gathered at a nearby tennis court and vowed not to disperse until a constitution was drafted |
| **Storming of the Bastille** | **14 July 1789** | Parisian crowd stormed the Bastille prison — a symbol of royal tyranny and absolutism; became France's national day |
| **Great Fear** | July–August 1789 | Peasant uprisings across rural France; attacked noble estates and burned feudal documents |

### Phase 2: Constitutional Monarchy (1789–1792)

| Event | Date | Detail |
|-------|------|--------|
| **Abolition of feudal privileges** | 4 August 1789 | National Assembly abolished feudal rights, tithes, and privileges of the nobility and clergy |
| **Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen** | **26 August 1789** | Foundational document — proclaimed liberty, equality, property, security, and resistance to oppression; sovereignty resides in the nation; "Men are born and remain free and equal in rights" |
| **Women's March on Versailles** | 5–6 October 1789 | Parisian women marched to Versailles demanding bread; forced the royal family to move to Paris |
| **Civil Constitution of the Clergy** | 1790 | Brought the Church under state control; clergy required to swear loyalty to the Constitution |
| **Flight to Varennes** | June 1791 | Louis XVI attempted to flee France; captured at Varennes — destroyed remaining public trust in the monarchy |
| **Constitution of 1791** | September 1791 | Established a constitutional monarchy with a unicameral Legislative Assembly; limited suffrage (active citizens only) |

### Phase 3: The Republic and Reign of Terror (1792–1794)

| Event | Date | Detail |
|-------|------|--------|
| **France declared a Republic** | 22 September 1792 | Monarchy abolished; First French Republic proclaimed |
| **Execution of Louis XVI** | **21 January 1793** | Louis XVI guillotined at Place de la Révolution (now Place de la Concorde), Paris; charged with treason |
| **Reign of Terror** | 1793–1794 | Led by **Maximilien Robespierre** and the **Jacobins**; **Committee of Public Safety** exercised dictatorial power; mass executions — estimated **16,000–40,000 killed** by the guillotine; targets included Queen **Marie Antoinette** (executed 16 October 1793), moderate Girondins, and anyone suspected of counter-revolutionary sentiment |
| **Fall of Robespierre** | **28 July 1794** (9 Thermidor) | Robespierre himself was arrested and guillotined; the Terror ended |

### Phase 4: The Directory (1795–1799)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Government** | Executive of five Directors; moderate republic |
| **Character** | Corruption, instability, and military dependence |
| **End** | **Napoleon Bonaparte's coup** — **9 November 1799** (18 Brumaire); Napoleon became First Consul; the Revolution ended and the Napoleonic era began |

## Napoleon Bonaparte (1799–1815)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **First Consul** | 1799–1804; concentrated power; brought stability after revolutionary chaos |
| **Emperor** | Crowned himself **Emperor of the French** on **2 December 1804** at Notre-Dame Cathedral, Paris |
| **Napoleonic Code (1804)** | Codified French law; abolished feudal privileges; established equality before the law, property rights, and secular civil law; influenced legal systems worldwide (including the Indian Penal Code's structure) |
| **Military conquests** | Dominated continental Europe; conquered much of Italy, Germany, Spain, Netherlands; spread revolutionary ideals (nationalism, secularism, legal equality) through conquest |
| **Continental System** | Economic blockade of Britain; ultimately failed |
| **Invasion of Russia (1812)** | Catastrophic defeat; Grande Armée of ~600,000 was reduced to ~100,000 by the Russian winter and scorched-earth tactics |
| **Defeat at Waterloo** | **18 June 1815** — decisively defeated by British (Duke of Wellington) and Prussian (Blücher) forces at Waterloo, Belgium |
| **Exile** | Exiled to Saint Helena (South Atlantic); died there on **5 May 1821** |

### Congress of Vienna (1814–1815)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Redraw the map of Europe after Napoleon's defeat; restore the pre-revolutionary order |
| **Key figures** | **Metternich** (Austria), **Castlereagh** (Britain), **Tsar Alexander I** (Russia), **Talleyrand** (France) |
| **Principles** | Legitimacy (restore legitimate rulers), compensation, balance of power |
| **Impact** | Created a relatively stable European order that lasted until 1848; but suppressed nationalism and liberalism, which erupted in the Revolutions of 1848 |

## Impact and Significance

| Domain | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Political** | Destroyed absolute monarchy in France; established popular sovereignty; inspired democratic movements worldwide |
| **Legal** | Declaration of Rights of Man; Napoleonic Code — influenced legal systems globally |
| **Nationalism** | French nationalism spread to other European peoples, inspiring the unification movements in Italy and Germany |
| **Social** | Abolished feudal privileges; established (in principle) legal equality |
| **On India** | French Revolution's ideals influenced Indian nationalist thinkers; the concept of fundamental rights in the Indian Constitution draws from the Declaration of Rights of Man; Tipu Sultan had connections with revolutionary France |
| **Legacy** | "Liberty, Equality, Fraternity" remains the motto of the French Republic and a foundational principle of modern democracies |
