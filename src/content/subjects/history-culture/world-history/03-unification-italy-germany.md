## Overview

The 19th century was the age of nationalism in Europe. The ideals of the French Revolution — popular sovereignty, national identity, and self-determination — inspired movements to create unified nation-states. Italy and Germany, both fragmented into multiple small states, achieved unification through a combination of ideological inspiration, diplomatic strategy, and military force. These processes reshaped the European balance of power and set the stage for the conflicts of the 20th century.

## Unification of Italy (1815–1871)

### Background

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pre-unification** | Italy was divided into multiple states — Kingdom of Sardinia (Piedmont), Kingdom of the Two Sicilies, Papal States, duchies of Tuscany, Parma, Modena, and territories under Austrian control (Lombardy, Venetia) |
| **Congress of Vienna (1815)** | Restored the old order; Austrian domination over northern Italy; Italian nationalism suppressed |
| **Risorgimento** | "Resurgence" — the Italian nationalist movement for unification |
| **Secret societies** | **Carbonari** — revolutionary secret societies active from the 1820s; staged uprisings (1820, 1831) that were suppressed |

### Key Figures

| Leader | Role | Epithet |
|--------|------|---------|
| **Giuseppe Mazzini** | Ideologue and revolutionary; founded **Young Italy (1831)** — a movement demanding a unified, democratic Italian republic; inspired revolutionary spirit through writings and organisational work | "**Soul/Spirit**" of Italian unification |
| **Count Camillo di Cavour** | Prime Minister of Piedmont-Sardinia (1852–1861); master diplomat and strategist; modernised Piedmont's economy and army; allied with France against Austria | "**Brain**" of Italian unification |
| **Giuseppe Garibaldi** | Military hero; led the **Expedition of the Thousand (I Mille, 1860)** — conquered Sicily and Southern Italy with approximately 1,000 volunteer **Redshirts** | "**Sword**" of Italian unification |
| **King Victor Emmanuel II** | King of Piedmont-Sardinia; became the first King of unified Italy | "**Honest King**" (Re Galantuomo) |

### Stages of Unification

| Stage | Year | Details |
|-------|------|---------|
| **Revolutions of 1848** | 1848 | Uprisings across Italian states — Mazzini established a short-lived **Roman Republic**; all revolts crushed by Austrian and French forces |
| **Cavour's diplomacy** | 1852–1859 | Modernised Piedmont; allied with **Napoleon III** of France (Pact of Plombières, 1858); provoked Austria into declaring war |
| **Franco-Austrian War** | 1859 | Austria defeated at the Battles of Magenta and Solferino; **Lombardy** annexed to Piedmont; Tuscany, Parma, Modena, and Romagna voted to join |
| **Garibaldi's campaign** | 1860 | Expedition of the Thousand conquered Sicily (May 1860) and marched northward through Southern Italy; handed territories to **Victor Emmanuel II** at the meeting at Teano (26 October 1860) |
| **Proclamation of Kingdom** | **17 March 1861** | Kingdom of Italy proclaimed with Victor Emmanuel II as king; capital at **Turin** (moved to Florence in 1865) |
| **Venetia acquired** | 1866 | Gained through alliance with Prussia during the **Austro-Prussian War** (Seven Weeks' War) |
| **Rome captured** | **20 September 1870** | Italian forces captured Rome after French troops withdrew (Franco-Prussian War); **Rome became the capital in July 1871** |
| **"Roman Question"** | 1870–1929 | Pope refused to accept the loss of temporal power; resolved only by the **Lateran Treaty (1929)** between Mussolini's government and the Vatican — created the independent Vatican City State |

## Unification of Germany (1864–1871)

### Background

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pre-unification** | Germany was a patchwork of **39 states** within the **German Confederation** (established 1815); dominated by Austria and Prussia |
| **Zollverein (1834)** | Customs union led by Prussia; removed internal tariffs; created economic unity before political unity; excluded Austria |
| **Frankfurt Parliament (1848–49)** | Attempted to create a unified, liberal Germany; offered the German crown to the Prussian king Frederick William IV — he refused ("a crown from the gutter"); parliament dissolved |
| **Rivalry** | Austria and Prussia competed for leadership of the German states |

### Key Figure: Otto von Bismarck

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Title** | **Minister-President of Prussia** (1862–1890); **Chancellor of the German Empire** (1871–1890) |
| **Epithet** | "**Iron Chancellor**" |
| **Ideology** | **Realpolitik** — practical politics driven by national interest rather than ideology or morality |
| **Famous quote** | "The great questions of the day will not be settled by speeches and majority decisions… but by **Blood and Iron**" (1862 speech to the Prussian Parliament) |
| **Strategy** | Isolated each opponent diplomatically before defeating them militarily; manipulated crises to provoke wars at advantageous moments |

### Three Wars of Unification

| War | Year | Opponent | Key Events | Outcome |
|-----|------|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Danish War** | 1864 | Denmark | Fought jointly with Austria over Schleswig and Holstein | Schleswig and Holstein taken from Denmark; administered jointly by Prussia (Schleswig) and Austria (Holstein) |
| **Austro-Prussian War** (Seven Weeks' War) | 1866 | Austria | Battle of **Sadowa (Königgrätz)** — decisive Prussian victory (3 July 1866) | Austria excluded from German affairs; **North German Confederation** formed under Prussian leadership; Italy gained Venetia |
| **Franco-Prussian War** | 1870–1871 | France | Bismarck provoked France with the **Ems Dispatch** (edited telegram made the French ambassador's meeting with King William seem insulting); Battle of **Sedan** (1 September 1870) — Napoleon III captured | France defeated; southern German states joined the Confederation; Alsace-Lorraine annexed by Germany |

### Proclamation of the German Empire

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Date** | **18 January 1871** |
| **Venue** | **Hall of Mirrors, Palace of Versailles** — a deliberate humiliation of France |
| **Kaiser** | King **William I** of Prussia proclaimed **German Emperor** (Kaiser) |
| **Structure** | Federal empire of 25 states dominated by Prussia; Bismarck became Chancellor |
| **Constitution** | Universal male suffrage for the Reichstag (parliament); but real power lay with the Kaiser and the Chancellor (not accountable to parliament) |

## Comparison: Italian vs German Unification

| Feature | Italy | Germany |
|---------|-------|---------|
| **Leading state** | Piedmont-Sardinia | Prussia |
| **Key strategist** | Cavour (diplomatic) | Bismarck ("Blood and Iron") |
| **Military leader** | Garibaldi (revolutionary volunteer) | Prussian Army (professional) |
| **Ideologue** | Mazzini (democratic republican) | No single ideologue; Bismarck was pragmatic |
| **Foreign ally** | France (against Austria) | None needed (Prussia strong enough) |
| **Method** | Combination of diplomacy, popular uprising, and military action | Primarily military (three wars) |
| **Completed** | 1871 (Rome) | 1871 (Versailles) |
| **Outcome** | Constitutional monarchy; relatively weak state | Powerful military empire; dominant in Europe |
