## Introduction

The period from 1919 to 1945 witnessed the most catastrophic sequence of events in modern history — the rise of totalitarian ideologies, the second global war, and a genocide that remains a defining moral reference point for international law. UPSC GS Paper I regularly tests the interwar context, the nature of fascism and Nazism, the causes and key phases of World War II, and the post-war institutional order.

---

## The Interwar Crisis: Seeds of the Second War

### Treaty of Versailles (June 28, 1919)

The peace settlement that ended World War I planted the seeds of the next conflict. Germany bore the severest burden.

| Provision | Details |
|---|---|
| War Guilt Clause (Article 231) | Germany forced to accept sole responsibility for causing WWI |
| Reparations | Set at $33 billion (equivalent to ~$423 billion in 2019) |
| Territorial losses | Germany lost 13% of its territory (10% of population): Alsace-Lorraine to France; Polish Corridor created; Danzig became a Free City; all overseas colonies forfeited |
| Military restrictions | Army capped at 100,000 men; no air force; submarines banned; Rhineland demilitarised |

**German reaction:** The Treaty was denounced as a *Diktat* (dictated peace). The "stab-in-the-back" (*Dolchstoßlegende*) myth — that Germany was undefeated militarily but betrayed by civilians and politicians — became a powerful nationalist narrative.

### The Weimar Republic's Structural Weaknesses (1919–1933)

- Hyperinflation crisis (1923): postage stamps costing billions of marks
- Relative stability under Stresemann (1924–1929)
- Great Depression (1929): unemployment in Germany rose to over 6 million by 1932
- Proportional representation produced fragmented coalitions incapable of governing
- Emergency decree powers (Article 48, Weimar Constitution) normalised authoritarian governance
- Communist and Nazi street violence destabilised public order

## Rise of Fascism in Italy (1919–1925)

### Context

Italy, though on the Allied side in WWI, felt cheated by the Paris Peace settlement — the "mutilated victory" (*vittoria mutilata*). Economic dislocation and fear of Bolshevik revolution created political instability.

### Mussolini and the Fascist Movement

- **Benito Mussolini** (1883–1945): former socialist journalist turned nationalist agitator
- Founded the **Fasci di Combattimento** in March 1919 in Milan
- Formed the **National Fascist Party (PNF)** in 1921
- **Blackshirts (Squadrismo):** paramilitary squads attacked socialist and trade union offices

### The March on Rome (October 1922)

Mussolini organised a march of tens of thousands of Blackshirts toward Rome. King Victor Emmanuel III, fearing civil war, invited Mussolini to form a government on **October 28, 1922**. Mussolini became Prime Minister at age 39.

### Consolidation of Power (1922–1925)

- Acerbo Law (1923): party winning 25% of votes would get two-thirds of seats — rigged electoral system
- Murder of socialist leader Giacomo Matteotti (1924): silenced opposition
- January 3, 1925 speech: Mussolini declared himself *Il Duce* (The Leader), establishing a one-party dictatorship
- **Corporate State:** economy organised into corporations representing workers and employers under Fascist control; class conflict replaced by class collaboration

## Rise of Nazism in Germany (1919–1934)

### From Fringe to Power

- **National Socialist German Workers' Party (NSDAP):** founded 1919; Hitler joined as member 39, became leader 1921
- **Beer Hall Putsch (November 1923):** Hitler's failed coup in Munich; sentenced to 5 years, served 9 months; wrote *Mein Kampf* in prison
- Electoral rise: NSDAP won 18.3% (1930), 37.4% (July 1932), fell to 33.1% (Nov 1932) — then manipulated into power

### Hitler Becomes Chancellor (January 30, 1933)

President Paul von Hindenburg appointed Hitler as Chancellor on **January 30, 1933**, believing conservative elites could control him.

### Consolidation Through Crisis and Terror

**Reichstag Fire (February 27, 1933):**
The Reichstag building burned down. The Nazis blamed the Communists (Dutch communist Marinus van der Lubbe was arrested). The **Reichstag Fire Decree** suspended civil liberties; thousands of Communists and Social Democrats were arrested.

**Enabling Act (March 1933):**
The Reichstag voted to give Hitler the power to rule by decree for four years, bypassing Parliament. Only the Social Democrats voted against.

**Night of the Long Knives (June 30 – July 2, 1934):**
Hitler ordered the execution of SA (Brownshirts) leader Ernst Röhm and other rivals — over 85 known killed. This cemented the loyalty of the regular army (Wehrmacht) to Hitler.

**After Hindenburg's death (August 1934):**
Hitler merged the offices of President and Chancellor, becoming *Führer und Reichskanzler*. The army swore a personal oath of allegiance to Hitler.

## Nazi Ideology

| Pillar | Content |
|---|---|
| Racial supremacy | Aryan race superior; Jews, Roma, Slavs as "sub-human" (*Untermenschen*) |
| Anti-Semitism | Jews as racial enemies responsible for Germany's humiliation; culminated in Holocaust |
| Lebensraum | "Living space" — Germany needed to conquer Eastern Europe for settlement |
| Führerprinzip | Leadership principle — absolute authority flows from the Führer downward |
| Anti-Marxism | Communism = Jewish conspiracy; must be destroyed |
| Totalitarianism | Total state control of politics, economy, culture, education |

## Road to War (1936–1939)

### Spanish Civil War (1936–1939)

- General Francisco Franco's Nationalist forces rebelled against the elected Republican government
- Germany and Italy backed Franco; Soviet Union backed the Republic; Western democracies observed non-intervention
- **Significance:** rehearsal for WWII — Germany tested Blitzkrieg tactics, Luftwaffe techniques (bombing of Guernica, April 1937)
- Franco's victory (1939) added another authoritarian power to Europe

### Appeasement Policy

- Britain and France sought to satisfy Hitler's demands to avoid another war
- **Anschluss (March 1938):** Germany annexed Austria without opposition
- **Sudetenland Crisis:** Hitler demanded German-populated Sudetenland of Czechoslovakia
- **Munich Agreement (September 29–30, 1938):** British PM Neville Chamberlain and French PM Daladier agreed to Hitler's demands without consulting Czechoslovakia — classic appeasement
- Chamberlain's claim: "peace for our time" — proved illusory when Germany occupied the rest of Czechoslovakia in March 1939

### Nazi-Soviet Pact (August 23, 1939)

Germany and the Soviet Union signed a non-aggression pact with a secret protocol dividing Eastern Europe into spheres of influence — enabling Hitler to fight on only one front initially.

## World War II (1939–1945): Major Phases

### Outbreak (September 1, 1939)

Germany invaded Poland on **September 1, 1939** using *Blitzkrieg* (lightning war) — combined armour, motorised infantry, and air support in coordinated rapid attack. Britain and France declared war on Germany on **September 3, 1939**.

### Phase 1: German Dominance (1939–1941)

| Event | Date | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Fall of Poland | September 1939 | Blitzkrieg demonstrated; partition with USSR |
| Fall of France | June 1940 | Maginot Line bypassed; Dunkirk evacuation |
| Battle of Britain | July–October 1940 | First German defeat; RAF survived Luftwaffe onslaught |
| North Africa Campaign begins | 1940 | Rommel's Afrika Korps vs British forces |

### Phase 2: War Expands (1941)

**Operation Barbarossa (June 22, 1941):** Germany invaded the Soviet Union — the largest military operation in history. Initial German advance was spectacular; eventually bogged down by Soviet resistance, logistics overstretch, and the Russian winter.

**Pearl Harbor (December 7/8, 1941):** Japan attacked the US naval base at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii. The US declared war on Japan (December 8); Germany and Italy declared war on the US (December 11) — making the war truly global.

### Phase 3: Turning Points (1942–1943)

| Battle | Date | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Battle of El Alamein | October–November 1942 | British victory; Rommel retreated; "end of the beginning" (Churchill) |
| Battle of Stalingrad | August 1942 – February 1943 | German 6th Army (300,000 men) destroyed; greatest turning point on Eastern Front |
| Battle of Midway | June 1942 | US navy destroyed Japanese carrier fleet; Pacific turning point |

### Phase 4: Allied Advance (1943–1945)

- **Allied landings in Sicily and Italy (1943):** Mussolini deposed (July 1943); Italy surrendered (September 1943)
- **D-Day / Operation Overlord (June 6, 1944):** Largest seaborne invasion in history; Allied forces landed on Normandy beaches
- **Liberation of Paris:** August 25, 1944
- **Battle of the Bulge (December 1944):** Last major German offensive; failed
- **V-E Day (May 8, 1945):** Germany unconditionally surrendered; Victory in Europe Day

### Phase 5: Pacific War and End

- **Island-hopping strategy:** US forces captured key Pacific islands (Guadalcanal, Iwo Jima, Okinawa) to establish air bases
- **Atomic bombings:**
  - **Hiroshima:** August 6, 1945 — "Little Boy" uranium bomb; ~70,000–80,000 killed instantly
  - **Nagasaki:** August 9, 1945 — "Fat Man" plutonium bomb; ~40,000 killed instantly; radiation casualties brought total deaths to 100,000+
- **Soviet declaration of war on Japan:** August 8, 1945
- **V-J Day (September 2, 1945):** Japan formally surrendered aboard USS Missouri; end of WWII

## The Holocaust

The Holocaust was the state-sponsored, systematic genocide of six million Jews (and millions of others — Roma, disabled persons, political opponents, Soviet POWs) by Nazi Germany.

### Key Milestones

| Event | Date | Details |
|---|---|---|
| Nuremberg Laws | September 1935 | Stripped Jews of citizenship; prohibited Jewish-German marriage |
| Kristallnacht | November 9–10, 1938 | Organised pogrom; 7,500+ Jewish businesses destroyed; 30,000 Jews arrested |
| Wannsee Conference | January 20, 1942 | Senior Nazi officials coordinated the "Final Solution to the Jewish Question"; systematic extermination plan |
| Extermination Camps | 1942–1944 | Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec — gas chambers used for mass murder |

The Wannsee Conference referenced approximately 11 million Jews in Europe as targets. By the war's end, approximately **six million Jews** — two-thirds of European Jewry — had been murdered.

## Nuremberg Trials (1945–1946)

The International Military Tribunal at Nuremberg prosecuted senior Nazi war criminals.

| Category of Crime | Examples |
|---|---|
| Crimes against peace | Planning and waging aggressive war |
| War crimes | Violations of laws and customs of war |
| Crimes against humanity | Murder, extermination, enslavement, deportation of civilian populations |

- 24 major Nazi leaders tried; 12 sentenced to death (including Göring, Ribbentrop, Keitel)
- **Precedent set:** established that "following orders" is not a defence for crimes against humanity
- Foundation for the later **Genocide Convention (1948)**, **Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)**, and the **International Criminal Court (Rome Statute, 1998)**

## Post-War Order

### United Nations (1945)

- **UN Charter** signed on June 26, 1945, at San Francisco; came into force October 24, 1945
- Security Council: 5 permanent members (US, USSR, UK, France, China) with veto power
- General Assembly: one nation, one vote
- Replaced the failed League of Nations

### Bretton Woods System (1944)

Established at the UN Monetary and Financial Conference at Bretton Woods, New Hampshire (July 1944), laying the foundations of the post-war international economic order.

| Institution | Purpose |
|---|---|
| International Monetary Fund (IMF) | Exchange rate stability; balance of payments assistance |
| International Bank for Reconstruction and Development (IBRD/World Bank) | Reconstruction of war-damaged economies; development lending |
| GATT (1947) | Framework for reducing trade barriers (later became WTO in 1995) |

### Marshall Plan (1948)

US provided $13 billion in economic assistance to Western Europe for reconstruction — also served as bulwark against Communist influence.

### Origins of the Cold War

- **Iron Curtain speech (Churchill, March 1946):** division of Europe between Free West and Soviet-controlled East
- **Truman Doctrine (1947):** US committed to containing Communism
- **Berlin Blockade (1948–49):** Soviet blockade of West Berlin; Allied airlift
- Division of Germany into Federal Republic (West, 1949) and Democratic Republic (East, 1949)
- Formation of NATO (April 1949): Western military alliance

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Dates to memorise: March on Rome (October 1922); Hitler Chancellor (January 30, 1933); Reichstag Fire (February 27, 1933); Night of Long Knives (June 30–July 2, 1934); Operation Barbarossa (June 22, 1941); Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941); Wannsee Conference (January 20, 1942); D-Day (June 6, 1944); Hiroshima (August 6, 1945); V-E Day (May 8, 1945); V-J Day (September 2, 1945)
- Versailles reparations: $33 billion; Germany lost 13% territory
- Nuremberg Trials: 3 categories of crime — crimes against peace, war crimes, crimes against humanity
- Bretton Woods: IMF + World Bank — established 1944, operative 1945

**For Mains:**
- Analyse how the Treaty of Versailles caused WWII: economic humiliation, territorial grievances, democratic instability, rise of extremism
- Compare Fascism and Nazism: similarities (anti-communist, totalitarian, ultra-nationalist) and differences (racial ideology central only to Nazism)
- Significance of Nuremberg: precedent for international criminal law; "Nuremberg principles" in UNGA Resolution 95 (I) of 1946
- Post-war order: how Bretton Woods and the UN attempted to prevent future conflicts; origins of Cold War as unintended consequence
