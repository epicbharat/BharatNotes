## Overview

Post-Independence India (1947 onwards) encompasses the monumental task of nation-building — integrating 565 princely states, establishing democratic institutions, pursuing economic development through planned industrialisation, defending sovereignty in wars, and navigating the Cold War through non-alignment. The period also saw critical challenges: the Emergency, linguistic reorganisation, and the 1991 economic crisis that transformed India's trajectory.

---

## Integration of Princely States

At independence, India had **565 princely states** that were technically sovereign — the British paramountcy had lapsed. **Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel**, India's first Deputy Prime Minister and Home Minister, along with **V.P. Menon** (Secretary, Ministry of States), accomplished one of history's greatest feats of political integration.

### Key Accessions

| State | Method | Date | Key Details |
|-------|--------|------|-------------|
| **Kashmir** | Instrument of Accession | **26 October 1947** | Pakistan-backed tribal militias invaded on 22 October 1947. Maharaja Hari Singh signed the Instrument of Accession on 26 October; Governor-General Mountbatten accepted it on 27 October. Indian troops airlifted to Srinagar |
| **Junagadh** | Plebiscite | **20 February 1948** | Nawab acceded to Pakistan despite ~80% Hindu population. India imposed blockade; Nawab fled to Karachi. Plebiscite result: 190,779 voted for India, only 91 against (99.95%) |
| **Hyderabad** | Military action (Operation Polo) | **13–17 September 1948** | Nizam Mir Osman Ali Khan refused accession. Major General J.N. Chaudhuri led the "police action." Nizam surrendered on 17 September 1948 |

> **Exam Tip:** The three "problem states" at independence -- Kashmir, Junagadh, and Hyderabad -- used three different methods of accession: Kashmir (Instrument of Accession under duress of invasion), Junagadh (plebiscite after ruler's decision was overruled), Hyderabad (military action/police action). This distinction is frequently tested in Prelims.

### States Reorganisation

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Trigger** | Death of **Potti Sreeramulu** on 16 December 1952 after a fast-unto-death demanding a Telugu-majority state (Andhra) |
| **Commission** | **States Reorganisation Commission (SRC)**, constituted December 1953 |
| **Chairman** | **Justice Fazl Ali** (also called the Fazal Ali Commission) |
| **Members** | K.M. Panikkar and H.N. Kunzru |
| **Report submitted** | September 1955 |
| **Legislation** | **States Reorganisation Act, 1956** — reorganised states primarily along linguistic lines |
| **Outcome** | Created 14 states and 6 Union Territories |

> **Key Fact:** Andhra was the first state created on a linguistic basis (1 October 1953), even before the SRC submitted its report. This was a direct result of Potti Sreeramulu's death and the subsequent public outcry. The SRC then formalised the principle of linguistic reorganisation for the rest of India.

## Planned Economic Development

### Five-Year Plans

India adopted centralised economic planning through the **Planning Commission** (established 15 March 1950 via Cabinet Resolution; PM as ex-officio Chairman).

| Plan | Period | Model/Focus | Key Achievement |
|------|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **First** | 1951–56 | **Harrod-Domar model**; drafted by economist K.N. Raj; priority to agriculture, irrigation, and power (44.6% of outlay) | Targeted 2.1% GDP growth; achieved **3.6%** |
| **Second** | 1956–61 | **Mahalanobis model** (Feldman-Mahalanobis); designed by Professor P.C. Mahalanobis; prioritised heavy industries (steel, cement, machinery) | Foundation of India's industrial base — Bhilai, Rourkela, Durgapur steel plants |
| **Third** | 1961–66 | Aimed at self-sustained growth; defence expenditure increased after 1962 war | Disrupted by Indo-China War (1962), Indo-Pak War (1965), and droughts (1965-66) |
| **Fourth** | 1969–74 | "Growth with stability"; focus on agriculture and self-reliance | Green Revolution impact visible; but disrupted by 1971 war and oil crisis (1973) |
| **Fifth** | 1974–79 | Poverty alleviation ("Garibi Hatao"); minimum needs programme | Terminated one year early by the Janata Party government |

**Plan Holidays:** 1966–69 (annual plans due to economic disruption) and 1990–92 (political instability).

### Green Revolution

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Period** | Mid-1960s onwards (introduced in Punjab, 1966–67) |
| **Indian architect** | **M.S. Swaminathan** (1925–2023) — "Father of the Green Revolution in India"; Director-General of ICAR |
| **International partner** | **Norman Borlaug** (USA) — developed high-yielding wheat varieties in Mexico; Nobel Peace Prize, 1970 |
| **Key crops** | Wheat and rice (later extended to other crops) |
| **Impact** | Wheat production jumped to 17 million tonnes in 1968 (up 5 million from previous harvest); India became self-sufficient in food grains by the 1970s |
| **Criticism** | Regional disparity (Punjab, Haryana benefited most); environmental damage (soil degradation, water depletion); income inequality within agriculture |

### White Revolution (Operation Flood)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Launched** | 13 January 1970 |
| **Architect** | **Dr. Verghese Kurien** (1921–2012) — "Father of India's White Revolution" / "Milkman of India" |
| **Institution** | Chairman and founder of **Amul**; Chairman of **NDDB** (National Dairy Development Board, founded 1965) |
| **Three Phases** | Phase I (1970–80), Phase II (1981–85), Phase III (after 1985) |
| **Achievement** | Made India the **world's largest milk producer**, surpassing the USA in **1998** |
| **Model** | Cooperative-based: dairy cooperatives at village level linked to district and state unions (Amul model/Anand pattern) |

> **Common Mistake:** Students confuse the Green Revolution (agriculture, 1960s, M.S. Swaminathan) with the White Revolution (dairy, 1970, Verghese Kurien). Also frequently tested: the **Blue Revolution** (fisheries/aquaculture) and the **Yellow Revolution** (oilseeds, 1986-90).

### From Planning Commission to NITI Aayog

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Planning Commission** | Established 15 March 1950 via Cabinet Resolution (not by Constitution or statute) |
| **Replaced by** | **NITI Aayog** (National Institution for Transforming India) on **1 January 2015** via Cabinet Resolution |
| **Key difference** | Planning Commission was top-down; NITI Aayog follows a bottom-up, cooperative federalism approach |
| **Structure** | PM as Chairperson; Vice-Chairperson (full-time); all CMs and LG of UTs in Governing Council |

## Key Constitutional Developments

### Major Constitutional Amendments

| Amendment | Year | Key Changes |
|-----------|------|-------------|
| **1st Amendment** | 1951 | Added restrictions on Article 19(1)(a) — "public order" and "incitement to offence"; introduced Article 31A, 31B, and **Ninth Schedule** to protect zamindari abolition laws from judicial review; enabled reservation under Article 15(4) |
| **7th Amendment** | 1956 | Reorganised states in accordance with the States Reorganisation Act, 1956 |
| **24th Amendment** | 1971 | Affirmed Parliament's power to amend Fundamental Rights (response to Golaknath case) |
| **42nd Amendment** | 1976 | **"Mini Constitution"** — added "Socialist," "Secular," and "Integrity" to the Preamble; added **Fundamental Duties** (Part IV-A, Article 51A); curtailed judicial review; enacted during the Emergency under Indira Gandhi |
| **44th Amendment** | 1978 | Undid Emergency-era changes — removed Right to Property from FRs (made legal right under Article 300A); replaced "internal disturbance" with **"armed rebellion"** as Emergency ground; made Articles 20 and 21 non-suspendable; required written Cabinet recommendation for Emergency |
| **73rd Amendment** | 1992 | Constitutionalised **Panchayati Raj** — three-tier structure, 5-year elections, reservation for SCs/STs/women |
| **74th Amendment** | 1992 | Constitutional framework for **urban municipalities** — came into force 1 June 1993 |

> **Key Distinction:** The 42nd Amendment (1976) and 44th Amendment (1978) are almost always tested together. The 42nd expanded executive power during the Emergency; the 44th (under PM Morarji Desai, Janata Party) restored the balance. Remember: 44th Amendment made Right to Property a legal right (Article 300A), NOT a Fundamental Right -- this is one of the most frequently tested facts in Prelims.

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

| Case | Date of Judgment | Key Holding |
|------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Shankari Prasad v. Union of India (1951)** | 1951 | Parliament can amend Fundamental Rights under Article 368 |
| **Golaknath v. State of Punjab (1967)** | 1967 | 11-judge bench; Parliament CANNOT amend Fundamental Rights (overruled Shankari Prasad) |
| **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** | **24 April 1973** | 13-judge bench; **7:6 majority**. Established the **Basic Structure Doctrine** — Parliament can amend the Constitution but cannot destroy its basic structure (democracy, secularism, federalism, rule of law, judicial review, separation of powers) |
| **Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India** | **25 January 1978** | Expanded Article 21 — "procedure established by law" must be just, fair, and reasonable. Established the **"Golden Triangle"** linking Articles 14, 19, and 21. Overruled A.K. Gopalan (1950) |
| **Minerva Mills v. Union of India** | **31 July 1980** | Reinforced Basic Structure doctrine; 4:1 majority. Struck down Section 4 of 42nd Amendment. Held Parliament's amending power is limited and cannot be converted into unlimited power |

> **Exam Tip:** The evolution of judicial review on constitutional amendments follows a clear trajectory: Shankari Prasad (1951, Parliament can amend) → Golaknath (1967, Parliament cannot amend FRs) → 24th Amendment (1971, Parliament reasserts power) → **Kesavananda Bharati (1973, Parliament can amend but not basic structure)** → Minerva Mills (1980, reinforces basic structure). This sequence is a Prelims favourite.

## The Emergency (25 June 1975 – 21 March 1977)

### Background and Declaration

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Trigger** | On 12 June 1975, Justice Jagmohanlal Sinha of the **Allahabad High Court** found PM Indira Gandhi guilty of electoral malpractices under the Representation of the People Act, 1951, and disqualified her for 6 years |
| **Declaration** | Emergency proclaimed on the night of **25 June 1975** by President **Fakhruddin Ali Ahmed** under **Article 352** |
| **Ground cited** | **"Internal disturbance"** (threat to the security of India) |
| **Significance** | Third Emergency in India's history, but the **first declared in peacetime** (earlier two were during the 1962 and 1971 wars) |

### Key Features of the Emergency

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Fundamental Rights suspended** | Articles 14, 19, 21, and 22 effectively suspended; preventive detention used extensively under MISA (Maintenance of Internal Security Act) |
| **Press censorship** | Pre-censorship imposed on all media |
| **Political arrests** | Opposition leaders arrested — Jayaprakash Narayan, Morarji Desai, Atal Bihari Vajpayee, L.K. Advani, George Fernandes |
| **42nd Amendment (1976)** | Passed during Emergency — expanded executive power, curtailed judicial review, added Fundamental Duties |
| **20-Point Programme** | Economic programme announced for poverty alleviation, land reform, and abolition of bonded labour |
| **Forced sterilisation** | Controversial population control measures under Sanjay Gandhi |

### End of Emergency

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Elections announced** | January 1977; elections held **16–20 March 1977** |
| **Emergency lifted** | **21 March 1977** |
| **Result** | Congress defeated for the first time; **Janata Party** formed government on 24 March 1977 |
| **PM** | **Morarji Desai** became India's first non-Congress PM |
| **Legacy** | 44th Amendment (1978) introduced safeguards against future misuse; 25 June observed as **"Samvidhaan Hatya Diwas"** (since 2025) |

> **Warning:** The Emergency is a sensitive but heavily tested topic. Key facts to remember: (1) It was declared under Article 352 for "internal disturbance," NOT "armed rebellion" (the 44th Amendment later changed this ground); (2) the ADM Jabalpur case (1976) — SC's darkest hour — upheld suspension of Article 21 during Emergency (effectively overruled by the 44th Amendment); (3) the Shah Commission (Justice J.C. Shah) investigated Emergency excesses after 1977.

## Wars and Defence

### Indo-China War (1962)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Duration** | **20 October – 21 November 1962** |
| **Theatres** | Aksai Chin (western sector) and NEFA — North-East Frontier Agency, now Arunachal Pradesh (eastern sector) |
| **Trigger** | Border disputes arising from the undefined McMahon Line and China's occupation of Aksai Chin |
| **Result** | India suffered a military defeat; China declared unilateral ceasefire on 19 November, effective 21 November |
| **Aftermath** | China withdrew 20 km behind the LAC as it existed on 7 November 1959; India undertook major defence modernisation |
| **Political impact** | Defence Minister V.K. Krishna Menon resigned; shattered Nehru's "Hindi-Chini Bhai-Bhai" policy |

### Indo-Pakistan Wars

| War | Dates | Key Details | Outcome |
|-----|-------|-------------|---------|
| **First Kashmir War** | October 1947 – January 1949 | Pakistan-backed tribal invasion of Kashmir; India intervened after Instrument of Accession | UN-brokered ceasefire; Kashmir divided along ceasefire line |
| **1965 War** | August – September 1965 | Fought primarily over Kashmir; Pakistan launched Operation Gibraltar | UN ceasefire on 22–23 September 1965; **Tashkent Declaration** signed 10 January 1966 (PM Lal Bahadur Shastri died in Tashkent on 11 January 1966) |
| **1971 War (Bangladesh Liberation)** | **3–16 December 1971** | India supported East Pakistan's independence movement. Pakistani forces (93,000 troops) surrendered at Dhaka on **16 December 1971** | Bangladesh became independent; 16 December celebrated as **Vijay Diwas** |

### Shimla Agreement (2 July 1972)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Signed by** | PM **Indira Gandhi** and Pakistani President **Zulfikar Ali Bhutto** |
| **Key provisions** | Ceasefire line in Kashmir formalised as the **Line of Control (LoC)**; bilateral framework for resolving all disputes; no unilateral alteration of the LoC |
| **Significance** | Established the principle that India-Pakistan disputes would be resolved bilaterally (not through third-party mediation) |

### Nuclear Tests

| Test | Date | Key Details |
|------|------|-------------|
| **Pokhran-I (Smiling Buddha)** | **18 May 1974** | India's first nuclear test; underground implosion-type fission device at Pokhran, Rajasthan; described as "peaceful nuclear explosion"; led by **Raja Ramanna** (BARC Director) |
| **Pokhran-II (Operation Shakti)** | **11 May 1998** (3 tests) and **13 May 1998** (2 tests) | Series of 5 nuclear weapon tests at Pokhran; led by **Dr. R. Chidambaram** and **Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam**; PM: **Atal Bihari Vajpayee** |
| **Aftermath** | India declared itself a nuclear weapon state; US and other nations imposed sanctions; India adopted a **No First Use (NFU)** policy |

## Non-Aligned Movement (NAM)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Precursors** | **Bandung Conference** (1955, Indonesia) — 29 Asian and African nations; **Brioni Declaration** (19 July 1956) by Tito, Nehru, and Nasser |
| **First Summit** | **1–6 September 1961, Belgrade, Yugoslavia** |
| **Participants** | **25 countries**; 3 observers (Bolivia, Brazil, Ecuador) |
| **Founding leaders** | **Jawaharlal Nehru** (India), **Josip Broz Tito** (Yugoslavia), **Gamal Abdel Nasser** (Egypt), **Kwame Nkrumah** (Ghana), **Sukarno** (Indonesia) |
| **Core principles** | Non-alignment with either Cold War bloc; peaceful coexistence; sovereignty and territorial integrity; opposition to colonialism and imperialism |
| **Current members** | 120 countries (largest grouping after the UN) |

> **Key Point:** NAM was NOT neutrality — Nehru specifically rejected the idea that non-alignment meant sitting on the fence. It meant independent foreign policy judgement on each issue. India supported decolonisation, opposed apartheid, and took positions on Cold War issues based on merit, not bloc affiliation.

## 1991 Economic Reforms (LPG)

### The Crisis

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Context** | Balance of payments crisis — foreign exchange reserves dwindled to cover barely **2 weeks of imports** |
| **Causes** | Fiscal deficit, Gulf War (1990) disrupting oil supplies and remittances, collapse of the Soviet Union (major trade partner), political instability |
| **PM** | **P.V. Narasimha Rao** |
| **Finance Minister** | **Dr. Manmohan Singh** |
| **IMF role** | Reforms partly undertaken under IMF and World Bank conditionalities in exchange for emergency loans |

### Key Reform Measures

| Reform Area | Measures |
|-------------|---------|
| **Liberalisation** | Dismantled the **License Raj** — industrial licensing abolished for most sectors; MRTP Act restrictions eased |
| **Devaluation** | Rupee devalued by ~18–19% against the US dollar (July 1991) to boost exports |
| **Trade reforms** | Import restrictions eased; capital goods imports liberalised without prior government approval; tariffs reduced from peak rates of 300%+ to around 150% (further reduced in subsequent years) |
| **Privatisation** | Reduced public sector dominance; opened sectors to private and foreign investment; disinvestment of PSUs initiated |
| **Globalisation** | FDI norms liberalised; automatic approval route for FDI up to 51% in many sectors; rupee made convertible on current account (1994) |

### Impact of Reforms

| Indicator | Pre-Reform | Post-Reform |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **GDP growth** | "Hindu rate of growth" (~3.5% for decades) | Averaged 6–7% in the 2000s; touched 8–9% in peak years |
| **Foreign exchange reserves** | ~USD 1 billion (1991) | Over USD 600 billion (2024) |
| **IT sector** | Negligible | USD 283 billion revenue (FY 2024-25) |
| **Poverty** | ~45% (early 1990s) | ~5.3% by Tendulkar line (2022-23) |
| **Middle class** | Small | Over 500 million |

> **UPSC Relevance:** The 1991 reforms are tested across multiple papers — GS1 (post-independence consolidation), GS2 (role of government), GS3 (economic development), and even GS4 (ethics of reform — winners and losers). Always present both achievements (growth, poverty reduction, global integration) and criticisms (jobless growth, rising inequality, agrarian distress, crony capitalism).

## Previous Year Questions (PYQs)

### Prelims

**Q1. (2014):** The Parliament of India acquires the power to legislate on any item in the State List in the national interest if a resolution to that effect is passed by the:
(a) Lok Sabha by a simple majority of its total membership
(b) Lok Sabha by a majority of not less than two-thirds of its total membership
(c) Rajya Sabha by a simple majority of its total membership
(d) Rajya Sabha by a majority of not less than two-thirds of its members present and voting
**Answer:** (d) *(Article 249)*

**Q2. (2015):** There is a Parliamentary


 system of government in India because:
(a) the Lok Sabha is elected directly by the people
(b) the Parliament can amend the Constitution
(c) the Rajya Sabha cannot be dissolved
(d) the Council of Ministers is responsible to the Lok Sabha
**Answer:** (d)
