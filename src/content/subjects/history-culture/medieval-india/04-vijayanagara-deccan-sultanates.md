## Overview

South India in the medieval period was defined by the rivalry between the Hindu **Vijayanagara Empire** and the Islamic **Bahmani Sultanate** (and its five successor states). The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646) was the most powerful Hindu kingdom in medieval India, while the Bahmani Sultanate and its successors shaped the Deccan's political and cultural landscape.

---

## Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646)

### Foundation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Founded** | 1336 |
| **Founders** | Brothers **Harihara I** and **Bukka Raya I** of the **Sangama dynasty** |
| **Capital** | **Hampi** (on the banks of the Tungabhadra River, Karnataka) — **UNESCO World Heritage Site (1986)** |
| **Context** | Founded in response to the southward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate under Muhammad bin Tughlaq |
| **Sage Vidyaranya** | According to tradition, the brothers were inspired by the sage **Vidyaranya** (Madhava) of Sringeri to establish the kingdom — modern historians consider this a **political foundation myth** likely created ~200 years later by the Sringeri matha to reassert prestige; mention the tradition but note the scholarly scepticism |

### Four Dynasties

| Dynasty | Period | Notable Rulers |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| **Sangama** | 1336–1485 | Harihara I, Bukka I, Devaraya I, Devaraya II |
| **Saluva** | 1485–1505 | Saluva Narasimha — seized power to prevent disintegration |
| **Tuluva** | 1505–1570 | **Krishnadevaraya** (1509–1529) — greatest ruler |
| **Aravidu** | 1570–1646 | Tirumala Deva Raya — continued in diminished form after Talikota |

### Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529)

Widely regarded as one of the greatest rulers in Indian history:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Dynasty** | Tuluva (third ruler of the dynasty) |
| **Military victories** | Defeated the Sultan of **Bijapur** (Battle of Raichur, 1520), the Gajapatis of Odisha, and the Sultan of Golconda |
| **Administration** | Maintained a powerful standing army with imported horses (from the Portuguese and Arabs); efficient revenue system |
| **Literature** | Author of **Amuktamalyada** — a Telugu literary masterpiece on statecraft and devotion |
| **Ashtadiggajas** | His court had the famous **Eight Great Poets** (Ashtadiggajas) of Telugu literature — including **Allasani Peddana** (greatest, called **Andhra Kavita Pitamaha** — "grandfather of Telugu poetry"), Nandi Timmana, Dhurjati, and others |
| **Foreign visitors** | Portuguese traveller **Domingo Paes** visited during his reign and described Vijayanagara as one of the **most magnificent cities in the world** — larger than Rome, with thriving markets, grand festivals, and well-maintained infrastructure |
| **Title** | Assumed titles including **Yavana Rajya Sthapanacharya** ("restorer of the Yavana kingdom" — referring to his restoration of the deposed Sultan of Bijapur to the throne) |

### Other Notable Rulers

| Ruler | Key Fact |
|-------|----------|
| **Devaraya II** (1422–1446) | Sangama dynasty; most powerful ruler before Krishnadevaraya; recruited Muslim archers into the army |
| **Aliya Rama Raya** (regent, 1542–1565) | Son-in-law of Krishnadevaraya; de facto ruler; skilled diplomat who played the Deccan Sultanates against each other; this strategy ultimately backfired at Talikota |

### Foreign Travellers at Vijayanagara

| Traveller | Period | Notable Observations |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| **Nicolo de' Conti** (Italian) | c. 1420s | Described the city as having a circumference of ~60 miles and an army of 90,000 |
| **Abdur Razzaq** (Persian ambassador) | 1443 | Described Vijayanagara as a city with "no equal in the world"; noted its seven concentric fortification walls |
| **Domingo Paes** (Portuguese) | c. 1520 | Most detailed and admiring account — described Krishnadevaraya's court, the Mahanavami festival, and the city's prosperity |
| **Fernao Nuniz** (Portuguese) | c. 1535–37 (during **Achyutaraya's** reign, not Krishnadevaraya's) | Provided a history of the empire from its founding; chronicled the horse trade |

### Battle of Talikota / Rakkasa-Tangadi (23 January 1565)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Also called** | Battle of Rakkasa-Tangadi (the actual village where fighting occurred; "Talikota" is the nearby town) |
| **Combatants** | Vijayanagara (under regent Aliya Rama Raya) vs a combined alliance of four Deccan Sultanates — **Ahmadnagar, Bijapur, Golconda, and Bidar** (Berar had already been absorbed by Ahmadnagar) |
| **Cause** | Rama Raya's policy of playing sultanates against each other finally united them against Vijayanagara |
| **Result** | Decisive defeat of Vijayanagara; **Rama Raya was captured and beheaded** on the battlefield; his severed head was displayed to break Vijayanagara morale |
| **Aftermath** | Hampi was sacked, looted, and devastated for months; the capital was permanently abandoned; the city's ruins are now the UNESCO World Heritage Site |

> **Key Distinction:** The Battle of Talikota (1565) did NOT immediately end the Vijayanagara Empire. The **Aravidu dynasty** continued to rule from **Penukonda** and later **Chandragiri** until 1646. UPSC has tested this.

### Administrative System

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Provincial administration** | Empire divided into **Rajyas** (provinces) → **Nadus** (districts) → **Sthalas** (sub-districts) → **Gramas** (villages) |
| **Nayankara system** | Military governors (**Nayakas**) were assigned territories (**Nayankara**) in exchange for military service and revenue collection; similar to the Sultanate's Iqta but more decentralised |
| **Ayagar system** | Village administration through a group of **12 hereditary functionaries** — including the headman, accountant, watchman, potter, washerman, barber, etc.; ensured village self-sufficiency |
| **Economy** | Flourishing trade with Southeast Asia, China, Arabia, and Portugal; major ports: **Mangalore, Honavar, Bhatkal** on the west coast |
| **Currency** | Gold **Varaha** (also called **Pagoda**); silver **Tara**; copper **Jital** |
| **Horse trade** | Imported horses from Arabia and Persia through Portuguese middlemen — essential for cavalry |

## Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1527)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Founded** | 1347 — during a rebellion of Deccan amirs against **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** |
| **Founder** | **Zafar Khan** (took the title **Alauddin Bahman Shah**; also known as Hasan Gangu) |
| **Capital** | Initially **Gulbarga (Kalaburagi)**; shifted to **Bidar** by Ahmad Shah I (1432) |
| **Religion** | Islamic; maintained a cosmopolitan court with Persian, Arab, Turkish, and Indian elements |
| **Rivalry** | Constant warfare with the Vijayanagara Empire — the **Raichur Doab** (land between Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers) was the perennial flashpoint |

### Key Bahmani Figures

| Figure | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Mahmud Gawan** | Powerful prime minister (wazir) under Muhammad Shah III; a Persian scholar and administrator; reformed the provincial system; built a famous madrasa at Bidar; executed in 1481 due to court intrigues between Deccani and Afaqi factions |
| **Firoz Shah Bahmani** | Known for patronage of learning; said to have spoken multiple languages |

### Factionalism and Decline

The Bahmani court was divided between two rival groups:
- **Deccanis** — locally settled Muslims (Indian-origin)
- **Afaqis/Pardesis** — foreign-origin nobles (Persian, Arab, Turkish immigrants)

This factional conflict tore the sultanate apart, leading to its break-up into five independent kingdoms.

## Five Deccan Sultanates

| Sultanate | Year of Independence | Capital | Founded by |
|-----------|---------------------|---------|-----------|
| **Ahmadnagar** (Nizam Shahi) | 1490 | Ahmadnagar | Malik Ahmad Nizam Shah |
| **Bijapur** (Adil Shahi) | 1490 | Bijapur | Yusuf Adil Shah |
| **Berar** (Imad Shahi) | 1490 | Ellichpur (Achalpur) | Fathullah Imad Shah |
| **Bidar** (Barid Shahi) | c. 1492 | Bidar | Amir Barid |
| **Golconda** (Qutb Shahi) | 1518 | Golconda (later Hyderabad) | Quli Qutb Shah |

### Notable Deccan Sultanate Highlights

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Bijapur — Gol Gumbaz** | Tomb of Muhammad Adil Shah (completed 1656); has the **second-largest dome in the world** (after St Peter's Basilica, Rome); famous "Whispering Gallery" |
| **Golconda — Diamond trade** | Golconda was the world's premier source of diamonds until the 18th century; the Koh-i-Noor and Hope diamonds reportedly originated from Golconda mines |
| **Chand Bibi** | Queen of Bijapur and regent of Ahmadnagar; defended Ahmadnagar against Mughal siege by Akbar's general (1595); ultimately assassinated by her own nobles |
| **Talikota alliance** | The four sultanates (minus Berar, already absorbed by Ahmadnagar) united to defeat Vijayanagara in 1565 |
| **Mughal absorption** | Ahmadnagar conquered by Shah Jahan (1636); Bijapur (1686) and Golconda (1687) by Aurangzeb; Bidar and Berar absorbed earlier |

## UPSC Relevance

### Prelims Focus Areas
- Vijayanagara: founded 1336, Harihara I and Bukka I, capital Hampi (UNESCO WHS 1986)
- Four dynasties: Sangama, Saluva, Tuluva, Aravidu
- Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529): Amuktamalyada, Ashtadiggajas, Battle of Raichur (1520)
- Battle of Talikota (1565): combined Deccan Sultanates vs Vijayanagara; Rama Raya killed; empire continued under Aravidu dynasty
- Nayankara system and Ayagar system (12 hereditary village functionaries)
- Bahmani Sultanate: 1347, Gulbarga then Bidar, Mahmud Gawan, Deccani-Afaqi factionalism
- Five Sultanates: Ahmadnagar (1490), Bijapur (1490), Berar (1490), Bidar (1492), Golconda (1518)
- Gol Gumbaz (Bijapur): second-largest dome in the world
- Domingo Paes and Abdur Razzaq: foreign travellers at Vijayanagara

### Mains Focus Areas
- Why did the Vijayanagara Empire fall despite being wealthy and powerful?
- Vijayanagara as a cultural synthesis — Hindu-Islamic architectural influences
- Compare the Nayankara system with the Mughal Mansabdari and Sultanate Iqta systems
- Bahmani-Vijayanagara rivalry: religious conflict or political competition?
- Deccan Sultanates and their contributions to art, architecture, and literature
- Role of foreign trade (Portuguese, Arab) in the economy of Vijayanagara
