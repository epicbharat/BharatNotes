## Overview

South India in the medieval period was defined by the rivalry between the Hindu **Vijayanagara Empire** and the Islamic **Bahmani Sultanate** (and its five successor states). The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646) was the most powerful Hindu kingdom in medieval India, while the Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1527) and its five successor states — Ahmadnagar, Bijapur, Berar, Bidar, and Golconda — shaped the Deccan's political and cultural landscape.

The **Raichur Doab** (the fertile land between the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers) was the principal theatre of conflict between these powers. Despite their political rivalry, the period witnessed remarkable cultural exchange — Vijayanagara adopted Islamic architectural motifs (arches, domes, stucco decoration), while the Deccan Sultanates patronised local languages, music, and art forms. Both the Vijayanagara capital (Hampi) and the Sultanate capitals produced architectural masterpieces that survive as major heritage sites today.

## Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646)

### Foundation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Founded** | 1336 |
| **Founders** | Brothers **Harihara I** and **Bukka Raya I** of the **Sangama dynasty** |
| **Capital** | **Vijayanagara** (modern **Hampi**), on the banks of the **Tungabhadra River**, Karnataka — **UNESCO World Heritage Site (1986)** |
| **Context** | Founded in response to the southward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate under Muhammad bin Tughlaq; succeeded the erstwhile Hindu kingdoms of the **Hoysalas, Kakatiyas, and Yadavas** |
| **Sage Vidyaranya** | According to tradition, the brothers were inspired by the sage **Vidyaranya** (Madhava) of Sringeri to establish the kingdom — modern historians consider this a **political foundation myth** likely created ~200 years later by the Sringeri matha to reassert prestige; mention the tradition but note the scholarly scepticism |
| **Early expansion** | Harihara I consolidated the core territories in Karnataka; Bukka I (r. 1356–1377) expanded southward and established control over most of the peninsula south of the Krishna River |

### Four Dynasties

| Dynasty | Period | Notable Rulers |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| **Sangama** | 1336–1485 | Harihara I, Bukka I, Devaraya I, Devaraya II |
| **Saluva** | 1485–1505 | Saluva Narasimha — seized power to prevent disintegration |
| **Tuluva** | 1505–1570 | **Krishnadevaraya** (1509–1529) — greatest ruler |
| **Aravidu** | 1570–1646 | Tirumala Deva Raya — continued in diminished form after Talikota |

### Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529)

Widely regarded as one of the greatest rulers in Indian history and the most powerful king in the peninsula during his reign:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Dynasty** | Tuluva (third ruler of the dynasty) |
| **Battle of Raichur (1520)** | Defeated **Ismail Adil Shah** of Bijapur on 19–20 May 1520 to capture the strategically vital **Raichur Doab** (land between the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers); Portuguese musketeers aided the siege of Raichur fort |
| **Conquest of Odisha** | Captured the Gajapati forts of **Udayagiri** (1512–13) and **Kondavidu**; drove Gajapati king **Prataparudra Deva** to his capital Cuttack; Prataparudra sued for peace and gave his daughter **Princess Jaganmohini** in marriage; Krishnadevaraya generously returned all lands north of the Krishna River |
| **Other victories** | Defeated the Sultan of **Golconda**; subjugated rebel Nayakas in the Tamil country |
| **Administration** | Maintained a powerful standing army with imported horses (from the Portuguese and Arabs); efficient revenue system |
| **Literature** | Author of **Amuktamalyada** — a Telugu epic poem recounting the sacred union of **Ranganayaka** (Vishnu) and **Andal** (Alvar poet-saint); also a treatise on statecraft |
| **Ashtadiggajas** | His court had the famous **Eight Great Poets** (Ashtadiggajas) of Telugu literature — **Allasani Peddana** (greatest, called **Andhra Kavita Pitamaha**), **Nandi Timmana**, **Madayyagari Mallana**, **Dhurjati**, **Ayyalaraju Ramabhadrudu**, **Pingali Surana**, **Ramarajabhushanudu**, and **Tenali Ramakrishna** |
| **Tenali Ramakrishna** | Poet, scholar, and special advisor; renowned for his wit and humour; one of the Ashtadiggajas; his clever tales remain popular in Indian folklore |
| **Foreign visitors** | Portuguese traveller **Domingo Paes** visited during his reign (c. 1520–22) and described Vijayanagara as one of the **most magnificent cities in the world** — larger than Rome, with thriving markets, grand festivals (especially the **Mahanavami** celebrations), and well-maintained infrastructure |
| **Title** | Assumed titles including **Yavana Rajya Sthapanacharya** ("restorer of the Yavana kingdom" — referring to his restoration of the deposed Sultan of Bijapur to the throne) |

### Other Notable Rulers

| Ruler | Key Fact |
|-------|----------|
| **Devaraya II** (1422–1446) | Sangama dynasty; most powerful ruler before Krishnadevaraya; recruited Muslim archers into the army |
| **Aliya Rama Raya** (regent, 1542–1565) | Son-in-law of Krishnadevaraya; de facto ruler; skilled diplomat who played the Deccan Sultanates against each other; this strategy ultimately backfired at Talikota |

### Foreign Travellers at Vijayanagara

| Traveller | Period | Notable Observations |
|-----------|--------|---------------------|
| **Nicolo de' Conti** (Italian) | c. 1420 | Earliest surviving European account; visited during **Devaraya I's** reign; described the city's circumference as ~60 miles with an army of 90,000 men; noted stone-built dwellings, elaborate irrigation channels, and carved temples; his account was recorded in Latin by **Poggio Bracciolini**, the Pope's secretary |
| **Abdur Razzaq** (Persian ambassador) | 1443 | Ambassador of **Shah Rukh** of Persia; visited during **Devaraya II's** reign; described Vijayanagara as a city where "the eye has not seen nor ear heard of any place resembling it upon the whole earth"; noted its **seven concentric fortification walls**; wrote his account in *Matla-us-Sadain wa Majma-ul-Bahrain* |
| **Domingo Paes** (Portuguese) | c. 1520–22 | Most detailed and admiring account — described Krishnadevaraya's court, the **Mahanavami festival**, and the city's prosperity; wrote the *Chronica dos reis de Bisnaga*; praised the king as an able administrator who personally led campaigns |
| **Fernao Nuniz** (Portuguese) | c. 1535–37 (during **Achyutaraya's** reign, not Krishnadevaraya's) | Provided a history of the empire from its founding; chronicled the horse trade with the Portuguese |

### Battle of Talikota / Rakkasa-Tangadi (23 January 1565)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Also called** | Battle of Rakkasa-Tangadi (the actual village where fighting occurred; "Talikota" is the nearby town) |
| **Combatants** | Vijayanagara (under regent **Aliya Rama Raya**) vs a combined alliance of four Deccan Sultanates — **Hussain Nizam Shah I** (Ahmadnagar), **Ali Adil Shah I** (Bijapur), **Ali Barid Shah I** (Bidar), and **Ibrahim Quli Qutb Shah** (Golconda); Berar had already been absorbed by Ahmadnagar |
| **Cause** | Rama Raya's policy of playing sultanates against each other finally united them against Vijayanagara |
| **Turning point** | Vijayanagara forces initially gained the upper hand, but the battle turned when **Muslim commanders (the Gilani brothers)** in Rama Raya's army **defected mid-battle**, switching their artillery and cavalry to the Sultanate side |
| **Result** | Decisive defeat of Vijayanagara; **Rama Raya was captured and beheaded** on the battlefield; his severed head was displayed to break Vijayanagara morale |
| **Aftermath** | Hampi was sacked, looted, and devastated for months; the capital was permanently abandoned; the city's ruins are now the UNESCO World Heritage Site; **Tirumala Deva Raya** (Rama Raya's brother) fled with the treasury to **Penukonda** |

> **Key Distinction:** The Battle of Talikota (1565) did NOT immediately end the Vijayanagara Empire. The **Aravidu dynasty** continued to rule from **Penukonda** and later **Chandragiri** until 1646. UPSC has tested this.

### Administrative System

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Provincial administration** | Empire divided into **Rajyas** (provinces) → **Nadus** (districts) → **Sthalas** (sub-districts) → **Gramas** (villages) |
| **Nayankara system** | Military governors (**Nayakas**) were assigned territories (**Nayankara**) in exchange for military service and revenue collection; similar to the Sultanate's Iqta but more decentralised |
| **Ayagar system** | Village administration through a group of **12 hereditary functionaries** — including the headman, accountant, watchman, potter, washerman, barber, etc.; ensured village self-sufficiency |
| **Economy** | Flourishing trade with Southeast Asia, China, Arabia, and Portugal; major ports: **Mangalore, Honavar, Bhatkal** on the west coast |
| **Currency** | Gold **Varaha** (also called **Pagoda**); silver **Tara**; copper **Jital** |
| **Horse trade** | Imported horses from Arabia and Persia through Portuguese middlemen — essential for cavalry |

## Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1527)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Founded** | **3 August 1347** — during a rebellion of Deccan amirs against **Muhammad bin Tughlaq** |
| **Founder** | **Zafar Khan / Hasan Gangu** (took the title **Alauddin Bahman Shah**); crowned with the support of influential Deccani Chishti Sufi Shaikhs |
| **Capital** | Initially **Gulbarga (Kalaburagi)**; shifted to **Bidar** by **Ahmad Shah I** (1432) |
| **Extent** | At its peak, the Bahmani Sultanate controlled the entire Deccan from coast to coast — from **Dabhol** (west) to **Masulipatnam** (east) |
| **Religion** | Islamic; maintained a cosmopolitan court with Persian, Arab, Turkish, and Indian elements |
| **Rivalry** | Constant warfare with the Vijayanagara Empire — the **Raichur Doab** (land between Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers) was the perennial flashpoint |
| **End** | Last ruler **Kalimullah Shah** (1525–1527) was a puppet of the Barid Shahi governors of Bidar; fled the throne in 1527, formally ending the dynasty |

### Key Bahmani Figures

| Figure | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Mahmud Gawan** (wazir, 1466–1481) | Persian-born scholar and powerful prime minister under **Muhammad Shah III**; reformed the provincial system by dividing the kingdom into **8 tarafs** (provinces) with checks on governors' power; built the famous **Mahmud Gawan Madrasa** at Bidar (a centre of Islamic learning); his centralising reforms threatened the landed aristocracy; **executed in 1481** due to a forged letter fabricated by the Deccani faction — his death is considered the **beginning of the Bahmani decline** |
| **Firoz Shah Bahmani** (r. 1397–1422) | Known for patronage of learning and religious tolerance; said to have spoken multiple languages including Deccani, Persian, Marathi, and Telugu; promoted cultural synthesis |
| **Ahmad Shah I** (r. 1422–1436) | Shifted the capital from Gulbarga to **Bidar**; known as **Ahmad Shah Wali** ("the saint"); expanded the kingdom's territories |

### Factionalism and Decline

The Bahmani court was divided between two rival groups:
- **Deccanis** — locally settled Muslims (Indian-origin); resented foreign dominance of high offices
- **Afaqis/Pardesis** — foreign-origin nobles (Persian, Arab, Turkish immigrants); dominated key administrative and military posts

Mahmud Gawan, himself an Afaqi, attempted to balance these factions through administrative reforms, but his execution in 1481 removed the last stabilising force. Within a decade, the sultanate fragmented into five independent kingdoms.

## Five Deccan Sultanates

| Sultanate | Year of Independence | Capital | Founded by |
|-----------|---------------------|---------|-----------|
| **Ahmadnagar** (Nizam Shahi) | 1490 | Ahmadnagar | Malik Ahmad Nizam Shah |
| **Bijapur** (Adil Shahi) | 1490 | Bijapur | Yusuf Adil Shah |
| **Berar** (Imad Shahi) | 1490 | Ellichpur (Achalpur) | Fathullah Imad Shah |
| **Bidar** (Barid Shahi) | c. 1492 | Bidar | Amir Barid |
| **Golconda** (Qutb Shahi) | 1518 | Golconda (later Hyderabad) | Quli Qutb Shah |

### Notable Deccan Sultanate Highlights

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Bijapur — Gol Gumbaz** | Tomb of **Muhammad Adil Shah** (r. 1627–1656; completed 1656); has the **second-largest unsupported dome in the world** (after St Peter's Basilica, Rome); external diameter ~44 m, internal diameter ~38 m; famous **Whispering Gallery** where sounds echo seven times and whispers carry across 37 metres |
| **Golconda — Diamond trade** | Golconda was the world's premier source of diamonds until the 18th century; the Koh-i-Noor and Hope diamonds reportedly originated from Golconda mines |
| **Golconda — Charminar** | Built in **1591** by **Muhammad Quli Qutb Shah** (5th Qutb Shahi ruler) to mark the founding of **Hyderabad** and the beginning of the second Islamic millennium; designed by Iranian architect **Mir Momin Astrawadi**; the city was planned on a grid with Charminar at its centre |
| **Chand Bibi** | Queen of Bijapur and regent of Ahmadnagar; defended Ahmadnagar against Mughal siege by Akbar's general (1595); ultimately assassinated by her own nobles |
| **Talikota alliance** | The four sultanates (minus Berar, already absorbed by Ahmadnagar) united to defeat Vijayanagara in 1565 |
| **Mughal absorption** | Ahmadnagar conquered by Shah Jahan (1636); Bijapur (1686) and Golconda (1687) by Aurangzeb; Bidar and Berar absorbed earlier |

## Art and Architecture

### Vijayanagara Architectural Legacy

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Style** | Fusion of **Chalukya, Hoysala, and Chola** traditions with some Indo-Islamic elements; characterised by soaring **gopurams** (towered gateways), intricate relief carvings, and monolithic sculptures |
| **Vittala Temple (Hampi)** | Built during **Krishnadevaraya's** reign (begun c. 1513); famous for its iconic **stone chariot** (a shrine in the shape of a chariot) and **56 musical pillars** that produce different notes when tapped |
| **Hazara Rama Temple** | Royal chapel built in the early 15th century during **Devaraya I's** reign; outer walls depict festive processions; inner walls narrate the **Ramayana** in carved friezes; four pillars carved in Hoysala style |
| **Virupaksha Temple** | Dedicated to Lord Shiva; the oldest functioning temple at Hampi; expanded under Devaraya II and Krishnadevaraya; its gopuram dominates the site |
| **Urban planning** | Hampi was laid out with broad avenues, water tanks, marketplaces, and an extensive network of **canals and aqueducts**; temples served as centres of arts, music, festivals, and community life |

### Deccan Sultanate Architecture

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Gol Gumbaz (Bijapur)** | Largest dome in India; second-largest unsupported dome globally; Whispering Gallery (see above) |
| **Charminar (Hyderabad)** | Four-towered gateway monument; symbol of Hyderabad; built 1591 (see above) |
| **Mahmud Gawan Madrasa (Bidar)** | Built c. 1472 by the Bahmani wazir; originally three storeys with lecture halls, a library, and a mosque; decorated with Persian-style coloured tiles; partially destroyed by a gunpowder explosion in the 17th century |
| **Ibrahim Rauza (Bijapur)** | Tomb of **Ibrahim Adil Shah II** (completed c. 1627); considered one of the most **aesthetically refined** Islamic monuments in India; said to have inspired the Taj Mahal |

## Vijayanagara vs Bahmani — Administrative Comparison

| Feature | Vijayanagara Empire | Bahmani Sultanate |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Provincial system** | **Rajyas** (provinces) headed by royal princes or Nayakas | **Tarafs** (provinces) — initially 4, expanded to 8 by Mahmud Gawan |
| **Military land grants** | **Nayankara system** — Nayakas held territories in exchange for military service and revenue | **Iqta-like** grants to provincial governors; centralised by Mahmud Gawan's reforms |
| **Village administration** | **Ayagar system** — 12 hereditary functionaries (headman, accountant, watchman, etc.) | Village headmen (deshmukhs) and revenue collectors (deshpandes); less institutionalised |
| **Revenue** | Land revenue + trade customs; gold **Varaha** (Pagoda) currency | Land revenue based on measurement; influenced by Delhi Sultanate practices |
| **Religious policy** | Hindu state; tolerated other religions; recruited Muslim soldiers | Islamic state; Persian court culture; Deccani-Afaqi factional rivalry shaped governance |
| **Trade orientation** | Strong maritime trade via west-coast ports (Mangalore, Bhatkal, Honavar); horse imports via Portuguese | Overland trade; connections with Persia and Arabia; Dabhol was a key port |
| **Decline trigger** | Battle of Talikota (1565) — combined Sultanate assault | Deccani-Afaqi factionalism; execution of Mahmud Gawan (1481) accelerated break-up |

### Key Exam Distinctions

- The Battle of Talikota (1565) did NOT end the Vijayanagara Empire — the Aravidu dynasty continued until 1646 from Penukonda and Chandragiri.
- The Bahmani Sultanate broke up into five states, not four — Berar was later absorbed by Ahmadnagar before the Battle of Talikota.
- Golconda became independent in 1518 (not 1490 like the other three); it was the last to break away from the Bahmanis.
- Mahmud Gawan was an Afaqi (foreign-origin) noble who tried to curb factional politics — his execution by the Deccani faction hastened the Bahmani decline.
- The Nayankara system is comparable to the Iqta (Delhi Sultanate) and Jagir (Mughal) systems but was more decentralised and linked military service directly to territorial assignment.

## Vocabulary

### Nayankara
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈnɑːjənkɑːrə/
- **Definition:** A system of military land tenure in the Vijayanagara Empire under which commanders (Nayakas) were assigned territories in exchange for maintaining a stipulated number of troops, collecting revenue, and remitting a share to the imperial treasury.
- **Origin:** From Sanskrit *nāyaka* ("leader, military chief") combined with the suffix *-kara* ("office, holding"); the system evolved as the Vijayanagara Empire's method of governing distant provinces through loyal military governors.

### Ayagar
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈɑːjəɡɑːr/
- **Definition:** A system of village administration in the Vijayanagara Empire comprising twelve hereditary functionaries — including the headman, accountant, watchman, and various artisans — who collectively managed local governance in exchange for tax-free land grants.
- **Origin:** From a Kannada-Telugu compound meaning "village servant" or "holder of an office"; the functionaries held hereditary rights over their positions, which could even be sold or mortgaged, reflecting the deeply rooted local self-governance tradition in South India.

### Amara-Nayaka
- **Pronunciation:** /əˈmɑːrə ˈnɑːjəkə/
- **Definition:** A military commander in the Vijayanagara Empire who held a territorial assignment called an *amaram*, with obligations to maintain troops for the king, pay revenue to the imperial treasury, and administer the assigned region.
- **Origin:** From Sanskrit *amara* ("immortal, battle") combined with *nāyaka* ("leader, chief"); the system was influenced by the Delhi Sultanate's *iqta* model but was more decentralised, with Amara-Nayakas functioning as semi-autonomous provincial military governors.

## Key Terms

### Krishna Deva Raya
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈkrɪʃnə ˈdeɪvə ˈrɑːjə/
- **Definition:** The greatest ruler of the Vijayanagara Empire (r. 1509–1529) of the Tuluva dynasty, who defeated the Bijapur Sultanate at the Battle of Raichur (1520), conquered the Gajapati kingdom of Odisha, patronised Telugu literature through his court of Ashtadiggajas (Eight Great Poets), and authored the Telugu epic *Amuktamalyada*.
- **Origin:** A Sanskrit compound name: *Krishna* (कृष्ण, "dark, black," a name of the deity Vishnu), *Deva* (देव, "god, divine"), and *Raya* (a Dravidianised form of Sanskrit *rāja*, "king"); thus "King Krishna Deva."

### Battle of Talikota
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈtɑːlɪkoʊtə/
- **Definition:** The decisive battle fought on 23 January 1565 (also called the Battle of Rakkasa-Tangadi) in which a combined alliance of four Deccan Sultanates — Ahmadnagar, Bijapur, Bidar, and Golconda — defeated the Vijayanagara Empire, resulting in the death of regent Aliya Rama Raya and the destruction of Hampi, though the empire continued under the Aravidu dynasty until 1646.
- **Origin:** Named after the town of Talikota in present-day Bijapur district, Karnataka, near which the battle was fought; *Rakkasa-Tangadi*, the alternative name, refers to the actual villages where the fighting took place.
