## Introduction

The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646 CE) was the **last great Hindu empire of South India**, rising as a bulwark against the southward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate. At its zenith under **Krishnadevaraya**, it dominated most of the Deccan and South India. The contemporary Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1527) and its successor Deccan Sultanates defined the competing Muslim political power in the Deccan. Their interplay — alternating conflict, alliance, and cultural synthesis — is one of the most dynamic chapters of medieval Indian history.

---

## Origins of the Vijayanagara Empire (1336 CE)

### Foundation

- **Founded:** 1336 CE on the southern bank of the **Tungabhadra River** at a site called **Vijayanagara** (City of Victory), in modern-day **Hampi**, Karnataka.
- **Founders:** Brothers **Harihara I (Hakka)** and **Bukka Raya I** — both of the Sangama family, believed to be of the Yadava clan.
- **Earlier service:** Harihara and Bukka had served under the **Kakatiya rulers of Warangal** and later under the Hoysalas before the Delhi Sultanate captured them.
- **Madhava Vidyaranya's role:** The revered sage **Vidyaranya (also called Madhava-Vidyaranya)**, a pontiff of the **Sringeri Sharada Peetham**, is traditionally credited with inspiring and blessing the foundation of the empire. He gave divine sanction to the enterprise and served as its philosopher-guide.
- **Significance:** The empire rose to protect Hindu traditions and resist the Muslim invasions that had devastated the Hoysala, Kakatiya, and Yadava kingdoms.

### Harihara I (1336–1356)

- First ruler; established the empire and consolidated territories in Karnataka and Tamil Nadu.
- Initially converted to Islam while in captivity; reconverted to Hinduism under Vidyaranya's influence.
- Capital: **Vijayanagara (Hampi)**.

## Four Dynasties of Vijayanagara

| Dynasty | Period | Notable Rulers | Key Features |
|---------|--------|----------------|--------------|
| **Sangama** | 1336–1485 | Harihara I, Bukka I, Devaraya I, Devaraya II | Founding dynasty; wars with Bahmani Sultanate; Devaraya II expanded northwards |
| **Saluva** | 1485–1505 | Saluva Narasimha | Short-lived; ended civil wars; administrative reforms |
| **Tuluva** | 1505–1570 | Vira Narasimha, **Krishnadevaraya**, Achyutadevaraya, Sadashivaraya | **Golden Age**; greatest military and cultural achievements |
| **Aravidu** | 1570–1646 | Tirumala, Sriranga I, Venkatapati Raya II | Post-Talikota decline; capital shifted to Penukonda, then Chandragiri |

### Devaraya II (r. 1424–1446) — Sangama Dynasty

- One of the most powerful Sangama rulers; known as **Gajabetekara** (hunter of elephants).
- Repelled Bahmani attacks; controlled most of South India.
- Notable for including **Muslim archers and cavalry** in his army — early example of religious pragmatism.
- The Persian traveller **Abdur Razzaq** visited his court (1443) and left a glowing account of Vijayanagara's prosperity.

## Krishnadevaraya (1509–1529) — The Greatest Ruler

Krishnadevaraya of the **Tuluva dynasty** is universally regarded as the greatest ruler of Vijayanagara — celebrated for military genius, administrative efficiency, and extraordinary patronage of art and literature.

### Military Achievements

- **Defeated** the Bijapur Sultanate (1510) and captured the Raichur Doab — a fertile territory between the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers.
- **Sacked Gulbarga and Bidar** (1512) — penetrated deep into Bahmani territory.
- **Battle of Diwani (1520):** Defeated the combined forces of Bijapur and Bidar; captured Gulbarga.
- **Northern campaigns:** Extended control to Orissa (Kalinga).
- Maintained excellent diplomatic relations with the **Portuguese** (through Goa) — received horses and military technology in exchange for trade privileges.

### Administration Under Krishnadevaraya

- **Centralised administration:** Empire divided into provinces (**Rajya**), districts (**Nadu** or **Sthala**), and villages.
- **Nayankara System:** The backbone of military and provincial administration (see below).
- **Ayagar System:** Village administration by hereditary functionaries (see below).
- Maintained a strong **standing army** with cavalry, infantry, elephants, and war boats.
- Portuguese traveller **Domingo Paes** visited the court (~1520) and described Krishnadevaraya as "the most feared and perfect king," noting the empire's wealth and military strength.

### Literary Achievements

- Krishnadevaraya was himself a **great scholar and poet** in Telugu and Sanskrit.
- Telugu work: **Amuktamalyada** (The Giver of the Worn Garland) — a devotional poem in Telugu about the Andal Alvar saint.
- Sanskrit works: **Jambavati Kalyanam** (a drama) and **Usha Parinayam**.
- He patronised the **Ashtadiggajas** — eight great Telugu poets, including **Allasani Peddana** (the greatest, called **Andhra Kavita Pitamaha** = grandfather of Telugu poetry).
- **Other notable poets:** Tenali Ramakrishna (celebrated for wit), Nandi Thimmana.

### Religious Policy

- Personally a **Vaishnava** (devotee of Vishnu/Krishna) but showed reverence for Shaivism and other traditions.
- Donated generously to both Vaishnava and Shaiva temples.
- Maintained friendly relations with Sufi saints.
- Employed Muslim officials in the army and administration.

## Vijayanagara Administration

### Nayankara System

The **nayankara** (also spelled **nayaka** or **nayankara**) system was the cornerstone of Vijayanagara's provincial and military administration — comparable to European feudalism.

- The ruler granted **amaram** (land rights) to military chiefs called **nayakas** (or nayankara) in exchange for maintaining a stipulated contingent of cavalry, infantry, and elephants for the king's service.
- Nayakas were responsible for: collecting revenue from their territory, maintaining order, and supplying troops when called.
- Nayakas could sub-grant land to lesser chiefs (**amaranayakas**) — creating a pyramid of military obligation.
- They retained a portion of revenue; sent the rest (along with tribute) to the king.
- Nayakas sent **representatives** to the royal court — one overseeing the nayaka army at the capital, one handling the nayaka's affairs at the court.
- **Weakness:** As the empire weakened after Talikota, nayakas became increasingly autonomous, ultimately fragmenting into independent nayak kingdoms (Madurai, Tanjore, Gingee nayakas).

### Ayagar System

The **ayagar** system (also "ayyangar") organised **village administration** through hereditary functionaries.

- Each village had a set of **12 hereditary officials** (Ayagars) who collectively managed village affairs.
- Key village officials: **Karnam** (village accountant), **Reddi/Headman** (revenue collection), **Talaiyari** (village watchman/police), **Prohita** (temple priest), smith, carpenter, potter, washerman, barber.
- Each ayagar received a share of village produce (called **manyam** land) or a portion of village taxes — hereditary compensation for their services.
- **Significance:** The ayagar system ensured stable local governance; it persisted well beyond the Vijayanagara period in South Indian villages.

### Revenue System

- Land revenue was the primary source — usually 1/6th of produce, varying by land type.
- **Customs duties** on trade were significant — Vijayanagara commanded major trade routes.
- **Temple taxes** and fees from bazaars, fairs, and ports.

## Economy and Trade

- **Agriculture:** Rice (in river valleys), millet, cotton; irrigation through tanks (erris) and wells.
- **Horse trade:** Critical weakness — South India did not breed quality war horses; horses imported from Arabia and Central Asia via the Persian Gulf and Malabar coast. The Portuguese controlled this trade after capturing Goa (1510).
- **Portuguese alliance:** Krishnadevaraya cultivated Portuguese friendship for access to horses and gunpowder weapons. Portuguese had a factory at Vijayanagara.
- **Temple towns:** Major temples were also economic hubs — bazaars around temples generated revenue; pilgrimage brought commerce.
- **Textile and spice trade:** Exported cotton textiles, spices, rice; imported horses, elephants, precious metals, and luxury goods.
- **Abdur Razzaq's account (1443):** Described Vijayanagara as the most prosperous city he had ever seen — seven concentric walls, bazaars full of goods, a thriving population.
- **Domingo Paes (~1520):** Described Krishnadevaraya's capital as "as large as Rome, very beautiful to the sight."

## Vijayanagara Architecture and Culture

### Vijayanagara Style

Vijayanagara architecture is a synthesis of three styles: **Chalukyan/Hoysala** (Deccan), **Dravidian** (South Indian), and **Indo-Islamic** (for secular buildings). Key characteristics:
- **Gopurams (gateway towers):** Very tall, elaborately carved — a Dravidian feature pushed to new heights.
- **Mandapas (halls):** Elaborate pillared halls with intricate carvings, including the celebrated **musical pillars**.
- **Kalyana mandapa (marriage hall):** A feature specific to Vijayanagara temple complexes.
- **Secular buildings:** Indo-Islamic influences seen in the **Lotus Mahal**, **Elephant Stables**, and **Queen's Bath** at Hampi.
- **Hazara Rama Temple:** Royal temple at Hampi; walls covered with friezes depicting the Ramayana story — remarkable narrative sculpture.

### Hampi — UNESCO World Heritage Site

- **Hampi** (in present-day Vijayanagara district, Karnataka) was the capital of the empire from 1336 to 1565 CE.
- **Inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1986** (as "Group of Monuments at Hampi").
- Over **1,600 surviving remains** spread across **4,100 hectares**.
- UNESCO describes it as "an austere, grandiose site" representing the last great Hindu kingdom.

**Key monuments at Hampi:**

| Monument | Significance |
|----------|--------------|
| **Vittala Temple** | Finest example of Vijayanagara architecture; **56 musical pillars** (emit musical notes when struck); iconic **stone chariot** in the courtyard |
| **Virupaksha Temple** | Oldest active temple; dedicated to Shiva; predates the empire; still in worship |
| **Hazara Rama Temple** | Royal chapel; walls covered with bas-relief Ramayana narrative |
| **Lotus Mahal** | Elegantly designed pavilion showing Indo-Islamic synthesis |
| **Elephant Stables** | 11 chambers for royal elephants; shows Islamic architectural influence |
| **Queen's Bath** | Royal bathing complex; arched corridors, balconies over the bath |
| **Matanga Hill** | Overlooks the entire ruins; sunset views |

### Literature

- **Telugu literature:** Reached its zenith — known as **Prabandha (classical composition) age**. Ashtadiggajas were the eight jewels of Krishnadevaraya's court.
- **Kannada literature:** Equally patronised; **Kumara Vyasa** (15th century) wrote the **Gadugina Bharata** (Kumara Vyasa Bharata) in Kannada.
- **Sanskrit:** Royal patronage maintained Sanskrit scholarship; commentaries on Vedanta.
- **Music:** Carnatic music tradition strengthened; the Ashtadiggajas were also musicians.

## Battle of Talikota (1565 CE)

Also known as the **Battle of Rakshasi-Tangadi** (after the actual battlefield location near Rakshasi-Tangadi villages, about 70 km from Talikota), this was a **watershed battle** that shattered the Vijayanagara Empire.

### Background

- By the mid-16th century, **Aliya Rama Raya** (son-in-law of Krishnadevaraya) was the de facto ruler — he controlled the weak king Sadashivaraya.
- Rama Raya was an aggressive and manipulative politician who played the Deccan Sultanates against each other — at times allying with Bijapur against Ahmadnagar, then with Ahmadnagar against Bijapur.
- His **insulting treatment of the Sultans** — forcing their ambassadors to stand in his presence, addressing Sultans dismissively — created deep personal animosity.
- In response, the **four Deccan Sultanates** — **Bijapur, Ahmadnagar, Golconda, and Bidar** — formed an unprecedented coalition against Vijayanagara. Berar was not involved.

### The Battle — January 23, 1565

- Coalition forces: Approximately 80,000–1,00,000 on each side (estimates vary widely in sources).
- Initially, the Vijayanagara army performed well.
- **Decisive moment:** Two Muslim commanders on the Vijayanagara side — Ibrahim Khan and Gilani Khan — **defected to the Sultanate coalition** during the battle, creating panic and chaos in Vijayanagara's ranks.
- **Rama Raya was captured and beheaded** on the battlefield by the Sultan of Ahmadnagar.
- The Vijayanagara army collapsed and fled.

### Aftermath and Decline

- The coalition forces **looted and burnt Hampi** for several months — the city was never resettled; it became the ruins visible today.
- **Tirumala** (Rama Raya's brother) escaped with surviving troops and the treasury, shifting the capital eventually to **Penukonda** (later Chandragiri).
- The Aravidu dynasty continued nominally until ~1646 CE, but the empire was a shadow of its former self.
- **Nayak kingdoms became independent:** The Nayakas of Madurai, Tanjore, Gingee, and Ikkeri broke away, establishing their own kingdoms.
- **Significance:** The Battle of Talikota permanently ended Vijayanagara's dominance in South India; it marked the end of the last major Hindu imperial power.

## Bahmani Sultanate (1347–1527)

### Foundation

- **Founded:** 1347 CE.
- **Founder:** **Zafar Khan** (took the title **Alauddin Hasan Bahman Shah**) — also known as **Hasan Gangu**. He was a governor under Muhammad bin Tughlaq who rebelled when the Sultan's authority weakened.
- **Capital:** First at **Gulbarga (Ahsanabad)**; later shifted to **Bidar (1425)** under Alauddin Shah II.
- The Sultanate controlled much of the Deccan plateau — roughly modern Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh.

### Chronology and Rulers

| Phase | Key Ruler | Significance |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| **Early** (1347–1422) | Alauddin Hasan, Mujahid Shah, Firuz Shah | Constant wars with Vijayanagara over Raichur Doab and Tungabhadra region |
| **Peak** (1422–1481) | Ahmad Shah (capital to Bidar), **Muhammad Shah III** | Period of Mahmud Gawan's power |
| **Decline** (1481–1527) | Multiple weak rulers | Fragmentation into 5 Deccan Sultanates |

### Mahmud Gawan — Prime Minister and Scholar

**Mahmud Gawan** (c. 1411–1481) was the greatest minister of the Bahmani Sultanate — a Persian immigrant who rose to become the Prime Minister (Wazir-ul-Mamalik) and dominated the Sultanate under Muhammad Shah III.

- **Administrative reforms:** Divided the Sultanate into more provinces (tarafs) to reduce power of provincial governors; revised tax collection; reformed the army.
- **Scholar and patron:** Founded a magnificent **Madrasa (theological college)** at **Bidar** (one of the finest in the medieval world); wrote poetry in Persian; maintained correspondence with scholars across the Islamic world.
- **Fate:** Executed in 1481 on false charges of treason — a catastrophic blunder by the Sultan that removed the only force holding the Sultanate together.

### Relations with Vijayanagara

- Constant warfare over the **Raichur Doab** (fertile land between Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers) — changed hands repeatedly.
- Religion was secondary; both Sultanates and Vijayanagara employed soldiers and officials of the other religion.
- Cultural exchange was significant — Persian literary influence, architectural fusion.

---

## The Five Deccan Sultanates (Post-1490)

After the death of Mahmud Gawan (1481) and the weakening of central authority, **five provincial governors declared independence**:

| Sultanate | Capital | Founded | Founder | Notable |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Ahmadnagar (Nizam Shahi)** | Ahmadnagar | 1490 | Malik Ahmad Bahri | First to declare independence; later incorporated into Mughal Empire (1636) |
| **Bidar (Barid Shahi)** | Bidar | ~1492 | Qasim Barid (Amir Barid I) | Smallest sultanate; last to fall |
| **Bijapur (Adil Shahi)** | Bijapur | 1490 | Yusuf Adil Shah | Famous for **Gol Gumbaz**; great architectural patronage |
| **Berar (Imad Shahi)** | Ellichpur | 1490 | Fatullah Imad-ul-Mulk | Absorbed by Ahmadnagar in 1574 |
| **Golconda (Qutb Shahi)** | Golconda/Hyderabad | 1518 | Quli Qutb-ul-Mulk | Famous for diamonds (Golconda mines), Charminar (1591) |

### Alliance Against Vijayanagara (1565)

Despite their mutual rivalries, the four major Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur, Ahmadnagar, Golconda, Bidar) united **temporarily** against Vijayanagara — their shared grievance against Rama Raya's provocations overcoming their conflicts. After Talikota, they resumed fighting each other and eventually fell to the Mughals (Ahmadnagar 1636, Bijapur 1686, Golconda 1687).

## Bijapur: Architecture and Gol Gumbaz

**Bijapur (modern Vijayapura, Karnataka)** was the capital of the Adil Shahi Sultanate and became a major centre of Deccani art and architecture.

### Gol Gumbaz (1626–1656)

- **Mausoleum of Muhammad Adil Shah** (died 1656); built by his architect Yaqut of Dabhol.
- Contains the second-largest dome in the world (after St. Peter's Basilica, Rome) — outer diameter approximately **38 metres**.
- The **Whispering Gallery**: a gallery inside the base of the dome with remarkable acoustic properties — a whisper on one side can be heard clearly on the opposite side.
- The dome rests on **8 intersecting arches** — an engineering marvel.
- The mausoleum stands on a 66.4-metre square plinth.

### Ibrahim Rauza

- Mausoleum of Ibrahim Adil Shah II (r. 1580–1627).
- Considered more aesthetically refined than Gol Gumbaz; known as the "Taj Mahal of the Deccan" by some scholars.
- Features intricate stone jali (lattice) work.

## Persian and Syncretic Cultural Influences in the Deccan

The Deccan Sultanates were a crucible of cultural synthesis — **Persian, Turkic, Indian Hindu, and local South Indian** traditions merged.

### Persian Influence

- **Court language:** Persian was the official language; administrative records, poetry, and chronicles were in Persian.
- **Literature:** The Deccan Sultanates patronised Persian poetry; Bijapur's Ibrahim Adil Shah II (nicknamed **Jagad Guru** — teacher of the world) wrote Kitab-e-Nauras, a book of songs in a mixed idiom of Persian, Sanskrit, and Dakhni Urdu.
- **Architecture:** Persian arches, domes, minarets, and garden layouts (charbagh) combined with Indian decorative traditions.

### Dakhni Urdu (Deccani)

- The Deccan Sultanates were the first major patrons of **Urdu literature** — known then as "Dakhni" or "Hindi."
- Earliest Urdu poetry written in the Deccan courts (Golconda, Bijapur) — predating the North Indian Urdu tradition by decades.
- **Nizami** and **Wajhi** were notable Dakhni Urdu poets at Golconda.

### Sufism in the Deccan

- The **Chishti, Qadiri, and Shattari** Sufi orders were active in the Deccan.
- **Khwaja Banda Nawaz Gesudaraz** (died 1422): The most celebrated Sufi of the Deccan; buried at Gulbarga; his shrine is a major pilgrimage site.
- Sufis promoted syncretic traditions — worshipped by both Muslims and Hindus; bridged communities.

## Comparison: Vijayanagara vs Bahmani/Deccan Sultanates

| Aspect | Vijayanagara | Deccan Sultanates |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Religion** | Hindu (Vaishnava, Shaiva) — but employed Muslims | Muslim (Sunni/Shia) — but employed Hindus |
| **Official language** | Telugu, Kannada, Sanskrit | Persian (official), Dakhni Urdu |
| **Architecture** | Dravidian + Indo-Islamic secular buildings | Persian + Deccani synthesis; massive domes |
| **Military** | Nayankara system; employed Muslim cavalry and archers | Regular army; employed Hindu infantry |
| **Trade** | Portuguese horse trade; temple towns | Inland and coastal; diamond trade (Golconda) |
| **Decline** | Battle of Talikota (1565) | Mughal conquest (Ahmadnagar 1636, Bijapur 1686, Golconda 1687) |

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Know the four dynasties in order: **Sangama → Saluva → Tuluva → Aravidu** (mnemonic: **S**ome **S**tudents **T**ry **A**ll).
- Krishnadevaraya = Tuluva dynasty, 1509–1529, greatest ruler.
- Battle of Talikota = **1565 CE** (also called Rakshasi-Tangadi).
- Hampi = UNESCO WHS **1986**; Vittala Temple = **56 musical pillars**.
- Bahmani Sultanate founded **1347** by Alauddin Hasan Bahman Shah (Hasan Gangu).
- Five Deccan Sultanates: Bijapur, Golconda, Ahmadnagar, Bidar, Berar (Berar first absorbed by Ahmadnagar in 1574).
- Gol Gumbaz = Bijapur = Muhammad Adil Shah's tomb = second-largest dome.

**For Mains:**
- For Vijayanagara administration: always cover **both Nayankara** (provincial/military) **and Ayagar** (village level) systems, showing how they complemented each other.
- For Krishnadevaraya: cover at least three dimensions: military (Raichur Doab, Portuguese relations), literary (Amuktamalyada, Ashtadiggajas), administrative.
- For Battle of Talikota: explain **why** the Sultanates united (Rama Raya's provocations + defection of Muslim commanders within Vijayanagara army = internal weakness).
- **Syncretic culture** is a key UPSC theme — use the Deccan Sultanates as prime examples: Dakhni Urdu, Sufi shrines, Ibrahim Adil Shah II's Kitab-e-Nauras.
- Hampi's ruins are a powerful current-affairs peg — UNESCO heritage, conservation issues, Karnataka's tourism.
- **Abdur Razzaq (1443)** and **Domingo Paes (~1520)** are important foreign travellers who described Vijayanagara — cite them in Mains answers for factual grounding.
