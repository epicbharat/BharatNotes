## Overview

Folk theatre and traditional performing arts constitute the living cultural heritage of India — rooted in community life, religious ritual, and local languages. Unlike classical arts with formalised grammar, folk traditions are fluid, participatory, and regionally distinct. This topic is examined in GS1 (Indian Heritage and Culture) and may appear in Essay.

---

## Folk Theatre: Concept and Social Functions

**Folk theatre** (also called traditional or rural theatre) is community-based performing art practised across India, characterised by:
- **Religious and ritual origins** — performances at temple festivals, seasonal fairs, lifecycle events
- **Open-air performance** — in village squares, temple courtyards, or makeshift stages
- **Audience participation** — spectators integral to performance, not passive consumers
- **Blend of forms** — drama, music, dance, humour, satire, and devotion combined
- **Oral tradition** — scripts transmitted orally and adapted for each performance context

Folk theatre served as pre-modern mass communication for communities without print literacy — conveying mythology, social critique, and political commentary.

## Major Folk Theatre Forms by Region

### North India

**Nautanki — Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan**

- **Origin:** Developed primarily in **Uttar Pradesh** (Kanpur, Hathras, Mathura belt) and Rajasthan; flourished in the 19th and early 20th centuries
- **Character:** Operatic folk drama — highly musical, with extensive use of sung dialogue; themes from mythology, romance, history, and social issues
- **Music:** Accompanied by nagara (kettledrum), harmonium, sarangi, and cymbals
- **Themes:** Stories of Laila-Majnu, Heer-Ranjha, Amar Singh Rathore, episodes from Ramayana and Mahabharata
- **Style:** Originally performed only by male artists; female roles played by men; gender integration came later
- **Influence:** Was influenced by Parsi theatre in the 20th century, incorporating its stage techniques

**Swang — Haryana and Rajasthan**

- Similar to Nautanki; music-dominant folk drama form
- Themes: Episodes of saints, mythology, social satire
- Key regions: Rohtak district of Haryana

**Ramlila — North India (especially Varanasi and Rampur)**

- Dramatisation of Tulsidas's **Ramcharitmanas** during Navratri/Dussehra
- **Ramnagar Ramlila** (Varanasi) is particularly famous — enacted over 31 days on different open grounds; patronised by Maharaja of Kashi
- **Ramlila of Rampur** and several other varieties are UNESCO-recognised
- **UNESCO ICH:** Ramlila — the traditional performance of Ramcharitmanas — inscribed on the **UNESCO Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2008**

### West India

**Tamasha — Maharashtra**

- **Origin:** Evolved from folk forms — **Gondhal**, **Jagran**, and **Kirtan** — and reached prominence in the 17th–18th century, associated with Shahir (folk poet) tradition
- **Character:** Combines music (lavni), dance, and drama; known for **Lavni** — a rhythmic, sensuous dance form integral to Tamasha
- **Performers:** Traditionally performed by women (called **muralis** or **kolhatis**) — unlike North Indian forms where women's roles were played by men
- **Shahirs:** Poet-performers who compose and sing satirical, romantic, or devotional songs; notable Shahirs — Honaji Bala, Patthe Bapurao, Annabhau Sathe
- **Themes:** Romantic, historical, mythological, and often sharp political/social satire
- **Structure:** Begins with a **Gan** (prayer to Ganesha), followed by **Gaulan** (devotional segment), then the main play

**Bhavai — Gujarat and Rajasthan**

- **Origin:** Traditionally associated with the **Thakar** community; linked to worship of Goddess Amba (Bhavai = offering to Amba)
- **Character:** Acrobatic, satirical, and richly comic; social satire on caste, priests, merchants, and social hypocrisies
- **Structure:** Series of loosely connected plays called **Vesha** (characters/sketches) — over 360 traditional Veshas exist
- **Performance:** Often involves acrobatics — balancing pots on head, walking on stilts, performing stunts
- **Notable feature:** A single multi-role performer (Ranganayak) can play multiple characters in a single performance

### East India

**Jatra — West Bengal and Odisha**

- **Origin:** The word "Jatra" means journey or procession; originated as devotional processional theatre, particularly associated with Vaishnava bhakti (Krishna worship) in Bengal
- **Historical patronage:** Strong patronage during Bengal Renaissance; Jatra became vehicle for patriotic themes under colonial rule — companies performed plays on freedom movement
- **Character:** Highly operatic — extensive use of music and song; dramatic acting style (more stylised than naturalistic)
- **Structure:** All-night performances traditionally; mobile stage with audience seated on all sides (**arena staging**)
- **Modern Jatra:** Commercial industry in Bengal with large production companies; mix of mythological, historical, and contemporary social themes
- **Notable figures:** Mukunda Das (patriotic Jatra playwright); Sisir Kumar Bhaduri

**Gambhira — West Bengal (Malda)**

- Mask dance dedicated to Shiva (as Gambhira — the benevolent); performed during Gajan festival
- Satirical commentary on social issues through dialogue between grandfather and grandson characters

### South India

**Yakshagana — Karnataka (Coastal Karnataka)**

- **Origin:** Developed in **coastal Karnataka** (Dakshina Kannada, Udupi, Uttara Kannada) from the 16th century
- **Character:** All-night theatrical performance combining elaborate costume, stylised dance, music (mridanga, chande — cylindrical drum), and improvised dialogue
- **Language:** Mix of Kannada and Tulu; stories drawn from Hindu epics (Ramayana, Mahabharata, Puranas)
- **Costume:** Elaborate and distinctive — towering headgear (kirita), face paint, and jewellery; different character types (Sattvik, Rajas, Tamas) have specific costume styles
- **Performance:** Traditionally performed from dusk to dawn (**all-night**); performed in open mandap or open ground
- **Organisation:** Performed by professional troupes called **melas** or **bayalata** companies; Yakshagana also has a more refined indoor form called **Tenkutittu**

**Therukoothu / Therukuthu — Tamil Nadu**

- **Origin:** The word means "street play" (Theru = street, Koothu = play)
- **Character:** Open-air folk theatre; performed on streets or temple grounds, particularly during annual **Mariamman** and other temple festivals
- **Themes:** Primarily based on **Mahabharata** — stories of Draupadi's disrobing, Karna's death, Abhimanyu's death; also Ramayana and Puranic stories
- **Performance:** Traditionally all-night; performers in elaborate costumes with large face paint; highly stylised body movement
- **Community:** Traditionally associated with specific caste communities but becoming more open

**Krishnanattam — Kerala**

- Created in the 17th century by **Zamorin of Calicut (Manaveda)** — a series of 8 plays based on Krishna's life (from birth to departure from earth)
- Performed exclusively at the **Guruvayur Temple**, Kerala
- Considered the precursor to Kathakali — shares its elaborate costume and makeup tradition

**Koodiyattam (Kutiyattam) — Kerala**

- **Oldest surviving** classical form of Sanskrit theatre in the world
- Performed in temple theatres called **Koothambalams**
- Language: Sanskrit dialogue with Prakrit and Malayalam
- Training: 10–15 years of rigorous training; sophisticated eye-expression (neta abhinaya) and hand gesture (hasta abhinaya) system
- **UNESCO ICH:** Inscribed on the **UNESCO Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2008** (originally proclaimed a Masterpiece in 2001)

### Northeast India

**Ankiya Nat (Bhaona) — Assam**

- **Creator:** **Srimanta Sankardeva** (c. 1449–1568 CE) — saint-poet-reformer; created the form as part of his **Eka Sharana Nama Dharma** (Neo-Vaishnavite) movement
- **Character:** One-act plays (Ankiya Nat = single-act play in Assamese); themes from Bhagavata Purana, Ramayana
- **Performance:** Enacted as **Bhaona** in the namghars (prayer halls) of **Sattras** (Vaishnavite monasteries)
- **Language:** Written in Vrajawali (a literary language mixing Brajabhosha and Assamese)
- **Sutradhara:** A narrator-manager who remains on stage throughout, linking scenes and explaining action
- **First play:** **Chihna Yatra** — Sankardeva's first dramatic composition; an outdoor spectacle

## Puppetry Traditions of India

Puppetry is one of India's oldest performing arts, with traditions spanning over 2,000 years. Indian puppetry is classified into four types based on the mechanism of control:

### 1. String Puppets (Marionettes)

| Form | State | Key Features |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Kathputli** | Rajasthan | Most famous Indian puppetry; colourfully dressed wooden puppets; often no lower body; controlled by single string attached to head; stories of Rajput heroes and folklore |
| **Gombeyatta** | Karnataka | Jointed wooden puppets; more articulated than Kathputli; themes from Puranas and folk stories |
| **Kundhei** | Odisha | Lightweight wooden puppets; themes from Mahabharata and Ramayana |
| **Bommalattam** | Tamil Nadu | Heaviest string puppets in India; also use rods; technically demanding |

### 2. Shadow Puppets

| Form | State | Key Features |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Tholu Bommalata** | Andhra Pradesh | Transparent/translucent leather puppets; large, colourful, multi-jointed; stories from Ramayana and Mahabharata; also called **Tholu Bommalatam** |
| **Togalu Gombeyaatta** | Karnataka | Leather puppets; smaller and less colourful than Tholu Bommalata; multiple size categories representing different character types |
| **Tholpavakoothu** | Kerala | Leather shadow puppets used exclusively for enacting Kamba Ramayana (Tamil Ramayana); performed at Bhagavati temples |
| **Bommalattam** (shadow version) | Tamil Nadu | Overlap with string puppets; some shadow puppet tradition also present |

### 3. Rod Puppets

| Form | State | Key Features |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Putul Nautch / Darer Putul** | West Bengal | Wooden rod puppets; themes from Mahabharata, Ramayana, and Bengal folklore; controlled by rods attached to neck and hands |
| **Kathi Kundhei** | Odisha | Rod puppets; related to Bengal tradition |

### 4. Glove Puppets

| Form | State | Key Features |
|------|-------|-------------|
| **Pavakathakali** | Kerala | Glove puppets designed and dressed exactly like Kathakali performers; stories from Mahabharata; highly artistic |
| **Sakhi Kundhei** | Odisha | Glove puppets in Odisha |

## Institutional Framework for Performing Arts

### Sangeet Natak Akademi

- Established: **1952** — India's first national academy of the arts
- Function: Supports and promotes performing arts including music, dance, theatre, and folk arts
- **Sangeet Natak Akademi Award:** Given annually for outstanding contribution to Indian performing arts
- **Sangeet Natak Akademi Fellowship (Ratna Sadasya):** Highest honour — given to outstanding artists
- Documentation role: Archives folk theatre traditions, recordings, and manuscripts

### Zonal Cultural Centres (ZCCs)

**7 Zonal Cultural Centres** established by the Ministry of Culture to preserve and promote regional cultural heritage:

| ZCC | Headquarters |
|-----|-------------|
| North Zone | Patiala |
| South Zone | Thanjavur |
| East Zone | Kolkata |
| West Zone | Udaipur |
| North-East Zone | Dimapur |
| North-Central Zone | Allahabad (Prayagraj) |
| South-Central Zone | Nagpur |

ZCCs organise folk festivals, folk theatre competitions, and provide financial support to folk artists.

### National School of Drama (NSD)

- Established: **1959** (autonomous under Ministry of Culture from 1975)
- New Delhi
- India's premier institution for training in theatre arts
- Organises **Bharat Rang Mahotsav** — India's largest international theatre festival

### UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage (ICH): India

India has **15 elements** inscribed on UNESCO's ICH Lists, including performing arts:

| Element | Inscription Year |
|---------|----------------|
| Ramlila — traditional performance of Ramcharitmanas | 2008 |
| Koodiyattam (Kutiyattam), Sanskrit theatre | 2008 |
| Vedic chanting | 2008 (proclaimed 2003) |
| Mudiyettu, ritual theatre of Kerala | 2010 |
| Chhau dance | 2010 |
| Sankirtana, ritual singing of Manipur | 2013 |
| Kalbelia folk songs and dances of Rajasthan | 2010 |

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Memorise form → state → distinctive feature (the table above is your cheat sheet)
- Koodiyattam: Kerala, UNESCO ICH 2008, Sanskrit drama, oldest surviving
- Ramlila: UNESCO ICH 2008, Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas
- Sangeet Natak Akademi: established 1952
- Puppetry: Kathputli (Rajasthan, string), Tholu Bommalata (AP, shadow), Pavakathakali (Kerala, glove), Putul Nautch (West Bengal, rod)
- Ankiya Nat: Assam, Srimanta Sankardeva (1449–1568), Neo-Vaishnavite

**For Mains:**
- Structure answer: overview → form by region → institutional support → UNESCO recognition
- Distinguish between state-specific features — examiners test specificity
- Relevance of folk theatre: social cohesion, oral history, religious syncretism, democratic expression in pre-literate communities
- Threats: urbanisation, commercial cinema, OTT, migration of rural performers, loss of patronage
- Government support: ZCCs, Sangeet Natak Akademi, UNESCO nominations, Kala Samman awards

**Essay connection:** Indian folk theatre as a medium of democracy, social critique, and cultural continuity.
