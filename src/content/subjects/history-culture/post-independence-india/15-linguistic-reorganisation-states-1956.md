## Introduction

At independence in 1947, India inherited an internal administrative map drawn by British colonial convenience — provinces and princely states whose boundaries ignored the linguistic and cultural identities of the people living within them. The demand to redraw these boundaries along linguistic lines had been part of the Congress movement since 1920. Resolving it took three decades, multiple commissions, a martyr's sacrifice, and periodic political crises — from the creation of Andhra in 1953 to the formation of Telangana in 2014. The reorganisation of states on linguistic lines remains the single most consequential restructuring of India's federal map after partition.

---

## Background — The Demand During Independence

**1920:** The Indian National Congress reorganised its own provincial units along linguistic lines at its **Nagpur Session**, creating Pradesh Congress Committees (PCCs) that followed linguistic boundaries — an implicit endorsement of the linguistic principle for future state formation.

**1927–1945:** Multiple Congress resolutions affirmed the linguistic principle. The demand intensified as independence approached, with Andhra (Telugu speakers in Madras Presidency), Karnataka (Kannada speakers), and Maharashtra (Marathi speakers in Bombay Province) being the most vocal.

**Post-independence hesitation:** The Constituent Assembly leadership — Nehru in particular — was cautious. The wounds of Partition were fresh; there was fear that reorganisation along linguistic or cultural lines would encourage secessionism and further fragmentation. The **Dar Commission (S.K. Dhar, 1948)** and the **JVP Committee (Jawaharlal Nehru, Vallabhbhai Patel, Pattabhi Sitaramayya, 1949)** both recommended against immediate reorganisation on linguistic grounds, preferring administrative convenience.

## Potti Sreeramulu — The Martyr of the Telugu Cause

### The Fast Unto Death

**Potti Sreeramulu** (born 16 March 1901) was a Gandhian activist and a Congress veteran from Andhra. He had previously undertaken fasts for various causes, including the entry of Harijans into temples. In 1952, as demands for a separate Andhra State grew frustrated by Government inaction, Sreeramulu began a **fast unto death on 19 October 1952**, demanding a separate Telugu-speaking state carved out of the Madras Presidency.

Despite his deteriorating health, the Government did not act. Sreeramulu died after **58 days of fasting, on the night of 15–16 December 1952**. His death sparked widespread riots across Andhra. The scale of public outrage left the Government with no choice.

### The Government's Response

On **19 December 1952**, Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru announced that a separate Andhra State would be formed. On **1 October 1953**, the **State of Andhra** was inaugurated, carved out of the northern Telugu-speaking districts of Madras Presidency, with its capital at **Kurnool**. It was India's first state created on a purely linguistic basis.

Sreeramulu's sacrifice set a precedent that linguistic demands, backed by sufficient popular pressure, would be accommodated within the constitutional framework — and compelled the Government to appoint a formal commission to deal with the issue systematically.

## States Reorganisation Commission (1953)

### Constitution and Membership

In December 1953, the Government appointed the **States Reorganisation Commission (SRC)**, chaired by retired Supreme Court judge **Justice Syed Fazl Ali**, with two other members:
- **Hriday Nath Kunzru** — politician and social reformer
- **Kavalam Madhava Panikkar** — diplomat, historian, and writer

### Report (1955)

The SRC submitted its report on **30 September 1955**. Key recommendations:
- Redraw state boundaries primarily along linguistic lines, while considering factors of administrative viability, financial self-sufficiency, and national unity.
- Create a unified state for Kannada speakers (Karnataka) and merge Telangana with Andhra.
- The commission opposed a separate Vidarbha and a separate Maharashtra-Gujarat bifurcation at that stage.

The report was a landmark: it acknowledged the linguistic principle as the **primary organising basis** for state formation in India, while placing limits on extreme fragmentation.

---

## States Reorganisation Act, 1956

The **States Reorganisation Act, 1956** came into force on **1 November 1956**. It abolished the complex Part A, B, C, and D state classifications inherited from the Constitution and created a uniform system of States and Union Territories.

### Outcome

The Act reorganised India into **14 States and 6 Union Territories**.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| New states created | Andhra Pradesh (merged Andhra + Telangana), Kerala, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Maharashtra + Gujarat (still combined as Bombay State) |
| Part C states abolished | Converted to Union Territories (Himachal Pradesh, Manipur, Tripura, etc.) |
| Principle | Primarily linguistic, with administrative and security considerations |
| Effective date | 1 November 1956 |

November 1 is celebrated as **Rajyotsava Day** in Karnataka (and as a formation anniversary in other states created by the SRA 1956).

## Post-1956 Reorganisations

### Maharashtra and Gujarat — 1960

The SRC had recommended keeping Marathi-speaking and Gujarati-speaking areas within a unified Bombay State, which sparked intense agitation — particularly in Maharashtra, where the **Samyukta Maharashtra movement** demanded a separate Marathi state with Mumbai as its capital. After years of agitation, the **Bombay Reorganisation Act, 1960** bifurcated Bombay State:
- **Maharashtra** (Marathi-speaking) — effective **1 May 1960**
- **Gujarat** (Gujarati-speaking) — effective **1 May 1960**

Mumbai (Bombay) became the capital of Maharashtra.

### Punjab, Haryana, and Himachal Pradesh — 1966

Following demands from both Sikh Punjabi-speaking communities (for a Punjabi Suba) and Hindi-speaking communities in Punjab, the **Punjab Reorganisation Act, 1966** was enacted. It came into effect on **1 November 1966**, creating:
- **Punjab** — Punjabi-speaking, predominantly Sikh
- **Haryana** — Hindi-speaking
- **Himachal Pradesh** — hill districts (later upgraded to full state in 1971)

Chandigarh was made a **Union Territory** and the shared capital of both Punjab and Haryana.

### Telangana — 2014

The demand for a separate Telangana state from the merged Andhra Pradesh (1956) was decades old, rooted in grievances about resource distribution, employment, and cultural distinctiveness. The movement intensified from 2009 onward.

The **Andhra Pradesh Reorganisation Act, 2014** was passed by Lok Sabha on **18 February 2014** and by Rajya Sabha on **20 February 2014**, receiving Presidential assent on **1 March 2014**. On **2 June 2014**, the State of **Telangana** became India's 28th state, with **Hyderabad** as its capital (shared with residual Andhra Pradesh for a transitional period).

## Language and Nationhood — The Debate

Linguistic reorganisation raised fundamental questions about the relationship between language, identity, and national unity.

| Argument for linguistic reorganisation | Argument against |
|---|---|
| Democracy requires administration in the people's language | Might encourage regionalism, fissiparousness, linguistic chauvinism |
| Cultural identity and linguistic identity are inseparable | Language-based states may conflict — Belgaum/Belagavi (Karnataka-Maharashtra border dispute) |
| Governance in the mother tongue is more accessible and accountable | Federal asymmetries — large states (UP, Maharashtra) vs. small states (Goa, Sikkim) |
| Congress itself organised on linguistic lines since 1920 | Economic viability varies dramatically across linguistic states |

**The verdict of history:** India's linguistic reorganisation has, on balance, strengthened democracy and governance by bringing the state closer to the people in their own languages. The fears of fragmentation proved largely unfounded — regional identity and national identity coexisted and were both accommodated within the constitutional framework.
