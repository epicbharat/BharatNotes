## Introduction

Gender justice refers to the fair treatment of women and men in law, policy, and social practice. Despite significant constitutional guarantees, India continues to grapple with domestic violence, workplace harassment, child sexual abuse, discriminatory personal laws, and structural inequality. This chapter traces India's evolving gender justice framework — from constitutional provisions to landmark legislation and judicial pronouncements — all critical for UPSC GS Papers I and II.

## Constitutional Provisions for Women's Rights

| Article | Provision |
|---------|-----------|
| **Article 14** | Right to equality before law and equal protection of laws |
| **Article 15(1)** | State shall not discriminate on grounds of sex |
| **Article 15(3)** | State may make special provisions for women and children (protective discrimination) |
| **Article 16(2)** | No discrimination in public employment on grounds of sex |
| **Article 39(a)** | State to direct policy towards equal right to livelihood for men and women |
| **Article 39(d)** | Equal pay for equal work for both men and women |
| **Article 42** | State to make provision for just and humane conditions of work and maternity relief |
| **Article 51A(e)** | Fundamental Duty — to renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women |

## Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (PWDVA)

### Background

Before PWDVA 2005, women experiencing domestic violence had limited recourse — only criminal remedies under **Section 498A IPC** (cruelty by husband/relatives), which was often inadequate as it did not provide for civil protection, residence rights, or maintenance.

### Key Features of PWDVA 2005

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Nature** | Civil law — primarily provides protection orders, not criminal punishment |
| **Scope** | Covers physical, sexual, verbal/emotional, and economic abuse |
| **Applicability** | Wife, female live-in partner, sisters, mothers, daughters living in shared household |
| **Respondent** | Husband, male live-in partner, his relatives |
| **Remedies available** | Protection orders, residence orders, monetary relief, custody orders, compensation orders |
| **Shared household right** | Woman has right to reside in shared household regardless of title/ownership |
| **Timeline** | Magistrate must dispose of application within **60 days** |
| **Support system** | Protection Officers (appointed by state governments); registered NGOs as service providers |

### Section 498A IPC / BNS

- **Section 498A IPC** (now **Section 85–86, Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023**): Cruelty by husband or his relatives — cognisable, non-bailable, non-compoundable offence
- Punishable with imprisonment up to **3 years** plus fine
- Supreme Court in *Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014)*: directed police to not automatically arrest in Section 498A cases; magistrates to apply mind before authorising remand

## Sexual Harassment at Workplace — POSH Act 2013

### From Vishaka to POSH

**Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997):** A landmark Supreme Court judgment that laid down **Vishaka Guidelines** on sexual harassment at workplace following the gang rape of Bhanwari Devi (a social worker in Rajasthan) in 1992. The guidelines required employers to:
- Prohibit sexual harassment
- Set up Complaints Committees

These guidelines remained the law for 16 years until the POSH Act was enacted.

### Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 (POSH Act)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Enacted** | 2013 |
| **Applicability** | All workplaces (organised + unorganised) with 10+ employees |
| **Definition** | Sexual harassment includes unwelcome sexually coloured behaviour — physical, verbal, or non-verbal; quid pro quo; hostile work environment |
| **Internal Complaints Committee (ICC)** | Mandatory in every workplace with 10+ employees; chaired by a senior woman employee; external member required |
| **Local Complaints Committee (LCC)** | District-level committee for unorganised sector, domestic workers, small establishments |
| **Inquiry timeline** | Inquiry to be completed within **90 days** |
| **Penalties** | Employer: cancellation of registration, fine up to ₹50,000 (doubled for repeat offence) |

## POCSO Act 2012 (Protection of Children from Sexual Offences)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Full name** | Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act, 2012 |
| **Scope** | Protects children (under 18) from sexual assault, sexual harassment, pornography |
| **Gender-neutral** | Protects both boys and girls |
| **Special courts** | Mandatory special courts designated for POCSO trials |
| **Mandatory reporting** | Any person aware of an offence under POCSO must report it to police; failure is an offence |
| **Child-friendly procedures** | In-camera trials, no direct cross-examination, no disclosure of child's identity |
| **Amendment 2019** | Death penalty added for aggravated sexual assault; stringent provisions against child pornography |

## Triple Talaq — Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019

### Background

**Talaq-e-Biddat** (instant triple talaq) allowed a Muslim husband to dissolve the marriage by pronouncing "talaq" three times simultaneously — a practice considered instantaneous and irrevocable.

### Shayara Bano v. Union of India (2017)

The Supreme Court in a **3:2 majority** (judgment delivered **22 August 2017**) held instant triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) **unconstitutional** and violative of Article 14 (equality) and Article 15 (non-discrimination):

- **Majority (3 judges):** Justices Rohinton Nariman, U.U. Lalit, and Kurian Joseph — held practice manifestly arbitrary; not an essential Islamic practice protected under Article 25
- **Dissent (2 judges):** Chief Justice J.S. Khehar and Justice Abdul Nazeer — held uncodified personal law outside judicial review

### Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019

Following the Supreme Court judgment, Parliament enacted this Act making instant triple talaq:
- **Void and illegal**
- A **cognisable, non-bailable offence** punishable with up to **3 years' imprisonment** plus fine
- Entitles the aggrieved woman to maintenance and custody of minor children

## Women's Reservation — 106th Constitutional Amendment, 2023

### Key Provisions

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Full name** | Constitution (One Hundred and Sixth Amendment) Act, 2023 ("Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam") |
| **Introduced** | 19 September 2023 (special session of Parliament) |
| **Lok Sabha vote** | Passed with 454 votes in favour, 2 against |
| **Rajya Sabha vote** | Passed unanimously — 214 votes in favour |
| **Reservation** | **33% of seats** in Lok Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and Delhi Legislative Assembly |
| **Duration** | 15 years from commencement |
| **Sub-quota** | Reservation for SC/ST women within the 33% |
| **Trigger** | Implementation requires completion of **census + delimitation** — not immediately effective |

### Significance and Critique

- India's women's representation in Parliament is currently ~15%, well below the global average of ~26%
- Reservation linked to delimitation means implementation unlikely before the next delimitation exercise (post-2026 Census)
- Debates: No sub-quota for OBC women within the 33%; rotation of reserved constituencies every delimitation cycle may affect political continuity

## Labour Law Provisions for Women

### Maternity Benefit Act 1961 and 2017 Amendment

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Paid maternity leave** | Increased from 12 weeks to **26 weeks** (for first 2 children); 12 weeks for 3rd child onwards |
| **Adoption/surrogacy** | 12 weeks maternity leave for adopting mothers |
| **Crèche facility** | Mandatory for establishments with 50+ employees |
| **Work from home** | Employer may allow work from home option post maternity leave |

### Equal Remuneration Act 1976 → Code on Wages 2019

- The Equal Remuneration Act 1976 prohibited discrimination in pay on grounds of sex
- Subsumed into the **Code on Wages, 2019** (one of four Labour Codes) — which maintains the principle of non-discrimination in wages

### Dowry Prohibition Act 1961

- Prohibits giving or taking dowry
- **Section 3:** Punishment — imprisonment up to 5 years + fine of ₹15,000 or the value of dowry, whichever is higher
- **Challenges:** Low conviction rate, underreporting, social acceptance of "gifts"

## NCRB Data — Crimes Against Women

Based on the **National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) Crime in India 2022 report**:

| Category | Data |
|----------|------|
| **Total crimes against women (2022)** | 4,45,256 cases (up from 3,71,503 in 2020) |
| **Crime rate (per lakh women population)** | 66.4 (2022) vs 64.5 (2021) |
| **Cruelty by husband/relatives (Section 498A)** | 31.4% of all crimes — single largest category |
| **Kidnapping and abduction** | 19.2% |
| **Assault with intent to outrage modesty** | 18.7% |
| **Rape** | 7.1%; 31,516 cases recorded in 2022 |
| **Highest crime state** | Uttar Pradesh (65,743 cases, 2022) |
| **Most unsafe metro** | Delhi — ~3 rape cases per day; 14,158 total crimes against women |

**NFHS-5 (2019-21) finding:** 32% of ever-married women reported physical, sexual, or emotional violence by their husbands.

**Trend note (NCRB 2023):** Crimes against women increased by ~4%, recording approximately 4,71,000 cases.

## Gender Gap — WEF Global Gender Gap Index

**World Economic Forum Global Gender Gap Report 2024:**
- **India's rank:** **129 out of 146 countries** (down from 127 in 2023)
- **Gender gap closed:** 64.1%
- **Challenges:** Very low economic parity (39.8%); women's parliamentary representation at 17.2%; ministerial representation at 6.9%
- **South Asia rank:** 5th (behind Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Bhutan)
- **Global topper:** Iceland (consistently ranks 1st)

## Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (BBBP)

- **Launched:** 22 January 2015, Panipat, Haryana (by PM Modi)
- **Ministry:** Women and Child Development + Health + Education (joint campaign)
- **Focus:** Improve Sex Ratio at Birth (SRB) and girls' education
- **Progress:** SRB improved from 918 (2014-15) to 934 (2019-20) per 1,000 male births (national average); significant gains in initial 100 pilot districts
- **Issue:** NFHS-5 shows SRB improvement has plateaued in some states; gender-biased sex selection continues

## Fast-Track Courts for Rape — Post-Nirbhaya

**Nirbhaya Case:** The gang rape and murder of a young woman in Delhi on **16 December 2012** triggered nationwide outrage and major legal reforms:
- **Verma Committee (Justice J.S. Verma):** Recommended comprehensive reforms; implemented through Criminal Law (Amendment) Act 2013
- **Criminal Law Amendment Act 2013:** Expanded definition of rape; added new offences (acid attack, voyeurism, stalking); introduced minimum 7-year sentence; death penalty for rape resulting in death
- **Fast-Track Special Courts (FTSCs):** 1,023 FTSCs established (as per government data); dedicated courts for POCSO and rape cases
- **Nirbhaya Fund:** ₹1,000 crore corpus for women's safety initiatives; expanded to ₹7,000 crore+ over years

4. Connect to **governance** (GS II) — institutional mechanisms, fast-track courts, One Stop Centres
5. Connect to **social issues** (GS I) — patriarchy, dowry, gender socialisation, NFHS data

**Important Cases to Remember:**
- *Vishaka v. Rajasthan (1997)* — workplace sexual harassment guidelines

- *Arnesh Kumar v. Bihar (2014)* — guidelines on arrest under Section 498A
