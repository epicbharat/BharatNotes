## India's Gender Equality Profile

### WEF Global Gender Gap Index 2024

India ranked **129th out of 146 countries** in the **WEF Global Gender Gap Report 2024** (18th edition), a decline of two places from 127th in 2023. India has closed **64.1% of its overall gender gap**.

Sub-index performance:
- **Economic participation and opportunity:** 39.8% parity — among the lowest globally, placing India with Bangladesh, Sudan, Iran, Pakistan, and Morocco.
- **Educational attainment:** Moderate; near parity at primary level but gaps widen at higher education.
- **Health and survival:** Low score due to skewed sex ratio.
- **Political empowerment:** Relatively stronger due to women heads of state/government in India's history.

Within South Asia, India ranks fifth — behind Bangladesh (99), Nepal (111), Bhutan (124), and Sri Lanka (125).

### Female Labour Force Participation Rate (FLFPR)

According to the **Periodic Labour Force Survey (PLFS) 2023-24** (July 2023–June 2024), India's female LFPR has risen significantly:

- **Overall FLFPR:** 41.7% in 2023-24, up from 23.3% in 2017-18.
- **Rural FLFPR:** 47.6% (up from 24.6% in 2017-18) — driven partly by MGNREGS participation and agricultural self-employment.
- **Urban FLFPR:** 28.0% (up from 20.4% in 2017-18) — remains low by international standards.
- **Female unemployment rate:** Declined to 3.2% in 2023-24 from 5.6% in 2017-18.
- **Self-employment among employed women:** Rose sharply to 67.4% — raising questions about quality of employment.

Structural barriers to female workforce participation include unpaid care work, mobility constraints, patriarchal social norms, lack of safe transport, and inadequate childcare infrastructure.

## Constitutional and Legal Framework

### Constitutional Provisions

- **Article 14:** Equality before law.
- **Article 15(1) & (3):** Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of sex; State may make special provisions for women.
- **Article 16:** Equal opportunity in public employment.
- **Article 39(a) & (d):** DPSP — equal right to livelihood and equal pay for equal work.
- **Article 42:** DPSP — just and humane conditions of work and maternity relief.
- **Article 44:** DPSP — uniform civil code (relevant to personal law discrimination).
- **Article 51A(e):** Fundamental duty to renounce practices derogatory to dignity of women.

### Key Legislative Milestones

| Year | Legislation / Judgment | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| 1983 | Criminal Law (Amendment) Act | Post-Mathura rape case; custody rape criminalised; reversed burden of proof |
| 1987 | Dowry Prohibition (Amendment) Act | Strengthened anti-dowry provisions |
| 2005 | Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act | Civil remedy for domestic violence including emotional abuse |
| 2005 | Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act | Daughters given equal coparcenary rights in ancestral property |
| 2013 | Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (POSH) Act | Codified Vishaka guidelines from 1997 Supreme Court judgment |
| 2013 | Criminal Law (Amendment) Act | Post-Nirbhaya (Dec 2012); expanded definition of rape; stalking and voyeurism criminalised |
| 2019 | Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act | Triple Talaq criminalised after Supreme Court declared it unconstitutional in 2017 (Shayara Bano case) |
| 2023 | Constitution (106th Amendment) Act | Women's Reservation Act — 33% seats in Lok Sabha, State Assemblies (from delimitation onwards) |

## Women's Reservation Act, 2023

The **Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam** — Constitution (106th Amendment) Act, 2023 — was passed unanimously by Parliament and signed by the President on **28 September 2023**. Key provisions:

- **One-third (33%) of seats** reserved for women in the Lok Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and Delhi Legislative Assembly.
- Reservation applies within existing SC/ST quota seats as well.
- Reservation will come into effect **after the first delimitation following the 2026 Census**, making implementation likely in the 2029 or subsequent elections.
- Reservation is **rotational** across constituencies and will continue for **15 years** from commencement.

Despite reservations at Parliament and State Assembly levels being absent until delimitation, India's current women's representation stands at about **17.2% in the Lok Sabha** and 6.9% in ministerial positions (Gender Gap Index 2024 data).

## Feminist Movements in India

### First Wave — Social Reform (19th–Early 20th Century)

Focus on eliminating social evils: widow remarriage (backed by reformers like Ram Mohan Roy and Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar), abolition of sati, age of marriage, women's education. Led largely by elite male reformers rather than women themselves.

### Second Wave — Post-Independence (1950s–1980s)

Women's organisations engaged with the new constitutional framework — legal rights to property, education, political participation. The **Mathura rape case (1972)** and its acquittal verdict sparked a national movement; open letters from academics and lawyers led to the **1983 Criminal Law Amendment** strengthening custodial rape law.

The **Chipko Movement (1973)** in Uttarakhand saw women as frontline environmental activists protecting forests — an intersection of gender and ecological movements.

### Third Wave — Contemporary (1990s–Present)

- **Anti-rape protests (December 2012):** The Nirbhaya gang-rape case triggered mass street protests, leading to the Criminal Law Amendment Act 2013.
- **#MeToo India (2018):** Social media-driven exposure of workplace sexual harassment across media, entertainment, academia, and NGO sectors.
- **Anti-Triple Talaq movement:** Muslim women petitioners led the Supreme Court challenge resulting in the Shayara Bano judgment (2017) and subsequent legislation.
- **Sabarimala (2018):** Supreme Court ruled that the exclusion of women of menstruating age (10–50 years) from the Sabarimala temple violated Articles 14, 15, and 25. The ruling generated both support and significant opposition.

## Key Government Schemes & Programmes

- **Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (BBBP):** Launched 2015 to address declining Child Sex Ratio (CSR); focus districts initially in Haryana, Rajasthan, UP; expanded nationally.
- **PM Ujjwala Yojana:** Free LPG connections to women in BPL households — reduces indoor air pollution, time spent on fuelwood collection, and improves women's health.
- **One Stop Centres (Sakhi Centres):** Integrated support to women affected by violence — police, legal aid, medical services, psychological counselling.
- **Nirbhaya Fund:** Corpus of ₹1,000 crore established in 2013; used for women's safety projects including Safe City Initiative, CCTV surveillance, helplines (Dial 100, 181).
- **Shakti Shalinis:** Shelter homes for women in distress.
- **Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act, 2017:** Extended paid maternity leave to 26 weeks (for first two children) in establishments with ≥10 employees.

## SHG Movement & Women's Financial Inclusion

The **Self Help Group (SHG)** movement, supported by NABARD and state governments, has been transformative for rural women's financial and social empowerment:

- Over **1.2 crore SHGs** are linked to banks (SHG-Bank Linkage Programme).
- Women constitute the overwhelming majority of SHG members.
- **Lakhpati Didi initiative** (Union Budget 2024-25) targets enabling 3 crore women in SHGs to earn ≥₹1 lakh per year through skill development and microenterprise support.

## Gender Pay Gap & Workforce Challenges

Despite rising FLFPR, India's gender pay gap remains substantial. The **Equal Remuneration Act, 1976** mandates equal pay for equal work regardless of gender, but enforcement is weak particularly in the informal sector where most women work. Women are concentrated in low-productivity, low-pay sectors: agriculture, domestic work, garment manufacturing, and informal trade.
