## Overview

Communalism remains one of the most persistent threats to India's social fabric. At its core, communalism is the ideology that people sharing a religion necessarily share common social, political, and economic interests -- and that these interests are distinct from, or opposed to, those of other religious communities. India's Constitution responds to this challenge through a distinctive model of secularism that guarantees equal respect for all religions rather than strict separation of religion and state.

For UPSC, this topic is central to GS-1 (Indian Society) and also intersects with GS-2 (governance, constitutional provisions). Questions typically test understanding of communalism's roots, constitutional safeguards, and analytical capacity to evaluate policies for national integration.

## Communalism — Concept and Types

### Definition

Communalism is an ideology that divides society into distinct religious communities whose interests are portrayed as being divergent or mutually antagonistic. It moves through stages -- from mild assertions of shared identity to extreme forms involving hatred and violence.

### Three Stages of Communalism

| Stage | Characteristic | Example |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| **Mild / Liberal** | People of the same religion share common secular interests (social, political, economic) | Cultural organisations promoting a community's welfare |
| **Moderate** | Interests of one religious community are dissimilar and divergent from other communities | Demands for separate representation, reservations on religious lines |
| **Extreme / Militant** | Interests of different religions are mutually antagonistic and incompatible; based on fear and hatred | Communal riots, targeted violence, ethnic cleansing |

### Dimensions of Communalism

| Dimension | Manifestation |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Political communalism** | Use of religion for electoral mobilisation; vote-bank politics; communal polarisation before elections |
| **Social communalism** | Segregation of communities in housing, social boycotts, inter-community mistrust |
| **Economic communalism** | Perception that one community controls economic resources at the expense of another; competition for jobs and business |
| **Ideological communalism** | Propagation of religious supremacy or exclusivism through literature, social media, and public discourse |

## Historical Roots of Communalism

### Colonial Divide-and-Rule Policy

The British colonial strategy of "divide and rule" systematically deepened Hindu-Muslim divisions to weaken the growing nationalist movement.

| Colonial Measure | Year | Impact |
|-----------------|------|--------|
| **Partition of Bengal** | 1905 | Lord Curzon partitioned Bengal ostensibly for administrative efficiency but effectively along Hindu-Muslim lines; reversed in 1911 after protests |
| **Morley-Minto Reforms** | 1909 | Introduced separate electorates for Muslims -- institutionalised communal representation for the first time |
| **Lucknow Pact** | 1916 | Congress-Muslim League agreement on separate electorates -- short-term unity but long-term communal entrenchment |
| **Communal Award** | 1932 | Ramsay MacDonald extended separate electorates to depressed classes; Poona Pact (Gandhi-Ambedkar) replaced it with reserved seats |
| **Two-Nation Theory** | 1940s | Muhammad Ali Jinnah argued that Hindus and Muslims were distinct nations requiring separate homelands -- culminated in Partition (1947) |

### Post-Independence Communal Violence

India has witnessed recurring episodes of communal violence since independence, with devastating human and social costs.

| Incident | Year | Key Facts |
|----------|------|-----------|
| **Partition violence** | 1947 | Estimated 1 million killed; 1.5 crore displaced across India and Pakistan |
| **Jabalpur riots** | 1961 | Among the first major post-independence riots; prompted creation of National Integration Council |
| **Ahmedabad riots** | 1969 | 660 killed (430 Muslims, 230 Hindus); over 48,000 lost property; worst Hindu-Muslim violence since Partition at that time |
| **Nellie massacre** | 1983 | Over 1,383 officially killed (unofficial estimates far higher) in Assam -- rooted in anti-foreigner agitation |
| **Anti-Sikh violence** | 1984 | Following assassination of PM Indira Gandhi; estimated 3,000 Sikhs killed, primarily in Delhi |
| **Bombay riots** | 1992-93 | Following Babri Masjid demolition (6 December 1992); over 900 killed |
| **Gujarat violence** | 2002 | Triggered by Godhra train burning; 790 Muslims and 254 Hindus killed (official figures reported to Parliament); 2,548 injured |
| **Muzaffarnagar riots** | 2013 | Over 60 killed; approximately 50,000 internally displaced -- the largest displacement after Partition |

> **For Mains:** When discussing communal violence, avoid presenting it as a simple Hindu-Muslim binary. Communalism manifests across all communities (anti-Sikh 1984, anti-Christian violence in Kandhamal 2008). The systemic factors -- failure of police, political complicity, delayed judicial response -- are more important than cataloguing events. Discuss the structural causes and institutional remedies.

## Indian Secularism

### The Indian Model

Indian secularism follows the principle of **Sarva Dharma Sambhava** (equal respect for all religions) rather than the strict "wall of separation" model of the United States or French laicite. The state does not identify with any particular religion but engages with all religions equally.

### Constitutional Framework

| Provision | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **Preamble** | "Secular" added by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976 -- but the Supreme Court has held that India was secular from the time it adopted the Constitution; the amendment merely made explicit what was already implicit |
| **Article 14** | Equality before law -- no discrimination on grounds of religion |
| **Article 15** | Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth |
| **Article 25** | Freedom of conscience; right to freely profess, practise, and propagate religion -- subject to public order, morality, and health; state can regulate economic, financial, political or other secular activities associated with religious practice |
| **Article 26** | Freedom to manage religious affairs -- every religious denomination has the right to establish and maintain institutions, manage its own affairs in matters of religion, and own/acquire property |
| **Article 27** | No person shall be compelled to pay taxes for promotion of any particular religion |
| **Article 28** | No religious instruction in state-funded educational institutions; institutions aided by the state cannot compel attendance at religious instruction |
| **Article 29-30** | Protection of interests of minorities -- right to conserve distinct language, script, or culture; minorities' right to establish and administer educational institutions |
| **Article 44** | Directive Principle -- the state shall endeavour to secure a Uniform Civil Code (UCC) |
| **Article 51A(e)** | Fundamental Duty -- to promote harmony and spirit of common brotherhood among all people, transcending religious, linguistic, and regional diversities |

### Key Features of Indian Secularism

| Feature | Explanation |
|---------|------------|
| **Equal respect, not indifference** | State engages with religion positively (funds pilgrimages, manages temples, grants minority rights) rather than maintaining strict neutrality |
| **No state religion** | Unlike Pakistan or UK, India has no official religion |
| **Positive discrimination** | State can intervene in religion for social reform (e.g., abolishing untouchability, reforming personal laws) |
| **Religious freedom with limits** | Freedom of religion subject to public order, morality, health -- essential religious practices doctrine developed by courts |
| **Minority rights** | Special protection for linguistic and religious minorities (Articles 29-30) |

> **For Prelims:** "Secular" was added to the Preamble by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976, during the Emergency. However, the Supreme Court (S.R. Bommai v. Union of India, 1994) held that secularism is part of the basic structure of the Constitution and cannot be amended.

## Hate Speech — Legal Framework

### IPC Provisions (now replaced by BNS)

| Old Provision (IPC) | New Provision (BNS, 2023) | Offence | Punishment |
|---------------------|--------------------------|---------|------------|
| **Section 153A** | **Section 196 BNS** | Promoting enmity between groups on grounds of religion, race, caste, community, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, place of birth, residence, language | Up to 3 years imprisonment, or fine, or both (up to 5 years if committed in places of worship) |
| **Section 295A** | **Section 299 BNS** | Deliberate and malicious acts intended to outrage religious feelings of any class | Up to 3 years imprisonment, or fine, or both |

### Key Changes in BNS 2023

- **Electronic communication** explicitly included as a mode of spreading disharmony
- **Gender identity and sexual orientation** added as protected grounds -- a progressive step
- Expanded scope to cover digital platforms and online hate speech

### Other Relevant Laws

| Law/Provision | Scope |
|---------------|-------|
| **Representation of the People Act, 1951 (Section 123)** | Corrupt practice to promote feelings of enmity on grounds of religion/caste during elections |
| **Information Technology Act, 2000 (Section 66A)** | Struck down by Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) as unconstitutional |
| **Cable Television Networks (Regulation) Act, 1995** | Prohibits transmission of content promoting communal disharmony |

## National Integration Council (NIC)

### Overview

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Established** | 1961 by PM Jawaharlal Nehru following a national conference on communal harmony |
| **Inaugurated** | June 1962 |
| **Chairman** | Prime Minister of India (ex officio) |
| **Membership** | Originally 147 members; includes cabinet ministers, chief ministers, opposition leaders, media heads, civil society representatives, industrialists |
| **Purpose** | Address issues of communalism, casteism, regionalism, and linguism; promote national integration |
| **Reconstituted** | Multiple times -- last reconstituted in October 2013 under PM Manmohan Singh |
| **Status** | Non-statutory body; advisory in nature; no binding powers |

### Key Recommendations Over the Years

- Stricter implementation of laws against communal violence
- Strengthening of secular education in schools
- Fast-track courts for communal violence cases
- Police reforms to ensure communal sensitivity
- Media guidelines to prevent communal incitement

## National Foundation for Communal Harmony (NFCH)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Established** | 19 February 1992 under the Societies Registration Act, 1860 |
| **Administrative ministry** | Ministry of Home Affairs |
| **Primary objective** | Physical and psychological rehabilitation of child victims of communal, caste, ethnic, or terrorist violence -- particularly their care, education, and training |
| **Activities** | Provides financial assistance for rehabilitation; sponsors research on communal harmony; confers awards for outstanding contribution to national integration |
| **Communal Harmony Campaign** | Organises "Flag Day" annually to collect funds for child victims |

## Role of Media in Communalism

### Positive Role

| Function | How It Helps |
|----------|-------------|
| **Information dissemination** | Factual reporting can counter rumours that trigger violence |
| **Watchdog** | Exposing communal politicians, hate speech, and administrative failures |
| **Bridge-building** | Showcasing inter-community cooperation, shared cultural heritage |
| **Public awareness** | Campaigns for communal harmony, constitutional values |

### Negative Role and Challenges

| Problem | Description |
|---------|------------|
| **Sensationalism** | TRP-driven coverage that inflames tensions rather than informing |
| **Communal framing** | Identifying perpetrators/victims by religion in crime reporting; reinforcing stereotypes |
| **Social media echo chambers** | WhatsApp forwards, Twitter (X) trends, and YouTube videos spreading hate speech, deepfakes, and doctored content |
| **Fake news** | Misinformation triggering mob violence (e.g., lynchings following WhatsApp rumours) |
| **Polarisation** | Partisan media outlets that cater to specific community sentiments rather than objective reporting |

> **For Mains:** The role of social media in communal polarisation is a high-relevance contemporary issue. Discuss the tension between freedom of expression (Article 19) and the need to regulate hate speech. Reference the Supreme Court's Shreya Singhal judgment (2015) and the IT Act's intermediary guidelines. Suggest a balanced approach: platform accountability, media literacy, and independent fact-checking mechanisms.

## Constitutional Safeguards for Social Harmony

### Summary of Protections

| Category | Provisions |
|----------|-----------|
| **Fundamental Rights** | Articles 14, 15, 16 (equality); Articles 25-28 (religious freedom); Articles 29-30 (minority rights) |
| **Fundamental Duties** | Article 51A(e) -- promote harmony; Article 51A(f) -- value composite culture |
| **Directive Principles** | Article 38 (social order for welfare); Article 44 (Uniform Civil Code); Article 46 (promote weaker sections) |
| **Criminal law** | BNS Sections 196, 299 (hate speech); Section 302 (rioting); Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act, 1971 |
| **Electoral law** | Section 123 of RPA, 1951 -- appeal to religion/caste is a corrupt practice |
| **Emergency provision** | Article 355 -- Union duty to protect states from internal disturbance |

### Inter-Faith Dialogue and Grassroots Integration

- **Sarva Dharma Sammelan** -- multi-faith congregations promoting understanding
- **Textbook reform** -- NCERT guidelines for secular, inclusive curriculum content
- **Communal Harmony Campaigns** -- NGOs, religious leaders, and civil society organisations working at grassroots level
- **Judiciary's role** -- Supreme Court's proactive stance on secularism (S.R. Bommai, 1994; Babri Masjid verdict, 2019)

## Landmark Judicial Pronouncements on Secularism

| Case | Year | Significance |
|------|------|-------------|
| **S.R. Bommai v. Union of India** | 1994 | Secularism is part of the basic structure; President's Rule imposed on grounds of secularism was upheld; state governments acting against secular principles can be dismissed under Article 356 |
| **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** | 1973 | Established the basic structure doctrine -- while "secularism" was not explicitly named, later courts read it into the basic structure |
| **Ismail Faruqui v. Union of India** | 1994 | Acquisition of disputed Babri Masjid site upheld; mosque not essential to Islam; reinforced state's regulatory power over religious places |
| **Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (Sabarimala)** | 2018 | Entry of women of all ages into Sabarimala temple upheld; right to worship is a constitutional right; exclusion based on biological characteristic is unconstitutional |
| **M. Siddiq v. Mahant Suresh Das (Ayodhya)** | 2019 | Disputed land in Ayodhya allotted for Ram temple; 5-acre alternative land for mosque; court held that demolition of Babri Masjid was illegal |
| **Shayara Bano v. Union of India** | 2017 | Triple talaq (talaq-e-biddat) declared unconstitutional; reinforced gender equality within religious personal law |

### Secularism vs. Communalism — Conceptual Comparison

| Aspect | Secularism | Communalism |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Core idea** | Equal treatment of all religions by the state | Religious identity is the primary basis of political identity |
| **Approach to diversity** | Celebrates pluralism; seeks harmony through equal respect | Sees diversity as division; views other communities with suspicion |
| **Constitutional basis** | Preamble, Articles 14-16, 25-30 | Has no constitutional basis; violates constitutional values |
| **Attitude to state** | State should be neutral and reformative | State should favour one community over others |
| **Vision of society** | Composite culture -- shared citizenship | Segregated communities with competing interests |

## Uniform Civil Code (UCC) Debate

### Constitutional Position

Article 44 of the Constitution (Directive Principle) directs the state to "endeavour to secure for the citizens a uniform civil code throughout the territory of India."

### Arguments For and Against

| Arguments For UCC | Arguments Against UCC |
|-------------------|----------------------|
| Promotes national integration and common citizenship | Threatens religious and cultural autonomy of minorities |
| Removes gender discrimination embedded in personal laws | Forced uniformity may violate Article 25 (freedom of religion) |
| Fulfils the Directive Principle under Article 44 | India's diversity requires accommodation, not homogenisation |
| Simplifies legal framework -- one law for all citizens | Timing and process matter -- should emerge through consensus, not imposition |
| Goa already has a uniform civil code (Portuguese Civil Code, 1867) | Risk of majoritarian imposition in the absence of genuine consultation |

> **For Mains:** The UCC debate sits at the intersection of secularism, minority rights, gender justice, and federalism. A nuanced answer should acknowledge that personal laws contain gender-discriminatory elements (Shah Bano case, 1985; Shayara Bano, 2017) while also recognising minority communities' concerns about cultural autonomy. Reference the Law Commission's 2018 consultation paper which noted that a UCC is "neither necessary nor desirable at this stage" but advocated reform within personal laws.

## Key Terms for Quick Revision

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Communalism** | Ideology that religious identity determines common political, social, and economic interests -- divides society along religious lines |
| **Secularism** | Constitutional principle of equal respect for all religions; no state religion; freedom of conscience |
| **Sarva Dharma Sambhava** | "Equal respect for all religions" -- the philosophical foundation of Indian secularism |
| **Two-Nation Theory** | Doctrine that Hindus and Muslims are two separate nations requiring separate homelands; basis of Partition |
| **42nd Amendment** | 1976 amendment that added "secular" and "socialist" to the Preamble |
| **Basic Structure Doctrine** | Supreme Court ruling that certain features of the Constitution (including secularism) cannot be amended |
| **NIC** | National Integration Council -- advisory body chaired by PM to promote national unity |
| **NFCH** | National Foundation for Communal Harmony -- rehabilitates child victims of communal/ethnic violence |
| **BNS Section 196** | Replaces IPC 153A -- criminalises promoting enmity between groups on grounds of religion, caste, etc. |
| **BNS Section 299** | Replaces IPC 295A -- criminalises deliberate acts outraging religious feelings |
| **Hate speech** | Speech that attacks or disparages a group based on religion, race, caste, or other identity -- not specifically defined in Indian law but covered under multiple provisions |
| **Essential Religious Practices** | Doctrine developed by Indian courts to determine which religious practices are constitutionally protected |

## Exam Strategy

> **For Mains Answer Writing:** Communalism-secularism is a high-frequency GS-1 topic. Structure answers around: historical roots (colonial legacy), constitutional framework (Articles 25-28, Preamble), contemporary challenges (social media hate speech, political polarisation), and institutional responses (NIC, NFCH, judicial interventions). Always provide a balanced perspective -- acknowledge that both majority and minority communalism exist. Cite specific examples and constitutional provisions. For questions on secularism, contrast the Indian model with Western models and discuss the Uniform Civil Code debate.

> **For Prelims:** Key facts -- "Secular" added by 42nd Amendment (1976); Articles 25-28 deal with religious freedom; S.R. Bommai (1994) held secularism is basic structure; NIC established 1961; NFCH established 1992; BNS Section 196 replaces IPC 153A; BNS Section 299 replaces IPC 295A; separate electorates introduced by Morley-Minto Reforms (1909).
