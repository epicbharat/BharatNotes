## Overview

Tribal communities constitute one of India's most historically marginalised populations. Their rights intersect constitutional provisions (Articles 15, 46, 338A, 342), specific legislation (Forest Rights Act 2006, PESA 1996), and landmark judgments (Niyamgiri 2013). This topic is critical for GS1 (Indian society), GS2 (vulnerable sections, governance), and GS3 (environment, displacement).

---

## Tribal Population: Data and Distribution

According to the **Census 2011**:
- Tribal population: **10.42 crore (~104 million)**
- Share of total population: **8.6%** of India
- Share of rural population: **11.3%** of total rural population
- Number of ethnic groups: **705 Scheduled Tribe communities** across 30 States and Union Territories
- Urban tribal population: ~1.04 crore

**States with largest tribal populations:** Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Odisha, Rajasthan, Jharkhand, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, West Bengal.

**Tribal-majority states:** Mizoram (~94%), Nagaland (~86%), Meghalaya (~86%), Arunachal Pradesh (~68%), Manipur (~35%), Tripura (~31%).

## Fifth Schedule — Scheduled Areas

**Article 244(1)** provides for administration of **Scheduled Areas** under the Fifth Schedule.

**10 States** with Fifth Schedule areas: Andhra Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Himachal Pradesh, Jharkhand, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Odisha, Rajasthan, Telangana.

### Key Features of Fifth Schedule
- **Governor's powers:** Governor may direct that any Act of Parliament or State Legislature does not apply to Scheduled Areas, or may apply with modifications
- **Tribal Advisory Council (TAC):** Constituted in each State having Scheduled Areas; 3/4 members must be ST representatives from the State legislature; advises Governor on tribal welfare
- **Regulation-making power:** Governor may make Regulations for peace and good governance of Scheduled Areas — specifically to prohibit or restrict transfer of land by/among STs, regulation of money lending to STs

### PESA Act 1996

The **Provisions of the Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act, 1996** was enacted on **24 December 1996** to extend Part IX of the Constitution (Panchayati Raj) to Scheduled Areas with essential modifications to protect tribal self-governance.

**Objective:** Self-governance through traditional Gram Sabhas for tribal communities.

**Key Provisions:**

| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Gram Sabha supremacy** | Gram Sabha (adult village residents) is the unit of self-governance; competence to safeguard traditions, customs, cultural identity, community resources |
| **Mandatory consultation** | Gram Sabha must be consulted before land acquisition, resettlement and rehabilitation of displaced persons |
| **Minor forest produce** | Gram Sabha has ownership over Minor Forest Produce (MFP) |
| **Customary law** | Recognition of customary laws and dispute resolution mechanisms |
| **Alcohol regulation** | Gram Sabha may prohibit/regulate manufacture and sale of intoxicants |
| **Money lending** | Power to regulate/prohibit money lending to tribals |
| **Mines and minerals** | Mandatory recommendations from Gram Sabha/Panchayat before grant of prospecting licences/mining leases |
| **Land alienation** | Prevention of alienation of tribal lands and restoration of unlawfully alienated lands |

**Implementation:** 8 of 10 Fifth Schedule states have framed PESA Rules; Odisha and Jharkhand had pending draft rules as of 2025.

## Sixth Schedule — Tribal Areas

**Articles 244(2) and 275(1)** provide for administration of tribal areas in **4 northeastern states**: Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, and Mizoram.

### Autonomous District Councils (ADCs)
- **Autonomous Districts** are created within the states
- Each district has an **Autonomous District Council (ADC)** — a legislative body with powers to make laws on specified subjects
- **Autonomous Regional Councils (ARCs)** for autonomous regions within districts
- ADCs can make laws on: land management, forests (other than reserved), water use, shifting cultivation, regulation of money lending, social customs

**Significance:** Sixth Schedule provides far stronger autonomy than Fifth Schedule — ADCs have actual legislative powers and their own courts.

## Forest Rights Act 2006

**Full name:** The Scheduled Tribes and Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (Recognition of Forest Rights) Act, 2006.

**Enacted:** 18 December 2006; came into force **1 January 2008**.

**Objective:** To recognise and vest rights of forest-dwelling communities that were deprived of their rights during colonial period and after independence.

### Who is Covered?

| Category | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Scheduled Tribes** | Any member of an ST who primarily resides in forests and depends on forests for livelihood |
| **Other Traditional Forest Dwellers (OTFDs)** | Any member of other traditional forest dwelling communities who has primarily resided in forests for 75 years or more before 13 December 2005 |

### Types of Rights Recognised

**Individual Forest Rights (IFRs):**
- Right to live in and cultivate forest land — up to **4 hectares** (not exceeding what was being occupied on 13 December 2005)
- Right to use minor forest produce
- Other community rights such as nistar rights (customary rights)

**Community Forest Rights (CFRs):**
- Rights over community forest resources — management and protection
- Right of communities to protect, regenerate, conserve, and manage community forest resources
- Rights over disputed lands between revenue and forest departments
- Rights of traditional seasonal resource access of nomadic/pastoralist communities
- Rights of primitive tribal groups (PVTGs) to habitats

**Habitat Rights (Section 3(1)(e)):**
- Specifically for PVTGs — rights over their traditional territory, socio-cultural practices, livelihoods, and natural and cultural heritage

### Gram Sabha's Central Role
- Gram Sabha is the **authority to initiate and examine claims**
- Forest Rights Committee (FRC) constituted by Gram Sabha
- Decision-making chain: Gram Sabha → Sub-Divisional Level Committee (SDLC) → District Level Committee (DLC)

### Implementation Status

As per government data (cumulative till **May 2025**):
- Claims filed at Gram Sabha level: **51,23,104**
- Titles distributed: **25,11,375** (~49% of claims)
- CFR titles specifically: over 1 lakh communities have received CFR titles

**Concerns with implementation:** Rampant rejections without adequate reasons, forest department resistance, lack of awareness, inability to furnish documents, eviction of forest dwellers pending verification.

## Niyamgiri Case (2013): Landmark Judgment

**Case:** Orissa Mining Corporation vs Ministry of Environment and Forests (2013) — Supreme Court

**Issue:** Whether bauxite mining could be allowed in the Niyamgiri hills (Odisha) — a sacred hill for the **Dongria Kondh** tribe.

**SC Judgment:** The Court directed that **Gram Sabhas** of the concerned tribal villages must decide whether the project affects their religious, cultural, and forest rights. The Court recognised the Gram Sabha's right to give or withhold **consent** for any project affecting their habitat and sacred groves.

**Outcome:** 12 Gram Sabhas unanimously rejected the mining project.

**Significance:**
- Established **free, prior and informed consent (FPIC)** principle in Indian jurisprudence
- Recognised tribal religious and cultural rights as legally enforceable
- Affirmed that FRA 2006 rights must be settled before clearances

## Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs)

**Definition:** Sub-groups within STs identified as the most marginalised — characterised by:
- **Pre-agricultural level of technology** (food gathering, hunting, shifting cultivation)
- **Stagnant or declining population**
- Extremely low literacy levels
- Subsistence or pre-subsistence level economy

**Total PVTGs:** **75 groups** spread across **18 states and 1 UT** (Andaman & Nicobar Islands).

The list was expanded from 52 to 75 in **1993** based on the Dhebar Commission criteria.

### Major PVTGs — Examples by State

| PVTG | State | Notable Feature |
|------|-------|----------------|
| **Jarawa** | Andaman & Nicobar | Highly isolated, minimal contact policy |
| **Sentinelese** | Andaman & Nicobar | North Sentinel Island, strictly isolated |
| **Chenchu** | Andhra Pradesh/Telangana | Hunter-gatherers, Nagarjunasagar area |
| **Birhor** | Jharkhand/Odisha/Chhattisgarh | Nomadic, rope-making, hunter-gatherers |
| **Toda** | Tamil Nadu (Nilgiris) | Pastoralist, known for embroidery |
| **Korwa** | Chhattisgarh/Jharkhand | Hunters and gatherers |
| **Abujhmaria** | Chhattisgarh (Abujhmarh forest) | Minimal government contact |
| **Raji** | Uttarakhand | Also called Van Rawat, forest dwellers |

## PM-JANMAN Scheme 2023

**Full name:** Pradhan Mantri Janjati Adivasi Nyaya Maha Abhiyan (PM-JANMAN)

**Launched:** **15 November 2023** (Janjatiya Gaurav Diwas)

**Coverage:** 75 PVTGs in 18 states and 1 UT

**Budget:** **₹24,104 crore** (converging multiple ministries' funds)

**Implementing Ministries:** **11 Ministries** converging for the first time for tribal welfare.

**9 Critical Interventions:**

| Intervention | Ministry |
|-------------|----------|
| Pucca housing (4.9 lakh homes) | MoRD (PM-AWAS Gramin) |
| Roads to PVTG habitations | MoRT&H (PM Gram Sadak Yojana) |
| Drinking water | Ministry of Jal Shakti |
| Mobile medical units | Ministry of Health |
| Anganwadi centres | WCD Ministry |
| Multipurpose centres | Tribal Affairs Ministry |
| Hostel facilities | Tribal Affairs Ministry |
| Van Dhan Vikas Kendras | Tribal Affairs Ministry |
| Mobile connectivity | DoT |

**Target beneficiaries:** **44.64 lakh PVTGs** across approximately 22,544 PVTG villages.

## Tribal Displacement and Land Alienation

### Development-Induced Displacement

An estimated **40–50% of those displaced by large dams, mines, and other development projects** since independence have been tribals (who constitute only 8.6% of the population). Key cases:
- **Sardar Sarovar Dam** (Narmada): Adivasi displacement in Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra — led by Narmada Bachao Andolan (Medha Patkar)
- **Vedanta mining (Niyamgiri):** Dongria Kondh resistance — see Niyamgiri case above
- **POSCO steel plant (Odisha):** Gram Sabha opposition, eventual cancellation

### Land Alienation Laws

State laws preventing transfer of tribal land to non-tribals:
- **Andhra Pradesh (now AP and Telangana) — LTR Act:** Land Transfer Regulation prohibiting transfer of tribal lands to non-tribals
- **Chhattisgarh, Odisha, Madhya Pradesh:** Similar protective provisions
- **PESA Act:** Empowers Gram Sabhas to prevent alienation and restore alienated lands

**Limitation:** Benami transfers, marriage-based transfers, encroachments persist despite legal protection.

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Census 2011 data: 8.6%, 104 million, 705 communities
- PESA states: 10 states under Fifth Schedule; PESA enacted 24 December 1996
- Sixth Schedule: 4 NE states — Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram
- FRA 2006: Individual forest rights up to 4 ha; Gram Sabha is the initiating authority
- PVTGs: 75 groups, 18 states + 1 UT (A&N Islands), expanded from 52 to 75 in 1993
- PM-JANMAN: November 2023, ₹24,104 crore, 9 interventions, 11 ministries

**For Mains:**
- Distinguish Fifth Schedule (10 states, TAC, Governor's powers) from Sixth Schedule (4 NE states, ADCs with legislative powers)
- FRA implementation gap: 49% titles granted — reasons for gap (department resistance, document requirements, awareness)
- Niyamgiri case: FPIC principle; Gram Sabha veto as constitutional recognition of tribal self-determination
- Development vs displacement: give data on over-representation of tribals in displacement
- PESA vs FRA: complementary frameworks — PESA for governance, FRA for rights recognition

**Key Terms:**
- Minor Forest Produce (MFP), Community Forest Resources (CFR), Habitat Rights, Free Prior Informed Consent (FPIC), Van Dhan Vikas Kendras, Janjatiya Gaurav Diwas (15 November)
