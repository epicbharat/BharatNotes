International ethics is the application of moral reasoning to the conduct of states, international organisations, and global governance structures. The UPSC GS4 syllabus explicitly covers "ethical issues in international relations and funding" — a section that connects abstract moral philosophy to India's lived foreign policy dilemmas. This chapter prepares candidates to reason systematically through dilemmas involving sovereignty, human rights, global justice, and India's responsibilities as a rising power.

## Why International Ethics Matters for UPSC

Unlike domestic ethics — where shared laws, institutions, and cultures provide moral frameworks — international relations operates in an **anarchic environment** (no world government, no enforceable global law). This raises distinctive ethical questions:

- Do states have moral obligations beyond their own citizens?
- When is the use of force justified?
- Who should bear the cost of global problems like climate change?
- Is foreign aid a moral duty or a tool of manipulation?
- Should India vote its values or its interests at the UN?

These are not merely philosophical — they arise in UPSC case studies, essay questions, and current affairs analysis.

## Just War Theory (Jus Bellum)

The oldest systematic framework for international ethics, Just War Theory asks: when and how may states use military force?

### Jus ad Bellum (Right to Go to War)

A war is just only if ALL of the following conditions are met:

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Just cause** | War is fought to defend against aggression, protect innocent lives, or rectify serious injustice |
| **Right intention** | Goal must be the just cause itself, not conquest, profit, or revenge |
| **Proportionality** | Expected benefits must outweigh expected harms |
| **Last resort** | All peaceful alternatives (diplomacy, sanctions) must have been exhausted |
| **Legitimate authority** | War must be declared by a proper authority (state government, UNSC authorisation) |
| **Reasonable chance of success** | No justification for futile conflicts that only cause additional suffering |

### Jus in Bello (Conduct During War)

Even in a just war, conduct must remain ethical:

| Principle | Content |
|---|---|
| **Distinction** | Must distinguish combatants from civilians; civilians may not be targeted |
| **Proportionality** | Collateral civilian harm must not be excessive relative to military gain |
| **Military necessity** | Force used only to the extent necessary for military objectives |
| **Prohibited weapons** | Chemical, biological, and certain conventional weapons are banned |
| **Humane treatment** | Prisoners of war must be treated according to Geneva Conventions |

### Jus post Bellum (After War)

Increasingly recognised as a third element: occupying forces have obligations to help rebuild, restore order, and deliver justice — not simply withdraw after combat. This was relevant in Iraq and Afghanistan.

**UN Charter framework:** Article 51 permits self-defence (individual or collective). Chapter VII authorises the UNSC to authorise force to maintain or restore international peace and security. Critics note the P5 veto means the UNSC can be paralysed (as in Syria) even when mass atrocities occur.

**Ethical critique of drone strikes:** Drone warfare raises serious just war questions — **targeted killings** in third-party territories (Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia) raise issues of sovereignty, proportionality, and accountability. Who decides the target? What is the error rate? Are civilian casualties proportionate to military gain?

## Human Rights: Universalism vs Cultural Relativism

The **Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR, 1948)** asserts that all human beings possess inherent rights by virtue of their humanity — regardless of nationality, culture, or religion. But two competing views exist:

### Universalism

**Position:** Human rights are universal — the same rights apply everywhere, derived from the inherent dignity of persons.

**Arguments for:**
- If rights depend on culture, there is no basis to condemn genocide, torture, or slavery anywhere
- The UDHR was adopted by a broad coalition of nations, including non-Western ones
- Core rights (freedom from torture, right to life) are recognised across most cultures
- Power asymmetry: "cultural relativism" is often invoked by governments, not the people they oppress

### Cultural Relativism

**Position:** What counts as a right is culturally embedded; Western liberal rights cannot be universally imposed.

**Arguments for:**
- Collective and community rights (family, land) may matter more than individual rights in many societies
- The UDHR was drafted primarily by Western powers; the voice of colonised peoples was limited
- Rights hierarchies differ — some societies prioritise economic and social rights (right to food, housing) over civil and political rights
- The **"Asian Values" debate** (Lee Kuan Yew, Mahathir): social harmony and economic development may require limitations on individual freedoms

### India's Position

India supports **universalism** in principle (active in UDHR drafting; prominent role through B.R. Rau) but frequently invokes **sovereignty and non-interference** at the UN when votes concern other countries' internal affairs. India has been cautious on country-specific resolutions (Sri Lanka, Myanmar) — balancing values with neighbourhood relations.

## Theories of Global Justice

### John Rawls: Limited International Obligations

In **"The Law of Peoples" (1999)**, Rawls extended his justice theory internationally. He concluded:
- Liberal and "decent" peoples (non-liberal but respectful of basic human rights) can coexist in a "Society of Peoples"
- States owe **a "duty of assistance"** to "burdened societies" — those lacking the capacity to organise themselves as just societies
- But there is **no global difference principle** — Rawls did not think global institutions should redistribute to maximise the condition of the globally worst-off
- This is more modest than many expected from the author of the Difference Principle

**Critique:** Rawls seems to accept a world where enormous inequalities between nations can persist, as long as all societies become "well-ordered." His framework has been criticised as too state-centric.

### Peter Singer: Global Obligations Without Borders

In **"Famine, Affluence, and Morality" (1972)** and later work, Singer argues:
- If you can prevent something bad (suffering, death from poverty) without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, you have an obligation to do so
- **Geography is morally irrelevant** — the suffering of a child in Bihar and a child in Manchester deserve equal weight
- Affluent individuals and nations have strong moral obligations to give substantially to reduce global poverty
- This underlies the **effective altruism** movement — using evidence to maximise the good done per rupee/dollar donated

**Critique:** Singer's demanding principle would require wealthy people to give until they reach marginal utility equality — most consider this supererogatory (beyond ordinary duty).

### Thomas Pogge: Negative Duty to Stop Harming

**Thomas Pogge** (Columbia University) makes a structurally different argument in **"World Poverty and Human Rights" (2002)**:
- Rich nations and global institutions are not merely failing to help the poor — they are **actively harming them** through:
  - **TRIPS agreement** (intellectual property rules that raise medicine prices in poor countries)
  - **Tax havens** (allow corporations to avoid taxes in developing countries)
  - **Arms exports** to authoritarian regimes
  - **"Resource privilege"** — buying resources from dictators, which funds oppression
- This converts the obligation from a demanding positive duty (Singer) to a less controversial **negative duty** — stop causing harm
- India relevance: India has fought for TRIPS flexibilities (compulsory licensing for HIV drugs — Cipla's generic ARVs in 2001); has pushed for global tax reform at G20

### Climate Justice

Climate change raises intergenerational and international justice issues simultaneously:

| Principle | Content |
|---|---|
| **CBDR (Common But Differentiated Responsibilities)** | Developed countries historically emitted most greenhouse gases; bear greater responsibility for mitigation |
| **Right to development** | Developing nations cannot be asked to sacrifice growth that developed nations already achieved |
| **Intergenerational justice** | Present generations must not impose irreversible climate damage on future generations (John Rawls — "just savings principle"; Hans Jonas — "responsibility for the future") |
| **Loss and Damage** | Nations most vulnerable to climate change (small island states, Bangladesh) deserve compensation for losses they did not cause |
| **Climate finance** | Rich countries promised $100 billion/year by 2020 to help developing countries — commitment frequently unmet |

India's position: India signed the **Paris Agreement (2015)** and has committed to Net Zero by 2070; champions the principle of CBDR; advocates for climate justice and technology transfer to developing nations.

## Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

The **Responsibility to Protect** doctrine was endorsed at the **UN World Summit in September 2005** (Outcome Document paragraphs 138–139). It represents a reframing of state sovereignty — from a right to rule, to a responsibility to protect.

### Three Pillars of R2P

| Pillar | Content |
|---|---|
| **Pillar I** | Each state has the primary responsibility to protect its population from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity |
| **Pillar II** | The international community has a responsibility to assist states in meeting this obligation (capacity building, early warning) |
| **Pillar III** | When a state is manifestly failing to protect (or is actively perpetrating atrocities), the international community may take collective action — including armed intervention — through the UNSC |

**Application:**
- **Libya (2011):** UNSC Resolution 1973 authorised NATO military intervention; Gaddafi's regime fell. Initially cited as R2P success, but the intervention became regime change — generating backlash and making states more cautious about endorsing Pillar III.
- **Syria (2011–present):** Mass atrocities occurred; UNSC was paralysed by Russian and Chinese vetoes. R2P failed its most serious test.

**India's position on R2P:** India is cautious — it voted for UNSC Resolution 1973 on Libya but expressed misgivings when the mission exceeded civilian protection. India emphasises sovereignty and non-interference; supports Pillars I and II but is sceptical of Pillar III. India worries about R2P being used selectively to justify regime change.

## Ethics of Foreign Aid

Foreign aid raises distinct ethical questions:

**Arguments that aid is a moral duty (Singer, Peter Unger):** Affluent nations can prevent serious suffering at minimal cost to themselves — not doing so is morally indefensible.

**Arguments against aid (Dambisa Moyo, "Dead Aid," 2009; Peter Bauer):**
- Aid creates dependency — African governments rely on aid rather than building tax systems and productive economies
- Aid funds corrupt governments — dictators divert aid to consolidate power
- Tied aid (aid conditional on buying donor country goods/services) serves donor commercial interests
- Aid undermines local businesses by flooding markets with free goods (food aid, second-hand clothes)

**Middle ground (Paul Collier, "The Bottom Billion"):**
- Targeted aid for specific purposes (vaccines, infrastructure, institutions) can be effective
- The problem is not aid per se but its design and governance

**India as both aid recipient and donor:**
- India still receives Official Development Assistance (ODA) from some partners (Japan for infrastructure)
- India is increasingly an aid provider — ITEC, LoCs, development finance to neighbours and Africa
- This dual role requires ethical consistency: India should not accept conditions from donors that it is unwilling to impose on its own aid recipients

**Conditionality ethics:** Western donors attaching human rights, governance, or democracy conditions to aid raises ethical questions — is this a genuine promotion of values, or an instrument of geopolitical leverage?

## International Criminal Court (ICC) and Accountability

The **International Criminal Court (ICC)**, established by the **Rome Statute (entered into force 2002)**, is the first permanent international criminal tribunal with jurisdiction over:
- Genocide
- Crimes against humanity
- War crimes
- Crime of aggression (added 2018)

**Key facts:**
- Seat: The Hague, Netherlands
- 124 member states (as of 2024)
- **Non-members:** USA, China, Russia (withdrew from Rome Statute process); India (not a party)

**India's position:** India has not ratified the Rome Statute, citing concerns about:
- Sovereignty and the ICC's jurisdiction over Indian nationals
- Political bias — perception that ICC disproportionately targets African leaders
- The veto power of P5 members meaning great power impunity persists

**Ethical tension:** The ICC represents the principle that no leader is above international law for mass atrocities — but its selective application (cases overwhelmingly from Africa, while atrocities by powerful states go unaddressed) undermines its moral legitimacy.

## Case Studies for UPSC Ethics Paper

**Case Study 1 — Humanitarian aid to a rights-violating government:**
India is approached by a neighbouring country experiencing famine. That country's government is engaged in ethnic persecution. Should India provide food aid? Ethical considerations: humanitarian imperative (obligation to prevent suffering); legitimacy concern (aid may stabilise a rights-violating regime); conditionality (attach strings or not?); sovereignty of recipient vs moral agency of donor.

**Case Study 2 — UN vote on human rights resolution:**
India must vote on a UN Human Rights Council resolution criticising the human rights record of a country with which it has strategic economic ties. Ethical considerations: India's commitment to multilateralism and human rights; sovereignty and non-interference principle; strategic consequences of antagonising a partner; whether abstention is an ethical choice.

**Case Study 3 — Climate justice at COP:**
India's negotiator is told by the government to hold firm on CBDR and reject a binding emissions target, despite scientific advice that current trajectories are catastrophic. Ethical considerations: intergenerational justice; India's right to development; global solidarity; the difference between national interest and global interest.

## Exam Strategy

**For Mains (GS4):**
- Just War Theory is frequently tested — be clear on all six jus ad bellum criteria and two jus in bello principles
- R2P three pillars: state responsibility → international assistance → collective action; Libya as application, Syria as failure
- Global justice: contrast Rawls (modest international obligations), Singer (demanding positive duty), Pogge (negative duty to reform global institutions)
- Climate justice: CBDR principle, intergenerational justice, loss and damage — link to India's own position
- ICC: non-membership of USA, China, Russia, India — ethical implications; selective justice concern
- For case studies: use a structured format — identify stakeholders, competing values, courses of action, ethical justification for choice

**Key thinkers to remember:**
- **Augustine and Aquinas** — origins of Just War Theory
- **John Rawls** — "Law of Peoples"; duty of assistance (not global difference principle)
- **Peter Singer** — moral cosmopolitanism; geography irrelevant; effective altruism
- **Thomas Pogge** — negative duty; global institutions harm the poor
- **Dambisa Moyo** — "Dead Aid"; critique of traditional foreign aid model
