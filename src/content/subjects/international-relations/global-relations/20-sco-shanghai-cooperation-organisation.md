## Origins and Evolution

The **Shanghai Cooperation Organisation (SCO)** is a Eurasian political, economic, and security intergovernmental organisation. It traces its origins to the **Shanghai Five**, a grouping formed on **26 April 1996** among China, Russia, Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, and Tajikistan to resolve border demarcation and demilitarisation issues along the former Soviet-Chinese frontier.

On **15 June 2001**, the SCO was formally established in Shanghai when **Uzbekistan** joined the Shanghai Five, bringing membership to six. The SCO Charter was signed on **7 June 2002** in St. Petersburg and entered into force on **19 September 2003**.

**Headquarters:** Beijing, China (Secretariat)
**Headquarters of RATS:** Tashkent, Uzbekistan (Regional Anti-Terrorist Structure)

**Observer States:** Mongolia; Afghanistan (inactive due to Taliban governance)

**Dialogue Partners (14):** Sri Lanka, Turkey, Cambodia, Azerbaijan, Nepal, Armenia, Egypt, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Kuwait, Myanmar, the Maldives, and the United Arab Emirates

India and Pakistan were admitted as full members at the **Astana Summit, June 2017** — a significant expansion that gave the organisation a South Asian dimension and collectively made the SCO home to about **40% of the world's population** and approximately **30% of global GDP**.

## SCO Charter: Purposes and Principles

The **SCO Charter (2002)** defines the organisation's purposes:

1. Strengthening mutual trust and good-neighbourliness among member states
2. Promoting effective cooperation in political, trade, economic, scientific-technical, cultural, educational, energy, transport, environmental, and other spheres
3. Jointly maintaining peace, security, and stability in the region
4. Moving towards establishing a new, democratic, just, and rational political and economic international order

**"Shanghai Spirit"** — the SCO's guiding principle — emphasises: mutual trust, mutual benefit, equality, consultation, respect for cultural diversity, and pursuit of common development.

## Three Pillars of SCO Cooperation

The SCO operates across three broad pillars:

### 1. Security Cooperation
- **Regional Anti-Terrorist Structure (RATS):** Based in Tashkent; the SCO's permanent body for coordinating counter-terrorism, separatism, and extremism (collectively called the "Three Evils")
- Member states share intelligence on terrorist groups, conduct joint military exercises (e.g., **Peace Mission** series of exercises)
- Counter-narcotics cooperation — particularly important given Afghanistan's opium production

### 2. Economic Cooperation
- Facilitation of trade and investment among member states
- SCO Business Council and SCO Interbank Consortium
- Energy cooperation — Russia, Kazakhstan, and Uzbekistan are significant energy producers; China and India are major consumers
- Transport and connectivity — SCO promotes land transport corridors linking China, Central Asia, Russia, and South Asia

### 3. Cultural and Humanitarian Cooperation
- SCO University (network of universities in member states)
- Youth exchanges, cultural festivals
- SCO Tourism Programme

## India and the SCO

India's engagement with the SCO has evolved through three phases:

**Phase 1 (2005-2017): Observer status** — India was an observer since the Tashkent Summit of 2005; participated in deliberations without voting rights.

**Phase 2 (2017 onwards): Full membership** — India and Pakistan's simultaneous admission at Astana 2017 was a result of years of diplomatic effort, particularly India's push following the nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG) entry attempt. China backed Pakistan's simultaneous entry as a condition for India's membership.

**India's priorities within SCO:**
- **Counter-terrorism:** India has consistently pushed for action against state-sponsored cross-border terrorism — but has been constrained by China-Pakistan alignment within RATS
- **Connectivity:** India has opposed the **CPEC (China-Pakistan Economic Corridor)** component of the Belt and Road Initiative as it passes through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir; India therefore does not endorse the BRI-related connectivity projects
- **Multilateralism:** India has used SCO summits to engage with Central Asian nations and build diplomatic capital
- **Energy and trade:** Accessing Central Asian energy resources and markets

**Tension:** India's most significant challenge within the SCO is the structural alignment of China and Pakistan against Indian positions — particularly on terrorism, CPEC, and Kashmir.

## SCO and UPSC Relevance

The SCO is relevant to multiple UPSC themes:

- **India's neighbourhood and extended neighbourhood** — Central Asia connects to India's energy and trade interests
- **Counter-terrorism diplomacy** — RATS and the limits of multilateral counter-terrorism where state sponsors of terrorism are members
- **India-China-Russia triangle** — SCO is one of the few platforms where all three interact
- **Multilateralism vs. bilateral tensions** — India engaging Pakistan at SCO while bilateral relations remain fraught
- **Connectivity** — India's non-participation in CPEC/BRI vs. SCO's connectivity agenda

## Exam Strategy

**Non-negotiable facts:**
- SCO founded: **15 June 2001** (Shanghai Five existed since 1996)
- Headquarters: **Secretariat — Beijing; RATS — Tashkent**
- India and Pakistan joined: **June 2017** (Astana Summit)
- Iran joined: **2023**; Belarus joined: **2024** — total **10 members**
- SCO Charter signed: **7 June 2002**, in force from **19 September 2003**
- "Three Evils": **terrorism, separatism, extremism** — core SCO security mandate

**Conceptual angle for Mains:** SCO represents India's paradox — it is a founding-principle multilateral body that India values for Central Asia access and great-power engagement, yet India's core strategic concerns (CPEC, cross-border terrorism) are structurally blocked within the SCO by China-Pakistan solidarity.
