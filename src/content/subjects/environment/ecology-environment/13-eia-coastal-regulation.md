## Overview

Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) is the cornerstone of India's environmental governance -- the process by which the potential environmental consequences of a proposed project are evaluated before it receives clearance to proceed. The Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) framework provides a parallel regulatory mechanism specifically for India's 7,516.6 km coastline and its fragile marine ecosystems. Together, EIA and CRZ constitute the primary "green clearance" architecture through which development is reconciled with environmental protection.

For UPSC, this topic is among the most frequently tested in both Prelims and Mains under GS3 (Environment) -- questions test understanding of the EIA process, the controversies around the Draft EIA 2020, CRZ zone classifications, and the tension between development and conservation.

## Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)

### What Is EIA?

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Definition** | A systematic process to identify, predict, and evaluate the environmental consequences of a proposed project or development activity before a decision is made on whether to grant clearance |
| **Legal basis** | Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 -- Section 3; first EIA Notification issued in **1994**; replaced by the current **EIA Notification, 2006** |
| **Objective** | Ensure informed decision-making by incorporating environmental considerations into the planning process; prevent environmental damage before it occurs |
| **Regulator** | Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC) for Category A projects; State Environment Impact Assessment Authority (SEIAA) for Category B projects |

### Project Categories Under EIA 2006

| Category | Clearance Authority | EIA Required? | Public Consultation? |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Category A** | MoEFCC (through Expert Appraisal Committee -- EAC) | Mandatory full EIA | Mandatory |
| **Category B1** | SEIAA (through State Expert Appraisal Committee -- SEAC) | Full EIA required | Mandatory |
| **Category B2** | SEIAA (through SEAC) | No EIA required (only application Form-I) | Not required |

> **For Prelims:** Category A = MoEFCC + EAC; Category B = SEIAA + SEAC. The screening stage determines whether a Category B project is B1 (full EIA required) or B2 (exempted).

## Four Stages of the EIA Process

### Stage 1: Screening

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Determines whether a project requires a full EIA study |
| **Applies to** | Category B projects only (Category A automatically requires full EIA) |
| **Done by** | SEAC at the state level |
| **Outcome** | Project classified as B1 (full EIA required) or B2 (no EIA, simplified process) based on location sensitivity, scale, and potential impact |

### Stage 2: Scoping

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Defines the boundaries, focus, and methodology of the EIA study |
| **Done by** | EAC (Category A) or SEAC (Category B1) |
| **Output** | **Terms of Reference (ToR)** -- specifies which environmental parameters must be studied: air quality, water quality, soil, biodiversity, socio-economic impacts, noise, and more |
| **Timeline** | ToR must be issued within **60 days** of receipt of complete application; if not, deemed granted |
| **Baseline data** | The project proponent must collect baseline environmental data for **one season** (reduced from the earlier requirement of one full year under certain conditions) |

### Stage 3: Public Consultation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Allows local communities and stakeholders to express concerns about the project's environmental and social impacts |
| **Components** | (a) **Public hearing** conducted at or near the project site; (b) **Written responses** from stakeholders |
| **Organised by** | State Pollution Control Board (SPCB) or UT Pollution Control Committee |
| **Timeline** | Public hearing must be conducted within **45 days** of the SPCB receiving the request |
| **Notice** | At least **30 days** advance notice in local newspapers and on the SPCB website |
| **Exemptions** | Certain projects exempted from public consultation: modernisation/expansion projects (within existing premises, no increase in pollution load), projects in industrial estates with prior EA, and Category B2 projects |

### Stage 4: Appraisal

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Final evaluation of the EIA report, Environmental Management Plan (EMP), and public consultation proceedings |
| **Done by** | EAC (Category A) or SEAC (Category B1) |
| **Outcome** | Recommendation for or against environmental clearance (EC); final decision by MoEFCC (Category A) or SEIAA (Category B) |
| **EC validity** | Typically **7 years** for construction projects (extendable); **30 years** for mining projects (with 5-yearly reviews) |
| **Conditions** | EC granted with specific conditions -- emission limits, compensatory measures, monitoring requirements, compliance reports |

> **For Mains:** The EIA process is designed to be participatory (public consultation), science-based (EIA report), and expert-reviewed (EAC/SEAC appraisal). However, critics argue that in practice, EIA reports are prepared by consultants hired by the project proponent (conflict of interest), public hearings are often poorly conducted, and compliance monitoring is weak.

## Draft EIA Notification, 2020 — Controversies

| Issue | Draft 2020 Provision | Criticism |
|-------|---------------------|----------|
| **Post-facto clearance** | Allowed projects that began without prior EC to apply for clearance after the fact (with penalty) | Supreme Court has held that post-facto EC is "alien to environmental jurisprudence" and "anathema to the EIA notification"; legitimises violations |
| **Reduced public consultation** | Public hearing period shortened from 30 days to 20 days; expanded list of exemptions from public consultation | Curtails democratic participation in environmental decision-making; silences affected communities |
| **Expanded exemptions** | Several project categories exempted from EIA: inland waterways, expansion/modernisation up to 50% of capacity, projects in "linear" categories | Exempts environmentally significant projects from scrutiny |
| **Border area exemption** | Projects within 100 km of border areas exempted from public consultation on grounds of "strategic" importance | Large areas in northeastern, northern, and western India excluded from public participation |
| **Compliance reports** | Changed from half-yearly to annual self-monitoring reports | Reduces oversight frequency; delays detection of violations |
| **Violation window** | Introduced a "violation window" allowing projects operating in violation to apply for clearance | Incentivises "construct first, seek permission later" approach |

| **Current status** | The Draft EIA 2020 faced massive public opposition (over 20 lakh comments received during public consultation); as of March 2026, the final notification has not been issued; the EIA Notification 2006 (as amended) remains in force |

## Strategic Environmental Assessment (SEA)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Definition** | Assessment of environmental impacts at the policy, plan, or programme level -- as opposed to EIA which assesses individual projects |
| **Significance** | Evaluates cumulative and regional impacts of multiple projects; can prevent environmental damage at a strategic level before individual projects are proposed |
| **India's status** | No formal SEA framework in India; recommended by various committees and expert groups; some states have attempted SEA for industrial corridors and special economic zones |
| **International practice** | EU SEA Directive (2001/42/EC) requires SEA for plans and programmes; widely practised in Europe, Canada, and New Zealand |

## Environmental Clearance for Specific Sectors

### Mining

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Clearance required** | EC under EIA 2006 + Forest Clearance (if in forest area) under Forest Conservation Act, 1980 + Mining Lease under MMDR Act, 1957 |
| **Key issues** | Deforestation, water table depletion, air pollution (PM10/PM2.5), displacement of communities, biodiversity loss |
| **Mine closure plan** | Mandatory mine closure plan with financial assurance; progressive mine closure during operation; final closure and rehabilitation after mining ends |

### Infrastructure (Highways, Railways, Airports)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Linear projects** | Highways, railways, pipelines -- require EC if they pass through ecologically sensitive areas; Wildlife Clearance if through Protected Areas |
| **Key issues** | Habitat fragmentation, wildlife corridors disrupted, noise pollution, land acquisition |
| **Mitigation** | Wildlife underpasses/overpasses, noise barriers, compensatory afforestation |

## Compensatory Afforestation (CAMPA)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Legal basis** | Compensatory Afforestation Fund Act, **2016**; Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority (CAMPA) |
| **How it works** | When forest land is diverted for non-forest purposes (mining, infrastructure, industry), the user agency must: (a) provide equivalent non-forest land for afforestation, OR (b) pay the **Net Present Value (NPV)** of the diverted forest land |
| **Fund size** | Accumulated corpus of approximately **Rs 95,000 crore**; Rs 54,685 crore transferred from ad-hoc CAMPA to the statutory CAMPA (with Supreme Court approval, January 2019) |
| **Distribution** | **90%** to State CAMPA funds; **10%** retained by the National CAMPA (Centre) |
| **Use** | Compensatory afforestation, assisted natural regeneration, wildlife habitat improvement, forest management, community participation |
| **Criticism** | Survival rate of planted trees often low; compensatory plantations cannot replicate old-growth forest biodiversity; long delays in fund utilisation by states |

> **For Prelims:** CAMPA: Compensatory Afforestation Fund Act 2016; ~Rs 95,000 crore corpus; 90:10 (State:Centre) distribution; mandatory when forest land is diverted for non-forest use.

## Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification, 2019

### Evolution of CRZ Framework

| Notification | Year | Key Feature |
|-------------|------|-------------|
| **CRZ 1991** | 1991 | First CRZ notification under EPA 1986; established the concept of regulating coastal areas |
| **CRZ 2011** | 2011 | Revised categories; introduced Island Protection Zone (IPZ) |
| **CRZ 2019** | **2019** | Current framework; replaced CRZ 2011; more flexible approach; reduced No Development Zone (NDZ) in densely populated rural areas |

### CRZ Categories Under CRZ 2019

| Category | Area Covered | Key Restrictions |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **CRZ-I(A)** | Ecologically sensitive areas: mangroves, coral reefs, sand dunes, mudflats, national parks, marine parks, wildlife habitats, salt marshes, turtle nesting grounds, areas of archaeological importance | No new construction permitted; only activities related to conservation and traditional use |
| **CRZ-I(B)** | Intertidal zone -- area between Low Tide Line (LTL) and High Tide Line (HTL) | Land reclamation, bunding, or disturbing the natural course of seawater prohibited |
| **CRZ-II** | Developed urban areas -- substantially built-up areas within existing municipal/urban limits with >50% built-up plots | Buildings permitted on the landward side of existing road or authorised structures; FSI norms as per town planning regulations |
| **CRZ-III(A)** | Rural areas with population density >2,161 per sq km (2011 Census) | NDZ of **50 metres** from HTL; agriculture, horticulture, gardens, pastures, parks, and playfields permitted in NDZ |
| **CRZ-III(B)** | Rural areas with population density <2,161 per sq km | NDZ of **200 metres** from HTL |
| **CRZ-IV(A)** | Water area from LTL to **12 nautical miles** seaward | No untreated sewage/effluent discharge; no dumping of solid waste; traditional fishing rights protected |
| **CRZ-IV(B)** | Water areas of tidal-influenced water bodies (creeks, rivers, backwaters, estuaries) | Similar restrictions as CRZ-IV(A) |

### Key Features of CRZ 2019

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **NDZ relaxation** | Reduced NDZ from 200m to 50m in densely populated rural areas (CRZ-III(A)) -- allows construction closer to the coastline in areas already under population pressure |
| **Tourism** | Temporary tourism facilities (beach shacks, eco-tourism) permitted in CRZ-III areas with state government approval |
| **Floor Space Index (FSI)** | CRZ-II areas can use FSI norms as per existing town planning rules -- removes the earlier freeze on FSI |
| **Coastal Zone Management Plans (CZMPs)** | Mandatory for all coastal states/UTs; maps demarcating HTL, LTL, hazard line, and CRZ classifications; prepared by the National Centre for Sustainable Coastal Management (NCSCM) |
| **Island Coastal Regulation Zone (ICRZ)** | Separate regulations for Andaman & Nicobar and Lakshadweep islands; 20m NDZ for islands with population <400/sq km |

> **For Mains:** The CRZ 2019 Notification relaxed several restrictions, especially the NDZ reduction in densely populated rural areas. Critics argue this opens fragile coastlines to construction and tourism-driven degradation, particularly in ecologically sensitive states like Goa, Kerala, and Odisha. Defenders argue the previous NDZ of 200m was impractical in already populated areas and denied livelihood opportunities to coastal communities. Discuss.

## Role of Expert Appraisal Committee (EAC)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Constitution** | Constituted by MoEFCC under EIA Notification 2006; separate EACs for different sectors (mining, thermal power, infrastructure, industry, etc.) |
| **Composition** | Experts in environment, ecology, engineering, health, social sciences; includes academicians and professionals |
| **Function** | Conducts scoping (issues ToR), appraises EIA reports, recommends grant or rejection of EC |
| **Decision** | EAC recommendation is advisory; final decision rests with MoEFCC (for Category A) |
| **Concerns** | Conflict of interest (some members have industry links), rushed appraisals (too many projects per meeting), inadequate site visits |

## National Green Tribunal (NGT) and Environmental Clearance

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Established** | **18 October 2010** under the National Green Tribunal Act, 2010 |
| **Purpose** | Effective and expeditious disposal of cases relating to environmental protection and conservation of forests |
| **Jurisdiction** | Cases arising under 7 environmental legislations: EPA 1986, Water Act 1974, Air Act 1981, Forest Conservation Act 1980, PLLA Act 1991, Biodiversity Act 2002, and EIA Notification matters |
| **Structure** | Principal Bench in New Delhi; Circuit Benches in Bhopal, Pune, Kolkata, and Chennai |
| **Significance for EIA** | NGT has emerged as a key forum for challenging environmental clearances, EIA violations, and CRZ breaches; has passed landmark orders on river pollution, waste management, and illegal construction in CRZ areas |
| **Powers** | Can award relief, compensation, and restitution of property and environment; orders are binding; appeals lie to the Supreme Court within 90 days |
| **Criticism** | Vacancies in expert members, limited enforcement capacity, states sometimes delay compliance with NGT orders |

## Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 — The Legal Foundation

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Enacted** | **23 May 1986** (in the wake of the Bhopal Gas Tragedy, 1984) |
| **Parent legislation** | The umbrella law under which all environmental notifications (EIA 2006, CRZ 2019) are issued |
| **Key provisions** | Section 3: Central Government power to take measures for environmental protection; Section 5: Power to give directions; Section 6: Rules for environmental standards; Section 7: No handling of hazardous substances except in compliance |
| **Penalties** | Imprisonment up to 5 years and/or fine up to Rs 1 lakh; continuing offence: additional fine up to Rs 5,000/day |
| **Significance** | Provides the legal basis for the entire EIA and CRZ framework; without EPA 1986, MoEFCC cannot issue environmental notifications |

## Forest Clearance — Forest Conservation Act, 1980

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Regulates diversion of forest land for non-forest purposes (mining, infrastructure, industry, hydroelectric projects) |
| **Amended** | Forest (Conservation) Amendment Act, **2023** -- renamed to Forest Conservation (Amendment) Act; exempted certain categories of land (within 100 km of international borders for "strategic" projects; forests not recorded as forest in government records before 1980) |
| **Two-stage clearance** | Stage I: In-principle approval by MoEFCC; Stage II: Final approval after compliance with conditions (compensatory afforestation, NPV payment, wildlife clearance if needed) |
| **Relation to EIA** | Many projects require BOTH environmental clearance (EIA Notification) AND forest clearance (FCA 1980) -- separate but often simultaneous processes |
| **CAMPA link** | All diversion of forest land requires compensatory afforestation and NPV payment into the CAMPA fund |

> **For Prelims:** EPA 1986 = umbrella law for EIA and CRZ. Forest Conservation Act 1980 (amended 2023) = regulates forest land diversion. NGT = established 2010; cases under 7 environmental laws; principal bench New Delhi.

## Vocabulary

### Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)
- **Pronunciation:** /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmɛntəl ˈɪmpækt əˌsɛsmənt/
- **Definition:** A systematic, interdisciplinary process to identify, predict, evaluate, and mitigate the biophysical, social, and other relevant effects of a proposed development project before a decision on environmental clearance is made -- serving as a preventive tool that integrates environmental considerations into the planning and decision-making process.
- **Origin:** The concept originated with the US National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA, 1969), which required federal agencies to assess environmental impacts of major actions; India adopted EIA through the EIA Notification, 1994 (under EPA, 1986), replaced by the current EIA Notification, 2006.

### No Development Zone (NDZ)
- **Pronunciation:** /noʊ dɪˈvɛləpmənt zoʊn/
- **Definition:** A designated buffer area along India's coastline, measured from the High Tide Line (HTL) landward, within which construction of buildings and other development activities are prohibited or severely restricted -- designed to protect fragile coastal ecosystems from encroachment and to serve as a natural buffer against storm surges and sea-level rise.
- **Origin:** Introduced under the CRZ Notification, 1991; the NDZ width varies by category: 50 metres in densely populated rural areas (CRZ-III(A)) and 200 metres in less populated rural areas (CRZ-III(B)) under the CRZ Notification, 2019.

## Key Terms

### Expert Appraisal Committee (EAC)
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈɛkspɜːrt əˈpreɪzəl kəˈmɪti/
- **Definition:** A multi-disciplinary committee constituted by MoEFCC under the EIA Notification 2006, comprising experts in environmental science, ecology, engineering, health, and social sciences, tasked with scoping (determining Terms of Reference), appraising Environmental Impact Assessment reports, and recommending the grant or rejection of environmental clearance for Category A projects -- the equivalent body at the state level is the State Expert Appraisal Committee (SEAC) for Category B projects.
- **Context:** Separate sector-specific EACs exist for mining, thermal power, infrastructure, river valley projects, and industrial projects; EAC recommendations are advisory -- the final decision rests with MoEFCC or SEIAA; concerns have been raised about rushed appraisals, conflicts of interest, and inadequate site visits.
- **UPSC Relevance:** GS3 (Environment). Prelims: EAC for Category A (MoEFCC), SEAC for Category B (SEIAA). Mains: evaluate the effectiveness of the EAC system in ensuring rigorous environmental appraisal; discuss reforms needed.

### Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority (CAMPA)
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈkæmpə/
- **Definition:** A statutory body established under the Compensatory Afforestation Fund Act, 2016, to manage and utilise funds collected from user agencies that divert forest land for non-forest purposes -- the funds (comprising Net Present Value of diverted forests, compensatory afforestation costs, and penal compensatory afforestation amounts) are used for afforestation, forest management, wildlife habitat improvement, and community participation across India.
- **Context:** The accumulated CAMPA corpus is approximately Rs 95,000 crore, of which Rs 54,685 crore has been transferred from ad-hoc CAMPA to the statutory body (January 2019, with Supreme Court approval); funds are distributed 90% to states and 10% to the Centre; however, utilisation has been slow, and critics question whether planted monocultures can replace the biodiversity of natural forests.
- **UPSC Relevance:** GS3 (Environment, Biodiversity). Prelims: CAMPA Act 2016, fund corpus (~Rs 95,000 crore), 90:10 distribution. Mains: evaluate the effectiveness of compensatory afforestation as a tool for forest conservation; discuss the tension between infrastructure development and forest protection.
