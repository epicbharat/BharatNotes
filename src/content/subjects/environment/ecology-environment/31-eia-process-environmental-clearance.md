## Introduction

**Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)** is the process of evaluating the likely environmental effects of a proposed project before it is approved. India's EIA regime is governed by the **EIA Notification, 2006** (issued under Section 3 of the **Environment (Protection) Act, 1986**). EIA serves as the gatekeeper between development ambitions and environmental protection — a recurring theme in GS3 questions on environment governance, biodiversity, and sustainable development.

---

## Legal Framework

### Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 (EPA)

- Enacted after the 1984 **Bhopal Gas Tragedy** (and inspired by the 1972 Stockholm Conference on Human Environment)
- Provides the umbrella framework for all environmental regulations in India
- Under Section 3, the Central Government has powers to take measures to protect and improve environmental quality
- The **EIA Notification 2006** is the primary subsidiary legislation under EPA 1986 for project-level clearance

### EIA Notification, 2006

- Notified on **14 September 2006** by the Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC)
- Replaced the earlier EIA Notification of **1994**
- Makes environmental clearance (EC) mandatory for **specified categories of projects and activities** before any construction or expansion begins

---

## Project Categories — A and B

The 2006 notification classifies projects into two categories based on scale, potential environmental impact, and national/inter-state significance:

### Category A — Central Appraisal

- Projects with potentially **significant national or inter-state environmental impacts**
- Appraised at the **national level** by:
  - **Impact Assessment Agency (IAA):** MoEFCC
  - **Expert Appraisal Committee (EAC):** Sector-specific committees of technical experts (e.g., EAC for Mining, EAC for Infrastructure, EAC for Industry)
- Examples: Large mining projects (>50 ha), thermal power plants (>500 MW), river valley projects (>50 MW hydropower), nuclear power plants, large chemical industries, airports, ports, expressways

### Category B — State Appraisal

- Projects with primarily **local or state-level** environmental impacts
- Appraised at the **state level** by:
  - **State Environment Impact Assessment Authority (SEIAA)**
  - **State Level Expert Appraisal Committee (SEAC)**

Category B is further divided:
- **B1:** Requires full EIA study and public hearing
- **B2:** Does not require an EIA report (granted clearance based on pre-feasibility report and Form-1 only)

---

## The EIA Process — Step by Step

### Stage 1 — Screening

- Determines whether a project requires full EIA or falls under B2 (no EIA needed)
- Proponent submits **Form-1** (basic project information)
- SEIAA/EAC decides the category and scope

### Stage 2 — Scoping

- Applicable for **Category A and B1** projects
- The EAC/SEAC issues **Terms of Reference (ToR)** — the specific issues the EIA study must cover for that project
- Proponent prepares the ToR application; EAC grants ToR within 60 days
- Scoping determines the depth and breadth of the EIA study

### Stage 3 — EIA Study and Preparation of EIA/EMP Report

- Proponent conducts the **EIA study** (typically 12–18 months, covering one full seasonal cycle)
- EIA report must cover: baseline environmental data, project description, impact prediction, mitigation measures, **Environmental Management Plan (EMP)**, alternatives analysis, and risk assessment
- Must be conducted by an **accredited EIA consultant** (accredited by Quality Council of India — QCI)

### Stage 4 — Public Hearing

- **Mandatory** for all Category A projects and Category B1 projects
- Conducted by the **State Pollution Control Board (SPCB)** or **Union Territory Pollution Control Committee**
- At least **30 days' notice** must be given via local newspapers and official gazette before the public hearing
- Hearing must be held at/near the project site; local communities, NGOs, and affected persons can raise concerns
- **Exemptions:** Linear projects (roads, pipelines) in difficult terrain; certain strategic/defence projects; expansion projects below threshold; projects in notified SEZs may have modified procedures

### Stage 5 — Appraisal

- EAC/SEAC reviews the EIA report + public hearing proceedings + responses to public concerns
- Can request additional information, site visits, or revised EMP
- EAC provides recommendations to the competent authority

### Stage 6 — Grant or Rejection of Environmental Clearance (EC)

- **Category A:** MoEFCC grants or rejects EC, based on EAC recommendation
- **Category B:** SEIAA grants or rejects EC, based on SEAC recommendation
- EC is typically valid for **5 years** (10 years for mining projects) from the date of commencement
- EC includes **binding conditions** — project proponent must submit compliance reports every 6 months

## Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification, 2019

Coastal areas are governed by the **CRZ Notification, 2019** (issued under EPA 1986; replaces CRZ 2011 Notification). The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change issues and administers CRZ regulations.

### CRZ Zones

| Zone | Description | Regulatory Approach |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **CRZ-I** | Ecologically sensitive areas — mangroves, coral reefs, national parks, wildlife sanctuaries, inter-tidal zones | Strictest regulation; no development permitted; MoEFCC clearance required |
| **CRZ-II** | Urban areas that were developed up to the shoreline before 1991 (municipal limits) | Development permitted along existing streets; no new construction seaward of existing buildings |
| **CRZ-IIIA** | Rural areas, densely populated (>2,161 persons/km²) | No Development Zone (NDZ): **50 metres** from High Tide Line (HTL) — reduced from 200 m in 2011 notification |
| **CRZ-IIIB** | Rural areas, sparsely populated | NDZ: **200 metres** from HTL |
| **CRZ-IV** | Aquatic areas from Low Tide Line (LTL) to 12 nautical miles seaward | Focus on fisheries regulation, sand mining prohibition |

**Clearance authority under CRZ 2019:**
- MoEFCC: handles CRZ-I and CRZ-IV clearances
- State governments/SCZMAs (State Coastal Zone Management Authorities): handle CRZ-II and CRZ-III clearances

**Key 2019 changes from 2011:**
- CRZ-IIIA NDZ reduced to 50 m (from 200 m) — highly controversial; critics say it opens densely populated rural coasts to commercial development
- More projects permitted in CRZ areas (tourism, infrastructure)
- Hazard line mapping mandated as a climate adaptation measure
- Simplified procedures for eco-tourism in CRZ-I areas (mangroves, coral)

## Forest Clearance — Forest Conservation Act, 1980

### Forest Conservation Act, 1980 (FCA)

- Prohibits diversion of forest land for **non-forestry purposes** without prior approval of the Central Government
- Two-stage clearance: **Stage I (In-Principle)** → conditions fulfilled → **Stage II (Final Clearance)**
- Implemented by Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change

### Forest (Conservation) Amendment Act, 2023

The **Van Sanrakshan Evam Samvardhan Adhiniyam (Forest Conservation Amendment Act), 2023** — passed by Lok Sabha on 26 July 2023 and Rajya Sabha on 2 August 2023; received Presidential assent in August 2023.

**Key provisions:**

| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Title change** | Renamed to "Van Sanrakshan Evam Samvardhan Adhiniyam" (Forest Conservation and Enhancement Act) |
| **100 km border exemption** | Forest land within **100 km from international borders, Line of Control (LoC), or Line of Actual Control (LAC)** can be used for **strategic linear projects of national importance and national security** without prior forest clearance |
| **Security infrastructure** | Up to **10 ha** of forest land for security-related infrastructure; up to 5 ha in Left Wing Extremism (LWE) affected districts for public utility projects |
| **Village connectivity** | Up to **0.10 ha** for roads providing connectivity to habitation |
| **New permitted activities** | Infrastructure for frontline forest staff, eco-tourism facilities, safari, zoo within forest areas are no longer treated as "non-forestry activities" — exempt from FCA |
| **Surveys exempt** | Surveys and investigations (geological, geophysical, remote sensing) in forest areas are not considered "non-forestry activities" — no FCA clearance needed |
| **Carbon sink targets** | Amendment aims to support India's NDC target of creating 2.5–3.0 billion tonnes of additional carbon sink by 2030 |

**Controversy around the 2023 Amendment:**
- Critics (environmentalists, tribal rights groups) argue the 100 km border exemption covers large forested areas including critical northeast India habitats; combined with LWE exemptions, significant forest areas may be opened up without clearance
- Tribal communities fear their rights under the **Forest Rights Act, 2006** may be bypassed in strategic zones
- However, government argues national security requirements justify expedited procedures for border areas

## EIA Draft 2020 — Controversy

The Ministry of Environment released a **Draft EIA Notification, 2020** proposing to replace the 2006 notification. It was heavily criticised and has **not been enacted** (as of 2026):

**Key controversial provisions:**
- **Post-facto regularisation:** Allowed grant of EC to projects that had already started construction without clearance (subject to penalty) — critics called this "legalising violations"
- **Reduced public consultation window:** Proposed reducing the public hearing notice period from 30 to 20 days
- **Expanded exemptions:** Proposed exempting more categories of projects from public hearing (border projects, inland waterways, expansion projects)
- **Strategic projects:** Government/defence projects could be kept confidential and exempt from public scrutiny
- **Online submissions only:** Required objections to be submitted online — potentially excluding non-digital rural communities

The draft was challenged by NGOs and state governments; the Supreme Court and High Courts stayed its notification pending consideration. It exemplifies the tension between "ease of doing business" and environmental democracy.

## National Green Tribunal (NGT) and Environmental Clearances

- The **National Green Tribunal (NGT)**, established under the **National Green Tribunal Act, 2010**, hears appeals against environmental clearances and violations
- NGT can hear challenges to ECs within **30 days** of grant (by aggrieved parties)
- NGT has the power to stay EC, impose penalties, and order remediation
- Key NGT roles: challenging Vedanta Sterlite copper plant EC (Tamil Nadu); monitoring Ganga pollution; challenging EIA exemptions
- NGT has suo motu jurisdiction to take up environmental matters; over 38,000 cases filed since inception
- NGT decisions can be appealed only to the **Supreme Court** of India

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- EIA Notification: issued **14 September 2006** under **EPA 1986**
- Category A: MoEFCC + **EAC**; Category B: **SEIAA + SEAC**
- Public hearing: conducted by **SPCB**; minimum **30 days' notice**
- CRZ 2019: **4 zones** (CRZ I-IV); CRZ-IIIA NDZ = **50 m**; CRZ-IIIB NDZ = **200 m**
- Forest Amendment 2023: **100 km** border exemption for strategic linear projects
- NGT: established under **NGT Act, 2010**; challenges to EC within **30 days**
- EIA Draft 2020: not enacted; controversial for post-facto regularisation

**For Mains (GS3):**
- EIA as environmental democracy: public hearing is the most contested stage — community voice vs. project viability
- CRZ 2019 relaxation: development opportunity (coastal tourism, infrastructure) vs. coastal ecosystem degradation, climate vulnerability (rising sea levels)
- Forest Amendment 2023: security imperative (100 km border exemption) vs. tribal rights (FRA) and biodiversity conservation in northeast India
- EIA Draft 2020: post-facto regularisation normalises violation — enforcement failure; UPSC may ask about challenges in EIA implementation
- NGT's role: quasi-judicial environmental watchdog; strengthened by Bhopal Gas case legacy; but faces challenges of limited execution capacity and industry lobbying
