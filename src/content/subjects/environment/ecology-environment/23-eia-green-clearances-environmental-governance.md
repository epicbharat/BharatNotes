## Legal Framework for Environmental Clearances

India's environmental governance rests on a **multi-layered legal architecture** anchored in the **Environment Protection Act, 1986 (EPA)**. Section 3 of the EPA grants the Central Government sweeping powers to take measures to protect and improve the environment — this is the enabling provision under which the **EIA Notification 2006** and the **CRZ Notification 2019** derive their authority.

| Legislation | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Environment Protection Act | 1986 | Umbrella legislation, enables MoEFCC to issue notifications |
| EIA Notification | 2006 | Mandatory environmental clearance for 39 project categories |
| Forest Conservation Act | 1980 (amended 2023) | Regulates diversion of forest land for non-forest uses |
| Wildlife Protection Act | 1972 | Clearances from National Board for Wildlife (NBWL) |
| CRZ Notification | 2019 | Regulates development in coastal areas |
| CAMPA Act | 2016 | Compensatory afforestation fund management |
| NGT Act | 2010 | Specialised environmental tribunal |

## EIA Notification 2006 — Core Framework

The **EIA Notification, 2006** (S.O. 1533(E), dated 14 September 2006) replaced the earlier 1994 notification and made environmental clearance **mandatory for 39 categories of projects and activities** listed in its Schedule. It was issued under Section 3 of the Environment Protection Act, 1986.

### Project Categorisation

Projects are classified into two categories based on the spatial extent of impact, magnitude of investment, and potential environmental sensitivity:

| Category | Appraisal Authority | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Category A** (national-level) | MoEFCC through Expert Appraisal Committee (EAC) | Thermal power plants ≥500 MW, major ports, nuclear plants, bulk drug manufacture, river valley projects ≥50 MW |
| **Category B** (state-level) | SEIAA (State EIA Authority) through SEAC (State Expert Appraisal Committee) | Hotels, highways, small-scale industries, residential townships |
| **Category B1** | Requires full EIA study | Mining projects ≥5 ha, highways 4+ lane, 100–300 km length |
| **Category B2** | No EIA required; simplified assessment | Small projects with low environmental impact |

**Key principle:** All Category A projects automatically require a full EIA. Category B projects are screened by SEAC to determine if they fall into B1 or B2.

## The EIA Process — Step by Step

The EIA Notification 2006 prescribes a **four-stage process** with an additional preparation phase:

### Stage 1 — Screening

Applicable only to **Category B projects**. The SEAC examines Form 1 (application) to determine whether the project needs a full EIA (B1) or only Form 1 scrutiny (B2). Category A projects skip this stage — they always require a full EIA.

### Stage 2 — Scoping

The EAC or SEAC issues **Terms of Reference (ToR)** — the blueprint specifying the methodology, geographic extent, baseline parameters, and specific issues to be studied. The applicant submits proposed ToR along with Form 1; the committee may conduct a site visit if necessary. ToR must be issued within **60 days** of receiving the application.

### Stage 3 — Public Consultation

**Mandatory for most Category A and B1 projects** (except certain sensitive/defence projects and certain industrial expansions within existing premises).

Public consultation has **two components:**
1. **Public hearing** at the project site or nearest city — presided over by a nominee of the **District Magistrate/Collector**; open to all affected persons; minutes recorded in the local language
2. **Written responses** from the public, NGOs, and government bodies

The proponent must submit the **Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) / Draft EIA Report** before the public hearing so affected communities can review it. A minimum **30-day notice** must be given before the public hearing.

> **EIS vs EIA:** The Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) is the report prepared by the project proponent documenting potential impacts. The EIA is the entire assessment *process*. UPSC exams use both terms — know the distinction.

### Stage 4 — Appraisal

The EAC or SEAC examines the EIS, public hearing minutes, and supporting documents. The Expert Appraisal Committee may visit the site. The committee submits its recommendations to the competent authority (MoEFCC or SEIAA), which then grants or rejects the **Environmental Clearance (EC)**.

**Time limits:** The MoEFCC must dispose of Category A applications within **105 days**; SEIAA must act within **105 days** for Category B projects.

### Post-Clearance Obligations

- **Compliance monitoring** by the State Pollution Control Board (SPCB)
- **Half-yearly compliance reports** submitted by proponents on the MoEFCC's Parivesh portal
- **Environmental Management Plan (EMP)** to be implemented

## Draft EIA 2020 — Controversy and Withdrawal

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change released the **Draft EIA Notification 2020** to replace the 2006 notification. It attracted massive public opposition and was eventually **not finalised** (as of 2026).

### Key Concerns Raised

| Provision | Criticism |
|---|---|
| Post-facto clearance | Would have allowed projects to begin operations *before* obtaining EC, then apply retrospectively — rewarding violations |
| Reduced public hearing window | Proposed 20 days' notice (down from 30); limited public participation |
| Expanded exemptions | Wider categories of projects exempted from public consultation — defence, border projects, inland waterways, solar parks |
| Suo motu cognisance | Removed provisions for third-party reports triggering an appraisal |
| Compliance reporting | Reduced from twice a year to once a year |

Civil society, environmentalists, and NGT criticised the draft as weakening the environmental protection framework. The government received over **1.7 million public comments** — the highest for any draft notification in India's history.

## Forest Clearances — Forest (Conservation) Act

### Original FCA 1980

The **Forest Conservation Act, 1980 (FCA)** requires Central Government approval for **diversion of forest land** for non-forest purposes (mining, roads, dams, etc.). No state government can dereserve forests or permit diversion without Central clearance.

### Forest (Conservation) Amendment Act, 2023

The **Forest (Conservation) Amendment Act, 2023** (No. 15 of 2023) — also renamed **Van (Sanrakshan Evam Samvardhan) Adhiniyam** — was passed by Lok Sabha on 26 July 2023 and Rajya Sabha on 2 August 2023.

**Key changes:**

| Provision | Detail |
|---|---|
| Scope of "forest" | Applies only to land notified under Indian Forest Act 1927 or recorded in government records on/after 25 October 1980 — excludes many "deemed forests" |
| Exemptions for security | Projects within **100 km of international borders/LAC/LoC** exempted — covers large swaths of ecologically sensitive Himalayan and northeastern forests |
| New permitted activities | Zoos and safaris (government-owned), eco-tourism facilities within forest working plans, silviculture operations |
| Strategic linear projects | Up to **0.10 ha** for road/railway connectivity to habitations allowed without FC; up to **10 ha** for security infrastructure |
| LWE districts | Up to **5 ha** for public utilities in Left Wing Extremism-affected districts |

**Criticism:** The 100 km exemption covers roughly **2/3 of northeast India**, which contains some of the country's most biodiverse forests. Environmentalists warned this would accelerate forest loss.

### Two-Stage Forest Clearance Process

For projects that require forest clearance under FCA:

**Stage I (In-Principle Approval):** The state government processes the proposal, obtains the consent of the gram sabha (for tribal areas), compensatory afforestation plan, and net present value payment. The Regional Office of MoEFCC examines and forwards to the FAC (Forest Advisory Committee). Stage I approval is "in-principle" — project can begin preparation but no forest land can be broken.

**Stage II (Final Clearance):** After all conditions of Stage I are met, the State submits proof of compensatory afforestation and NPV payment. MoEFCC grants Stage II clearance, after which forest land diversion can begin.

## CAMPA — Compensatory Afforestation Fund

### Background

The **Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority (CAMPA)** was constituted by the Supreme Court in 2002 following the *Godavarman Thirumulpad v. Union of India* case. Over the years, funds from user agencies (companies diverting forest land) accumulated — reaching approximately **₹95,000 crore** with the ad hoc CAMPA by the time the statute was passed.

### CAMPA Act 2016

The **Compensatory Afforestation Fund Act, 2016** (Act 38 of 2016) was enacted to give statutory backing to CAMPA. It came into effect on **30 September 2018** (along with Rules 2018).

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| National CAMPA | Administers the National Compensatory Afforestation Fund |
| State CAMPA | Each state has its own fund; 90% of funds go to states, 10% retained by Centre |
| Sources of funds | Compensatory afforestation payments, net present value (NPV) of forests, penal CA, additional CA |
| Fund usage | Afforestation, assisted natural regeneration, forest protection, wildlife conservation |
| District-level plans | Annual plans prepared by State CAMPA based on district forest officer inputs |

**Net Present Value (NPV):** The economic value of ecosystem services foregone due to diversion of forest land — calculated per hectare based on forest quality and type. NPV rates were revised upward by the Supreme Court in 2008.

## Coastal Clearances — CRZ Notification 2019

The **Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification, 2019** replaced the 2011 notification. It classifies the coast into four zones:

| Zone | Description |
|---|---|
| **CRZ-I** | Ecologically sensitive areas — mangroves, coral reefs, national parks, turtle nesting grounds; strictest restrictions |
| **CRZ-II** | Developed coastal urban areas; construction regulated |
| **CRZ-III** | Rural and semi-urban areas; divided into IIIA (density >2,161/sq km) and IIIB (density <2,161/sq km) |
| **CRZ-IV** | Aquatic zone — territorial waters up to 12 nautical miles and tidal influenced water bodies |

**Key change:** CRZ-IIIA areas now have **No Development Zone (NDZ) of only 50 metres** from the High Tide Line (HTL), compared to 200 metres earlier — enabling development closer to the coast in dense areas.

CRZ clearances for CRZ-I and CRZ-IV are issued by **MoEFCC**; for CRZ-II and CRZ-III, clearances are at the **state level** — reducing the central government's burden but raising concerns about state oversight.

## Wildlife Clearances — NBWL

Projects that fall within or are proposed adjacent to **National Parks, Wildlife Sanctuaries, Conservation Reserves, and Community Reserves** require additional clearance from the **National Board for Wildlife (NBWL)** under the Wildlife Protection Act, 1972.

The NBWL, chaired by the Prime Minister, has a **Standing Committee** (chaired by the Minister of Environment) for project clearance. The Supreme Court's **Central Empowered Committee (CEC)** also monitors compliance in forest and wildlife matters under Godavarman and related orders.

## National Green Tribunal (NGT)

### Establishment

The **National Green Tribunal (NGT)** was established under the **National Green Tribunal Act, 2010** and became operational on **4 July 2011**. India became the **third country** in the world to establish a specialised environmental court (after Australia and New Zealand) and the **first developing country** to do so.

### Structure and Benches

| Bench | Location | Jurisdiction |
|---|---|---|
| Principal Bench | New Delhi | North Zone — general and residual |
| Western Bench | Mumbai | West Zone — Maharashtra, Gujarat, Goa, Rajasthan, MP, Chhattisgarh |
| Central Bench | Bhopal | Central Zone |
| Southern Bench | Chennai | South Zone — Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Karnataka |
| Eastern Bench | Kolkata | East Zone — West Bengal, Odisha, Bihar, Jharkhand, Northeast |

### Jurisdiction

NGT has jurisdiction over:
- All civil cases relating to **substantial questions relating to environment** including enforcement of legal rights
- Acts listed in Schedule I: Water (Prevention & Control of Pollution) Act, Forest (Conservation) Act, EPA 1986, Biological Diversity Act, and others
- Appeals against orders of environmental authorities
- Compensation for environmental damage

**What NGT cannot do:** Criminal jurisdiction, matters not covered under Schedule I laws, matters where the High Court has already taken up the issue under writ jurisdiction.

### Key Powers

- Suo motu cognisance of environmental issues (post-2012 judicial interpretation)
- Time-bound disposal — efforts to decide cases within **6 months**
- Power to award compensation and restore damaged areas

## Gaps in India's EIA Framework

### Cumulative Impact Assessment (CIA)

India's EIA is **project-specific** — it does not require Cumulative Impact Assessment studying the combined effect of multiple projects in a basin or region. The Ganga basin, for example, hosts hundreds of hydropower projects whose cumulative impact on flows has never been formally assessed as a whole. Expert committees have repeatedly called for mandatory CIA for sensitive river valleys.

### Post-Project Monitoring

Post-clearance compliance monitoring remains **weak**. Studies by Centre for Science and Environment (CSE) and the CAG have found:
- Many projects do not submit compliance reports on time
- CPCB/SPCB inspections are infrequent
- Environmental Management Plans (EMPs) are often treated as formalities

### Third-Party Audits

Unlike global best practices, India lacks a mandatory system of **third-party environmental audits** for ongoing projects.

## Environmental Governance Structure

### MoEFCC

The **Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC)** is the nodal agency for environmental governance. Key wings:
- **Environment Division:** EIA, CRZ clearances, hazardous substances regulation
- **Forest Division:** FCA clearances, CAMPA oversight
- **Wildlife Division:** NBWL secretariat, wildlife trade regulation
- **Climate Change Division:** NAPCC implementation, INDC/NDC coordination

### Pollution Control Bodies

| Body | Level | Key Function |
|---|---|---|
| CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board) | National | Standards, consent to establish for major industries, air/water quality monitoring |
| SPCBs (State Pollution Control Boards) | State | Consent to operate, enforcement, district-level monitoring |
| PCCs (Pollution Control Committees) | Union Territories | Equivalent to SPCBs in UTs |

### Parivesh Portal

The **Parivesh (Pro-Active and Responsive facilitation by Interactive, Virtuous and Environmental Single-window Hub)** portal was launched in 2018 as a single-window system for environment, forest, wildlife, and CRZ clearances — reducing delays and improving transparency.

## Green Ratings vs EIA

EIA is a **regulatory requirement**. Voluntary **green building ratings** operate separately:

| Rating System | Full Form | Governing Body | Scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| GRIHA | Green Rating for Integrated Habitat Assessment | MNRE / TERI | National rating for buildings and habitats |
| IGBC | Indian Green Building Council | CII-IGBC | Buildings (based on LEED adaptation) |
| LEED India | Leadership in Energy & Environmental Design | USGBC (adapted for India) | Commercial and residential buildings |

Green ratings focus on **energy efficiency, water conservation, and material use** — they are not substitutes for EIA which covers broader ecosystem and pollution impacts.

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Memorise the **four stages** of EIA: Screening → Scoping → Public Consultation → Appraisal
- Know the Category A vs B distinction (national vs state)
- NGT: established 2011, 5 benches (Delhi, Mumbai, Bhopal, Chennai, Kolkata), 3rd country globally
- CAMPA: Act 2016, effective 2018, 90:10 (state:centre) fund distribution
- CRZ-I = most sensitive; CRZ-IIIA = 50m NDZ (post-2019)
- Section 66A was struck down — not applicable to EIA but appears in environment law questions

**For Mains (GS3 — Environment):**
- EIA controversies: Draft 2020 (post-facto clearance, expanded exemptions) shows weakening of framework
- Cumulative Impact Assessment gap — use this as a concrete structural weakness
- Link NGT to judicial activism in environment: suo motu powers, compensation jurisprudence
- CAMPA: mention ₹95,000 crore accumulated as evidence of widespread forest diversion; question whether compensation equals conservation
- FCA 2023: 100 km border exemption = potential threat to Northeast biodiversity — link to Act East Policy tensions
- Structure a Mains answer: Legal framework → EIA process → Gaps → Way forward

**Mnemonic for EIA stages:** **S**tudy **S**cope **P**eople **A**pprove = Screening → Scoping → Public Consultation → Appraisal
