## Introduction

India's digital economy has undergone a transformative shift over the past decade, driven by a unique stack of Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI) that has no parallel globally. From Aadhaar-linked identity verification to UPI-enabled real-time payments, India has built population-scale digital systems that have reshaped financial inclusion, governance, and commerce. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) recognized UPI as the world's largest retail fast-payment system by transaction volume in June 2025, accounting for approximately 49% of global real-time payment transactions.

This chapter covers the full spectrum of India's digital economy — from the foundational India Stack to emerging areas like CBDC, cryptocurrency regulation, and the Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC).

---

## India Stack — The Digital Public Infrastructure

India Stack is a set of open APIs and digital public goods, built as interoperable layers, enabling presence-less, paperless, and cashless service delivery at population scale. The term was coined by iSPIRT (Indian Software Product Industry Round Table) to describe the collection of government-operated digital infrastructure systems.

### The Four Layers

| Layer | System | Function | Scale |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Identity** | Aadhaar (UIDAI) | Biometric-based unique digital identity | 1.4 billion+ enrolments |
| **Payments** | UPI, BBPS, AePS | Real-time interoperable digital payments | 228+ billion transactions in CY2025 |
| **Data** | DigiLocker, Account Aggregator | Consent-based digital document and financial data sharing | 67.63 crore DigiLocker users (March 2026); 950+ crore documents |
| **Health** | ABDM (Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission) | Digital health records, health IDs | Rolled out from 2021 |

### Why India Stack Matters for UPSC

India Stack is a frequent topic in both Prelims (factual questions on Aadhaar, UPI, DigiLocker) and Mains (essays on digital governance, financial inclusion, DPI as a global model). As of February 2026, India has signed agreements with 23 countries to share its DPI model. Countries like Nigeria, Kenya, and Bangladesh have adopted Aadhaar-inspired digital ID systems, while Ghana's GhIPSS and Nigeria's eNaira are modelled after UPI.

## Unified Payments Interface (UPI)

### What is UPI?

The Unified Payments Interface is a real-time payment system developed by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI), launched on 11 April 2016. It enables instant fund transfers between bank accounts through a mobile platform, using a Virtual Payment Address (VPA) instead of sharing bank account details.

### Key Features

- **Interoperability:** Works across all banks and payment apps (PhonePe, Google Pay, Paytm, BHIM, etc.)
- **24/7 availability:** Real-time settlement, including weekends and holidays
- **Zero transaction cost:** No charges for person-to-person (P2P) transactions
- **Multiple use cases:** P2P transfers, merchant payments, bill payments, IPO subscriptions, tax payments

### UPI Transaction Data

| Year | Volume (Billion Transactions) | Value (Lakh Crore Rs) |
|---|---|---|
| 2020 | 22.3 | 41.0 |
| 2021 | 38.7 | 71.5 |
| 2022 | 74.0 | 125.9 |
| 2023 | 117.6 | 182.8 |
| 2024 | 172.2 | 246.8 |
| 2025 | 228.3 | 299.7 |

As of 2024, UPI had approximately 491 million users and 65 million merchants.

In January 2026, UPI recorded an all-time monthly high of 21.7 billion transactions worth Rs 28.33 lakh crore.

### UPI Market Share (App-wise)

PhonePe and Google Pay together dominate approximately 85% of UPI transactions, with Paytm, CRED, and other apps sharing the remaining market.

### UPI Internationalization

UPI has been taken live in multiple countries for cross-border payments:

| Country | Integration Partner | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Singapore | PayNow | Live; 8,000+ merchants |
| UAE | NIOPAY | Live |

Cross-border UPI transaction volumes grew over 20 times — from 37,060 in FY24 to over 7,55,000 in FY25. NPCI International Payments Limited (NIPL) plans to expand UPI to 4-6 additional countries by 2025-26, targeting key Indian tourist destinations in Southeast Asia.

## Other Digital Payment Systems

### RuPay

RuPay is India's indigenous card payment network launched by NPCI in 2012. It operates as a domestic alternative to Visa and Mastercard, with significantly lower merchant discount rates. RuPay cards are issued under the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY), making them the backbone of financial inclusion through debit cards.

### BHIM (Bharat Interface for Money)

Launched in December 2016, BHIM is a UPI-based payment app developed by NPCI. It was designed to promote digital payments among first-time users, especially in rural and semi-urban areas.

### AePS (Aadhaar-enabled Payment System)

AePS allows Aadhaar-linked bank account holders to perform basic banking transactions — cash withdrawal, balance inquiry, fund transfer — through a micro-ATM using biometric authentication. It is especially significant for last-mile financial inclusion in areas without ATMs or bank branches.

### NETC FASTag

The National Electronic Toll Collection (NETC) system enables automatic toll payments through FASTags. FASTag was made mandatory for all vehicles from 15 February 2021, significantly reducing waiting times at toll plazas and improving highway logistics.

### BBPS (Bharat Bill Payment System)

An interoperable bill payment system operated by NPCI, allowing consumers to pay utility bills, insurance premiums, loan EMIs, and other recurring payments through a single platform.

## CBDC — The Digital Rupee (e-Rupee)

### What is CBDC?

A Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) is a digital form of fiat money issued by the central bank. Unlike cryptocurrency, a CBDC is legal tender, backed by the sovereign, and has the same value as physical currency.

### RBI's CBDC Pilots

| Variant | Name | Launch Date | Use Case |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Wholesale** | e₹-W (Digital Rupee — Wholesale) | 1 November 2022 | Settlement of secondary market transactions in government securities |
| **Retail** | e₹-R (Digital Rupee — Retail) | 1 December 2022 | Person-to-person and person-to-merchant payments |

### Retail CBDC (e₹-R) Details

- **Technology:** Based on blockchain (distributed ledger technology)
- **Form:** Digital tokens in the same denominations as physical currency
- **Pilot banks (Phase 1):** SBI, ICICI Bank, Yes Bank, IDFC First Bank
- **Pilot banks (Phase 2):** Bank of Baroda, Union Bank of India, HDFC Bank, Kotak Mahindra Bank
- **Initial cities:** Mumbai, New Delhi, Bengaluru, Bhubaneswar, Chandigarh
- **Milestone:** RBI achieved its target of 1 million daily transactions on the retail CBDC on 27 December 2023

### CBDC vs Cryptocurrency vs UPI

| Parameter | CBDC (e₹) | Cryptocurrency | UPI |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Issuer** | RBI (Central Bank) | Decentralized (no issuer) | NPCI (facilitator); banks settle |
| **Legal tender** | Yes | No (not legal tender in India) | Transfers existing legal tender |
| **Backing** | Sovereign guarantee | Market-determined value | Bank deposits |
| **Volatility** | None (1 e₹ = 1 Rs) | High | None |
| **Technology** | Blockchain-based | Blockchain-based | Centralised payment rails |
| **Anonymity** | Limited (managed by RBI) | Pseudo-anonymous | KYC-linked |

## Cryptocurrency Regulation in India

### Timeline of Key Developments

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 2013 | RBI issues first caution against virtual currencies |
| 2018 | RBI circular prohibits regulated entities from dealing with crypto exchanges |
| 2020 | **Internet and Mobile Association of India v. RBI** — Supreme Court quashes the RBI ban, holding that the absolute prohibition was a "disproportionate measure" as RBI could not demonstrate actual harm to its regulated entities |
| 2022 | **Union Budget 2022-23** introduces taxation: 30% flat tax on income from Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) under Section 115BBH; 1% TDS on transfers exceeding specified thresholds under Section 194S; no set-off of losses against other income; only cost of acquisition allowed as deduction |
| 2023 | Crypto exchanges and service providers brought under PMLA (Prevention of Money Laundering Act); must register with FIU-IND (Financial Intelligence Unit) and comply with AML/KYC norms |
| 2026 | No comprehensive crypto regulation bill has been enacted; crypto remains "taxed but unregulated" |

### India's Position at G20

During India's G20 presidency (2023), India pushed for a coordinated global framework for crypto regulation. The G20 New Delhi Leaders' Declaration endorsed the IMF-FSB Synthesis Paper, which recommended a comprehensive regulatory approach rather than an outright ban.

## JAM Trinity — Jan Dhan, Aadhaar, Mobile

The JAM trinity is the convergence of three pillars that underpin India's financial inclusion and direct benefit transfer architecture:

### Components

| Pillar | Details | Scale (2024) |
|---|---|---|
| **Jan Dhan** | Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY) — zero-balance bank accounts | 54+ crore accounts; Rs 2.39 lakh crore total deposits; 55.6% women account holders; 66.6% in rural/semi-urban areas |
| **Aadhaar** | 12-digit unique biometric identity | 141 crore+ enrolments |
| **Mobile** | Mobile phone connectivity | 119 crore+ subscribers (66.1 crore urban, 52.7 crore rural) |

### Impact on Direct Benefit Transfers (DBT)

The JAM trinity powers the Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) mechanism, which has:

- Integrated **1,206 welfare schemes** as of FY 2024-25
- Transferred Rs **6.7 lakh crore** in FY 2024-25 alone
- Eliminated ghost beneficiaries and middlemen, leading to estimated savings of over Rs 3.48 lakh crore (cumulative, as claimed by the government)

### How DBT Works

1. Beneficiary's Aadhaar is seeded with their Jan Dhan bank account
2. Government transfers the benefit amount directly into the linked bank account
3. Beneficiary receives SMS notification on mobile
4. Cash can be withdrawn via AePS (micro-ATM) using biometric authentication

## Account Aggregator Framework

### What is an Account Aggregator?

Account Aggregators (AAs) are a new class of NBFCs licensed by the RBI under the NBFC-Account Aggregator (Reserve Bank) Directions, 2016. They facilitate consent-based sharing of financial data between Financial Information Providers (FIPs — banks, insurance companies, mutual funds) and Financial Information Users (FIUs — lenders, wealth managers).

### Key Features

- **Consent-based:** No data moves without the explicit, informed, revocable consent of the customer
- **Data blind:** AAs cannot read, store, or use the data they transfer — they are merely a pipeline
- **Interoperable:** Any FIP can share data with any FIU through any AA
- **Launched:** September 2021 (operational framework)
- **Self-regulatory body:** Sahamati (industry collective)

### Growth

As of early 2026, approximately 70 million individuals have consented to share their data through the AA framework, with about 10 million new consents generated monthly.

### Significance for Financial Inclusion

The AA framework enables the "cash-flow-based lending" model — small businesses and individuals without collateral or formal credit history can share their bank statements and GST filings digitally, allowing lenders to assess creditworthiness based on actual cash flows rather than traditional collateral.

## Open Network for Digital Commerce (ONDC)

### What is ONDC?

ONDC is an open protocol-based network for digital commerce, conceptualized by the Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade (DPIIT). It aims to democratize e-commerce by decoupling buyers and sellers from platform-specific ecosystems.

### Key Facts

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Incorporated** | December 2021 (Section 8 company) |
| **Pilot launch** | 29 April 2022 in five cities — Delhi NCR, Bhopal, Bengaluru, Shillong, Coimbatore |
| **Public beta** | 30 September 2022 (Bangalore Urban) |
| **Founding members** | Quality Council of India, Protean eGov Technologies Ltd |
| **Protocol** | Based on Beckn Protocol — open-sourced, platform-agnostic |

### How ONDC Differs from Amazon/Flipkart

| Feature | Traditional Platforms | ONDC |
|---|---|---|
| **Architecture** | Closed, proprietary ecosystem | Open, interoperable network |
| **Seller access** | Must register on each platform separately | Register once, discoverable across all ONDC apps |
| **Buyer apps** | Platform-specific (Amazon app, Flipkart app) | Multiple buyer-side apps (Paytm, Magicpin, etc.) |
| **Commission** | High platform fees (15-40%) | Lower network charges |
| **Data ownership** | Platform owns customer data | Seller retains customer data |

### Domains Covered

ONDC covers retail (grocery, food), mobility (ride-hailing), logistics, hospitality, and is expanding into healthcare and financial services.

## RBI Digital Lending Guidelines (2022)

On 2 September 2022, the RBI issued comprehensive guidelines to regulate the digital lending ecosystem, based on the recommendations of a Working Group on Digital Lending (November 2021).

### Key Provisions

| Area | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Disbursement** | All loan amounts must be disbursed directly into the borrower's bank account — no pass-through or pool accounts of Lending Service Providers (LSPs) |
| **Key Fact Statement (KFS)** | Borrowers must receive a standardized KFS before signing, containing APR (Annual Percentage Rate), all fees, recovery mechanism, and cooling-off/look-up period |
| **Data privacy** | LSPs cannot access mobile phone data (contacts, photos, storage) beyond what is essential; only three categories of data permitted — name, contact details, and KYC information |
| **Grievance redressal** | Regulated Entities (REs) and LSPs must appoint a nodal grievance redressal officer |
| **Credit reporting** | All digital loans must be reported to credit bureaus |
| **First Loss Default Guarantee (FLDG)** | Capped at 5% of the loan portfolio — prevents LSPs from bearing disproportionate risk |
| **Cooling-off period** | Borrowers have a look-up period during which they can exit the loan without penalty |

### Significance

These guidelines brought the unregulated fintech lending space under RBI oversight, addressing concerns about predatory lending, usurious interest rates, coercive recovery practices, and misuse of personal data by lending apps.

## Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023

The Digital Personal Data Protection (DPDP) Act received Presidential assent on 11 August 2023. It establishes a comprehensive framework for digital personal data protection in India.

### Key Provisions

| Feature | Provision |
|---|---|
| **Applicability** | All digital personal data processed within India; also applies to processing outside India if related to offering goods/services to individuals in India |
| **Consent** | Personal data may be processed only for a lawful purpose upon consent; consent may not be required for "legitimate uses" (voluntary sharing, State services) |
| **Data Fiduciary obligations** | Maintain accuracy, keep data secure, delete data once purpose is met |
| **Significant Data Fiduciaries** | Must appoint a Data Protection Officer and an independent data auditor |
| **Rights of Data Principals** | Right to information, correction, erasure, and grievance redressal |
| **Children's data** | Verifiable parental/guardian consent required; behavioral monitoring and targeted advertising directed at children are banned |
| **Penalties** | Up to Rs 250 crore for non-compliance; up to Rs 200 crore for failure to notify breaches or breach of child-related obligations |
| **Data Protection Board** | Established under Section 18 to adjudicate complaints and disputes |

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:** Expect factual questions on UPI (launch year, NPCI, transaction milestones), CBDC (wholesale vs retail pilot dates), JAM trinity components, ONDC, and DPDP Act provisions. The comparison table between CBDC, cryptocurrency, and UPI is high-yield.

**For Mains GS-III:** Questions typically ask about financial inclusion, digital divide, regulation of fintech, or India's DPI model. Structure answers around: (a) what the system is, (b) how it promotes inclusion, (c) challenges (digital divide, privacy, cybersecurity), (d) way forward.

**Common Mains questions:**

- "India's Digital Public Infrastructure has become a model for the Global South." Discuss with reference to India Stack and UPI.
- Critically examine the regulatory challenges posed by cryptocurrencies in India.
- How has the JAM trinity transformed the delivery of government welfare schemes?
- Discuss the significance of the Account Aggregator framework for financial inclusion and credit access.
- Evaluate the impact of the DPDP Act, 2023 on the fintech ecosystem in India.
