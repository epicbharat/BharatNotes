## Cooperative Movement in India — Historical Background

| Phase | Period | Key Developments |
|-------|--------|-----------------|
| **Origins** | 1904 | Cooperative Societies Act, 1904 — first legislation enabling formation of credit cooperatives to free farmers from moneylenders |
| **Expansion** | 1912 | Cooperative Societies Act, 1912 — allowed non-credit cooperatives (marketing, consumer, housing) |
| **Post-Independence** | 1950s–70s | Cooperatives integrated into Five-Year Plans; state governments promoted cooperative sugar mills, dairy cooperatives, and credit societies |
| **White Revolution** | 1970–96 | Operation Flood (Amul model) — transformed India into the world's largest milk producer through a three-tier dairy cooperative structure |
| **Liberalisation era** | 1991 onward | Cooperative banks faced competition from commercial banks; governance concerns grew |
| **Constitutional status** | 2011 | 97th Amendment gave constitutional recognition to cooperatives |
| **New Ministry** | 2021 | Ministry of Cooperation established on 6 July 2021 to strengthen the cooperative ecosystem |

## Types of Cooperative Societies

| Type | Function | Notable Examples |
|------|----------|-----------------|
| **Credit Cooperatives** | Provide short-term and medium-term agricultural and non-agricultural loans | PACS (village level), District Central Cooperative Banks (DCCBs), State Cooperative Banks (StCBs) |
| **Marketing Cooperatives** | Help producers sell output at fair prices, eliminating middlemen | NAFED (National Agricultural Cooperative Marketing Federation) |
| **Consumer Cooperatives** | Purchase goods in bulk and sell to members at lower prices | Kendriya Bhandar, Apna Bazar, Super Bazar |
| **Dairy Cooperatives** | Collect, process, and market milk and dairy products | Amul (GCMMF), Mother Dairy |
| **Producer Cooperatives** | Pool resources for production of goods — raw materials, tools, processing units | IFFCO (Indian Farmers Fertiliser Cooperative, registered 1967 — one of the world's largest cooperatives) |
| **Housing Cooperatives** | Purchase land, construct houses, and allot them to members | State-level housing cooperative federations |
| **Sugar Cooperatives** | Process and market sugarcane | Maharashtra's cooperative sugar mills — over 100 factories |
| **Fisheries Cooperatives** | Provide credit, equipment, and marketing support to fishermen | State fisheries cooperative federations |

## 97th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2011

The 97th Amendment gave constitutional status to cooperative societies. Key provisions:

| Provision | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Article 19(1)(c)** | Right to form cooperative societies made a fundamental right |
| **Article 43-B** | New Directive Principle — State shall promote voluntary formation, autonomous functioning, democratic control, and professional management of cooperatives |
| **Part IX-B** | New part added (Articles 243-ZH to 243-ZT) covering incorporation, elections, audit, and governance of cooperatives |
| **Maximum board term** | 5 years; election to be conducted before expiry of term |
| **Audit** | Annual accounts to be audited within 6 months of the close of the financial year |
| **Reservation** | One seat reserved for SC/ST and two for women on the board of every cooperative |
| **Supreme Court ruling** | In *Union of India v. Rajendra N. Shah* (2021), the Supreme Court struck down provisions that applied to state cooperative societies, holding that Parliament did not obtain ratification from half the state legislatures as required under Article 368(2) for matters on the State List. Provisions applying to multi-state cooperatives were upheld. |

## Ministry of Cooperation

Established on 6 July 2021 as a separate Union Ministry to provide a dedicated administrative, legal, and policy framework for cooperatives.

Key initiatives:

| Initiative | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Multi-State Cooperative Societies (Amendment) Act, 2023** | Enacted on 3 August 2023; establishes Cooperative Election Authority; creates a Cooperative Rehabilitation, Reconstruction and Development Fund; allows any cooperative society to merge into an existing multi-state cooperative |
| **Model Bye-Laws for PACS** | Finalised in January 2023 — allows PACS to diversify into 25+ activities including dairy, fisheries, storage, and input supply |
| **National Cooperative Database** | Digital repository of all cooperatives for planning and monitoring |
| **PACS Computerisation** | Centrally sponsored project for 2022-23 to 2026-27 (details below) |

## NABARD — National Bank for Agriculture and Rural Development

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Established** | 12 July 1982 under NABARD Act, 1981 (Act 61 of 1981) |
| **Origin** | Created by transferring agricultural credit functions of RBI and refinance functions of Agricultural Refinance and Development Corporation (ARDC); recommended by the B. Sivaraman Committee (1979) |
| **Headquarters** | Mumbai |
| **Ownership** | Fully owned by the Government of India (RBI divested its stake in 2019) |
| **Mandate** | Apex development bank for promotion and development of agriculture, small-scale industries, cottage and village industries, handicrafts, and other rural crafts |

### Core Functions of NABARD

| Function | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Refinance** | Provides refinance to State Cooperative Banks (StCBs), Regional Rural Banks (RRBs), commercial banks, and other financial institutions for agricultural and rural lending — both short-term (production/marketing credit) and long-term (investment credit) |
| **Direct finance** | Lends to state governments for rural infrastructure under RIDF (Rural Infrastructure Development Fund) |
| **Supervision** | Conducts inspection of StCBs and DCCBs under powers delegated by RBI (Section 35(6) of Banking Regulation Act) |
| **Development** | Promotes SHGs, farmers' clubs, financial literacy, watershed development, and tribal development programmes |
| **Rural Infrastructure** | Manages RIDF, Warehouse Infrastructure Fund, and NABARD Infrastructure Development Assistance (NIDA) |

### NABARD Key Funds

| Fund | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **RIDF (Rural Infrastructure Development Fund)** | Created in 1995-96; domestic commercial banks with shortfall in priority-sector lending deposit funds here; NABARD lends to state governments for rural infrastructure (roads, bridges, irrigation, drinking water) |
| **LTIF (Long-Term Irrigation Fund)** | For completion of identified major and medium irrigation projects |
| **MIFF (Micro Irrigation Fund)** | Promotes micro-irrigation (drip, sprinkler) |
| **FPO Fund** | Rs 1,000 crore for equity grants to Farmer Producer Organisations |

## Cooperative Banking Structure — Three-Tier System

### Short-Term Credit Structure

| Tier | Institution | Level | Function |
|------|------------|-------|----------|
| **Apex** | State Cooperative Bank (StCB) | State | Receives deposits; borrows from NABARD; lends to DCCBs |
| **Middle** | District Central Cooperative Bank (DCCB) | District | Acts as bridge between StCB and PACS; lends to PACS and individuals |
| **Base** | Primary Agricultural Credit Society (PACS) | Village | Provides short-term crop loans directly to farmers; accepts deposits |

### Long-Term Credit Structure

| Tier | Institution | Level |
|------|------------|-------|
| **Apex** | State Cooperative Agriculture and Rural Development Bank (SCARDB) | State |
| **Primary** | Primary Cooperative Agriculture and Rural Development Bank (PCARDB) | Taluk/District |

### RBI Regulation of Cooperative Banks

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **StCBs and DCCBs** | Regulated by RBI under Banking Regulation Act, 1949 (as applicable to cooperative societies); inspection powers delegated to NABARD |
| **PACS** | Not regulated by RBI — they are not "banks" under the BR Act; governed by respective State Cooperative Societies Acts |
| **Urban Cooperative Banks (UCBs)** | Brought under stricter RBI supervision through the Banking Regulation (Amendment) Act, 2020 |
| **Dual control issue** | Cooperative banks face dual regulation — RBI controls banking functions while state Registrar of Cooperative Societies controls management and administration |

## Kisan Credit Card (KCC) Scheme

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Launched** | August 1998 — introduced by public-sector banks on the recommendation of R.V. Gupta Committee |
| **Objective** | Provide adequate and timely credit support to farmers for cultivation, post-harvest expenses, consumption needs, and asset maintenance |
| **Coverage (Dec 2024)** | 7.72 crore operative KCC accounts with total outstanding of Rs 10.05 lakh crore |
| **Extended to** | Animal husbandry and fisheries farmers (from 2018-19) |
| **Loan limit** | Rs 3 lakh at concessional rate of 7% per annum — increased to Rs 5 lakh in Union Budget 2025-26 |
| **Interest subvention** | 1.5% subvention provided to lending institutions on KCC loans up to the limit |
| **Prompt repayment incentive** | Additional 3% incentive for timely repayment — effective interest rate reduced to 4% |
| **For animal husbandry/fisheries** | Interest benefit applicable up to Rs 2 lakh |
| **Issuing banks** | Commercial banks, RRBs, cooperative banks, and small finance banks |

## Modified Interest Subvention Scheme (MISS)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Provide interest subvention to banks so that farmers get short-term crop loans at affordable rates |
| **Continuation** | Approved for FY 2025-26 by the Union Cabinet |
| **Mechanism** | Banks provide loans at 7%; Government gives 1.5% subvention to banks; farmers who repay on time get 3% incentive — net cost to farmer: 4% |
| **Loan limit enhanced** | From Rs 3 lakh to Rs 5 lakh (Budget 2025-26) |
| **Beneficiaries** | Over 7.75 crore active KCC accounts form the backbone of this scheme |

## SHG-Bank Linkage Programme

Launched by NABARD in 1992, this is the world's largest coordinated financial inclusion programme.

| Indicator | Data (FY 2023-24) |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **SHGs with savings accounts** | 144.22 lakh SHGs — up from 118.93 lakh in 2021-22 (21.2% growth over 3 years) |
| **Families covered** | 17.75 crore households |
| **Women SHGs** | 83.52% of all SHGs are exclusively women groups |
| **Loans disbursed (FY 2023-24)** | Rs 2,09,286 crore to 54.82 lakh SHGs — 44% increase over FY 2022-23 (Rs 1,45,200 crore) |

### How the SHG-Bank Linkage Model Works

1. **Group formation** — 10-20 members (usually women) form an SHG and start internal savings
2. **Internal lending** — Members lend to each other from the pooled savings
3. **Bank linkage** — After 6 months of regular saving, the SHG opens a savings account with a bank
4. **Credit linkage** — Bank provides loans to the SHG (typically 4-8 times the group's savings)
5. **On-lending** — SHG lends to individual members based on group consensus
6. **Repayment** — High repayment rates (often 95%+) due to peer monitoring and social collateral

### Deendayal Antyodaya Yojana — National Rural Livelihoods Mission (DAY-NRLM)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Launched** | 2011 (restructured from SGSY — Swarnajayanti Gram Swarozgar Yojana) |
| **Ministry** | Ministry of Rural Development |
| **Objective** | Universal social mobilisation of rural poor households into SHGs and their federation at village, cluster, and block levels |

| **Interest subvention** | Women SHGs get loans at 7% from banks; further subvention reduces cost to 4% in 250 districts |

## Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY) — Crop Insurance

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Launched** | Kharif 2016 (replaced NAIS and MNAIS) |
| **Objective** | Provide comprehensive crop insurance to farmers against all non-preventable natural risks at minimum premium |
| **Premium rates** | Kharif: 2% of sum insured; Rabi: 1.5%; Commercial/horticultural: 5% |
| **Remaining premium** | Shared equally between Centre and state government (no cap since Kharif 2020 for unirrigated areas) |
| **Farmers enrolled (FY 2024-25)** | 4.19 crore farmers — highest since inception; 32% increase from 3.17 crore in 2022-23 |
| **Cumulative (2016 to 2024-25)** | 78.41 crore farmer applications insured; 22.67 crore claims settled worth Rs 1.83 lakh crore |
| **Budget 2025-26** | Rs 12,242 crore allocated; Union Cabinet approved continuation till 2025-26 with total budget of Rs 69,515.71 crore |
| **Global standing** | Largest crop insurance scheme in the world by farmer enrolments; third-largest by insurance premiums (as of 2023) |
| **Penalty for delay** | From Kharif 2024, 12% penalty is automatically added for delayed claim payments |
| **Technology** | Satellite imagery, drones, and weather stations used for yield estimation and faster claim settlement |

## Regional Rural Banks (RRBs)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Established** | 1975 under Regional Rural Banks Act, 1976 (Ordinance in 1975); based on recommendations of M. Narasimham Working Group |
| **Objective** | Combine rural reach of cooperatives with professional management of commercial banks — focus on small and marginal farmers, agricultural labourers, artisans, and small entrepreneurs |
| **Ownership** | Centre (50%), State Government (15%), Sponsor Bank (35%) |
| **Regulation** | Regulated by RBI; supervised by NABARD |
| **Current number (May 2025)** | 28 RRBs — down from 196 originally, after four rounds of amalgamation |
| **Branch network** | 22,966 branches across 26 states and 2 UTs |
| **Golden Jubilee** | RRBs celebrated 50 years in 2025 |

### Amalgamation Phases

| Phase | Period | RRBs Reduced To |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| Phase I | 2005–2010 | 196 to 82 |
| Phase II | 2012–2014 | 82 to 56 |
| Phase III | 2019–2021 | 56 to 43 |
| Phase IV | May 2025 | 43 to 28 ("One State, One RRB" policy) |

## Microfinance Sector

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Gross loan portfolio (March 2025)** | Rs 3,81,200 crore with approximately 14 crore active loans |
| **NBFC-MFI sector** | 168 MFIs across 29 states, 4 UTs, 563 districts; serving over 3 crore clients |
| **RBI regulatory framework** | NBFC-MFI Directions, 2025 — consolidated and modernised under scale-based regulation |
| **Qualifying asset norm** | Minimum 60% of total assets must be in microfinance loans (ongoing compliance) |
| **Borrower cap** | Maximum 3 micro-lenders per borrower (down from 4); total indebtedness capped at Rs 2 lakh (from January 2025) |
| **Key players** | NBFC-MFIs, banks, small finance banks, non-profit MFIs |

### Models of Microfinance in India

| Model | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **SHG-Bank Linkage** | Dominant model — NABARD-promoted; SHGs linked to formal banks |
| **MFI (Grameen) Model** | Microfinance institution lends directly to Joint Liability Groups (JLGs); modelled on Bangladesh's Grameen Bank |
| **Bank-led model** | Banks use Business Correspondents (BCs) to reach last-mile borrowers |

## Exam-Relevant Comparison: Cooperative Banks vs Commercial Banks

| Parameter | Cooperative Banks | Commercial Banks |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Registration** | Cooperative Societies Act (state) or Multi-State Cooperative Societies Act (central) | Companies Act / specific statutes (SBI Act, Nationalisation Acts) |
| **Area of operation** | Limited geographical area | Pan-India |
| **Governance** | Elected board by members (one member, one vote) | Board of directors; shareholders appoint |
| **Regulation** | Dual — RBI (banking) + Registrar of Cooperatives (management) | Single — RBI |
| **Priority sector lending** | Mandated to serve agricultural and rural sectors | 40% of net bank credit (domestic banks) |
| **Deposit insurance** | Covered by DICGC up to Rs 5 lakh | Covered by DICGC up to Rs 5 lakh |
| **Profitability** | Generally lower; many PACS run losses | Higher; driven by scale and diversified portfolios |

## Key Terms for UPSC

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Refinance** | Lending by NABARD to other banks/financial institutions so they can on-lend to farmers |
| **Interest subvention** | Government subsidy on interest rates to make farm credit cheaper |
| **Prompt repayment incentive** | Extra interest reduction for farmers who repay on time |
| **Joint Liability Group (JLG)** | Informal group of 4-10 individuals for mutual guarantee to avail bank loans without collateral |
| **Business Correspondent (BC)** | Agent appointed by a bank to provide financial services in areas without branches |
| **Priority Sector Lending (PSL)** | RBI mandates banks to lend a certain percentage to agriculture, MSMEs, education, housing, etc. |
| **DICGC** | Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation — insures bank deposits up to Rs 5 lakh |
| **Sahkar Se Samriddhi** | "Prosperity through Cooperation" — Government of India's vision for strengthening the cooperative movement |
