## Introduction

Soil is the foundation of agriculture and the basis of all terrestrial ecosystems. India's diverse physiography — from the Himalayan foothills to the Deccan plateau, from the Thar Desert to the Western Ghats — has produced an extraordinary variety of soil types. Yet, India faces a serious soil health crisis: **~97.85 million hectares** (29.7% of India's total geographical area) underwent land degradation during 2018-19 (ISRO/NRSC data), threatening food security and rural livelihoods. For UPSC, soil geography is critical for GS Paper I (physical geography) and GS Paper III (environment, agriculture, land management).

## Soil Formation — The CLORPT Factors

Soil formation (pedogenesis) is controlled by five factors, memorised as **CLORPT**:

| Factor | Influence |
|--------|-----------|
| **C — Climate** | Temperature and precipitation control weathering rate and organic matter decomposition |
| **L — Living organisms** | Microbes, earthworms, plant roots contribute to organic matter and soil structure |
| **O — Organic matter (parent material)** | The underlying rock or sediment determines mineral composition |
| **R — Relief/Topography** | Slope affects drainage, erosion, and accumulation |
| **P — Parent material** | Original rock type determines mineral nutrients |
| **T — Time** | Older soils are more weathered; young alluvial soils differ from ancient laterites |

## Classification of Indian Soils (ICAR)

The **Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR)** classifies Indian soils into 8 major types. The most important for UPSC:

### 1. Alluvial Soils (Most Extensive)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Area** | Most extensive soil type in India — covers ~43% of total land area |
| **Location** | Indo-Gangetic Plain, Brahmaputra valley, coastal deltas (Krishna, Godavari, Kaveri, Mahanadi) |
| **Formation** | Deposited by rivers — Himalayan rivers (Indus, Ganga, Brahmaputra) and peninsular rivers |
| **Nutrients** | Rich in potash, phosphoric acid, lime; deficient in nitrogen and organic matter |
| **Crops** | Wheat, rice, maize, sugarcane, cotton, legumes |
| **Sub-types** | Khadar and Bhangar (see below) |

**Khadar vs Bhangar:**

| Type | Characteristics |
|------|----------------|
| **Khadar** | New alluvium (recently deposited); found in flood plains and river beds; lighter in colour; more fertile; renewed annually |
| **Bhangar** | Old alluvium (older deposits above flood level); darker, coarser; contains **kankars** (nodules of impure calcium carbonate); slightly less fertile than Khadar |

### 2. Black Soil (Regur Soil / Black Cotton Soil)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Other names** | Regur soil, Black cotton soil |
| **Area** | ~5.46 lakh sq km (Deccan Plateau) |
| **Location** | Maharashtra (27%), Madhya Pradesh (21.3%), Gujarat (11.5%), Karnataka (9.2%), Andhra Pradesh (7.1%), Chhattisgarh |
| **Formation** | Weathering of **basaltic lava rocks** (Deccan Traps) — Cretaceous period volcanic eruptions |
| **Key characteristic** | **Self-ploughing** — clay mineral **montmorillonite** swells when wet (retains moisture) and develops wide, deep cracks when dry (soil particles fall into cracks, naturally aerating soil) |
| **Nutrients** | Rich in calcium carbonate, iron, magnesium, alumina; **poor in nitrogen, phosphorus, organic matter** |
| **Water retention** | High — holds moisture long after rains (suits cotton which needs dry harvesting period) |
| **Crops** | **Cotton** (ideal — the soil is even called "black cotton soil"), wheat, sorghum, linseed, Virginia tobacco, citrus |
| **Colour** | Dark black/grey — due to iron and titanium compounds in basalt |

### 3. Red Soil

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Peninsular India — Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Karnataka, Odisha, parts of Madhya Pradesh and Chhattisgarh |
| **Formation** | Weathering of ancient crystalline/metamorphic rocks (granites, gneisses) under hot, dry conditions |
| **Colour** | Red due to presence of **iron oxide (ferric oxide/haematite)** |
| **Nutrients** | Low fertility — poor in nitrogen, phosphorus, and humus; often needs fertilisers |
| **Texture** | Sandy to loamy; poor water retention |
| **Crops** | Groundnut, pulses, millets, cotton (with irrigation), rice (with irrigation) |
| **Sub-type** | Yellow variant found in waterlogged/humid areas — iron is hydrated, giving yellow colour |

### 4. Laterite Soil

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Areas with high rainfall and alternating wet-dry seasons — Western Ghats (Karnataka, Kerala), Assam, Meghalaya, Odisha, West Bengal (Purulia), Jharkhand |
| **Formation** | Intense **leaching** in high rainfall areas — silica, calcium, phosphorus washed out; iron and aluminium oxides (sesquioxides) accumulate, forming a hard crust |
| **Key characteristic** | **Leached soil** — silica leaches out; laterite hardens when exposed to air (used as building material — "laterite" from Latin "later" = brick) |
| **Nutrients** | Very poor in nitrogen, potash, silica, lime; acidic (pH 5.5–6.5) |
| **Crops** | **Cashew** (most suited — tolerates poor soil), tea, coffee, coconut, rubber; not suitable for food grains without heavy fertilisation |

### 5. Desert/Arid Soil

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Rajasthan (Thar Desert), parts of Gujarat and Haryana |
| **Formation** | Wind deposition (aeolian action); minimal weathering due to low rainfall |
| **Characteristics** | Sandy texture; high salinity; very low organic matter; alkaline (high pH due to calcium carbonate content) |
| **Nutrients** | Deficient in nitrogen and organic matter; some soils have high phosphate content |
| **Crops** | Drought-resistant crops with irrigation — bajra (pearl millet), barley, pulses; sparse vegetation |

### 6. Mountain Soils

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Himalayan region — J&K, Himachal Pradesh, Uttarakhand, Northeast; also Nilgiris, Western Ghats highlands |
| **Formation** | Young soils; immature (azonal); derived from weathering of nearby rocks; high organic matter but low time for weathering |
| **Characteristics** | Shallow, stony, acidic in forested areas; fertile on valley slopes |
| **Crops** | Apple, pear, peach, hops (Himachal); tea (Assam, Darjeeling); coffee (Nilgiris) |

## Land Degradation in India

### Scale of the Problem

Based on **ISRO's Desertification and Land Degradation Atlas of India** (published August 2021, data for 2018-19):

| Period | Degraded Area | % of Total Geographical Area (TGA) |
|--------|--------------|--------------------------------------|
| 2003-05 | 94.53 million ha | 28.76% |
| 2011-13 | 96.40 million ha | 29.32% |
| 2018-19 | **97.85 million ha** | **29.7%** |

India's total geographical area: **328.72 million hectares**

**Desertification (2018-19):** 83.69 million ha (up from 81.48 mha in 2003-05) — approximately **25.5%** of total land area

### Most Affected States (combined contribution to degradation/desertification):

Rajasthan, Maharashtra, Gujarat, Karnataka, Ladakh, Jharkhand, Odisha, Madhya Pradesh, and Telangana together account for **~23.79%** of the country's total degraded area.

### Types of Land Degradation

| Type | Cause | States Affected |
|------|-------|----------------|
| **Water erosion** | Rainfall, surface runoff, overland flow | Most of peninsular India, eastern India |
| **Wind erosion** | Wind action, removal of topsoil | Rajasthan, Gujarat, Haryana |
| **Waterlogging** | Poor drainage, over-irrigation | Punjab, Haryana, UP (canal command areas) |
| **Salinity/Alkalinity** | Irrigation without drainage; shallow water table | Rajasthan, Gujarat, UP, Maharashtra |
| **Gully erosion** | Concentrated runoff cutting into soil | Chambal ravines (MP-Rajasthan), UP |
| **Soil acidification** | Leaching, acid rain, excessive urea use | Northeast India, Kerala |
| **Mining/Industrial** | Open cast mining, industrial effluents | Jharkhand, Odisha, Chhattisgarh |

## UNCCD — UN Convention to Combat Desertification

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Full name** | United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification (UNCCD) |
| **Adopted** | 1994 |
| **Entered into force** | 1996 |
| **India's membership** | Yes (ratified) |
| **Secretariat** | Bonn, Germany |
| **Objective** | Combat desertification, mitigate drought effects, particularly in Africa; later expanded globally |
| **COP** | Conferences of Parties — COP 14 was held in **Greater Noida, India (2019)** — India was UNCCD COP 14 President |

### Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN)

**LDN** is a global target within UNCCD's framework for 2030 (aligned with SDG 15.3):

- **Definition:** "A state whereby the amount and quality of land resources necessary to support ecosystem functions and services and enhance food security remains stable or increases within specified temporal and spatial scales and ecosystems"
- **India's LDN target:** India committed to restoring **26 million hectares** of degraded land by 2030 (pledged at COP 14 in 2019)
- **Challenge:** Current degradation rate exceeds restoration rate

## Soil Conservation Measures

### Agricultural Practices

| Measure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Contour farming** | Ploughing along contour lines (across slopes) — reduces runoff velocity |
| **Terrace farming** | Step-like terraces on steep slopes — reduces slope length and velocity of runoff |
| **Strip cropping** | Alternating strips of crops with different erosion resistance — grasses between crop rows |
| **Cover crops** | Low-growing plants (legumes, grasses) between main crops — protect soil surface |
| **Mulching** | Covering soil with organic material — reduces evaporation and erosion |
| **Windbreaks/Shelterbelts** | Rows of trees/shrubs along field borders — reduces wind erosion |
| **Check dams** | Small structures across stream channels — slow runoff; recharge groundwater |

### Engineering Measures

| Measure | Application |
|---------|-------------|
| **Bunding** | Earthen embankments along contours — impound rainwater |
| **Gully reclamation** | Gully plugging with earthen/stone structures |
| **Drainage improvement** | Tile drainage to prevent waterlogging |

## Soil Health Card Scheme

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Launched** | **19 February 2015** (by PM Modi, at Suratgarh, Rajasthan) |
| **Ministry** | Agriculture and Farmers Welfare |
| **Parameters tested** | **12 parameters** — N, P, K (macro-nutrients); Zn, Fe, Cu, Mn, Bo (micro-nutrients); pH, EC (Electrical Conductivity), OC (Organic Carbon) |
| **Purpose** | Test farmer's soil every 3 years; provide crop-specific fertiliser recommendations |
| **Implementation** | 32 States and UTs; target: 14 crore farmers |
| **Cycle 1 (2015-17)** | 10.74 crore cards issued |
| **Cycle 2 (2017-19)** | 12.19 crore additional cards |
| **Budget** | ~₹568 crore allocated |
| **Impact** | Improved nutrient use efficiency; reduced over-application of urea |

## PMKSY and Watershed Development

### Pradhan Mantri Krishi Sinchayee Yojana (PMKSY)

Launched in 2015, PMKSY has four components — one dedicated to watershed management:

**Watershed Development Component (WDC-PMKSY):**

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Earlier scheme** | Integrated Watershed Management Programme (IWMP) merged into WDC-PMKSY |
| **Objective** | Integrated development of degraded watersheds — rainwater harvesting, soil conservation, groundwater recharge, biomass production |
| **Unit** | Watershed as the planning unit (typically 500–10,000 ha) |
| **Works** | Check dams, bunding, gully plugging, vegetation cover, groundwater recharge structures |
| **Funds** | Central: State = 60:40 (90:10 for NE/hilly states) |
| **Target** | Restore productivity of degraded lands; improve livelihoods in rainfed areas |

### Integrated Watershed Management — Key Steps

1. **Identification** of degraded watershed
2. **Treatment works** — land treatment (bunding, terracing) and drainage line treatment (check dams, gully plugging)
3. **Vegetative treatment** — plantation, grass seeding
4. **Groundwater management** — farm ponds, recharge structures
5. **Livelihood integration** — SHGs, livelihoods for landless

## Organic Farming and Soil Health

### National Mission for Sustainable Agriculture (NMSA)

- **Focus:** Climate-resilient agriculture; soil health management; resource conservation
- **Components:** Soil health management, rain-fed area development, climate change adaptation
- **Paramparagat Krishi Vikas Yojana (PKVY):** Organic farming clusters under NMSA
- **One Lakh Village Program:** Natural farming expansion
- **Zero Budget Natural Farming (ZBNF):** PM's promotion; Andhra Pradesh leading practitioner

### Soil Organic Carbon (SOC)

- Indian soils are generally **low in organic carbon** (most soils <0.5% OC vs ideal >1%)
- Building SOC through compost, green manure, crop residue management is essential
- SOC is also a **carbon sink** — improving soils contributes to climate change mitigation

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:** Focus on — soil types and their regions (Khadar/Bhangar distinction, self-ploughing of black soil, laterite leaching, cashew on laterite), ISRO land degradation figure (97.85 mha or 29.7%), UNCCD secretariat (Bonn), COP 14 location (Greater Noida, 2019), India's LDN target (26 million ha by 2030), Soil Health Card launch date (19 February 2015), number of parameters (12), PMKSY watershed component.

**For Mains (GS Paper I/III):** Key themes:
1. "India's land degradation crisis — causes, extent, and the role of watershed management"
2. "Compare the major soil types of India — distribution, characteristics, and agricultural significance" (GS I)
3. "Soil Health Card Scheme — objectives, implementation, and impact on sustainable agriculture" (GS III)
4. "India's commitment to Land Degradation Neutrality by 2030 — assess challenges and progress"
5. **GS I + III linkage:** Soil geography (GS I physical) connects to agricultural productivity, food security, and government schemes (GS III)

**Mnemonics:**
- Soil types: **"All Black Rocks Look Desert-hot"** (Alluvial, Black, Red, Laterite, Desert, Mountain)
- CLORPT factors: **"Climate-Life-Organic matter-Relief-Parent material-Time"**
- Black soil crops: **"Cotton Wheat Sorghum Tobacco"** (CWST)
- ISRO degradation: **"97.85 million hectares = 29.7% of India"**
- Soil Health Card parameters: **"3 macro-nutrients + 5 micro-nutrients + 3 physical/chemical = 11 params → actually 12"** (N, P, K, Zn, Fe, Cu, Mn, Bo, pH, EC, OC, Sulphur — some versions add S)
