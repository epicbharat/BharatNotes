## Classification of Indian Rivers

India's rivers can be classified on multiple bases: origin, nature of flow, and direction of discharge. Understanding these distinctions is essential for UPSC Geography.

### Himalayan vs Peninsular Rivers

| Feature | Himalayan Rivers | Peninsular Rivers |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Origin** | Glaciers and snowfields in the Himalayas | Western Ghats, Central Highlands, plateaus |
| **Nature of flow** | Perennial — fed by both glacial melt and rainfall | Mostly seasonal — dependent on monsoon rainfall (except Narmada and Tapi which are perennial due to rift valley) |
| **Age** | Younger — still actively cutting their valleys | Older — have reached mature or old stage with graded profiles |
| **Valley type** | Deep V-shaped gorges in upper course; wide floodplains in lower course | Shallow, broad valleys with gentle gradients |
| **Meanders** | Prominent in the plains; form oxbow lakes | Less prominent; rivers flow in comparatively straight courses |
| **Delta** | Large deltas (e.g., Sundarbans) | Both deltas (Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri) and estuaries (Narmada, Tapi) |
| **Drainage pattern** | Antecedent and consequent | Superimposed and consequent |

### Perennial vs Seasonal Rivers

**Perennial rivers** receive water throughout the year from glaciers (in winter/summer) and monsoon rainfall. The Ganga, Indus, and Brahmaputra systems are perennial. **Seasonal rivers** depend entirely on the monsoon and may reduce to trickles or dry up in summer — most peninsular rivers like the Krishna and Godavari fall in this category, though they carry enormous volumes during the monsoon season.

### East-flowing vs West-flowing Rivers

Most major Indian rivers flow eastward into the Bay of Bengal. Only two large peninsular rivers — the Narmada and the Tapi — flow westward into the Arabian Sea. Several smaller west-flowing rivers originate on the western slopes of the Western Ghats (e.g., Sharavathi, Periyar, Bharathapuzha). The reason for the westward flow of Narmada and Tapi is discussed separately below.

## The Indus River System

The Indus is one of the longest rivers in Asia, with a total length of approximately 3,180 km, of which about 709 km lies within India. It originates near the Bokhar Chu glacier in the Kailash range, close to Lake Mansarovar in Tibet (known there as Singi Khamban, meaning "Lion's Mouth"). Its total drainage area is approximately 11,20,000 sq km.

### Course

The Indus enters India in Ladakh (Union Territory of Jammu & Kashmir), flows northwest between the Ladakh and Zaskar ranges, then turns south and enters Pakistan through Gilgit-Baltistan. It finally drains into the Arabian Sea near Karachi.

### Major Tributaries

| Tributary | Origin | Length (approx.) | Key Facts |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Jhelum** | Verinag Spring, Kashmir | 725 km | Flows through Dal Lake and Wular Lake; enters Pakistan near Muzaffarabad |
| **Chenab** | Bara Lacha Pass (confluence of Chandra and Bhaga), Himachal Pradesh | 1,180 km | Largest tributary of the Indus by water volume; known as Chandrabhaga in the upper course |
| **Ravi** | Kullu hills near Rohtang Pass, Himachal Pradesh | 720 km | Flows through Chamba valley; forms part of the India-Pakistan border |
| **Beas** | Beas Kund near Rohtang Pass, Himachal Pradesh | 470 km | Entirely within India; joins the Sutlej at Harike in Punjab |
| **Sutlej** | Rakas Lake near Mansarovar, Tibet | 1,450 km | Longest of the five Punjab rivers; antecedent river cutting through the Himalayas; Bhakra Dam is built on it |

The five tributaries (Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej) together form the Panjnad ("five rivers") before joining the Indus in Pakistan. The right-bank tributaries of the Indus include the Shyok, Gilgit, Kabul, Kurram, and Gomal rivers.

### Indus Waters Treaty, 1960

The Indus Waters Treaty (IWT) was signed on 19 September 1960 in Karachi by Indian Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru and Pakistani President Ayub Khan, with the World Bank as a signatory and mediator. Negotiations lasted nine years.

**River allocation under the Treaty:**

| Category | Rivers | Allocated To | Permitted Uses by Other Party |
|----------|--------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| **Western Rivers** | Indus, Jhelum, Chenab | Pakistan | India allowed limited use for domestic, non-consumptive, and agricultural purposes; run-of-river hydropower permitted with restrictions |
| **Eastern Rivers** | Ravi, Beas, Sutlej | India | Pakistan allowed limited agricultural use from existing canals during a transition period |

India received control of roughly 20% of the total water carried by the six rivers, while Pakistan received about 80%.

**Suspension in 2025:** Following the Pahalgam terrorist attack of 22 April 2025 that killed 26 tourists, India suspended the Indus Waters Treaty on 23 April 2025, citing Pakistan's support for cross-border terrorism. The suspension remains in place as of March 2026, though a ceasefire was announced on 10 May 2025 following Operation Sindoor. This is a significant development for UPSC current affairs.

## The Ganga River System

The Ganga is the most important river system of India, with a total length of approximately 2,525 km. Its basin covers about 8,61,452 sq km — nearly 26% of India's total geographical area — and supports almost half of the country's population.

### Origin and Course

The Ganga originates as the **Bhagirathi** from the Gangotri Glacier at Gaumukh, at an elevation of 3,892 m in Uttarakhand. The Bhagirathi meets the **Alaknanda** at **Devprayag**, and from this confluence the river is known as the Ganga.

The Alaknanda is the longer source stream (hydrologically), while the Bhagirathi is considered the source in Hindu mythology. The five confluences (Panch Prayag) of Uttarakhand are: Vishnuprayag, Nandprayag, Karnaprayag, Rudraprayag, and Devprayag.

After emerging from the hills at Haridwar, the Ganga flows southeast across the Indo-Gangetic Plain through Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, enters West Bengal, and splits into two distributaries: the **Bhagirathi-Hooghly** (flowing through Kolkata to the Bay of Bengal) and the main stream that enters Bangladesh as the **Padma**.

### Major Tributaries

| Tributary | Bank | Origin | Key Facts |
|-----------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Yamuna** | Right | Yamunotri Glacier, Uttarakhand | Longest tributary of Ganga (~1,376 km); joins at Prayagraj (Sangam); its own tributaries include Chambal, Betwa, Ken, Sindh |
| **Son** | Right | Amarkantak Plateau, Madhya Pradesh | Major right-bank tributary in Bihar; joins near Patna |
| **Ghaghra (Karnali)** | Left | Mapchachungo Glacier, Tibet | Enters India in Uttarakhand; joins Ganga at Chapra, Bihar |
| **Gandak** | Left | Nepal Himalayas (near Tibet border) | Known as Narayani in Nepal; joins Ganga near Hajipur, Bihar |
| **Kosi** | Left | Near Mount Everest, Nepal | Known as the "Sorrow of Bihar" due to frequent course changes and flooding; joins Ganga near Kursela |
| **Gomti** | Left | Pilibhit, Uttar Pradesh | Joins Ganga at Varanasi |
| **Damodar** | Right (via Hooghly) | Chota Nagpur Plateau, Jharkhand | Known as "Sorrow of Bengal"; now tamed by the Damodar Valley Corporation (DVC) dams |

### Namami Gange Programme

Launched in June 2014, the Namami Gange Programme is a flagship initiative for effective pollution abatement, conservation, and rejuvenation of the River Ganga and its tributaries. Originally allotted Rs 20,000 crore, it was extended as Namami Gange Mission-II with a budgetary outlay of Rs 22,500 crore till 2026. Total budgetary allocation from 2014-15 to 2025-26 stands at Rs 26,824.86 crore. Key components include sewage treatment infrastructure, riverfront development, biodiversity conservation, and industrial effluent monitoring.

## The Brahmaputra River System

The Brahmaputra is one of the longest rivers in Asia, stretching over 2,900 km across Tibet, India, and Bangladesh. It has three distinct names along its course: **Yarlung Tsangpo** in Tibet, **Siang/Dihang** in Arunachal Pradesh, and **Brahmaputra** in Assam. In Bangladesh, it is called the **Jamuna**.

### Origin and Course

The Brahmaputra originates from the Chemayungdung Glacier near Lake Mansarovar in southwestern Tibet, roughly 100 km southeast of the lake. In Tibet, it flows eastward for approximately 1,100 km between the main Himalayan range and the Kailas Range.

Near Namcha Barwa (7,756 m), the river takes a dramatic U-turn, carving the world's deepest gorge (the Yarlung Tsangpo Grand Canyon), and enters India near the village of **Gelling** in Arunachal Pradesh. After flowing approximately 35 km southward, it is joined by the **Dibang** and **Lohit** rivers, and from this point is called the Brahmaputra.

### Key Characteristics

- **Braided channel:** In Assam, the Brahmaputra forms a braided river, often 10-20 km wide during floods, with numerous mid-channel islands (chars/chaporis). **Majuli** in Assam, formed by the Brahmaputra, is one of the largest river islands in the world.
- **High sediment load:** Carries enormous quantities of sediment, making the riverbed rise and causing frequent floods and channel shifts.
- **Floods:** The Brahmaputra basin is one of the most flood-prone regions in India, particularly in Assam.

### Major Tributaries in India

| Tributary | Bank | Key Facts |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| **Subansiri** | Left | Known as the "Gold River"; largest tributary of the Brahmaputra in India |
| **Manas** | Left | Originates in Bhutan; forms the Manas National Park (UNESCO World Heritage Site) along its banks |
| **Teesta** | Left | Originates in Sikkim (Tso Lhamo Lake); subject of India-Bangladesh water-sharing discussions |
| **Dibang** | Left | Joins near Sadiya; drains Arunachal Pradesh |
| **Lohit** | Left | Enters India from China; joins near Sadiya |
| **Dhansiri** | Right | Flows through Nagaland and Assam |
| **Kopili** | Right | Important for hydropower in Meghalaya-Assam |

## Major Peninsular Rivers

### Godavari — Dakshin Ganga

The Godavari is the largest peninsular river, with a length of approximately 1,465 km and a drainage basin of about 3,12,812 sq km — the largest river basin in peninsular India. It originates near **Trimbakeshwar** in Nashik district, Maharashtra, at an elevation of about 920 m.

**States:** Maharashtra, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Odisha (basin also extends to Madhya Pradesh, Karnataka, and Puducherry).

**Major tributaries:** Purna, Manjira (longest tributary), Pranhita (largest tributary by basin area, covering ~34% of Godavari's drainage), Indravati, Wainganga, Penganga, Sabari.

**Key dams:** Jayakwadi Dam (Maharashtra), Sriram Sagar (Telangana), Polavaram Dam (Andhra Pradesh — under construction on the Godavari).

### Krishna

The Krishna is the second largest east-flowing peninsular river, with a length of approximately 1,400 km. It originates at **Mahabaleshwar** in the Western Ghats, Maharashtra, at an elevation of about 1,337 m, and drains into the Bay of Bengal in Andhra Pradesh.

**States:** Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh.

**Major tributaries:** Bhima, Tungabhadra, Koyna, Ghatprabha, Malaprabha, Musi.

**Key dams:** Nagarjuna Sagar (Telangana-Andhra Pradesh border), Srisailam (Andhra Pradesh), Almatti (Karnataka), Koyna Dam (Maharashtra).

### Kaveri (Cauvery)

The Kaveri originates at **Talakaveri** in the Brahmagiri range of the Western Ghats, Karnataka, at an elevation of 1,341 m. It has a length of approximately 800 km and a basin area of about 81,155 sq km.

**States:** Karnataka, Tamil Nadu (basin also touches Kerala and Puducherry).

**Major tributaries:** Harangi, Hemavati, Kabini, Shimsha (left bank); Arkavathy, Bhavani, Noyyal, Amaravati (right bank).

**Key features:** The river forms the famous **Shivasamudram Falls** in Karnataka (one of India's first hydroelectric power stations, established 1902) and the fertile **Kaveri Delta** (historically called the "Granary of South India") in Tamil Nadu.

### Mahanadi

The Mahanadi rises in the **Sihawa hills** of Dhamtari district, Chhattisgarh, at an elevation of about 442 m. It has a length of approximately 858 km and a basin area of about 1,41,600 sq km.

**States:** Chhattisgarh, Odisha (basin also extends to Jharkhand, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh).

**Major tributaries:** Seonath, Hasdeo, Mand, Ib, Jonk, Tel.

**Key dam:** Hirakud Dam (Odisha) — one of the longest dams in the world.

### Narmada

The Narmada originates at **Amarkantak** in the Maikal range of the Vindhyan hills, Madhya Pradesh, at an elevation of about 1,057 m. It is approximately 1,312 km long, with a basin area of about 98,796 sq km.

**States:** Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Gujarat.

**Key features:** Flows westward through a rift valley between the Vindhya and Satpura ranges; forms an estuary (not a delta) at the Gulf of Khambhat.

**Major tributaries:** Tawa (largest tributary), Hiran, Barna, Kolar, Dudhi, Shakkar.

**Key dam:** Sardar Sarovar Dam (Gujarat), Indira Sagar Dam (Madhya Pradesh).

### Tapi (Tapti)

The Tapi originates near **Multai** in Betul district, Madhya Pradesh, at an elevation of about 752 m. It is approximately 724 km long and flows parallel to the Narmada, roughly 50-60 km to its south.

**States:** Madhya Pradesh (282 km), Maharashtra (228 km), Gujarat (214 km).

**Major tributaries:** Purna, Girna, Panjhra.

**Key features:** Flows westward through a rift valley between the Satpura range and the Ajanta hills; drains into the Arabian Sea at the Gulf of Khambhat near Surat.

## Why Do Narmada and Tapi Flow Westward?

Most major peninsular rivers flow eastward because the general slope of the Indian Peninsula is from west to east. However, the Narmada and Tapi are notable exceptions. The explanation lies in geology:

1. **Rift valley origin:** Both rivers flow through linear rift valleys (grabens) — blocks of the Earth's crust that subsided between parallel faults during tectonic activity associated with the Himalayan orogeny. The Narmada flows between the Vindhya Range (north) and the Satpura Range (south), while the Tapi flows between the Satpura Range (north) and the Ajanta Range (south).

2. **Fault-controlled course:** Two major faults — the Narmada North Fault and Narmada South Fault — run parallel to the river's course, marking the boundaries of the subsided block. The valley floor slopes westward, directing the rivers toward the Arabian Sea.

3. **No delta formation:** Because they flow through narrow, fault-bounded valleys, the Narmada and Tapi do not form deltas. Instead, they form estuaries at their mouths — a distinguishing feature from east-flowing rivers.

**Other west-flowing rivers** (Sabarmati, Mahi, Luni, Sharavathi, Periyar, Bharathapuzha) flow westward because they originate on the western slopes of the Western Ghats, which slope steeply toward the Arabian Sea.

## Drainage Patterns

A drainage pattern is the arrangement of a river and its tributaries, determined by the slope, underlying rock structure, and tectonic history of an area.

| Pattern | Shape | Controlling Factor | Indian Example |
|---------|-------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Dendritic** | Tree-like, branching in all directions | Uniform rock type, gentle slope | Northern plains (Ganga and its tributaries); parts of Godavari basin |
| **Trellis** | Main stream with tributaries joining at right angles | Alternating bands of hard and soft rock (fold mountains) | Himalayan rivers in the upper course; rivers of the Jhelum valley |
| **Radial** | Streams radiating outward from a central high point | Dome-shaped or conical hills/volcanoes | Rivers draining the Amarkantak Plateau (Narmada, Son, Mahanadi originate nearby); streams off Mount Girnar (Gujarat) |
| **Rectangular** | Right-angle bends in the main stream with tributaries joining at right angles | Strong jointing or faulting in bedrock | Parts of the Vindhyan Plateau; Kosi River in parts of its course |
| **Centripetal** | Streams converging inward toward a central depression | Enclosed basins, craters, valleys | Streams draining into Loktak Lake (Manipur); Imphal Valley |
| **Annular** | Ring-shaped pattern around a dome | Dome structure with alternating hard/soft rock layers | Parts of the Nilgiri Hills |
| **Parallel** | Streams flowing roughly parallel to each other | Steep, uniform slopes | Rivers of the Western Ghats' western slope (Sharavathi, Netravati) |

## Interstate Water Disputes

Water disputes between Indian states arise because rivers cross state boundaries, and water is a State subject (Entry 17, State List) while interstate rivers fall under Union jurisdiction (Entry 56, Union List). Article 262 of the Constitution empowers Parliament to legislate on interstate water disputes.

### Interstate River Water Disputes Act, 1956

Enacted under Article 262, this Act provides the mechanism for resolving disputes:

- A state government may request the Central Government to refer a dispute to a tribunal.
- If negotiations fail, the Centre constitutes a **Water Disputes Tribunal** under the Act.
- The tribunal's decision is final and binding (not appealable in the Supreme Court under Section 11, though the SC has intervened via its original jurisdiction under Article 131 or by issuing writs).
- **2002 Amendment:** Incorporated recommendations of the Sarkaria Commission; mandated a 1-year time frame for setting up a tribunal and a 3-year time frame for completing its proceedings.

## Interlinking of Rivers

### National Perspective Plan (1980)

The Government of India formulated the National Perspective Plan (NPP) for Water Resources Development in 1980 to transfer water from surplus basins to deficit basins. It has two components:

**Himalayan Rivers Development Component (14 links):** Envisages construction of storage dams on principal tributaries of the Ganga and Brahmaputra in India, Nepal, and Bhutan, with interlinking canals to transfer surplus flows of eastern Ganga tributaries to the west, and link the Brahmaputra system with the Ganga, and further with the Mahanadi.

**Peninsular Rivers Development Component (16 links):** Involves interlinking Mahanadi-Godavari-Krishna-Kaveri rivers, transferring surplus water from the Mahanadi and Godavari to water-scarce southern basins.

The **National Water Development Agency (NWDA)** has been entrusted with implementation. As of 2025, Pre-Feasibility Reports have been completed for all 30 link projects, Feasibility Reports for 26 projects, and Detailed Project Reports (DPRs) for 11 projects.

### Key Interlinking Projects

| Project | Rivers Linked | Status | Key Facts |
|---------|--------------|--------|-----------|
| **Ken-Betwa Link** | Ken (MP) to Betwa (UP) — both Yamuna tributaries | Foundation stone laid 25 December 2024; under construction | First project under the NPP; estimated cost Rs 45,000 crore; 8-year timeline; includes Daudhan Dam; will provide drinking water to 62 lakh people; 100 MW hydropower + 27 MW solar; concerns over impact on Panna Tiger Reserve |
| **Par-Tapi-Narmada Link** | Par, Tapi, Narmada (Gujarat) | Deferred/scrapped by Gujarat in 2022 due to tribal protests; DPR presented in Parliament in 2025 revived concerns | Estimated cost Rs 10,211 crore; 61 villages and 2,509 families to be affected; aimed at diverting water to Kutch and Saurashtra |
| **Godavari-Krishna Link** | Godavari surplus to Krishna basin | Feasibility study completed | Part of the Peninsular Component; Polavaram Dam on Godavari designed as a key storage point |
| **Mahanadi-Godavari Link** | Mahanadi surplus to Godavari basin | DPR stage | Part of the Peninsular Component |

## Vocabulary

### Tributary
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈtrɪbjʊtəri/
- **Definition:** A stream or river that flows into a larger river or lake, rather than directly into the sea.
- **Origin:** From Latin *tribūtārius* ("paying tribute"), from *tribūtum* ("tribute"), from *tribuere* ("to assign, divide among tribes"), from *tribus* ("tribe").

### Meander
- **Pronunciation:** /miˈændər/
- **Definition:** A sinuous curve or loop in a river's course, formed by lateral erosion and deposition as the river flows across a floodplain.
- **Origin:** From Latin *Maeander*, from Greek *Maiandros*, the ancient name of the Menderes River in southwestern Turkey, renowned for its winding course.

### Estuary
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈɛstjʊəri/
- **Definition:** A semi-enclosed coastal body of water with a free connection to the open sea, within which seawater is measurably diluted by freshwater from land drainage.
- **Origin:** From Latin *aestuārium* ("tidal inlet of the sea"), from *aestus* ("tide, heat, surge").

## Key Terms

### Indus Waters Treaty
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈɪndəs ˈwɔːtərz ˈtriːti/
- **Definition:** A water-sharing treaty signed on 19 September 1960 in Karachi between Indian PM Jawaharlal Nehru and Pakistani President Ayub Khan, brokered by the World Bank after nine years of negotiations, allocating the three eastern rivers (Ravi, Beas, Sutlej) to India and the three western rivers (Indus, Jhelum, Chenab) to Pakistan — giving India control of roughly 20% and Pakistan about 80% of the combined river waters.
- **Context:** The treaty is named after the Indus River system and emerged to resolve post-Partition water disputes. On 23 April 2025, India suspended the treaty, citing Pakistan's support for cross-border terrorism following the Pahalgam attack (22 April 2025) that killed 26 tourists. India stopped water flow on the Chenab from the Baglihar Dam and carried out off-season reservoir flushing at Salal and Baglihar projects. India's Home Minister stated the treaty will "never" be restored and water will be diverted for domestic use. Pakistan has called the suspension "weaponizing water." The term "suspension" does not appear in the treaty text — Article XII stipulates modification or termination only by mutual agreement.
- **UPSC Relevance:** GS1 Geography and GS2 International Relations. Prelims frequently tests which rivers are allocated to India vs Pakistan and the World Bank's role. Mains 2012 asked to evaluate whether a review of the treaty is in India's best interests. The 2025 suspension is a live current affairs topic connecting geography, diplomacy, and security — expect Mains questions on the legality of suspension, water as a geopolitical tool, and India's rights over western rivers under the treaty's limited-use provisions.

### Drainage Pattern
- **Pronunciation:** /ˈdreɪnɪdʒ ˈpætərn/
- **Definition:** The spatial arrangement of a river and its tributaries as determined by the slope, underlying rock structure, and tectonic history of an area, classified into distinct types including dendritic (tree-like branching on uniform slopes), trellis (tributaries joining at right angles in folded rock terrain), radial (streams radiating outward from a central elevated point), centripetal (streams converging into a depression or basin), and rectangular (right-angle bends following joints and faults in bedrock).
- **Context:** "Drainage" from Old French *drainer* ("to drain"), from Frankish *drahnjan*; "pattern" from Middle English *patron*, from Old French, meaning a model or template. In India, dendritic patterns are seen in the Ganga system on the uniform alluvial northern plains; radial patterns at Amarkantak where the Narmada, Son, and Mahanadi originate and flow in different directions; centripetal patterns at Sambhar Lake (Rajasthan) and Loktak Lake (Manipur); trellis patterns in the folded Singhbhum region of the Chota Nagpur Plateau; and rectangular patterns in the jointed rocks of the Vindhyan mountains.
- **UPSC Relevance:** GS1 Geography. Prelims tests matching drainage patterns with their definitions and Indian examples — a frequently asked match-the-following format. Mains expects explanation of how underlying geology (rock type, joints, faults, slope) determines drainage patterns, with specific examples. For high-scoring Mains answers, connect pattern type to rock structure: dendritic develops on homogeneous rock, trellis on alternating hard-soft bands, radial on dome-shaped or conical hills, and centripetal in enclosed basins.
