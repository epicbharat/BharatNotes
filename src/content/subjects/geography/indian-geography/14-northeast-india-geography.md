## Overview

Northeast India — comprising the **Eight Sisters** (Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Tripura, and Sikkim) — occupies approximately 262,184 km² (~8% of India's area). This chapter focuses on the **physical and economic geography** of the region in the context of Act East Policy, biodiversity conservation, and development programmes. The region's geography is defined by three physiographic elements: the **Eastern Himalayas**, the **Brahmaputra floodplain**, and the **Patkai-Naga-Lushai hill arc**.

---

## Physical Features

### 1. The Brahmaputra Valley

The Brahmaputra — one of the world's great rivers — enters India from Tibet through the Dihang Gorge (Arunachal Pradesh) after cutting a dramatic gorge through the Himalayas. In Assam it flows ~916 km as a braided river across a wide alluvial floodplain (~80–100 km wide), carrying an enormous sediment load (~735 million tonnes/year). This valley is:

- India's **most earthquake-prone region** — Seismic Zone V; the 1950 Assam-Tibet earthquake (M 8.6) raised the riverbed by several metres, permanently worsening flood behaviour
- The site of **Majuli**, the world's largest river island (~880 km²), though its area has been shrinking due to bank erosion
- The breadbasket of the Northeast: paddy, jute, tea (Assam provides ~52% of India's tea)

### 2. Patkai-Naga-Manipur Hills

A continuous arc of hill ranges forming the India-Myanmar border running from Arunachal Pradesh in the north to Mizoram in the south. Key features:

| Range | Location | Max Elevation |
|-------|----------|--------------|
| Patkai Bum | Arunachal Pradesh-Myanmar border | ~2,000 m |
| Naga Hills | Nagaland | ~3,826 m (Saramati — Nagaland's highest peak) |
| Barail Range | Assam-Nagaland border | ~1,961 m |
| Manipur Hills | Manipur | ~2,994 m (Iso Peak) |
| Mizo (Lushai) Hills | Mizoram | ~2,157 m (Phawngpui — Blue Mountain) |

### 3. Meghalaya Plateau

The Meghalaya (Shillong) Plateau is a detached block of the Precambrian Peninsular Plateau, separated from the Chhota Nagpur Plateau by the Garo-Rajmahal Gap. It receives among the highest rainfall in the world:
- **Mawsynram**: ~11,872 mm average annual rainfall — world's wettest place
- **Cherrapunji (Sohra)**: holds the record for most rain in a single calendar year (~26,471 mm, 1860–61)

The funnel-shaped topography channels the Bay of Bengal moisture directly into the plateau, explaining the extreme rainfall.

| River | Source | Key Feature |
|-------|--------|-------------|
| **Subansiri** | Tibet (Tibetan Plateau) | Largest tributary by flow; Lower Subansiri Hydroelectric Project (2,000 MW) |

| **Manas** | Bhutan Himalayas | Crosses Bhutan-Assam border; flows through Manas National Park |
| **Teesta** | Kangchenjunga area, Sikkim | Flows through Sikkim; bilateral water-sharing dispute with Bangladesh |
| **Barak** | Manipur hills | Drains southern Assam; enters Bangladesh as Surma and Kushiyara |

## Biodiversity — Indo-Burma Hotspot

Northeast India falls within the **Indo-Burma Biodiversity Hotspot** — one of the world's 36 recognised hotspots (Conservation International). The region's high biodiversity is driven by the convergence of the Eastern Himalayas, the Indo-Gangetic plain, and the Southeast Asian biome.

### Key Protected Areas

| Park / Reserve | State | UNESCO / Special Status | Key Species |
|---------------|-------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Kaziranga NP** | Assam | UNESCO World Heritage (1985) | ~2,600+ Indian one-horned rhinoceros (~70% world population); Bengal tiger; Asian elephant |
| **Manas NP** | Assam | UNESCO World Heritage (1992) | Pygmy hog (world's smallest wild pig); golden langur; Bengal florican |
| **Namdapha NP** | Arunachal Pradesh | — | Only Indian park with all 4 big cats (tiger, leopard, snow leopard, clouded leopard) |
| **Keibul Lamjao NP** | Manipur | — | World's only floating national park; Loktak Lake; Sangai (Eld's deer) |
| **Nokrek NP** | Meghalaya | UNESCO Biosphere Reserve | Red panda; Hoolock gibbon; wild citrus (*Citrus indica*) |

The **Hoolock Gibbon** — India's only ape — is found across Assam, Meghalaya, Arunachal Pradesh, Nagaland, Manipur, Mizoram, and Tripura. It is classified as Endangered (Western Hoolock) and Vulnerable (Eastern Hoolock) on the IUCN Red List.

## Act East Policy — Geographic Connectivity

India's **Act East Policy** (upgraded from "Look East" in 2014) positions Northeast India as its gateway to Southeast Asia. The geographic logic: the NE shares 4,500+ km of international borders with 5 countries (China, Myanmar, Bangladesh, Bhutan, Nepal) and is proximate to ASEAN nations via Myanmar.

| Connectivity Project | Route | Strategic/Economic Significance |
|---------------------|-------|--------------------------------|
| **IMT Trilateral Highway** | Moreh (Manipur) → Tamu → Mandalay → Mae Sot (Thailand), ~1,360 km | Connects India to ASEAN overland; ~70% complete; delayed by Myanmar instability |
| **Kaladan Multimodal Project** | Kolkata → Sittwe (sea) → Paletwa (river) → Mizoram (road) | Bypasses the Bangladesh corridor; connects Mizoram to the sea; cost ~$484 million |
| **Agartala-Akhaura Rail Link** | Agartala (Tripura) → Akhaura (Bangladesh), 12.24 km | Completed 2023; reduces Agartala-Kolkata rail time from ~31 hours to ~10 hours |
| **Bogibeel Bridge** | Dibrugarh-Dhemaji (Assam), 4.94 km over Brahmaputra | India's longest rail-cum-road bridge; opened 2018; cuts military mobilisation time to Arunachal Pradesh |

## North East Industrial Development Scheme (NEIDS), 2017

The **NEIDS 2017** was launched by the Ministry of Commerce & Industry to promote industrialisation in the NE:

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Coverage** | All 8 NE states: Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Sikkim, Tripura |
| **Eligible sectors** | Manufacturing and services (including Biotechnology and Hydel Power up to 10 MW) |
| **Investment cap** | Incentives capped at total investment in plant & machinery, subject to a maximum of Rs 200 crore per unit |
| **Operative period** | 01 April 2017 to 31 March 2022 (subsequently reviewed) |
| **Key incentives** | Capital investment incentive (30% of investment), Central Capital Interest Subvention, GST reimbursement, insurance subsidy |

NEIDS replaced the older North East Industrial & Investment Promotion Policy (NEIIPP), 2007, with a more streamlined incentive structure. The scheme acknowledges the geographic handicap of NE states — high transport costs, terrain challenges, small market size — which make industrial investment unviable without subsidies.

## Insurgency Geography

The physical geography of NE India — dense forests, rugged hill terrain, limited road access, and multiple international borders — has historically provided operational space for insurgent groups:

- **ULFA (United Liberation Front of Asom)**: Assam's plains and forests; anti-migration and sovereignty movement; ULFA(P) — pro-talks faction — signed peace accord framework 2022
- **NSCN (Nagaland)**: Naga Hills; Indo-Myanmar border terrain; Naga peace talks ongoing since Framework Agreement 2015
- **Arakan Army (Myanmar)**: While not Indian, its control of the Arakan region has disrupted the Kaladan Multimodal Project route

The **Free Movement Regime (FMR)** — which allowed border tribes to cross 16 km without a visa along the India-Myanmar border — was suspended in 2024 as India fenced the border to check infiltration and insurgent movement.
