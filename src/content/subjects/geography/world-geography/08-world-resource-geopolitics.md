## Overview

The 21st century's defining geopolitical conflicts are increasingly fought not over territory but over **resources** — critical minerals powering the clean energy transition, fossil fuels sustaining industrial economies, freshwater for food and survival, and agricultural commodities feeding billions. For UPSC, resource geopolitics bridges GS1 (World Geography), GS2 (International Relations), and GS3 (Energy, Environment). This chapter maps the geography of critical resources and the geopolitical fault lines they create.

## Critical Minerals Geopolitics

### What Are Critical Minerals?

Critical minerals are those essential to modern technology and the clean energy transition — electric vehicles, solar panels, wind turbines, semiconductors, and defence equipment — for which supply chains are geographically concentrated in a few countries, creating strategic vulnerability.

### Rare Earth Elements (REEs)

- **What:** 17 elements including lanthanides — neodymium, dysprosium, lanthanum, cerium, etc.
- **Uses:** Permanent magnets (EV motors, wind turbines), display screens, defence guidance systems
- **China's dominance:** China accounted for approximately **69% of global rare earth mine production** in 2024 (USGS data). Beyond mining, China controls ~91% of global REE separation and refining and ~94% of sintered permanent magnet production (IEA, 2024)
- **Reserves:** China holds ~38% of proven reserves; but other large reserves exist in Vietnam, Russia, Brazil, India (Odisha, Kerala monazite sands)
- **China's leverage:** In 2023–2024, China imposed export controls on gallium, germanium, and graphite — demonstrating willingness to weaponise mineral dominance
- **India:** Has significant REE deposits (monazite sands — Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh) but limited processing capacity

### Lithium — The "White Gold"

- **Uses:** Lithium-ion batteries — EVs, grid storage, portable electronics
- **Lithium Triangle:** Bolivia, Chile, Argentina hold approximately **56% of global lithium reserves** (FastMarkets/USGS data):
  - Bolivia: ~23 million tonnes (largest single-country reserve)
  - Argentina: ~23 million tonnes
  - Chile: ~11 million tonnes (but Chile is the second-largest producer)
- **Production leaders:** Australia is the world's largest lithium producer (hard rock spodumene); Chile is second
- **Geopolitics:** Bolivia nationalised lithium (state enterprise YLB); Argentina has federal mining disputes; Chile created a National Lithium Strategy (2023) with state role in future projects

### Cobalt

- **Uses:** EV batteries (cobalt-rich chemistries), aerospace alloys, cutting tools
- **DRC dominance:** The Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) produced approximately **76–84% of global cobalt** in 2024 (USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries 2024; Mining Technology analysis)
- **Concerns:** Artisanal and Small-scale Mining (ASM) in DRC — child labour, safety, environmental damage; geopolitical instability in eastern DRC (M23 conflict)
- **Diversification efforts:** Indonesia (laterite cobalt); Australia; Philippines; battery makers reducing cobalt content (lithium iron phosphate batteries)

### Nickel

- **Uses:** Stainless steel, EV batteries (NMC chemistry)
- **Top producers:** Indonesia (~50% of world mine production — laterite deposits), Philippines, Russia
- **Russia's role:** Russia is a major producer of high-grade Class 1 nickel — its 2022 invasion of Ukraine triggered Western discussions about nickel supply diversification

| Critical Mineral | Key Producer | India's Vulnerability |
|-----------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Rare Earth Elements | China (~69% production) | Import dependent for processed REE; own deposits underutilised |
| Lithium | Australia (production); Bolivia/Argentina (reserves) | No domestic lithium; critical for EV transition |
| Cobalt | DRC (~70-84%) | Entirely import dependent |
| Nickel | Indonesia (~50%) | Imports from Russia and Indonesia |

## Oil Geopolitics

### OPEC and OPEC+

- **OPEC (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries):** Founded 1960; 12 members (as of 2024) including Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Libya, Venezuela, Nigeria, Gabon, Equatorial Guinea, Congo, Algeria
- **OPEC+:** OPEC + 10 allied producers led by Russia — formed 2016 after oil price crash; together control ~40% of global oil production
- **Saudi Arabia:** Swing producer — adjusts output to stabilise or influence prices; world's second-largest oil producer

### Strait of Hormuz — World's Most Critical Energy Chokepoint

- **Location:** Between Iran and Oman; connects Persian Gulf to Gulf of Oman and Arabian Sea
- **Traffic:** In 2024, oil flow through the Strait of Hormuz averaged **~20 million barrels per day (b/d)** — approximately **20% of global petroleum liquids consumption** and over one-quarter of global seaborne oil trade (US EIA data)
- **LNG:** ~20% of global LNG trade also transits Hormuz (primarily Qatari LNG)
- **Vulnerability:** At its narrowest, only ~3.2 km wide for shipping lanes; any closure would trigger immediate global energy crisis
- **Iran threat:** Iran has repeatedly threatened to close the Strait during geopolitical tensions

### West Asia Instability and India

- India imports ~85% of its crude oil; West Asia supplies ~60% of India's crude imports (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE are top sources)
- Any disruption in West Asia directly impacts India's energy security, inflation, and current account deficit

## Water Geopolitics

### Nile Dispute — GERD (Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam)

- **GERD:** Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam on the Blue Nile in Ethiopia — Africa's largest hydroelectric project (~5,150 MW capacity); construction substantially completed
- **Dispute:** Ethiopia (upstream) vs Egypt (downstream) vs Sudan (midstream)
- **Egypt's position:** The Nile provides ~90% of Egypt's freshwater; any reduction in flow is existential. Egypt claims historical rights under 1929 and 1959 treaties (which Ethiopia rejects as "colonial era")
- **Ethiopia's position:** Sovereign right to develop its own natural resources; dam provides electricity to millions
- **Status (2024):** No binding agreement reached despite years of African Union-mediated talks; GERD is operational and filling continues — a major unresolved transboundary water conflict

### Mekong River — China's Dam Diplomacy

- **Mekong:** Originates in Tibetan Plateau (as Lancang River in China); flows through Yunnan, Myanmar, Laos, Thailand, Cambodia, Vietnam
- **China's dams:** China has built 11 large mainstream dams on the upper Mekong (Lancang); plans for more
- **Impact:** Downstream countries (Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam) report reduced water flow, disrupted fish migration (Mekong is one of world's most biodiverse river systems), altered sedimentation — threatening food security and livelihoods of ~60 million people
- **Mekong River Commission (MRC):** Established 1995; China is not a full member (only a "dialogue partner") — limits effectiveness

### India's Water Diplomacy

- **Indus Waters Treaty (1960):** India-Pakistan; World Bank brokered; divides rivers — Pakistan gets Indus, Jhelum, Chenab; India gets Ravi, Beas, Sutlej. India notified Pakistan of intent to modify the treaty in 2023 amid broader tensions
- **Brahmaputra/Yarlung Tsangpo:** No treaty with China; China's dam-building on upper reaches causes concern in India (flood surges, diversion fears)
- **Bangladesh:** Farakka Barrage dispute; 1996 Ganga Water Treaty; Teesta River — pending treaty since 2011 (blocked by West Bengal)

## Food Geopolitics

### Ukraine-Russia War and Global Food Security

- Ukraine and Russia together account for ~30% of global wheat exports and ~60% of global sunflower oil exports
- Russia's 2022 invasion disrupted Black Sea grain shipments, triggering a global food crisis
- Impact: Food import-dependent countries in Africa and Middle East (Egypt, Sudan, Somalia, Libya) faced severe food inflation; FAO Food Price Index hit record highs in March 2022
- **Black Sea Grain Initiative:** UN-brokered deal (July 2022) allowed grain exports from Ukraine; Russia withdrew in July 2023 — renewed disruption

## Arctic Resources and Sovereignty

- **Why the Arctic matters:** Estimated to hold **~30% of world's undiscovered natural gas** and **~13% of undiscovered oil** (USGS assessment); also rare earth deposits, fisheries, and new shipping routes
- **Arctic sea ice retreat:** Climate change is opening the **Northern Sea Route** (NSR) — cutting shipping distance from Asia to Europe by ~40% compared to Suez Canal route
- **Claims and tensions:** Arctic Council (8 members — USA, Russia, Canada, Norway, Denmark, Iceland, Finland, Sweden); Russia has the longest Arctic coastline and has aggressively asserted claims; NATO Arctic members have increased military presence
- **India's Arctic Policy:** Released in 2022 — India has a research station (Himadri) at Svalbard, Norway; interests in research, shipping routes, and resource access

## India's Critical Mineral Strategy

### KABIL — Khanij Bidesh India Limited

- **Established:** 2019; Joint Venture of NALCO, Hindustan Copper Limited (HCL), and MECL (40:30:30 ratio)
- **Under:** Ministry of Mines
- **Purpose:** Secure supply of critical and strategic minerals from overseas through acquisition, exploration, and development of mineral assets abroad

**Key KABIL bilateral agreements:**
- **Argentina:** Exploration and development contract with Argentine state company; exclusive rights for lithium blocks in Catamarca Province
- **Australia:** MoU with Critical Mineral Office (CMO) for lithium and cobalt assets
- **Chile:** NDA agreement with ENAMI for brine-type lithium exploration

### Broader India Policy

- India identified **30 critical minerals** (list released by Ministry of Mines, 2023) — lithium, cobalt, nickel, REEs, titanium, vanadium among priority
- **MoUs** signed with Australia, Chile, Zambia, Peru for mineral cooperation
- **National Critical Mineral Mission:** Announced in Union Budget 2024-25 — focuses on domestic exploration, processing capacity, and recycling
