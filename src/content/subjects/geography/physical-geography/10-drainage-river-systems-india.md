## The Drainage Divide

India's rivers drain into two major water bodies based on their direction of flow from the highlands:

| Drainage Basin | Rivers | Outlet |
|---------------|--------|--------|
| **Bay of Bengal** | Ganga, Brahmaputra, Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, Subarnarekha, Damodar | Bay of Bengal / Indian Ocean (East) |
| **Arabian Sea** | Indus (partially), Narmada, Tapi, Mahi, Sabarmati, Luni, Periyar, Sharavati | Arabian Sea (West) |

The **Western Ghats** form the principal watershed (water divide) separating the two drainage basins. The majority of India's rivers — by number and by volume — flow eastward.

---

## Classification of India's Rivers

India's rivers are broadly divided into two groups based on their origin, behaviour, and characteristics:

| Feature | Himalayan Rivers | Peninsular Rivers |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Origin** | Glaciers and snowfields in the Himalayas | Rain-fed; origin in peninsular plateaus and hills |
| **Nature** | **Perennial** — flow throughout the year | **Rain-fed (seasonal)** — swell in monsoon, reduce significantly in dry months |
| **Course** | Long; cut through young fold mountains via deep gorges (antecedent drainage) | Shorter; cross plateau surface; often have waterfalls at escarpments |
| **Valley shape** | Deep gorges (V-shaped), then wide alluvial plains | Mature valleys; broader, shallower in most reaches |
| **Sediment load** | Very high; build large deltas and floodplains | Moderate to low; hard rock erodes slowly |
| **Drainage type** | Antecedent (rivers predate mountain building) | Consequent and subsequent (follow topographic slope) |

## Himalayan River Systems

### 1. Indus System

The Indus rises from **Mansarovar Lake** (Tibet, China) near **Kailash range** at an elevation of ~5,180 m. It flows through J&K before crossing into Pakistan.

Key tributaries in India:
- **Punjab Rivers (Five Rivers)**: Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej — these are shared with Pakistan under the **Indus Waters Treaty (1960)**
- Under the treaty: India has exclusive rights over **Ravi, Beas, Sutlej** (Eastern rivers); Pakistan over **Indus, Jhelum, Chenab** (Western rivers)

### 2. Ganga System

The **Ganga** is India's largest river system by catchment area. It rises from **Gangotri Glacier** (Uttarakhand) as the Bhagirathi and is joined by the Alaknanda at **Devprayag** to form the Ganga proper.

| Tributary | Origin | States Drained | Joins Ganga at |
|-----------|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Yamuna | Yamunotri Glacier | Uttarakhand, HP, Delhi, UP | Prayagraj (Triveni Sangam) |
| Ghaghra | Himalayan glaciers (Nepal/Tibet) | Nepal, UP, Bihar | Near Chhapra |
| Gandak | Nepal Himalayas | Nepal, Bihar | Hajipur (Sonepur) |
| Kosi | Nepal Himalayas | Nepal, Bihar | Kosi Barrage area |
| Son | Amarkantak Plateau | MP, Jharkhand, UP, Bihar | Patna region |
| Chambal | Vindhya Range | MP, Rajasthan, UP | Yamuna |
| Betwa | Vindhya Range | MP, UP | Yamuna |
| Ken | Vindhya Range | MP, UP | Yamuna |
| Damodar | Chotanagpur Plateau | Jharkhand, West Bengal | Hooghly |

The Ganga enters the Bay of Bengal through a **delta** — the **Sundarbans Delta**, the world's largest delta (shared with Bangladesh).

### 3. Brahmaputra System

The **Brahmaputra** (Tsangpo in Tibet, Dihang after entering Arunachal Pradesh) is one of the world's longest rivers. It:
- Rises from **Chemayungdung glacier** in Tibet near Mansarovar
- Flows eastward across Tibet for ~1,700 km
- Makes a **hairpin bend** around Namcha Barwa and enters India through a **deep gorge** (some of the deepest gorges on Earth)
- Flows through Assam (where it is called Brahmaputra) and joins the Ganga-Padma system in Bangladesh
- Key tributaries: Subansiri, Jia Bharali, Dhansiri, Manas, Torsa, Lohit, Dibang

The Brahmaputra is notable for its **braided channels**, annual floods in Assam, and very high silt load.

## Peninsular River Systems

### Eastward-Flowing Peninsular Rivers (Bay of Bengal)

| River | Origin | Length | States | Delta/Estuary | Key Facts |
|-------|--------|--------|--------|--------------|-----------|
| **Mahanadi** | Chhattisgarh hills | ~858 km | Chhattisgarh, Odisha | Delta — Odisha coast | Hirakud Dam (longest earthen dam in India) |
| **Godavari** | Trimbakeshwar, Maharashtra | ~1,465 km | Maharashtra, Telangana, AP | Large delta — AP coast | Largest peninsular river; "Vridha Ganga" (Old Ganga) |
| **Krishna** | Mahabaleshwar, Maharashtra | ~1,400 km | Maharashtra, Karnataka, AP | Delta — AP coast | Major irrigation; Nagarjuna Sagar Dam |
| **Kaveri (Cauvery)** | Coorg, Karnataka (Talakaveri) | ~800 km | Karnataka, Tamil Nadu | Delta — TN coast | "Ganga of the South" (Dakshina Ganga); intense inter-state dispute |
| **Subarnarekha** | Ranchi, Jharkhand | ~395 km | Jharkhand, Odisha, West Bengal | Estuary | Passes through Jamshedpur |

### Westward-Flowing Peninsular Rivers (Arabian Sea)

Westward-flowing peninsular rivers are fewer but notable. They flow through rift valleys (Narmada and Tapi) rather than over the plateau surface, which is why they form **estuaries** (not deltas).

| River | Origin | Length | States | Outlet | Key Facts |
|-------|--------|--------|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Narmada** | Amarkantak, MP | ~1,312 km | MP, Maharashtra, Gujarat | Estuary — Gulf of Khambhat | Sardar Sarovar Dam; flows through **Narmada rift valley** |
| **Tapi (Tapti)** | Betul, MP (Satpura range) | ~724 km | MP, Maharashtra, Gujarat | Estuary — Gulf of Khambhat | Flows south of Narmada; rift valley origin |
| **Luni** | Pushkar hills, Rajasthan | ~495 km | Rajasthan, Gujarat | Rann of Kutch (drains into desert) | Saline in lower reaches; only significant river of Thar Desert |
| **Sabarmati** | Aravallis, Rajasthan | ~371 km | Rajasthan, Gujarat | Gulf of Khambhat | Passes through Ahmedabad |

## Drainage Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Occurrence in India |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Dendritic** | Tree-like branching; forms on uniform, flat terrain | Indo-Gangetic plains; Deccan plateau with horizontal sedimentary rocks |
| **Radial** | Rivers flow outward from a central elevated area | Amarkantak (source of Narmada, Son, Mahanadi); Girnar hills; Rajmahal hills |
| **Trellis** | Tributaries join main river at ~90°; reflects parallel ridges | Himachal Pradesh; Vindhya-Satpura region |
| **Centripetal** | Rivers flow inward toward a central depression/lake | Loktak Lake (Manipur); Chilika Lake region; Sambhar region |
| **Rectangular** | Right-angle bends; controlled by joints and faults | Deccan Plateau hard rock areas |
| **Parallel** | Several rivers flow nearly parallel to each other | Kathiawar Peninsula; Eastern Ghats region |

## Inter-State River Water Disputes

Water is a **State subject** under the Constitution (List II, Entry 17), but when disputes arise between states regarding inter-state rivers, Parliament has power under **Entry 56 of the Union List** to regulate inter-state rivers.

**Article 262**: Parliament may, by law, provide for adjudication of inter-state water disputes, and may exclude the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court.

**Inter-State River Water Disputes Act, 1956** provides for setting up of **Tribunals** for adjudication.

| Dispute | Rivers | States | Tribunal | Key Development |
|---------|--------|--------|---------|----------------|
| **Cauvery** | Cauvery and tributaries | Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Puducherry | Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (1990) — award 2007 | Supreme Court final order 2018; Cauvery Water Management Authority constituted |
| **Krishna** | Krishna | Maharashtra, Karnataka, AP, Telangana | Bachawat Tribunal (1969), Brijesh Kumar Tribunal (2004) | Final award 2010; bifurcation of AP complicates allocation |
| **Godavari** | Godavari | Maharashtra, AP, Madhya Pradesh, Karnataka, Odisha | Bachawat Tribunal (1969) | Award made; allocation agreed |
| **Mahanadi** | Mahanadi | Chhattisgarh, Odisha | Mahanadi Water Disputes Tribunal (2018) | Odisha alleges Chhattisgarh's barrages reduce flow |
| **Ravi-Beas** | Ravi, Beas | Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan | ERCP and Sutlej-Yamuna Link (SYL) controversy | SYL canal unbuilt; Punjab's Termination of Agreements Act (2004) struck down by Supreme Court |

## National River Linking Project (NRLP)

The NRLP proposes to transfer surplus water from water-rich rivers to water-deficit regions via a network of canals and reservoirs. The **National Water Development Agency (NWDA)** has identified **30 links** (16 under Peninsular Component + 14 under Himalayan Component).

**Ken-Betwa Link Project (KBLP)** — India's **first river interlinking project** to be implemented:
- Transfers surplus water from the **Ken River** (MP) to the **Betwa River** (UP) to irrigate drought-prone **Bundelkhand** region
- Benefits: Irrigation for 10.62 lakh hectares; drinking water for ~62 lakh people; 103 MW hydropower + 27 MW solar power
- Tripartite agreement signed on **World Water Day (22 March)** by MP, UP, and the Centre
- Project inaugurated; completion targeted by **2030**
- The project involves the **Daudhan Dam** on the Ken River in Panna National Park — a biodiversity concern (Tiger Reserve area)

## River Conservation Programmes

**Namami Gange Programme**: Launched in 2015 as a Flagship Programme with an outlay of ₹20,000 crore to rejuvenate the Ganga. Focus on sewage treatment, industrial effluent management, solid waste management on riverbanks, afforestation, and biodiversity conservation. Implemented by the **National Mission for Clean Ganga (NMCG)**.

**Other programmes**: National River Conservation Plan (NRCP) covers other rivers (Yamuna, Gomti, Damodar, Mahanadi, etc.) through the National River Conservation Directorate (NRCD).

## Exam Strategy Note

For **GS1 Prelims**, know the origin, mouth, length, and major tributaries of all key rivers; be clear on east vs west flowing rivers and why peninsular westward rivers form estuaries (rift origin, steep gradient). For **Mains**, river disputes (Cauvery, SYL, Ken-Betwa) connect physical geography to governance (tribunals, federalism), water security, and ecology. The Ken-Betwa project's biodiversity controversy (Panna Tiger Reserve) is excellent cross-linking material.
