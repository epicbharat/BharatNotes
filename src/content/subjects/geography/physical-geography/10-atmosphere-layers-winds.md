## Overview

The atmosphere is the gaseous envelope surrounding Earth, held in place by gravity. It is not a homogeneous gas column but is stratified into distinct layers based on temperature gradient, composition, and function. Understanding atmospheric structure is foundational to all UPSC climatology questions — from monsoon dynamics and tropical cyclones to jet streams and ozone depletion.

---

## Layers of the Atmosphere

### 1. Troposphere (0 – ~12 km)

The troposphere is the lowest and densest layer, containing ~75% of the atmosphere's mass and ~99% of its water vapour. All weather phenomena — clouds, rain, storms, cyclones — occur here.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Height** | ~8–9 km at the poles; ~16–18 km at the equator; ~12 km average |
| **Temperature trend** | Decreases with altitude at ~6.5°C per km (Normal Lapse Rate) |
| **Upper boundary** | **Tropopause** — a temperature inversion layer where temperature stabilises |
| **Key feature** | All weather events; convection; water cycle; jet streams occur at or near the tropopause |

The **Tropopause** acts as a "lid" on weather systems — thunderstorms that hit the tropopause spread laterally, creating the characteristic anvil-head shape of cumulonimbus clouds.

### 2. Stratosphere (~12 – 50 km)

The stratosphere lies above the tropopause. It contains the **ozone layer** (concentrated between 15–35 km), which absorbs harmful UV-B and UV-C radiation from the Sun. Unlike the troposphere, temperature increases with altitude in the stratosphere (because ozone absorbs solar UV).

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Height** | ~12 km to ~50 km |
| **Temperature trend** | Increases with altitude (temperature inversion) |
| **Ozone layer** | 15–35 km; ozone (O₃) absorbs UV radiation |
| **Upper boundary** | **Stratopause** (~50 km) |
| **Key feature** | Very dry; no weather; jet aircraft cruise here; site of ozone depletion (CFCs) |

### 3. Mesosphere (~50 – 85 km)

The mesosphere is the middle atmosphere, where temperature again decreases with altitude — reaching the coldest temperatures in the atmosphere (~-90°C at the mesopause).

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Height** | ~50 km to ~85 km |
| **Temperature trend** | Decreases with altitude |
| **Upper boundary** | **Mesopause** (~85 km); coldest point in the atmosphere |
| **Key feature** | Meteors burn up in the mesosphere (shooting stars); Noctilucent clouds form near the mesopause |

### 4. Thermosphere (~85 – 600 km)

The thermosphere is characterised by rapidly rising temperatures (due to absorption of extreme UV and X-rays by atomic oxygen and nitrogen), potentially exceeding 2,000°C — yet the gas is so thin that very little heat is conducted to objects within it.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Height** | ~85 km to ~600 km |
| **Temperature trend** | Increases dramatically with altitude |
| **Key sub-layer** | **Ionosphere** (60–1,000 km) — ionised gases reflect radio waves; enables long-distance radio communication; Aurora Borealis/Australis occur here |
| **ISS orbit** | International Space Station orbits at ~400 km — within the thermosphere |

### 5. Exosphere (~600 km and beyond)

The exosphere is the outermost, extremely thin fringe of the atmosphere gradually merging with interplanetary space. Atoms and molecules here are so sparse that they travel without collisions and can escape into space.

| Feature | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Height** | ~600 km to ~10,000 km |
| **Composition** | Mainly hydrogen and helium |
| **Boundary** | No distinct outer boundary — fades into space |

| Layer | Height Range | Temperature Trend | Key Feature |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Troposphere | 0–12 km | Decreases | All weather; 75% of mass |
| Stratosphere | 12–50 km | Increases | Ozone layer; no weather |
| Mesosphere | 50–85 km | Decreases | Meteors burn up |
| Thermosphere | 85–600 km | Increases | Ionosphere; radio waves; auroras |
| Exosphere | 600–10,000 km | N/A | Merges with outer space |

## Pressure Belts and Wind Belts

The Earth's surface is divided into alternating pressure belts caused by differential solar heating and Earth's rotation.

### The Seven Pressure Belts (Surface Level)

Starting from the equator and moving toward the poles:

1. **Equatorial Low (ITCZ)** — 0°: Intense solar heating causes rising air; low pressure; calm winds (Doldrums)
2. **Sub-tropical High** — 30°N and 30°S: Descending air warms and creates high pressure; desert belts (Sahara, Arabian, Australian deserts)
3. **Sub-polar Low** — 60°N and 60°S: Polar cold air meets warm westerlies; rising air; low pressure
4. **Polar High** — 90°N and 90°S: Cold, dense, descending air; high pressure

### Major Wind Belts

| Wind Belt | Pressure Zones | Direction | Key Feature |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Trade Winds** | Sub-tropical High → Equatorial Low | NE trades (N Hemisphere); SE trades (S Hemisphere) | Consistent, reliable; named "trade" as they drove sailing commerce |
| **Westerlies** | Sub-tropical High → Sub-polar Low | SW→NE (N Hemisphere); NW→SE (S Hemisphere) | Prevail in temperate zones (30°–60°); bring rainfall to west coasts |
| **Polar Easterlies** | Polar High → Sub-polar Low | NE (N Hemisphere); SE (S Hemisphere) | Cold, dry; from the poles |

## Coriolis Effect

The **Coriolis Effect** is the apparent deflection of moving objects (including air and water) due to Earth's rotation.

- In the **Northern Hemisphere**: moving objects deflect to the **right**
- In the **Southern Hemisphere**: moving objects deflect to the **left**
- At the **equator**: zero Coriolis effect (no deflection)

The Coriolis Effect explains why: trade winds blow from the NE (not due south), cyclones rotate anticlockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere, and ocean gyres circulate in opposite directions in each hemisphere.

## Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ)

The **ITCZ** is a belt of low pressure near the equator where the northeast trade winds (Northern Hemisphere) and southeast trade winds (Southern Hemisphere) converge. This convergence causes air to rise, cool, and produce heavy convective rainfall.

- The ITCZ is not a fixed line — it migrates seasonally following the thermal equator
- In summer (June-September), it shifts north over India, triggering the **southwest monsoon**
- In December-January, it moves south, bringing the **northeast monsoon** to southeast India and Sri Lanka

---

## Jet Streams

Jet streams are narrow bands of fast-moving westerly winds at high altitudes (near the tropopause, ~9–16 km). They influence weather patterns and monsoon onset.

| Type | Location | Speed | Significance |
|------|----------|-------|-------------|
| **Polar Front Jet Stream** | 60°N/S latitude; ~9–12 km | 120–240 km/h | Controls mid-latitude weather; brings storms and precipitation to temperate regions |
| **Sub-tropical Jet Stream** | ~25–30°N; ~12–15 km | 120–200 km/h | Controls India's monsoon: western arm brings dry NW winds in winter; eastern arm strengthens during monsoon |
| **Tropical Easterly Jet** | ~15°N; ~12–15 km | ~80–150 km/h | Develops over India during summer; associated with active southwest monsoon |

The **bifurcation of the sub-tropical jet stream** by the Tibetan Plateau is the key mechanism of the Indian monsoon — when the jet shifts north of the Himalayas in June, the ITCZ moves northward and the monsoon sets in over Kerala.

## Heat Budget of Earth

Earth maintains energy balance — the amount of solar radiation received equals the amount returned to space over time.

| Radiation | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Incoming solar radiation (Insolation) | 100 units (reference value) |
| Reflected by clouds and atmosphere | ~35 units (albedo) |
| Absorbed by atmosphere | ~14 units |
| Absorbed by Earth's surface | ~51 units |
| Earth surface radiation (long-wave) returned to atmosphere | ~51 units |

**Net result:** 100 units in — 100 units out (35 reflected + 65 radiated back). This balance maintains Earth's average temperature (~15°C).

**Greenhouse Effect:** Atmospheric gases (CO₂, H₂O vapour, CH₄, N₂O) absorb outgoing long-wave radiation and re-radiate it back, warming the surface. Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's surface temperature would be approximately -18°C.

**Prelims Focus:**
- Layer heights: Troposphere (0–12 km), Stratosphere (12–50 km), Mesosphere (50–85 km), Thermosphere (85–600 km)
- Ozone layer: in the stratosphere (15–35 km)

- Meteors burn up: in the mesosphere
- Auroras (Northern/Southern Lights): in the thermosphere (ionosphere)
- Coriolis deflection: right in NH, left in SH; zero at equator

- Trade Winds: NE trades (NH), SE trades (SH)
