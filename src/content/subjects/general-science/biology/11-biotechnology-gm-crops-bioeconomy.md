Biotechnology has emerged as one of the most consequential frontiers of science and policy for UPSC GS3. India is simultaneously the world's largest producer of Bt cotton, a global generics pharmaceutical powerhouse, a top vaccine manufacturer — and yet remains deeply divided on the safety and desirability of GM food crops. This chapter covers the full biotechnology landscape for UPSC: GM crops and their controversy, the regulatory architecture (GEAC, Rules 1989), CRISPR gene editing, India's rapidly growing bioeconomy, the BioE3 policy, biosafety concerns, and biopiracy cases.

## Biotechnology: Foundational Concepts

**Biotechnology** is the use of living organisms, cells, or biological systems to develop products or processes with practical applications. Modern biotechnology is largely built on the following tools:

| Tool | Concept | Application |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Recombinant DNA (rDNA) technology** | Cutting DNA at specific points (restriction enzymes) and inserting sequences from another organism | GM crops, insulin production, vaccines |
| **Transgenic organisms** | Organisms whose genome contains DNA from another species | Bt cotton (bacterial gene inserted into cotton), GM mustard |
| **Gene editing (CRISPR-Cas9)** | Precise editing of an organism's own DNA without introducing foreign DNA | Potential future crop improvement without "GMO" classification |
| **Cloning** | Producing genetically identical organisms | Livestock, therapeutic cloning (stem cells) |
| **Fermentation biotechnology** | Using microorganisms to produce products | Beer, antibiotics, biofuels, enzymes |
| **Tissue culture** | Growing plant cells/tissues in controlled conditions | Rapid propagation of disease-free plants |

## GM Crops in India

A **Genetically Modified (GM) crop** is one whose genome has been altered using recombinant DNA techniques, typically to introduce a trait from another organism (e.g., pest resistance, herbicide tolerance, drought tolerance).

### Bt Cotton: India's Only Approved GM Crop (Commercial)

**Bt cotton** is the **only GM crop approved for commercial cultivation in India**, introduced in **2002**.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Bt toxin** | Derived from the soil bacterium *Bacillus thuringiensis* (Bt); produces crystal proteins (Cry proteins) that are toxic to certain insects (Bollworm) |
| **Trait introduced** | Resistance to American bollworm (*Helicoverpa armigera*), pink bollworm (*Pectinophora gossypiella*) — major cotton pests |
| **Developer** | Mahyco-Monsanto (later Bayer) introduced Bollgard I and Bollgard II hybrids |
| **Approval** | GEAC approved Bollgard I in 2002; Bollgard II (two Bt genes) approved in 2006 |
| **Impact** | Cotton production doubled from ~15 million bales (pre-Bt) to ~35–37 million bales; pesticide use on cotton fell sharply |
| **Current coverage** | ~95% of India's 12 million ha cotton area under Bt cotton |
| **Challenges** | Pink bollworm developed resistance to Bollgard I; Bollgard II partially effective; secondary pests (whitefly, mealybug) increased; seed cost controversy |

### Bt Brinjal: Moratorium (2010)

GEAC had approved **Bt Brinjal** (resistant to fruit and shoot borer) in October 2009. However, then-Environment Minister **Jairam Ramesh** imposed an indefinite moratorium in **February 2010**, citing:
- Insufficient scientific consensus on safety
- Public opposition from farmers, consumers, and several state governments
- Need for more bio-safety tests and independent review

Bt Brinjal moratorium remains in place as of 2026 in India. Bangladesh has successfully cultivated Bt Brinjal commercially since 2014.

## GM Mustard (DMH-11): The Ongoing Controversy

**DMH-11** (Dhara Mustard Hybrid-11) is the GM mustard hybrid developed by Prof. Deepak Pental's team at the **Centre for Genetic Manipulation of Crop Plants (CGMCP), Delhi University**, with funding from the **National Dairy Development Board (NDDB)** and the **Department of Biotechnology (DBT)**.

### The Science

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Genes introduced** | *barnase* (causes male sterility) and *barstar* (restores fertility) from the soil bacterium *Bacillus amyloliquefaciens*; plus *bar* gene for herbicide tolerance |
| **Purpose** | The barnase-barstar system enables hybrid seed production in mustard (which is normally self-pollinating), potentially boosting yields by 25–30% |
| **Yield claim** | BRL I & II trials showed 28.15% yield advantage over Varuna (standard check variety) |
| **Significance** | Could be the **first GM food crop** and first GM oilseed to be commercially cultivated in India |

### Regulatory Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2002 | Research initiated by Delhi University team |
| 2014–2017 | Biosafety Research Level (BRL) trials conducted |
| 2017 | GEAC recommended environmental release; Union government stalled |
| **October 18, 2022** | GEAC (147th meeting) recommended environmental release of DMH-11 and its parental lines |
| **October 25, 2022** | Union government (MoEFCC) approved GEAC recommendation |
| 2023–2024 | Supreme Court hearing on petitions challenging the approval |
| **2024** | Division Bench split verdict: Justice Nagarathna **quashed** the GEAC approval; Justice Sanjay Karol **upheld** it |

The **split verdict** means the matter requires adjudication by a larger bench. As of March 2026, commercial cultivation of GM mustard has not been permitted pending judicial resolution.

### Key Controversies

| Opposition | Concern |
|-----------|---------|
| **Beekeepers** | Mustard flowers are key nectar source; herbicide-tolerant gene (*bar*) could reduce weed flowers, affecting bees and pollination |
| **Organic farming groups** | Fear contamination of conventional and organic mustard varieties through cross-pollination |
| **RSS-affiliated Swadeshi Jagaran Manch** | Ideological opposition to foreign-gene technology and multinationals |
| **Regulatory concerns** | Adequacy of safety testing; need for long-term food safety trials |
| **Justice Nagarathna's reasoning** | The government failed to follow proper biosafety evaluation protocols; environmental release is irreversible |

## Regulatory Framework for GM Organisms in India

### Key Legislation and Rules

| Law/Rule | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Environment Protection Act 1986** | Parent legislation under which biosafety rules were framed |
| **Rules for Manufacture, Use, Import, Export and Storage of Hazardous Microorganisms/Genetically Engineered Organisms or Cells (Rules 1989)** | The primary biosafety regulatory framework; notified under EPA 1986 |
| **Biological Diversity Act 2002** | Regulates access to biological resources; connected to GM research using Indian biodiversity |

### Regulatory Bodies (under Rules 1989)

| Body | Full Name | Role |
|------|-----------|------|
| **GEAC** | Genetic Engineering Appraisal Committee | Apex body under **MoEFCC**; approves environmental release of GM organisms for commercial use |
| **RCGM** | Review Committee on Genetic Manipulation | Under **DBT**; oversees research-level GM activities |
| **IBSC** | Institutional Biosafety Committee | At institution level; first line of review for GMO research |
| **SBCCs** | State Biotechnology Coordination Committees | State-level oversight |
| **DLC** | District Level Committees | District-level compliance |

**Key point for UPSC:** GEAC functions under the **Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEFCC)**, not the Ministry of Agriculture. This institutional location reflects the biosafety-first approach to GM regulation.

## CRISPR-Cas9: Gene Editing vs Genetic Modification

**CRISPR-Cas9** (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats + Cas9 protein) is a **precision gene-editing tool** that allows targeted modifications to an organism's own DNA.

### Key Distinction

| Aspect | Traditional GMO | CRISPR Gene Editing |
|--------|----------------|---------------------|
| **DNA source** | Foreign DNA from another species inserted | Native DNA edited; no foreign DNA in final product (in many applications) |
| **Example** | Bt cotton (bacterial gene in cotton) | CRISPR tomato with longer shelf life (Japan, 2021) |
| **Regulation (India)** | Requires full GEAC approval under Rules 1989 | **Grey area** — DBT issued draft guidelines (2022) proposing lighter regulation for CRISPR-edited organisms without foreign DNA |
| **WTO/trade concern** | EU treats all GM differently from conventional | EU's strict regulation of gene-edited crops creates trade implications |

India's **DBT draft guidelines (2022)** proposed:
- **SDN-1 (Site Directed Nuclease-1)**: No foreign DNA, edits within genome — exempt from GMO regulation
- **SDN-2**: Uses template for editing; regulated like conventional breeding
- **SDN-3**: Involves foreign DNA integration — regulated like full GMO

This distinction is crucial for UPSC: CRISPR without foreign DNA may not be a "GMO" under future Indian rules.

## India's Bioeconomy: From $10 Billion to $165 Billion

### Growth Trajectory

| Year | Bioeconomy Size | Key Driver |
|------|-----------------|-----------|
| 2014 | ~$10 billion | Pharma, agriculture |
| 2020 | ~$70 billion | Biopharma growth, vaccines |
| 2023 | ~$130 billion | COVID-19 vaccine manufacturing, biosimilars |
| **2024** | **~$165.75 billion** | Biopharma, bioagriculture, bio-industrial sectors |
| **Target 2030** | **$300 billion** | BioE3 policy, biomanufacturing push |
| Vision 2047 | **$1 trillion** | Amrit Kaal bioeconomy goal |

India's bioeconomy at **$165.75 billion (2024)** contributes approximately **4.2% of GDP** and makes India a global top-10 bioeconomy.

### DBT BioEconomy Report 2024

The **Department of Biotechnology (DBT)** releases an annual Bioeconomy Report. The 2024 report highlighted:
- Sixteenfold growth from $10 billion (2014) to $165.75 billion (2024)
- India has 8,000+ biotech startups
- India is the 3rd largest biotech hub in Asia-Pacific and 12th globally
- Pharmaceuticals/biopharmaceuticals constitute the largest segment (~65% of bioeconomy)

## BioE3 Policy 2024

The **BioE3 Policy (Biotechnology for Economy, Environment and Employment)** was approved by the Union Cabinet on **24 August 2024**.

### Key Objectives

| Objective | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Economy** | Accelerate India's bioeconomy to $300 billion by 2030; support biomanufacturing startups |
| **Environment** | Promote biofuels, biodegradable materials, bio-based chemicals to reduce fossil fuel dependency |
| **Employment** | Create skilled jobs in biotech manufacturing, bioinformatics, agri-biotech |

### Priority Sectors under BioE3

| Sector | Focus |
|--------|-------|
| **High-value biobased chemicals and enzymes** | Reduce imports; scale bio-manufacturing |
| **Smart proteins and functional foods** | Alternative proteins (precision fermentation, plant-based) |
| **Precision biotherapeutics** | Next-generation biologics, personalised medicine |
| **Climate-resilient agriculture** | Biotech solutions for drought/flood tolerance, nitrogen fixation |
| **Carbon capture and utilisation** | Biotech-based carbon sequestration |
| **Marine and space biotech** | Deep-tech frontier applications |

### Implementation Mechanism

- **Bio-foundries**: Networked national bio-foundry facilities for rapid prototyping of bio-manufacturing
- **BIRAC (Biotechnology Industry Research Assistance Council)**: Implements DBT's industry-interface schemes
- **National Biopharma Mission (NBM/i3)**: ₹1,500 crore ($250 million co-funded with World Bank); 101 projects, 150+ organisations, 30 MSMEs supported

## Biopharmaceuticals: India's Global Hub Status

India is one of the world's largest producers and exporters of biopharmaceuticals:

| Category | India's Position |
|----------|----------------|
| **Vaccines** | Largest global vaccine producer by volume; Serum Institute of India = world's largest vaccine manufacturer |
| **Generic formulations** | ~20% of global generic medicine volume; largest supplier to WHO prequalified procurement |
| **Biosimilars** | Growing rapidly; over 100 approved biosimilars in India |
| **Insulin** | Major producer; domestic brands (Biocon, Lupin, Wockhardt) |
| **Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs)** | Rapidly scaling; biosimilar mAbs for cancer, autoimmune diseases |

| Key Institution | Role |
|----------------|------|
| **BIRAC** | Biotechnology Industry Research Assistance Council — industry interface for DBT |
| **DBT** | Department of Biotechnology under Ministry of Science & Technology |
| **CSIR** | Council of Scientific and Industrial Research — research on pharma, materials, food |
| **CDSCO** | Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation — drug regulatory approval (equivalent of FDA) |

## Biosafety Concerns

### Key Risks from GM Organisms

| Risk | Explanation |
|------|------------|
| **Horizontal gene transfer** | Transgenes may transfer to soil bacteria or wild plant relatives, creating ecological changes |
| **Biodiversity impact** | Herbicide-tolerant GM crops may lead to monocultures, reducing agro-biodiversity |
| **Allergenicity** | Foreign proteins in GM food could trigger allergic reactions in susceptible populations |
| **Development of resistance** | Insects/weeds can develop resistance to Bt toxin or herbicide, creating "super-pests" and "super-weeds" |
| **Cross-pollination** | GM pollen can contaminate non-GM and organic crops in neighbouring fields |
| **Long-term food safety** | Inadequate long-term studies on chronic health effects in some GM crops |

### Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety

The **Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety** is a supplementary agreement to the **Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD)**, focused on the safe transfer, handling, and use of **Living Modified Organisms (LMOs)** — i.e., GM organisms.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Adopted** | January 29, 2000 (Montreal, Canada) |
| **In force** | September 11, 2003 |
| **India ratification** | India is a Party |
| **Key principle** | **Advance Informed Agreement (AIA)**: Exporting country must notify importing country and get consent before first transboundary movement of LMO for release into environment |
| **Precautionary principle** | Uncertainty about risks does not preclude a Party from taking measures to prevent harm |
| **Biosafety Clearing House (BCH)** | Online platform for exchanging biosafety information globally |

India's **Rules 1989** under the EPA are broadly aligned with the Cartagena Protocol's requirements for risk assessment and information exchange.

## Biopiracy: Neem, Turmeric & TKDL

**Biopiracy** refers to the misappropriation of biological resources and traditional knowledge of communities (often from the Global South) through intellectual property instruments (especially patents), without prior informed consent and without benefit-sharing.

### Famous Cases Involving India

#### Turmeric Patent (1995–1997)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Patent granted** | US Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) granted Patent No. US5401504A in March 1995 to two researchers at the University of Mississippi Medical Centre — for the use of turmeric in wound healing |
| **India's challenge** | CSIR filed a challenge proving turmeric's wound-healing use was documented in ancient Indian texts (Ayurveda); therefore **not novel** |
| **Outcome** | USPTO **revoked the patent in 1997** — one of the earliest successful challenges to biopiracy |

#### Neem Patent (1994–2005)

| Detail | Fact |
|--------|------|
| **Patent granted** | European Patent Office (EPO) granted a patent to W.R. Grace Co. and USDA on a neem-based fungicide (EP436257) in 1994 |
| **Challenge** | Dr. Vandana Shiva and other activists filed opposition citing traditional Indian use of neem |
| **Outcome** | EPO **revoked the patent in May 2000**; challenge confirmed in appeal in **2005** |

### Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL)

Established in **2001**, the **TKDL** is a joint initiative of the **Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR)** and the **Ministry of AYUSH**.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Document India's traditional medicinal knowledge in machine-readable format accessible to patent offices globally |
| **Content** | ~0.9 million formulations from Ayurveda, Unani, Siddha, and Yoga texts |
| **Languages** | Available in English, French, German, Spanish, Japanese |
| **Access** | Provided to patent examiners at USPTO, EPO, JPO, UKIPO, CIPO as prior art database |
| **Impact** | TKDL has been cited in over 200 patent examination proceedings, preventing grant of patents on traditional Indian knowledge |

> TKDL represents India's proactive "defensive protection" strategy — rather than patenting traditional knowledge (which may not meet novelty criteria), India documents it as prior art to prevent others from patenting it.

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- *Bacillus thuringiensis* = Bt cotton; Cry proteins are the insecticidal toxins — memorise the full name
- GEAC functions under **MoEFCC** (not MoAgriculture) — common trap question
- Bt cotton approved: 2002 (only commercially approved GM crop for cultivation)
- Bt brinjal moratorium: 2010 by Jairam Ramesh
- GM Mustard DMH-11: GEAC approval October 2022; Supreme Court split verdict 2024; no commercial cultivation yet
- CRISPR = gene editing, not genetic modification — the distinction between SDN-1 (no foreign DNA) and GMO is increasingly important
- Cartagena Protocol: under CBD; covers Living Modified Organisms (LMOs); Advance Informed Agreement principle
- TKDL: 2001; CSIR + Ministry of AYUSH; prevents biopiracy
- India's bioeconomy: $165.75 billion (2024); target $300 billion by 2030 (BioE3); $1 trillion by 2047
- BioE3 Policy: Cabinet approved **24 August 2024**
- Rules 1989: Under the Environment Protection Act 1986 — primary GMO regulation

**For Mains:**
- GM mustard structure: Science (barnase-barstar) → yield potential → regulatory journey (GEAC 2022 approval → SC split verdict 2024) → controversy (beekeepers, organic farming, RS-SJM) → current impasse
- Biopiracy/TKDL: Neem (EPO revoked 2005), Turmeric (USPTO revoked 1997) → TKDL as prior art database → 0.9 million formulations → defensive protection strategy
- Biosafety: Connect to Cartagena Protocol → precautionary principle → India's Rules 1989 framework
- Bioeconomy: Use data — $165.75 billion (2024), 4.2% of GDP, 8,000+ startups, 3rd largest Asia-Pacific hub
- BioE3 vs National Biopharma Mission: BioE3 is policy framework; NBM ($250 million with World Bank) is the execution vehicle
- CRISPR: Always distinguish gene editing (own DNA) vs GMO (foreign DNA) — regulatory grey area; SDN-1 exempt from GMO rules under DBT draft guidelines
