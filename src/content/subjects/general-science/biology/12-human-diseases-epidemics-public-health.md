India's public health landscape is shaped by a double burden of disease — persisting communicable diseases like tuberculosis and malaria on one hand, and a rapidly growing epidemic of non-communicable diseases like diabetes and cardiovascular conditions on the other. Understanding disease burden, health schemes, and India's public health responses is critical for UPSC GS Papers 2 and 3. This chapter also covers antimicrobial resistance, the One Health approach, and key health data from NFHS-5.

---

## Disease Classification: Communicable vs Non-Communicable

| Category | Definition | Examples |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| **Communicable (Infectious)** | Caused by pathogens (bacteria, viruses, parasites, fungi); can spread person-to-person or via vectors | TB, malaria, dengue, COVID-19, cholera |
| **Non-Communicable (NCDs)** | Not spread by pathogens; caused by genetic, lifestyle, and environmental factors | Diabetes, CVD, cancer, hypertension, COPD |

**India's disease transition:** NCDs now account for approximately **60% of all deaths in India** — yet communicable diseases remain a major burden, especially in rural and low-income populations. This "double burden" strains the health system.

## Major Communicable Diseases

### Tuberculosis (TB)

**India's TB burden:** India bears the **single largest share of global TB burden** — 26% of global TB cases in 2023 (WHO Global TB Report 2024 data), with an estimated 2.82 million new TB cases annually.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pathogen** | *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (bacteria) |
| **Transmission** | Airborne (droplet nuclei when infected person coughs/sneezes) |
| **Disease** | Primarily pulmonary (lung) TB; can affect other organs (extra-pulmonary) |
| **Drug-resistant forms** | MDR-TB (Multi-Drug Resistant), XDR-TB (Extensively Drug Resistant) |
| **India's death toll** | ~312,000 deaths annually (2022 estimate) |
| **India's target** | Eliminate TB by 2025 — 5 years ahead of global SDG target (2030) |

**National TB Elimination Programme (NTEP):**

NTEP (formerly RNTCP — Revised National TB Control Programme) is India's flagship TB control programme under the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare.

**NTEP framework:** Detect → Treat → Prevent → Build (DTPB)

| Scheme/Initiative | Details |
|------------------|---------|
| **Nikshay Poshan Yojana** | Financial support ₹1,000/month (raised from ₹500) to TB patients for nutrition throughout treatment; ₹3,202+ crore disbursed to 1.13 crore beneficiaries via DBT |
| **Pradhan Mantri TB Mukt Bharat Abhiyaan** | Community support through "Ni-kshay Mitras" (donors who support TB patients with nutrition, livelihood, education) |
| **CBNAAT** | Cartridge-Based Nucleic Acid Amplification Test — rapid molecular diagnosis for TB and drug resistance |
| **Free treatment** | All TB patients get free diagnosis, medicines (including MDR-TB drugs) under NTEP |

**Progress:** New TB cases in India reduced by 21% from 2015–2024 (WHO data); TB deaths reduced by 28% in the same period — one of the most significant declines globally.

### Malaria

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pathogen** | *Plasmodium* parasites (mainly *P. falciparum* and *P. vivax* in India) |
| **Vector** | *Anopheles* mosquito (female) |
| **Severe form** | *P. falciparum* — causes cerebral malaria, can be fatal |
| **Milder form** | *P. vivax* — dormant liver stage (hypnozoites); causes relapses |
| **Transmission** | Mosquito bite (not person-to-person) |

**National Framework for Malaria Elimination (NFME) 2016–2030:**
- Target: Malaria-free India by 2030
- State-specific strategies based on transmission intensity
- Integrated vector management: insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, larval source management

**Progress:** India achieved significant reduction in malaria cases — from 10.7 million cases (estimated, 2000) to around 1.96 million (2023 WHO estimate). India was removed from WHO's "High Burden to High Impact" group in 2023.

---

### Dengue and Chikungunya

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pathogen** | Dengue: Dengue virus (DENV 1–4 serotypes); Chikungunya: CHIKV virus |
| **Vector** | *Aedes aegypti* mosquito (also *Ae. albopictus*) |
| **Season** | Peak during and after monsoon (July–October) |
| **Dengue symptoms** | High fever, severe joint/muscle pain, rash; Dengue Haemorrhagic Fever (DHF) can be fatal |
| **No specific treatment** | Supportive care; no WHO-approved vaccine widely deployed in India |
| **Control** | Source reduction (eliminate stagnant water), biological control, community engagement |

Dengue incidence has increased 30-fold in the past 50 years globally (WHO). Urban India is particularly affected due to water storage practices and construction sites providing breeding grounds.

### COVID-19

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pathogen** | SARS-CoV-2 (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2) |
| **Declared pandemic** | WHO, March 11, 2020 |
| **India's first case** | January 30, 2020 (Kerala) |
| **India's vaccination** | India administered over 2.2 billion COVID-19 vaccine doses (2021–2023) — one of the world's largest vaccination campaigns |
| **Vaccines used** | Covishield (AstraZeneca/SII), Covaxin (Bharat Biotech), Sputnik V, ZyCoV-D, Corbevax |
| **CoWIN platform** | India's digital vaccination tracking platform — widely recognised as a global model |

**Lessons from COVID-19:**
- Importance of surge capacity in public health infrastructure
- Digital health infrastructure (CoWIN) enables mass vaccination
- One Health approach — zoonotic origin highlights human-animal-environment interface
- Global vaccine equity — COVAX mechanism; India's role as "pharmacy of the world" (donated/supplied vaccines to 100+ countries)

### Kala-azar (Visceral Leishmaniasis)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Pathogen** | *Leishmania donovani* (protozoan parasite) |
| **Vector** | *Phlebotomus argentipes* (sandfly) |
| **Endemic states** | Bihar, Jharkhand, Uttar Pradesh, West Bengal |
| **Target** | Elimination (<1 case/10,000 population at sub-district level) |
| **Treatment** | Liposomal Amphotericin B (single-dose; free under National Programme) |

India has made substantial progress — cases reduced from >200,000 (2002) to <10,000 in recent years.

---

### Lymphatic Filariasis

Caused by *Wuchereria bancrofti* (nematode); transmitted by *Culex* mosquitoes. Causes elephantiasis (lymphedema). India runs the National Programme for Elimination of Lymphatic Filariasis (NPELF) with mass drug administration (MDA) in endemic districts.

## Non-Communicable Diseases (NCDs)

NCDs now cause approximately **60% of deaths in India** and represent the leading cause of premature mortality. The National Programme for Prevention and Control of Non-Communicable Diseases (NP-NCD) is India's main response.

### Cardiovascular Diseases (CVDs)

- **Leading cause of death in India**
- Ischemic heart disease and stroke account for the majority of CVD deaths
- Risk factors: hypertension, diabetes, smoking, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, air pollution
- Rising in rural India — previously considered an urban disease

### Diabetes

| Indicator | Data |
|-----------|------|
| **Prevalence** | 101 million (10.1 crore) diabetics in India — ICMR-INDIAB study (2023) |
| **Global rank** | India is the country with the second highest number of diabetics globally (after China) |
| **Prediabetes** | Additional 136 million prediabetics in India |
| **Type** | Predominantly Type 2 diabetes |
| **Risk factors** | Obesity, physical inactivity, dietary patterns, genetic susceptibility |
| **Policy response** | National Programme for NCD (NP-NCD); Ayushman Bharat-HWC screening |

---

### Cancer

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Incidence** | ~14 lakh new cases/year in India |
| **Leading cancers** | Breast, cervical (women); oral, lung (men); colorectal rising |
| **National programme** | National Cancer Control Programme (NCCP); National Cancer Grid |
| **AIIMS expansion** | New AIIMS in various states aim to improve cancer care access |
| **Tobacco link** | ~27% of cancers in India linked to tobacco use |

### Hypertension

- The "silent epidemic" — large proportion undiagnosed
- ~188 million adults estimated to have hypertension in India
- Leading risk factor for stroke, heart disease, kidney failure
- NP-NCD includes population-level hypertension screening

## NFHS-5 (National Family Health Survey 5, 2019–21)

NFHS-5 is India's most comprehensive household health survey, covering 636,699 households, 724,115 women, and 101,839 men across all states and UTs.

**Key findings:**

| Indicator | NFHS-5 Finding | Trend vs NFHS-4 |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Total Fertility Rate (TFR)** | 2.0 (replacement level reached) | Declined from 2.2 |
| **Stunting (children <5)** | 35.5% | Improved from 38.4% |
| **Wasting (children <5)** | 19.3% | Slight improvement from 21% |
| **Anaemia in women (15–49)** | 57.0% | Worsened from 53.1% |
| **Anaemia in children (6–59 months)** | 67.1% | Worsened from 58.6% |
| **IMR (Infant Mortality Rate)** | 35.2/1,000 live births | Improved |
| **Institutional deliveries** | 88.6% | Improved from 78.9% |

**Key concern from NFHS-5:** Rising anaemia levels — especially among women and children — despite improvement in other indicators. Nutritional supplementation programmes need strengthening.

## Health Infrastructure in India

| Indicator | Status |
|-----------|--------|
| **Doctor-population ratio** | ~1:834 (WHO standard: 1:1,000); urban-rural divide severe |
| **Nurse-population ratio** | Below WHO recommended levels |
| **PHC/CHC/DH network** | NHM provides sub-centres, PHCs, CHCs, and district hospitals; significant gaps in rural areas |
| **Hospital beds** | ~1.4 per 1,000 population (WHO: 3 per 1,000) |

## National Health Mission (NHM)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Launched** | 2013 (merger of NRHM + NUHM) |
| **NRHM** | National Rural Health Mission — launched 2005 |
| **NUHM** | National Urban Health Mission — launched 2013 |
| **Key components** | ASHA workers (1.2 million), strengthened PHC/CHC, free drugs and diagnostics, MMR/IMR reduction |

## Ayushman Bharat

India's flagship health programme has two components:

### 1. PM-JAY (Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Coverage** | ₹5 lakh health insurance per family per year for secondary and tertiary hospitalisation |
| **Beneficiaries** | 12 crore+ poor and vulnerable families (~55 crore individuals) |
| **Hospitals** | Cashless, paperless treatment at any empanelled public or private hospital |
| **Status** | World's largest health insurance scheme |
| **Launched** | September 23, 2018 |

### 2. Ayushman Arogya Mandirs (Health and Wellness Centres)

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Original name** | Ayushman Bharat Health and Wellness Centres (AB-HWC) |
| **Renamed** | Ayushman Arogya Mandirs |
| **Target** | 1.5 lakh HWCs across India (1.78 lakh+ active as of mid-2025) |
| **Services** | Comprehensive Primary Health Care — NCDs screening, maternal/child health, mental health, oral and eye care, free drugs and diagnostics |
| **Significance** | Shifts PHC from selective to comprehensive care; brings NCD management to village level |

## Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR)

**AMR** occurs when bacteria, viruses, fungi, or parasites evolve mechanisms to survive drug treatments that would otherwise kill them.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Scale** | AMR already causes ~1.27 million deaths/year globally (Lancet, 2022); could cause 10 million/year by 2050 |
| **India's burden** | India is one of the world's highest consumers of antibiotics; high rates of drug-resistant infections |
| **Drug-resistant TB** | ~119,000 MDR/RR-TB cases/year in India — world's highest |
| **ESKAPE pathogens** | Key AMR bacteria: *Enterococcus, Staphylococcus aureus, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Acinetobacter baumannii, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Enterobacter* |

**India's National Action Plan on AMR (2017–2021; updated):**
- Awareness campaigns on antibiotic stewardship
- Surveillance of AMR through ICMR's AMR Surveillance Network (AMRSN)
- Infection prevention and control in hospitals
- Research and development for new diagnostics and therapeutics
- Regulation of antibiotic use in agriculture and veterinary sectors

**Root causes of AMR in India:**
- Over-the-counter availability of antibiotics
- Irrational prescription practices
- Self-medication
- Antibiotic use in poultry/livestock farming
- Poor sanitation enabling spread of resistant organisms

## One Health Approach

**One Health** is an integrated, unifying approach that recognises that the health of humans, animals, and the environment are deeply interconnected and interdependent.

| Rationale | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Zoonotic diseases** | ~60% of emerging infectious diseases originate in animals (COVID-19, SARS, Nipah, Ebola, avian influenza) |
| **AMR** | Resistance genes cycle between humans, animals, and environment |
| **Food safety** | Contaminated animal products transmit pathogens to humans |
| **Environmental health** | Habitat destruction drives contact between wildlife and humans — spillover events |

**Key players in India's One Health framework:**
- Ministry of Health and Family Welfare (human health)
- Ministry of Agriculture — Department of Animal Husbandry (animal health)
- Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (wildlife and ecosystem health)
- ICMR, ICAR, DBT, DST (research)
- India launched its **One Health Consortium** in 2021 under ICMR-DBT-ICAR collaboration

## Mental Health

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Burden** | NFHS-5: ~14.3% of the population has some form of mental disorder |
| **National Mental Health Programme (NMHP)** | Launched 1982; District Mental Health Programme (DMHP) under NHM |
| **Mental Healthcare Act 2017** | Decriminalised suicide attempt; guaranteed right to mental healthcare; established Mental Health Review Boards |
| **Key gap** | Treatment gap — >80% of people with mental illness receive no treatment |
| **Tele-MANAS** | National tele mental health programme; 24/7 helpline (Dial 14416) |

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- India's TB burden: 26% of global cases; target 2025 elimination; NTEP = National TB Elimination Programme
- Nikshay Poshan Yojana: ₹1,000/month nutritional support for TB patients (DBT)
- NFHS-5: TFR = 2.0 (replacement level); anaemia worsened; stunting improved
- PM-JAY: ₹5 lakh/family/year; 12 crore families; world's largest health insurance scheme
- ICMR-INDIAB: 101 million diabetics in India (2023)
- Dengue vector: *Aedes aegypti*; Malaria: *Anopheles*; Kala-azar: sandfly (*Phlebotomus*)
- MDR-TB = Multi-Drug Resistant; XDR-TB = Extensively Drug Resistant

**For Mains:**
- Double burden framework is a strong analytical structure — use it to open answers
- Connect AMR to One Health — resistance in animal sector feeds human sector
- Ayushman Bharat: mention both components — PM-JAY (insurance) + Ayushman Arogya Mandirs (primary care)
- Mental Health Act 2017 decriminalised suicide — use as a progressive step in governance answer
- COVID-19 lessons: surge capacity, CoWIN digital infrastructure, zoonotic origin = One Health
- NFHS-5 data: anaemia paradox — improving economy yet worsening anaemia = policy failure area
