## Introduction

Nuclear physics — the study of the atomic nucleus and the forces binding it — underpins both India's energy security strategy and its science and technology landscape. From India's ambitious three-stage nuclear programme to nuclear medicine, radiation safety, and the debates triggered by Fukushima and Chernobyl, this topic connects fundamental science to governance and policy. UPSC GS Paper III (Science and Technology) tests both conceptual understanding and applied dimensions.

---

## The Atomic Nucleus: Fundamentals

### Structure

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Nucleus | Dense core of atom; contains protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral) |
| Proton number (Z) | Atomic number; determines the element |
| Neutron number (N) | Varies among isotopes of the same element |
| Mass number (A) | A = Z + N; total number of nucleons |
| Nuclide | Specific nucleus characterised by Z and N |

**Isotopes:** Atoms of the same element (same Z) with different numbers of neutrons (different A).
Examples:
- Uranium-235 (Z=92, A=235) and Uranium-238 (Z=92, A=238) — both uranium, different mass
- Carbon-12 (stable) and Carbon-14 (radioactive)
- Hydrogen-1, Deuterium (H-2), Tritium (H-3)

**Nuclear forces:** The strong nuclear force holds protons and neutrons together in the nucleus, overcoming the electromagnetic repulsion between protons. It is extremely strong but acts only at very short range (< 3 femtometres).

## Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of radiation from unstable nuclei as they seek a more stable configuration. Discovered by Henri Becquerel (1896); studied by Marie and Pierre Curie.

### Types of Radiation

| Type | Nature | Composition | Penetrating Power | Ionising Power | Shielding |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Alpha (α)** | Particle | Helium nucleus (2p + 2n) | Very low (stopped by paper or skin) | Very high | Paper or skin |
| **Beta (β)** | Particle | Electron (β⁻) or positron (β⁺) emitted from nucleus | Moderate (stopped by aluminium foil) | Moderate | Aluminium sheet (few mm) |
| **Gamma (γ)** | Electromagnetic radiation | High-energy photons | Very high (penetrates most materials) | Low | Lead blocks or thick concrete |
| **Neutron** | Particle | Free neutron | Very high | Moderate | Hydrogen-rich materials (water, polyethylene) |

### Natural vs Artificial Radioactivity

- **Natural radioactivity:** Spontaneous decay of naturally occurring isotopes (uranium, thorium, radium, potassium-40, carbon-14)
- **Artificial radioactivity:** Radioactive isotopes produced by bombarding stable nuclei with neutrons or charged particles in a reactor or accelerator (e.g., Cobalt-60 for cancer treatment, Iodine-131 for thyroid treatment)

### Radioactive Decay Law and Half-Life

**Half-life (t½):** The time required for half of a radioactive sample to decay.

After each half-life, the remaining quantity halves:
- After 1 half-life → 50% remains
- After 2 half-lives → 25% remains
- After 3 half-lives → 12.5% remains

**Key half-lives:**

| Isotope | Half-Life | Application |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon-14 (C-14) | **5,730 years** | Archaeological dating (up to ~60,000 years) |
| Uranium-235 | 703.8 million years | Geological dating |
| Uranium-238 | 4.47 billion years | Age of Earth; geological dating |
| Plutonium-239 | 24,100 years | Nuclear reactors; weapons |
| Iodine-131 | 8.02 days | Thyroid cancer treatment |
| Cobalt-60 | 5.27 years | Cancer radiotherapy |
| Technetium-99m | 6 hours | Medical imaging (SPECT scans) |

### Carbon Dating (Radiocarbon Dating)

- Developed by Willard Libby (Nobel Prize, 1960)
- Living organisms continuously absorb Carbon-14 (produced by cosmic ray bombardment of N-14 in atmosphere)
- On death, C-14 is no longer replenished; it decays with a **half-life of 5,730 years**
- By measuring the ratio of C-14 to C-12 remaining, the age of organic material can be calculated
- **Effective range:** up to approximately 60,000 years

## Nuclear Fission

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy nucleus (typically U-235 or Pu-239) into two lighter nuclei (fission fragments), releasing enormous energy and **2–3 neutrons** per fission event.

### Chain Reaction

- Each fission event releases neutrons that can trigger further fissions
- **Sub-critical:** fewer than 1 fission per neutron → reaction dies out
- **Critical:** exactly 1 fission per neutron → steady, controlled chain reaction (nuclear reactor)
- **Supercritical:** more than 1 fission per neutron → exponentially growing chain reaction (nuclear bomb)

### Critical Mass

The minimum mass of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction:
- **Uranium-235:** ~56 kg (bare sphere; significantly less with a neutron reflector)
- **Plutonium-239:** ~11 kg (bare sphere; far more efficient fissile material)

### Energy Released

Einstein's mass-energy equivalence: **E = mc²**

Fission of 1 kg of U-235 releases energy equivalent to approximately 20,000 tonnes of TNT.

## Nuclear Fusion

Nuclear fusion is the joining of light nuclei (typically isotopes of hydrogen) at extremely high temperatures to form a heavier nucleus, releasing even more energy than fission per unit mass.

**Reaction:**
- Deuterium (H-2) + Tritium (H-3) → Helium-4 + free neutron + 17.6 MeV energy

**Conditions required:**
- Temperature: ~100–150 million °C (plasma state)
- High pressure and confinement time

**Why fusion is desirable:**
- Virtually limitless fuel (deuterium from seawater)
- No long-lived radioactive waste
- No risk of runaway chain reaction

**Current status:**
- Research reactors: ITER project (France) — largest experimental tokamak fusion reactor; under construction
- **National Ignition Facility (USA):** Achieved fusion ignition (energy output > energy input) in December 2022 — a historic milestone
- Commercial fusion power is estimated to be several decades away
- India participates in ITER

## Nuclear Reactors

A nuclear reactor is a device in which a controlled, sustained fission chain reaction produces heat for electricity generation.

### Key Components

| Component | Function | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Fuel** | Provides fissile material | Uranium-235 (enriched or natural), Plutonium-239, Thorium-232 |
| **Moderator** | Slows fast neutrons to thermal speeds for efficient fission | Heavy water (D₂O), Graphite, Light water (H₂O) |
| **Control rods** | Absorb neutrons to regulate reaction rate | Boron, Cadmium, Hafnium |
| **Coolant** | Carries heat from reactor core to steam generator | Water, Heavy water, CO₂, Liquid sodium |
| **Reflector** | Returns escaping neutrons to core | Graphite, Beryllium, Water |
| **Containment** | Prevents radioactive release | Steel-reinforced concrete structure |

### Types of Reactors

| Reactor Type | Fuel | Moderator | Coolant | Used In |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PHWR (Pressurised Heavy Water Reactor) | Natural uranium | Heavy water (D₂O) | Heavy water | India's Stage 1; CANDU (Canada) |
| PWR (Pressurised Water Reactor) | Enriched uranium | Light water | Light water | USA, France, Russia |
| BWR (Boiling Water Reactor) | Enriched uranium | Light water | Light water | USA, Japan (Fukushima) |
| RBMK (Graphite-moderated) | Enriched uranium | Graphite | Light water | USSR (Chernobyl) |
| FBR (Fast Breeder Reactor) | Plutonium-239 | None (no moderator) | Liquid sodium | India's Stage 2 (PFBR) |

## India's Three-Stage Nuclear Power Programme

Conceived by **Dr. Homi Jehangir Bhabha** in the 1950s, India's nuclear programme is designed to maximise self-reliance given India's resource profile:

| Resource | India's Global Share |
|---|---|
| Uranium reserves | ~1–2% of world reserves |
| **Thorium reserves** | **~25% of world reserves** (one of the largest globally) |

The programme uses a three-stage cycle to progressively utilise India's vast thorium reserves.

### Stage 1: Pressurised Heavy Water Reactors (PHWRs)

- **Fuel:** Natural uranium (not enriched — avoids dependence on enrichment technology)
- **Moderator/Coolant:** Heavy water (D₂O)
- **By-product:** Plutonium-239 (from U-238 + neutron) — collected for Stage 2
- **Operator:** Nuclear Power Corporation of India Ltd (**NPCIL**)
- **Status:** This stage is well-established; India has 18 PHWRs in operation

### Stage 2: Fast Breeder Reactors (FBRs)

- **Fuel:** Plutonium-239 (from Stage 1) + Thorium-232 blanket
- **Process:** Fast neutrons convert Thorium-232 into Uranium-233 (fissile) — "breeding" more fuel than consumed
- **By-product:** Uranium-233 — collected for Stage 3
- **Key reactor:** **Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor (PFBR)** — 500 MW, at **Kalpakkam** (Tamil Nadu)
- **Status:** PFBR constructed; India achieved a significant milestone toward Stage 2 operations (awaiting full commissioning as of 2025)
- **Coolant:** Liquid sodium (highly reactive — technical challenge)

### Stage 3: Thorium-Based Reactors

- **Fuel:** Uranium-233 (from Stage 2) + Thorium-232
- **Significance:** India's massive thorium deposits (over 500,000 tonnes) become primary fuel source
- **Status:** Still in R&D phase; design of Advanced Heavy Water Reactor (AHWR) underway at Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC)

## Nuclear Power Plants in India

As of April 2025, India has **25 nuclear reactors** at **7 nuclear power plant sites** with a total installed capacity of **8,880 MW**.

| Plant | Location | Operator | Technology | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tarapur Atomic Power Station (TAPS) | Maharashtra | NPCIL | BWR (Units 1–2); PHWR (Units 3–4) | India's first NPP; commissioned 1969; Units 1–2 use US-supplied BWR technology |
| Rajasthan Atomic Power Station (RAPS) | Rawatbhata, Rajasthan | NPCIL | PHWR | Rajasthan 7 connected to grid March 2025; Rajasthan 8 expected 2026 |
| Madras Atomic Power Station (MAPS) | Kalpakkam, Tamil Nadu | NPCIL | PHWR | Also site of PFBR (Stage 2) |
| Narora Atomic Power Station (NAPS) | Narora, Uttar Pradesh | NPCIL | PHWR | |
| Kakrapar Atomic Power Station (KAPS) | Surat, Gujarat | NPCIL | PHWR | 700 MW units — new generation PHWR |
| Kaiga Generating Station (KGS) | Kaiga, Karnataka | NPCIL | PHWR | |
| Kudankulam Nuclear Power Plant (KKNPP) | Tirunelveli, Tamil Nadu | NPCIL | VVER-1000 (Russian PWR) | India's largest NPP; 2,000 MW (2 × 1,000 MW); Russian collaboration; Units 3–6 under construction |

**India's nuclear generation (2024–25):** 56,681 million units (MU).

**Future expansion:** India targets nuclear capacity expansion to 100 GW by 2047 (*Viksit Bharat*); 11 more reactors under construction (8,700 MW combined).

## Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB)

The **Atomic Energy Regulatory Board (AERB)** was constituted in 1983 under the **Atomic Energy Act, 1962**, to carry out safety oversight functions.

**Functions:**
- Prescribes safety standards and codes for nuclear installations and radiation facilities
- Grants licences for siting, construction, and operation of nuclear power plants
- Regulatory oversight of all radiation applications (industrial, medical, research)
- Inspects nuclear facilities for safety compliance
- **Incident notification:** Under the Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act 2010, AERB must be notified of any nuclear incident within 15 days

**Restructuring:** The government proposed replacing AERB with the **Nuclear Safety Regulatory Authority (NSRA)** for greater independence; the NSRA Bill has been drafted but not yet enacted as of 2026.

## Radiation Safety

### Ionising vs Non-Ionising Radiation

| Type | Examples | Biological Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Ionising radiation** | Alpha, beta, gamma, X-rays, neutrons | Breaks chemical bonds; damages DNA; causes cancer, radiation sickness |
| **Non-ionising radiation** | UV, visible light, infrared, microwave, radio waves | Generally less harmful; UV causes sunburn; high-intensity microwave causes heating |

### Units of Radiation Measurement

| Unit | Measures | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| **Becquerel (Bq)** | Activity | 1 radioactive decay per second |
| **Gray (Gy)** | Absorbed dose | 1 joule of radiation energy absorbed per kg of matter |
| **Sievert (Sv)** | Effective dose | Accounts for biological effect of radiation type; 1 Sv = Gray × radiation weighting factor |

**ALARA Principle:** Radiation doses should be kept **As Low As Reasonably Achievable** — the guiding principle of radiation protection.

### Radiation Effects on Humans

| Dose (Sv) | Effect |
|---|---|
| 0.001–0.01 | Background / typical medical exposure; no measurable harm |
| 0.1–0.5 | Temporary blood count changes |
| 1–2 | Mild radiation sickness (nausea, fatigue); most recover |
| 2–6 | Severe radiation sickness; bone marrow damage; mortality risk |
| >6 | Lethal in most cases without treatment |

## Nuclear Accidents: Lessons Learned

### Chernobyl (April 26, 1986)

- **Location:** Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant, Ukrainian SSR (now Ukraine)
- **Reactor type:** RBMK — graphite-moderated, prone to instability at low power
- **Cause:** Safety test conducted with reactor at low power; reactivity surge caused explosion; graphite fire spread radioactive material
- **INES rating:** Level 7 (highest)
- **Consequences:** ~30 direct deaths; long-term radiation-related cancer deaths estimated in thousands to tens of thousands; 350,000 people evacuated; exclusion zone remains
- **Estimated damage:** $235–700 billion

### Fukushima Daiichi (March 11, 2011)

- **Location:** Fukushima Prefecture, Japan
- **Cause:** Magnitude 9.0 earthquake triggered 15-metre tsunami that overwhelmed the plant's 5.7m seawall; loss of cooling power led to three reactor meltdowns
- **INES rating:** Level 7
- **Reactor type:** BWR (Boiling Water Reactor) — same fundamental design as Tarapur Units 1–2
- **Consequences:** No direct radiation deaths; 154,000 evacuated; radioactive water management challenge persists; estimated damage $400–445 billion
- **Lessons for India:** Seismic/tsunami safety review of coastal nuclear plants (Madras, Kaiga, Kudankulam); AERB conducted safety reviews of all Indian plants post-Fukushima

## Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act, 2010

Enacted to provide a legal framework for **compensation** to victims of nuclear accidents and to enable India to join the international nuclear liability regime.

**Key provisions:**
- Operator (NPCIL) is strictly liable for nuclear damage up to ₹1,500 crore
- Central government liable beyond that, up to SDR 300 million (~₹2,100 crore)
- **Controversial Section 17(b):** The operator has the **right of recourse** against suppliers if the accident was caused by a latent defect in equipment or substandard services

**Controversy:** International suppliers (GE, Westinghouse) and the US/Russia argued Section 17(b) went beyond the international norm (Paris Convention/Vienna Convention) which places liability exclusively on the operator. This delayed US-India nuclear commerce for years after the 2008 Indo-US Nuclear Deal.

**SHANTI Act, 2026:** The government introduced the Strategic Handshake and Nuclear Trade Initiative (SHANTI) Act in 2026 to amend the liability regime and facilitate private sector entry into nuclear power, particularly with international suppliers. The Act has been controversial — critics argue it dilutes nuclear accountability.

## Nuclear Medicine

Nuclear medicine uses radioactive isotopes (*radiopharmaceuticals*) for diagnosis and treatment.

| Application | Isotope | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| PET (Positron Emission Tomography) scans | Fluorine-18 (FDG) | Cancer detection; brain function imaging |
| SPECT scans | Technetium-99m | Cardiac, bone, thyroid imaging |
| Thyroid cancer treatment | Iodine-131 | Selectively absorbed by thyroid; destroys cancer cells |
| Bone pain palliation | Strontium-89 | Bone metastases |
| External beam radiotherapy | Cobalt-60 | Cancer treatment |
| Sterilisation of medical devices | Gamma radiation (Co-60) | Kills microorganisms |

**Bhabha Atomic Research Centre (BARC):** Produces radioisotopes for medical and industrial use in India; operates research reactors (Dhruva, Apsara) and the Board of Radiation and Isotope Technology (BRIT) for medical isotope supply.

## Exam Strategy

**For Prelims:**
- Three types of radiation: alpha (helium nucleus, lowest penetration), beta (electron), gamma (EM, highest penetration)
- C-14 half-life: 5,730 years; used for dating up to ~60,000 years
- India's thorium reserves: ~25% of world; uranium: ~1–2%
- Stage 1: PHWRs, natural uranium, heavy water moderator, NPCIL
- Stage 2: FBRs, plutonium fuel, liquid sodium coolant, PFBR at Kalpakkam (500 MW)
- Stage 3: Thorium-based reactors, U-233 fuel
- Kudankulam: Russian VVER-1000 reactor; largest NPP in India (2,000 MW)
- AERB: constituted 1983, under Atomic Energy Act 1962
- Chernobyl: 1986, RBMK reactor, INES Level 7; Fukushima: 2011, BWR, INES Level 7
- CLNDA 2010: Section 17(b) supplier liability (controversial); SHANTI Act 2026

**For Mains:**
- India's three-stage programme: explain each stage, rationale (uranium scarcity + thorium abundance), current status
- Nuclear safety: institutional framework (AERB), ALARA principle, lessons from Chernobyl/Fukushima
- CLNDA 2010 controversy: how Section 17(b) hindered civilian nuclear commerce
- Nuclear medicine: role of radioisotopes in healthcare; BARC's contribution
- India's energy security: nuclear power's share; challenges (uranium supply, safety, waste); ITER participation
