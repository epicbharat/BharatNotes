## What is the Strait of Hormuz?

The **Strait of Hormuz** is a narrow, strategically vital waterway located between the **Persian Gulf** and the **Gulf of Oman**, connecting the oil-rich Gulf region to the open **Arabian Sea and Indian Ocean**. It has **Iran** on its northern coast and the **Musandam Peninsula** (shared by **Oman and the UAE**) on its southern coast. The strait is approximately **167 km (104 miles) long** and varies in width from **about 96 km (60 miles) to only 39 km (24 miles)** at its narrowest point.

The Strait of Hormuz is the **world's most important oil transit chokepoint**. During **2023-2025**, approximately **20% of the world's liquefied natural gas (LNG)** and **25% of all seaborne oil trade** passed through the strait annually. In 2025, an average of **20 million barrels per day (mb/d)** of crude oil and petroleum products were shipped through it. There is **no alternative route** for the major Gulf oil exporters (Saudi Arabia, Iraq, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar) to reach global markets in comparable volumes, making the strait indispensable to the **global energy supply chain**.

Shipping through the strait follows a **Traffic Separation Scheme (TSS)**: two **2-mile-wide** shipping lanes (one inbound, one outbound) separated by a **2-mile-wide buffer zone**. These shipping lanes are mostly in **Omani territorial waters**. Both Iran (1959) and Oman (1972) extended their territorial seas to **12 nautical miles**, meaning the strait is entirely within the combined territorial waters of the two countries, though international law guarantees the right of **transit passage** through straits used for international navigation.

## Current Status / Latest Data

- The **Strait of Hormuz** remains the world's most critical energy chokepoint amid ongoing geopolitical tensions between **Iran and Western powers**.
- In **March 2026**, media reports highlighted renewed concerns about the strait as tensions between the US and Iran escalated over nuclear negotiations and regional conflicts.
- **India** is heavily dependent on the strait -- approximately **60-65% of India's crude oil imports** pass through it, making any disruption a direct threat to India's energy security.
- Saudi Arabia's **East-West Pipeline** and the UAE's **Abu Dhabi Crude Oil Pipeline (ADCOP)** provide limited bypass capacity, but together can handle only about **6.5 mb/d** vs. the ~20 mb/d that passes through the strait.
- The US **Fifth Fleet** (based in Bahrain) maintains a permanent naval presence to ensure freedom of navigation.

## UPSC Exam Corner

### Prelims: Key Facts
- Connects **Persian Gulf** to **Gulf of Oman** (and the Arabian Sea)
- Bordered by **Iran** (north) and **Oman/UAE** (south)
- **25% of global seaborne oil** and **20% of global LNG** transits through it
- Narrowest point: **~39 km (24 miles)**
- India depends on the strait for **60-65% of crude oil imports**
- Bypass pipelines: Saudi East-West Pipeline + UAE ADCOP (~6.5 mb/d combined)

### Mains: Probable Themes
1. **Geopolitical significance** of the Strait of Hormuz -- global energy security and power dynamics
2. India's **energy vulnerability**: dependence on Gulf oil through the strait and need for diversification
3. **Maritime security** in the Persian Gulf -- Iran's asymmetric naval capabilities, US presence, and regional tensions
4. **Alternative routes and pipelines** to bypass the strait -- feasibility and limitations
5. Impact of a **potential closure** of the strait on global oil prices, India's economy, and the developing world
