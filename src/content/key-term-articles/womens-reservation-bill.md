## What is the Women's Reservation Bill?

The Women's Reservation Bill, officially enacted as the **Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam** through the **106th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2023**, reserves **one-third (33%) of seats** in the **Lok Sabha, State Legislative Assemblies, and the Delhi Legislative Assembly** for women. Passed in September 2023 with near-unanimous support in both Houses of Parliament, it represents the culmination of a **27-year legislative struggle** that began with the first bill introduced in **1996**.

The Act inserts new Articles **330A and 332A** into the Constitution, providing for reservation of seats for women in the Lok Sabha and State Assemblies respectively, including **one-third of seats reserved for SCs and STs**. The rotation of reserved constituencies will be determined after each delimitation exercise. The reservation is set to remain in force for **15 years** from commencement, though Parliament may extend it.

A critical caveat is that the Act's implementation is **tied to the completion of a census and subsequent delimitation exercise**. Since no census has been conducted after 2011, and delimitation based on a new census could take years, the Act is **not yet operational**. However, as of March 2026, the government is actively exploring **amending the Act to delink it from fresh delimitation**, potentially using the **2011 census as the baseline** to fast-track implementation before the **2029 Lok Sabha elections**.

The significance of this legislation cannot be overstated. India ranks **148th out of 193 countries** in women's parliamentary representation (IPU data), below global average of ~26%. At the **local body level**, where 33-50% reservation has been in place since 1993, women's representation in panchayats exceeds **46% nationally** — demonstrating that reservation is an effective tool for increasing women's political participation. Studies show that women-led panchayats invest more in **public goods like drinking water, roads, and education**, and female sarpanches serve as **role models** that shift community attitudes toward women's leadership. The 106th Amendment seeks to replicate this transformative impact at the national and state legislative levels.

## Current Status / Latest Data

- **Not yet operational**: The Act awaits census and delimitation — both pending as of March 2026.
- **March 2026**: The government initiated consultations to **amend the Act and delink it from fresh delimitation**, using the 2011 census as the legal trigger. This would require a fresh constitutional amendment.
- A **special Parliament session** is reportedly being planned to pass the necessary constitutional amendment for fast-tracking implementation before the 2029 Lok Sabha elections.
- **Political debate**: DMK leader Kanimozhi and CM M.K. Stalin raised concerns about the timing and methodology of using the 2011 census for delimitation; opposition parties suspect a broader delimitation agenda behind the move.
- **Current women representation**: Women constitute only about **15% of Lok Sabha** (82 of 543 seats in the 18th Lok Sabha, 2024) and approximately **10% of State Assembly members** nationwide.
- The Act does **not apply to the Rajya Sabha or State Legislative Councils**.
- The bill's **27-year journey**: Introduced in 1996, 1998, 1999; passed by Rajya Sabha in 2010; lapsed; finally passed in September 2023 during a special session in the new Parliament building.
- **Contrast with local bodies**: The 73rd and 74th Amendments (1993) already provide **33% reservation for women in panchayats and municipalities**, with many states (Bihar, Chhattisgarh, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh) having raised this to **50%**. Women's representation in panchayats exceeds 46% nationally, demonstrating the transformative impact of reservation.
- **OBC/Muslim women sub-quota demand**: AIMIM and other parties demanded a sub-quota within the 33% for OBC and minority women, which was not included in the final Act.

## UPSC Exam Corner

### Prelims: Key Facts
- 106th Amendment Act, 2023 — Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam
- Reserves 33% seats in Lok Sabha and State Assemblies for women
- Implementation linked to census + delimitation (not yet operational as of March 2026)
- Does NOT apply to Rajya Sabha or Legislative Councils
- 15-year sunset clause with option for Parliamentary extension
- Inserts Articles 330A and 332A into the Constitution
- 73rd/74th Amendments already provide 33% reservation for women in local bodies (many states raised to 50%)
- Women's representation in 18th Lok Sabha: ~15% (82 of 543 seats)

### Mains: Probable Themes
1. Critically evaluate the Women's Reservation Act, 2023 in terms of its potential impact on women's political participation in India
2. "The linking of women's reservation to delimitation has effectively postponed gender justice." Discuss
3. Analyse the arguments for and against sub-quotas for OBC and minority women within the 33% reservation
4. Compare the trajectory of women's reservation in local bodies (73rd/74th Amendments) with the delayed progress at the national level
