## What is Cultural Imperialism?

Cultural imperialism refers to the **domination of one culture over others** through the spread of values, norms, lifestyles, and media of a powerful nation — typically Western industrialised countries — at the expense of indigenous cultural identities. The concept, rooted in the works of scholars like **Herbert Schiller** and **Edward Said**, argues that globalisation is not culturally neutral but serves as a vehicle for **Western cultural hegemony**.

In the Indian context, cultural imperialism manifests through the growing influence of **Western consumerism, English-language dominance, Hollywood and global OTT platforms, fast-food chains, and corporate culture** on India's diverse traditions. Critics argue that what is promoted as "global culture" is largely a projection of **American and European values**, leading to the erosion of indigenous languages, art forms, and social norms. India, with its **22 scheduled languages and over 1,600 dialects**, faces particular vulnerability in linguistic homogenisation.

However, India's response to cultural imperialism is not one of passive acceptance. The concept of **glocalisation** — where local cultures selectively adopt and adapt global influences — offers a counter-narrative. Scholars like **Roland Robertson** argue that Indian society has demonstrated resilience through cultural hybridisation, blending global trends with local traditions (e.g., Bollywood's global reach, Indian cuisine's worldwide popularity, yoga's international adoption).

The debate around cultural imperialism in India operates at multiple levels. At the **macro level**, it concerns the dominance of English as the language of aspiration, global media conglomerates shaping narratives, and MNCs displacing local businesses and consumption patterns. At the **micro level**, it manifests in changing family structures, dating cultures, dietary habits (rise of fast food over traditional cuisine), and shifting value systems among urban youth. The **transformationalist perspective** (Anthony Giddens, Arjun Appadurai) offers a middle ground, arguing that globalisation creates new cultural forms rather than simply destroying old ones — Indian hip-hop, fusion music, and regional-language digital content are examples of this creative adaptation.

## Current Status / Latest Data

- India's **OTT market** has grown to become the world's fastest-expanding, with platforms like JioCinema, Hotstar, and ZEE5 producing substantial regional-language content — a counter to English-language dominance.
- **NEP 2020** mandates education in the mother tongue/regional language up to Class 5, aiming to reverse linguistic imperialism and promote multilingualism.
- India's **soft power exports** — yoga (International Day of Yoga since 2015), Ayurveda, Bollywood, cuisine, classical dance — represent a reverse cultural flow, challenging the one-way imperialism thesis.
- **UNESCO** classifies **197 Indian languages as endangered**, highlighting the ongoing threat of linguistic homogenisation driven by English and Hindi dominance.
- Religious and cultural revivalist movements continue as a response to perceived cultural erosion from Western globalisation.
- **Social media and digital platforms** have become new arenas of cultural imperialism, with Western-origin platforms (Instagram, YouTube, X) shaping aspirations, beauty standards, and consumer behaviour among Indian youth.
- The **"Make in India" and "Vocal for Local"** campaigns have an implicit cultural dimension, encouraging indigenous consumption patterns against global brand dominance.
- India's celebration of **International Mother Language Day** (21 February) and promotion of **Bhasha Certificates** reflect institutional efforts to preserve linguistic diversity.

## UPSC Exam Corner

### Prelims: Key Facts
- Herbert Schiller coined the term "cultural imperialism" in his 1976 work *Communication and Cultural Domination*
- Glocalisation = global + local; concept popularised by Roland Robertson
- UNESCO lists 197 Indian languages as endangered
- NEP 2020 promotes mother-tongue instruction up to Class 5
- International Day of Yoga was adopted by UNGA in 2014, celebrated since 21 June 2015
- India has 22 scheduled languages under the Eighth Schedule
- "McDonaldisation" (George Ritzer) describes the spread of Western fast-food culture and efficiency norms globally

### Mains: Probable Themes
1. "Globalisation is cultural imperialism in disguise." Critically examine this statement with Indian examples
2. Discuss how glocalisation challenges the thesis of cultural homogenisation in India
3. Evaluate the impact of Western media dominance on India's linguistic and cultural diversity
4. How has India leveraged its soft power to counter cultural imperialism on the global stage?
5. Analyse the role of digital platforms and social media in accelerating cultural homogenisation among Indian youth
